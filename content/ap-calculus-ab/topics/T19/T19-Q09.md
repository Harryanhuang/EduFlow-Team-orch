<question>
Let $F(x) = \int_0^x f(t)\,dt$ where f is continuous on [0, b]. Show that $F'(x) = f(x)$ using the limit definition of the derivative and the definition of the definite integral as a limit of Riemann sums.
</question>
<answer>
**Goal:** Prove the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Part I:
$$F(x) = \int_0^x f(t)\,dt \implies F'(x) = f(x)$$

**Step 1: Write F'(x) using the limit definition**

$$F'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{F(x+h) - F(x)}{h}$$

Substitute the definition of F:
$$F'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\int_0^{x+h} f(t)\,dt - \int_0^x f(t)\,dt}{h}$$

**Step 2: Use properties of definite integrals**

By the additivity property of integrals:
$$\int_0^{x+h} f(t)\,dt = \int_0^x f(t)\,dt + \int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt$$

Therefore:
$$F(x+h) - F(x) = \int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt$$

So:
$$F'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt}{h}$$

**Step 3: Express the integral as a Riemann sum**

For h ≠ 0, divide [x, x+h] into n subintervals with $\Delta t = h/n$:

$$\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(t_i^*)\,\Delta t = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(t_i^*)\,\frac{h}{n}$$

**Step 4: Factor out h and apply the limit**

$$\frac{\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt}{h} = \frac{h}{h} \cdot \left(\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(t_i^*)\right)$$

As h → 0 and n → ∞ (with the partition norm → 0):
- The subinterval [x, x+h] shrinks to the single point x
- By continuity of f, all sample points $t_i^*$ approach x
- Therefore $f(t_i^*) \to f(x)$ for all i

**Step 5: Apply the Mean Value Theorem for Integrals**

Since f is continuous on [x, x+h], there exists c ∈ [x, x+h] such that:
$$\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt = f(c) \cdot h$$

Thus:
$$\frac{\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt}{h} = f(c)$$

As h → 0, c → x (by squeeze on the interval), and by continuity:
$$\lim_{h \to 0} f(c) = f(x)$$

**Step 6: Conclude**

$$F'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt}{h} = f(x)$$

$$\boxed{F'(x) = f(x)}$$

**Alternative viewpoint using Riemann sums directly:**

Since $\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt \approx f(x) \cdot h$ for small h (using any left/midpoint/right sample):
$$\frac{1}{h}\int_x^{x+h} f(t)\,dt \to f(x) \quad \text{as} \quad h \to 0$$

This follows because as the interval shrinks, the Riemann sum's sample values all converge to f(x) by continuity.

This completes the proof that the derivative of the accumulation function is the original integrand.
</answer>
