The graph of $y = f'(x)$, the derivative of $f$, crosses the $x$-axis at $x = -2$, $x = 0$, and $x = 2$. The graph shows that $f'(x)$ is negative on $(-\infty, -2)$, positive on $(-2, 0)$, negative on $(0, 2)$, and positive on $(2, \infty)$. Which statement about the original function $f$ is false?

A) $f$ has a local minimum at $x = -2$
B) $f$ has a local maximum at $x = 0$
C) $f$ has a local minimum at $x = 2$
D) $f$ is decreasing on the interval $(-2, 0)$

## Answer
D

## Explanation
Use the First Derivative Test by reading the sign of $f'$:
- At $x = -2$, $f'$ changes from negative to positive, so $f$ has a local minimum. A is true.
- At $x = 0$, $f'$ changes from positive to negative, so $f$ has a local maximum. B is true.
- At $x = 2$, $f'$ changes from negative to positive, so $f$ has a local minimum. C is true.
- On $(-2, 0)$, $f'(x) > 0$, so $f$ is increasing, not decreasing. D is false.

Therefore the false statement is D.
