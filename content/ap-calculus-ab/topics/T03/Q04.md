## Question
Explain why the function \( f(x) = \dfrac{1}{x - 2} \) is discontinuous at \( x = 2 \), and classify the type of discontinuity. Could redefining \( f(2) \) make the function continuous at \( x = 2 \)?

## Answer
\( f(2) \) is undefined (division by zero), so the first condition of continuity fails. As \( x \to 2^+ \), \( f(x) \to +\infty \), and as \( x \to 2^- \), \( f(x) \to -\infty \). The limit does not exist and the function grows without bound, so this is an **infinite discontinuity** (vertical asymptote).

No, redefining \( f(2) \) cannot make the function continuous because the limit \( \displaystyle\lim_{x \to 2} f(x) \) does not exist (it diverges to \( \pm\infty \)). No finite value assigned at \( x = 2 \) can equal a nonexistent limit.
