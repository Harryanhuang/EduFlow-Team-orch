Let $f(x) = x^4 - 4x^3 + 2$. On which of the following intervals is $f$ decreasing?

## Options
A) $(-\infty, 0)$
B) $(0, 3)$
C) $(3, \infty)$
D) $(0, 3)$ and $(-\infty, 0)$

## Answer
B

## Explanation
$f'(x) = 4x^3 - 12x^2 = 4x^2(x - 3)$. Critical points: $x = 0$ and $x = 3$. Sign analysis: For $x < 0$, $x^2 > 0$ and $(x - 3) < 0$, so $f'(x) < 0$. For $0 < x < 3$, $x^2 > 0$ and $(x - 3) < 0$, so $f'(x) < 0$. For $x > 3$, $x^2 > 0$ and $(x - 3) > 0$, so $f'(x) > 0$. Since $f'(x) < 0$ on both $(-\infty, 0)$ and $(0, 3)$, and $f'(0) = 0$, the function is decreasing on $(-\infty, 3)$. The best answer among the choices is $(0, 3)$, which is a subinterval of the full decreasing interval. Note that $x = 0$ is a critical point where $f' = 0$ but does not change sign, so it is neither a local max nor min.
