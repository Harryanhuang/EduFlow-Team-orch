The graph of \(y = f(x)\) has the following behavior near \(x = 4\): as \(x\) approaches 4 from the left, \(f(x)\) approaches 7; as \(x\) approaches 4 from the right, \(f(x)\) approaches 2. Which statement is true?

## Options
A) \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 4} f(x) = 7\)
B) \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 4} f(x) = 2\)
C) \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 4} f(x) = 4.5\)
D) \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 4} f(x)\) does not exist.

## Answer
D

## Explanation
For the (two-sided) limit to exist, the left-hand limit and the right-hand limit must agree. Here \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 4^-} f(x) = 7\) and \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 4^+} f(x) = 2\). Since \(7 \neq 2\), the two-sided limit does not exist. This is a classic jump discontinuity scenario.
