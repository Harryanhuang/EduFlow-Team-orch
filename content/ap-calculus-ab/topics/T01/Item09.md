The function \(f\) is graphed below (described). The graph shows:
- For \(x < -1\): a line segment ending at an open circle at \((-1, 2)\)
- For \(-1 < x < 2\): a horizontal line at \(y = 1\), with a closed dot at \((-1, 1)\)
- For \(x > 2\): a line starting at an open circle at \((2, 1)\) and increasing

What is \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to -1} f(x)\)?

## Options
A) 1
B) 2
C) The limit does not exist.
D) Cannot be determined from the description.

## Answer
C

## Explanation
As \(x \to -1^-\), the graph approaches \(y = 2\) (the open circle). As \(x \to -1^+\), the graph stays at \(y = 1\) (the horizontal line). Since \(\lim_{x \to -1^-} f(x) = 2 \neq 1 = \lim_{x \to -1^+} f(x)\), the two-sided limit does not exist. The closed dot at \((-1, 1)\) tells us \(f(-1) = 1\), but that does not affect the limit.
