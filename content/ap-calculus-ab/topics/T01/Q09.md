**Question:** Consider \(f(x) = \begin{cases} \sin\left(\frac{1}{x}\right), & x \neq 0 \\ 0, & x = 0 \end{cases}\). Prove rigorously that \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} f(x)\) does not exist by exhibiting two sequences \(\{x_n\}\) and \(\{y_n\}\), both converging to 0, such that \(\lim_{n \to \infty} f(x_n) \neq \lim_{n \to \infty} f(y_n)\). Explain why this sequential criterion proves the limit does not exist.

**Answer:** Choose \(x_n = \dfrac{1}{2\pi n}\) and \(y_n = \dfrac{1}{\frac{\pi}{2} + 2\pi n}\). Both sequences converge to 0 as \(n \to \infty\). Now: \(f(x_n) = \sin(2\pi n) = 0\) for all \(n\), so \(\lim_{n \to \infty} f(x_n) = 0\). And \(f(y_n) = \sin\left(\frac{\pi}{2} + 2\pi n\right) = 1\) for all \(n\), so \(\lim_{n \to \infty} f(y_n) = 1\). Since \(0 \neq 1\), two different sequences approaching 0 produce different limit values for \(f\). By the sequential criterion for limits (if \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L\), then for every sequence \(x_n \to a\) with \(x_n \neq a\), we must have \(f(x_n) \to L\)), the limit does not exist.
