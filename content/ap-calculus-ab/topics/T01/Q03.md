**Question:** State the formal \(\varepsilon\)-\(\delta\) definition of \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L\). Explain the roles of \(\varepsilon\) and \(\delta\) and why the definition uses \(0 < |x - a|\) rather than \(|x - a|\).

**Answer:** For every \(\varepsilon > 0\), there exists a \(\delta > 0\) such that if \(0 < |x - a| < \delta\), then \(|f(x) - L| < \varepsilon\). The role of \(\varepsilon\) is to specify how close we want \(f(x)\) to be to \(L\) (the "challenge"). The role of \(\delta\) is to specify how close \(x\) must be to \(a\) to guarantee that closeness (the "response"). The condition \(0 < |x - a|\) means \(x \neq a\); the limit depends only on values of \(f\) near \(a\), not at \(a\) itself. This is why a function can have a limit at a point where it is undefined or has a different value.
