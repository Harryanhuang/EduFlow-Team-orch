<question>
Let \(f(x) = |3x - 6|\).

(a) Find \(f'(3)\).

(b) Explain why \(f'(2)\) does or does not exist.
</question>
<answer>
(a) For \(x > 2\), \(3x - 6 > 0\), so \(|3x - 6| = 3x - 6\).

Therefore \(f'(x) = 3\) for all \(x > 2\), and

\[f'(3) = 3.\]

(b) At \(x = 2\), the expression inside the absolute value is zero. We check the left- and right-hand derivatives.

Left-hand derivative (\(x < 2\)): \(|3x - 6| = -(3x - 6) = -3x + 6\), so the derivative is \(-3\).

Right-hand derivative (\(x > 2\)): \(|3x - 6| = 3x - 6\), so the derivative is \(3\).

Since \(-3 \neq 3\), the derivative **does not exist** at \(x = 2\). The graph has a sharp corner at this point.
</answer>
