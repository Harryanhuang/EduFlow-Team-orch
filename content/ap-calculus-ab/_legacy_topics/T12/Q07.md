The rate at which a bacteria culture grows is given by $P'(t) = \frac{500}{1 + e^{-0.5t}}$ bacteria per hour, where $t$ is measured in hours. Initially there are 100 bacteria.

(a) Explain why the population is always increasing.
(b) Is the population growing faster at $t = 2$ or $t = 6$? Justify without computing the values of $P'(2)$ and $P'(6)$.
(c) Find the limiting value of $P'(t)$ as $t \to \infty$. What does this tell you about the long-term growth rate?

## Answer
(a) $P'(t) = \frac{500}{1 + e^{-0.5t}}$. Since $e^{-0.5t} > 0$ for all $t$, the denominator $1 + e^{-0.5t} > 1 > 0$, so $P'(t) > 0$ for all $t$. Therefore the population is always increasing.

(b) The derivative of $P'(t)$ determines whether the growth rate is increasing or decreasing. $P''(t) = 500 \cdot \frac{0.5e^{-0.5t}}{(1 + e^{-0.5t})^2} > 0$ for all $t$ (since numerator and denominator are both positive). So $P'(t)$ is strictly increasing, meaning the culture grows faster at $t = 6$ than at $t = 2$.

(c) As $t \to \infty$, $e^{-0.5t} \to 0$, so $P'(t) \to \frac{500}{1 + 0} = 500$ bacteria per hour. The growth rate approaches 500 bacteria per hour as a horizontal asymptote — the culture approaches a constant maximum growth rate rather than unlimited exponential growth.
