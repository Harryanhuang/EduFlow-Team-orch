<question>
Prove that for a continuous function f on the closed interval [a, b], the limit as n → ∞ of the Riemann sum approaches the definite integral. Begin with the definition of the definite integral as a limit of Riemann sums.
</question>
<answer>
**Theorem:** If f is continuous on [a, b], then f is integrable on [a, b]; that is, $\lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*)\,\Delta x$ exists and equals $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$.

**Proof:**

*Step 1: Partition and Riemann sum*

Let P = {x₀, x₁, ..., xₙ} be a regular partition of [a, b] with:
$$\Delta x = \frac{b-a}{n}$$

Choose sample points $x_i^* \in [x_{i-1}, x_i]$ for each subinterval.

The Riemann sum is:
$$S_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*)\,\Delta x$$

*Step 2: Upper and lower sums*

Since f is continuous on [a, b], it is also bounded and uniformly continuous on that closed interval.

For each subinterval [x_{i-1}, x_i], let:
- $M_i = \sup\{f(x) : x \in [x_{i-1}, x_i]\}$
- $m_i = \inf\{f(x) : x \in [x_{i-1}, x_i]\}$

The upper sum: $U_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} M_i\,\Delta x$
The lower sum: $L_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} m_i\,\Delta x$

*Step 3: Convergence of partition norm to zero*

As $n \to \infty$, the mesh (norm) of the partition $\mu(P) = \max\{\Delta x_i\} \to 0$ because $\Delta x = (b-a)/n \to 0$.

*Step 4: Use uniform continuity*

For any $\epsilon > 0$, by uniform continuity of f, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $|x - y| < \delta$ implies $|f(x) - f(y)| < \epsilon/(b-a)$.

When $n > (b-a)/\delta$, we have $\Delta x < \delta$, so for each subinterval:
$$M_i - m_i < \frac{\epsilon}{b-a}$$

Therefore:
$$U_n - L_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (M_i - m_i)\,\Delta x < \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\epsilon}{b-a}\,\Delta x = \frac{\epsilon}{b-a}\cdot(b-a) = \epsilon$$

*Step 5: Riemann's criterion*

Since $U_n - L_n \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$, and $L_n \leq S_n \leq U_n$ for any choice of sample points, all Riemann sums (left, right, midpoint, or any tagged partition) converge to the same limit.

*Conclusion:*

By the definition of the definite integral as a limit of Riemann sums:
$$\boxed{\lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*)\,\Delta x = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx}$$

This holds for any continuous function f on [a, b], completing the proof.
</answer>
