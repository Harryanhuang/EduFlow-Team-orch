<question>
The trapezoidal rule $T_n$ approximates $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$. If $f$ is concave down on $[a, b]$, is $T_n$ an over- or under-approximation? Explain using the geometry of trapezoids.
</question>
<answer>
If $f$ is concave down on $[a, b]$, the trapezoidal rule $T_n$ is an **underestimate** of $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$.

The trapezoidal rule approximates the area under the curve by summing the areas of trapezoids formed by connecting the endpoints of each subinterval to the $x$-axis. When $f$ is concave down, the graph of $f$ lies **above** the line segment joining any two points on the curve. Geometrically, this means each trapezoid's top edge (the secant line) falls below the actual curve on that subinterval.

Since the trapezoids are drawn below the curve, their total area is less than the true signed area under the curve, resulting in an underestimate.
</answer>
