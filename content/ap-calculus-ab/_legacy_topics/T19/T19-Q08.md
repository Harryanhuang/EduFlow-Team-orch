<question>
A car's velocity is recorded at 5-second intervals: v(0) = 0, v(5) = 20, v(10) = 35, v(15) = 45, v(20) = 50 (in ft/s). Using this data, explain how to determine whether the left Riemann sum or the right Riemann sum gives a closer estimate to the actual distance traveled. Your analysis should include the role of concavity.
</question>
<answer>
**Given Data:**
$$\Delta t = 5\text{ s}, \quad t \in [0, 20]$$
$$v(0) = 0,\quad v(5) = 20,\quad v(10) = 35,\quad v(15) = 45,\quad v(20) = 50 \text{ ft/s}$$

**Step 1: Calculate both estimates**

*Left Riemann Sum (LRS):*
$$L = v(0)\Delta t + v(5)\Delta t + v(10)\Delta t + v(15)\Delta t$$
$$L = 5(0 + 20 + 35 + 45) = 5(100) = 500 \text{ ft}$$

*Right Riemann Sum (RRS):*
$$R = v(5)\Delta t + v(10)\Delta t + v(15)\Delta t + v(20)\Delta t$$
$$R = 5(20 + 35 + 45 + 50) = 5(150) = 750 \text{ ft}$$

*Average of the two:*
$$M = \frac{L + R}{2} = \frac{500 + 750}{2} = 625 \text{ ft}$$

**Step 2: Analyze the velocity function's behavior**

We examine the differences (approximating the acceleration):
- v(0→5): 20 ft/s increase over 5 s → avg accel = 4 ft/s²
- v(5→10): 15 ft/s increase → avg accel = 3 ft/s²
- v(10→15): 10 ft/s increase → avg accel = 2 ft/s²
- v(15→20): 5 ft/s increase → avg accel = 1 ft/s²

The velocity is **increasing** but the rate of increase is **decreasing** → velocity curve is **concave down**.

**Step 3: Apply the concavity rule for Riemann sums**

*For an increasing function* (v(t) is increasing):
- Left sum **underestimates** (rectangles below the curve)
- Right sum **overestimates** (rectangles above the curve)

*For a concave down function*:
- The midpoint sum is particularly accurate because the trapezoid (average of left and right) aligns with the actual curved area
- The trapezoidal rule $T = \frac{\Delta t}{2}(v_0 + 2v_5 + 2v_{10} + 2v_{15} + v_{20})$ gives: $T = \frac{5}{2}(0 + 2(20) + 2(35) + 2(45) + 50) = \frac{5}{2}(250) = 625$ ft

**Step 4: Determine which sum is closer**

Since the function is concave down:
- The left sum (500 ft) and right sum (750 ft) bracket the true value
- The midpoint or trapezoidal estimate (625 ft) is the most accurate single estimate
- The true distance is between 500 and 750 ft

To determine which is *closer* without knowing the true value:
- As n increases, both sums converge to the true value
- For a concave down function, the error in the left sum is roughly proportional to the function's second derivative (more negative → larger error)
- The **left sum underestimates less severely** than the right sum overestimates (due to the diminishing slope)

**Conclusion:**

For this concave down velocity function:
- The left Riemann sum (500 ft) gives an **underestimate**
- The right Riemann sum (750 ft) gives an **overestimate**
- The average (625 ft) best approximates the actual distance traveled
- Increasing n would tighten both estimates toward the true integral

The concavity tells us that neither sum is equally biased — the trapezoidal rule (geometric average of L and R) is the natural "best estimate" from endpoint-based approximations.
</answer>
