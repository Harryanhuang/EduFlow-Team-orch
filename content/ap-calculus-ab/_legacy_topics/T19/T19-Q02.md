<question>
If $f$ is increasing on $[a, b]$, explain why the left Riemann sum is an underestimate and the right Riemann sum is an overestimate of $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$.
</question>
<answer>
For an increasing function $f$, the left Riemann sum uses the left endpoint of each subinterval as the height of each rectangle. Since $f$ is increasing, the left endpoint is the **minimum** value of $f$ on that subinterval, so each rectangle underestimates the area under the curve. Summing these gives an underestimate of the integral.

The right Riemann sum uses the right endpoint as the height. On each subinterval, the right endpoint is the **maximum** value of $f$ (again because $f$ is increasing), so each rectangle overestimates the area. Thus the right sum is an overestimate of $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$.

Geometrically, the left rectangles fall entirely below the curve, while the right rectangles extend above it.
</answer>
