<question>
A particle moves with velocity $v(t)$. Explain how to use a Riemann sum to estimate the particle's total distance traveled from $t = a$ to $t = b$. How does this differ from estimating displacement?
</question>
<answer>
**Estimating total distance:**
To estimate total distance traveled, use a Riemann sum of $|v(t)|$:
$$\text{Distance} \approx \sum_{i=1}^{n} |v(t_i^*)|\,\Delta t$$

At each subinterval, take the absolute value of the velocity at the sample point before multiplying by $\Delta t$. This ensures that both positive velocity (forward motion) and negative velocity (backward motion) contribute positively to the sum.

**Estimating displacement:**
Displacement is estimated by a standard Riemann sum of $v(t)$ without absolute values:
$$\text{Displacement} \approx \sum_{i=1}^{n} v(t_i^*)\,\Delta t$$

Positive contributions represent forward motion; negative contributions represent backward motion, which cancel out in the sum.

**Key difference:** Total distance is always nonnegative, while displacement can be positive, negative, or zero. Distance $\geq$ |displacement|, with equality only when the particle never reverses direction.
</answer>
