The derivative of $f(x) = \dfrac{x^2}{\cos x}$ at $x = \pi$ is:

## Options
A) $-2\pi$
B) $2\pi$
C) $\pi^2$
D) $-2\pi + \pi^2$

## Answer
A

## Explanation
Using the quotient rule: $u = x^2$, $v = \cos x$, $u' = 2x$, $v' = -\sin x$.
$f'(x) = \frac{2x \cos x - x^2(-\sin x)}{\cos^2 x} = \frac{2x \cos x + x^2 \sin x}{\cos^2 x}$.
At $x = \pi$: $\cos \pi = -1$, $\sin \pi = 0$.
$f'(\pi) = \frac{2\pi(-1) + \pi^2(0)}{(-1)^2} = \frac{-2\pi}{1} = -2\pi$.
