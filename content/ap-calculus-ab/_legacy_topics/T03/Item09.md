Let \( f(x) = \dfrac{1}{x - 4} \). Which statement is true?

## Options
A) \( f \) has a removable discontinuity at \( x = 4 \).
B) \( f \) has a jump discontinuity at \( x = 4 \).
C) \( f \) has an infinite discontinuity at \( x = 4 \).
D) \( f \) is continuous at \( x = 4 \).

## Answer
C

## Explanation
As \( x \to 4^+ \), \( f(x) \to +\infty \). As \( x \to 4^- \), \( f(x) \to -\infty \). Since the function grows without bound near \( x = 4 \), the discontinuity is infinite (a vertical asymptote). The limit does not exist (even as \( \pm\infty \) from one side alone the two-sided limit does not exist), and no finite redefinition of \( f(4) \) could make the function continuous, so the discontinuity is non-removable.
