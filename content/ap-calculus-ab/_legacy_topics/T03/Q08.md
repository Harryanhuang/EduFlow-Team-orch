## Question
Consider the function
\[
f(x) = \begin{cases}
\dfrac{x^2 - a^2}{x - a}, & x \neq a \\
b, & x = a
\end{cases}
\]
where \( a \) and \( b \) are constants.

(a) For what values of \( a \) (if any) does \( f \) have a removable discontinuity at \( x = a \)? Justify.
(b) For what values of \( b \) (in terms of \( a \)) is \( f \) continuous at \( x = a \)?
(c) Can \( f \) ever have a jump discontinuity at \( x = a \)? Explain.

## Answer
(a) For any real value of \( a \): when \( x \neq a \), \( f(x) = \dfrac{(x+a)(x-a)}{x-a} = x + a \). So \( \displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = 2a \), which exists. However, at \( x = a \) the original expression \( \dfrac{x^2 - a^2}{x - a} \) is undefined (0/0 form). Since the limit exists but the expression is undefined at \( x = a \), the discontinuity is removable for every real \( a \).

(b) For continuity at \( x = a \), we need \( b = \displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = 2a \). So \( b = 2a \).

(c) No. A jump discontinuity requires that the left-hand and right-hand limits both exist as finite values but are unequal. Here both one-sided limits equal \( 2a \), so they always agree. The only discontinuity possible is removable (when \( b \neq 2a \)) or no discontinuity at all (when \( b = 2a \)).
