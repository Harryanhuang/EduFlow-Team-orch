The graph of $f$ is a smooth curve passing through the points $(-1, 0)$, $(0, 1)$, $(1, 2)$, and $(2, 1)$. Estimate $f'(0)$ and $f'(1)$. Describe the behavior of $f'(x)$ as $x$ increases from $-1$ to $2$.

<question>
The graph of $f$ is a smooth curve passing through the points $(-1, 0)$, $(0, 1)$, $(1, 2)$, and $(2, 1)$. Estimate $f'(0)$ and $f'(1)$. Describe the behavior of $f'(x)$ as $x$ increases from $-1$ to $2$.
</question>
<answer>
Estimating $f'(0)$ using secant slopes around $x = 0$:
$$f'(0) \approx \frac{f(1) - f(-1)}{1 - (-1)} = \frac{2 - 0}{2} = 1$$

Estimating $f'(1)$ using secant slopes around $x = 1$:
$$f'(1) \approx \frac{f(2) - f(0)}{2 - 0} = \frac{1 - 1}{2} = 0$$

This makes sense: the point $(1, 2)$ appears to be a local maximum (the curve rises to $(1, 2)$ and then falls), so the tangent there is approximately horizontal.

Behavior of $f'(x)$ as $x$ increases from $-1$ to $2$: $f'(x)$ is positive on $(-1, 1)$ (function increasing), approximately zero at $x = 1$ (horizontal tangent/local max), and negative on $(1, 2)$ (function decreasing). So $f'(x)$ decreases from positive to negative, indicating the graph is concave down.
</answer>
