The graph of \(f(x)\) has a vertical asymptote at \(x = -3\). As \(x \to -3^-\), the graph goes down without bound; as \(x \to -3^+\), the graph goes up without bound. Which is true?

## Options
A) \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to -3} f(x) = \infty\)
B) \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to -3} f(x) = -\infty\)
C) \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to -3} f(x) = 0\)
D) \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to -3} f(x)\) does not exist, and neither one-sided limit is the same signed infinity.

## Answer
D

## Explanation
We have \(\lim_{x \to -3^-} f(x) = -\infty\) and \(\lim_{x \to -3^+} f(x) = +\infty\). The two-sided limit does not exist because the one-sided limits are infinite and of opposite signs. Even if both were \(+\infty\) or both \(-\infty\), we would say the limit equals that infinity (in the extended real sense), but here they disagree in sign.
