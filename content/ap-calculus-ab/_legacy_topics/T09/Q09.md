<question>
A particle moves along a line with position

\[s(t) = e^{-t} \cos(2t).\]

(a) Find the velocity \(v(0)\).

(b) Determine whether the particle is speeding up or slowing down at \(t = 0\). Justify your answer.
</question>
<answer>
(a) Velocity is \(v(t) = s'(t)\). Apply the product rule and chain rule:

\[v(t) = -e^{-t}\cos(2t) + e^{-t}(-\sin(2t) \cdot 2) = -e^{-t}\cos(2t) - 2e^{-t}\sin(2t).\]

At \(t = 0\):

\[v(0) = -e^{0}\cos(0) - 2e^{0}\sin(0) = -1 - 0 = -1.\]

(b) Acceleration is \(a(t) = v'(t)\):

\[a(t) = \frac{d}{dt}\bigl[-e^{-t}\cos(2t)\bigr] + \frac{d}{dt}\bigl[-2e^{-t}\sin(2t)\bigr].\]

First term: \(e^{-t}\cos(2t) + 2e^{-t}\sin(2t)\).

Second term: \(2e^{-t}\sin(2t) - 4e^{-t}\cos(2t)\).

Combine:

\[a(t) = -3e^{-t}\cos(2t) + 4e^{-t}\sin(2t).\]

At \(t = 0\):

\[a(0) = -3(1)(1) + 0 = -3.\]

Since \(v(0) = -1 < 0\) and \(a(0) = -3 < 0\), velocity and acceleration have the **same sign** at \(t = 0\). Therefore the particle is **speeding up** at \(t = 0\).
</answer>
