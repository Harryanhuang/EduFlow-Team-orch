A function $f$ is continuous and differentiable on $(-\infty, \infty)$. The second derivative $f''$ exists and is zero at $x = 1$. Which statement must be true?

A) $f$ has a local extremum at $x = 1$
B) $f$ has a point of inflection at $x = 1$
C) $f$ could have a point of inflection at $x = 1$
D) Neither A nor B must be true

## Answer
D

## Explanation
If $f''(1) = 0$, this is only a preliminary indicator for a point of inflection. For an actual inflection point, we need the concavity to change at $x = 1$, which we cannot determine from $f''(1) = 0$ alone. Additionally, $f''(1) = 0$ tells us nothing about local extrema (which depend on $f'$). $f$ could have a local extremum at $x = 1$ (if $f'(1) = 0$ and the first derivative test applies), a point of inflection (if concavity changes), or neither. So neither A nor B must be true, making D the correct answer. C is incorrect because it says "could" which is true but not the best answer when "neither must be true" is available.
