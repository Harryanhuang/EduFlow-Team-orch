Rolle's Theorem is a special case of the Mean Value Theorem. Which additional condition distinguishes Rolle's Theorem from the general MVT?

## Options
A) $f$ must be a polynomial
B) $f(a) = f(b)$
C) $f'(c) = 0$ for exactly one value of $c$
D) $f$ must be increasing on $[a, b]$

## Answer
B) $f(a) = f(b)$

## Explanation
Rolle's Theorem states: If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$, differentiable on $(a, b)$, and $f(a) = f(b)$, then there exists at least one $c$ in $(a, b)$ such that $f'(c) = 0$. This is the Mean Value Theorem with the additional hypothesis that $f(a) = f(b)$. When $f(a) = f(b)$, the average rate of change $(f(b) - f(a))/(b - a) = 0$, so the MVT conclusion $f'(c) = (f(b) - f(a))/(b - a)$ becomes $f'(c) = 0$. The function need not be a polynomial, and there may be multiple values of $c$ where $f'(c) = 0$.
