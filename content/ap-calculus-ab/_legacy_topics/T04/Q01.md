Let $f(x) = x^3 - x - 1$. The function $f$ is continuous on $[1, 2]$, with $f(1) = -1$ and $f(2) = 5$. Use the Intermediate Value Theorem to explain why the equation $x^3 - x - 1 = 0$ has at least one real solution between $x = 1$ and $x = 2$.

## Answer
Since $f$ is a polynomial, it is continuous on all of $\mathbb{R}$, hence continuous on $[1, 2]$. We have $f(1) = 1 - 1 - 1 = -1 < 0$ and $f(2) = 8 - 2 - 1 = 5 > 0$. Since $0$ lies between $f(1) = -1$ and $f(2) = 5$, by the Intermediate Value Theorem there exists at least one $c \in (1, 2)$ such that $f(c) = 0$, i.e., $c^3 - c - 1 = 0$.
