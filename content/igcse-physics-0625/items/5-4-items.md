# Items: 5.4 — Ohm's law, V=IR, resistance of wires

### Question Q-5.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A resistor has a p.d. of 6 V across it and a current of 0.5 A flowing through it. Calculate its resistance.
**Answer**: 12 ohm
**Explanation**: R = V/I = 6/0.5 = 12 ohm. A common error is multiplying instead of dividing.
**Tags**: Ohm-law, resistance, calculation

### Question Q-5.4-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What does it mean for a conductor to obey Ohm's law?
**Answer**: The current through the conductor is directly proportional to the p.d. across it, provided temperature remains constant. Its I-V graph is a straight line through the origin.
**Explanation**: The constant temperature condition matters.
**Tags**: Ohm-law, definition, proportional

### Question Q-5.4-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A wire of length 1 m has a resistance of 4 ohm. What would be the resistance of a wire of the same material with (a) length 2 m, (b) length 0.5 m?
**Answer**: (a) 8 ohm. (b) 2 ohm.
**Explanation**: Resistance is proportional to length. Doubling length doubles resistance; halving length halves resistance.
**Tags**: resistance, length, proportionality

### Question Q-5.4-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A wire has a resistance of 10 ohm. What would be the resistance of a wire of the same material and length but with twice the cross-sectional area?
**Answer**: 5 ohm
**Explanation**: Resistance is inversely proportional to cross-sectional area. Doubling the area halves the resistance.
**Tags**: resistance, cross-sectional-area, proportionality

### Question Q-5.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe how the resistance of a metal wire changes with temperature.
**Answer**: The resistance of a metal increases with temperature because metal ions vibrate more and make it harder for electrons to pass through.
**Explanation**: This differs from an NTC thermistor.
**Tags**: resistance, temperature, metal

### Question Q-5.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: The I-V graph for a filament lamp is a curve that gets less steep as voltage increases. Explain why.
**Answer**: As voltage increases, current heats the filament. Higher temperature increases resistance, so current rises less for each extra volt.
**Explanation**: A filament lamp is non-ohmic because its temperature changes.
**Tags**: filament-lamp, I-V-graph, non-ohmic, temperature

### Question Q-5.4-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A 12 V supply gives a current of 0.30 A through a wire. The wire is replaced with one of the same material and length but half the cross-sectional area. Calculate the new current.
**Answer**: Original R = 12/0.30 = 40 ohm. Halving area doubles resistance, so new R = 80 ohm. New current = 12/80 = 0.15 A.
**Explanation**: This combines Ohm law with area dependence.
**Tags**: Ohm-law, wire-resistance, multi-step

### Question Q-5.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Describe an experiment to investigate how resistance of a wire depends on length, including how to make it a fair test.
**Answer**: Use a resistance wire, ammeter, voltmeter, power supply, switch, and sliding contact. Measure V and I for different lengths and calculate R = V/I. Keep material, thickness, and temperature constant; use low current or switch off between readings to reduce heating.
**Explanation**: Fair testing requires changing only length.
**Tags**: resistance-wire, experiment, fair-test

### Question Q-5.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A current-p.d. graph is straight through the origin up to 4 V, then curves and becomes less steep. Explain what this shows about resistance.
**Answer**: Up to 4 V the resistance is constant. Above 4 V the decreasing gradient means resistance increases, likely due to heating.
**Explanation**: For an I-V graph, gradient = I/V = 1/R.
**Tags**: I-V-graph, resistance, interpretation
