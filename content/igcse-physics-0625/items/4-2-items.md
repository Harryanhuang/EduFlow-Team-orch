# Items: 4.2 — Transverse vs longitudinal waves

### Question Q-4.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: State whether each of the following is a transverse or longitudinal wave: (a) light, (b) sound, (c) water wave, (d) a wave on a rope.
**Answer**: (a) Transverse, (b) Longitudinal, (c) Transverse (approximately), (d) Transverse.
**Explanation**: Light and rope waves are clearly transverse. Sound is longitudinal. Water waves are approximately transverse. A common error is classifying sound as transverse.
**Tags**: wave-types, transverse, longitudinal, classification

### Question Q-4.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Describe the difference between the direction of particle vibration and the direction of wave travel in a longitudinal wave.
**Answer**: In a longitudinal wave, particles vibrate parallel to the direction of wave travel. The particles oscillate back and forth along the same line that the wave moves.
**Explanation**: Parallel vibration is the defining feature. A common error is saying "perpendicular" (which is transverse).
**Tags**: longitudinal-wave, particle-motion, definition

### Question Q-4.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What are compressions and rarefactions in a sound wave?
**Answer**: Compressions are regions where particles are pushed close together (high pressure). Rarefactions are regions where particles are spread apart (low pressure). They alternate as the sound wave travels.
**Explanation**: These replace "crests" and "troughs" for longitudinal waves. A common error is calling them crests and troughs.
**Tags**: compression, rarefaction, sound-wave

### Question Q-4.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why transverse waves can be polarised but longitudinal waves cannot.
**Answer**: Transverse waves vibrate in a plane perpendicular to the direction of travel, so they can be restricted to one plane (polarised). Longitudinal waves vibrate along the direction of travel, so there is no "plane" to restrict.
**Explanation**: Polarisation requires a vibration direction that can be filtered. A common error is saying longitudinal waves "can be polarised with a filter."
**Tags**: polarisation, transverse, longitudinal, explanation

### Question Q-4.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What property do all waves (transverse and longitudinal) have in common?
**Answer**: All waves transfer energy without transferring matter. All waves can be reflected and have a speed, frequency, and wavelength.
**Explanation**: Energy transfer without matter transfer is the universal wave property. A common error is listing properties specific to one type.
**Tags**: wave, common-properties, conceptual

### Question Q-4.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A slinky spring is used to demonstrate waves. When one end is moved side to side, a wave travels along the spring. What type of wave is produced? Explain your answer.
**Answer**: A transverse wave is produced. The particles of the spring move perpendicular (side to side) to the direction of wave travel along the spring.
**Explanation**: Side-to-side motion creates transverse waves. A common error is confusing this with pushing and pulling (which creates longitudinal waves).
**Tags**: wave-demonstration, transverse, practical

### Question Q-4.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain why sound waves can travel through solids but not through a vacuum, while light waves can travel through both.
**Answer**: Sound waves are mechanical waves that require particles to transfer energy through vibrations. Solids have particles that can vibrate, but a vacuum has no particles. Light waves are electromagnetic and do not require a medium; they can travel through empty space.
**Explanation**: The key distinction is mechanical vs electromagnetic waves. A common error is saying "sound needs air" when it actually needs any medium with particles.
**Tags**: sound, light, medium, mechanical-waves, EM-waves

### Question Q-4.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student claims that all waves transfer both energy and matter. Is this statement correct? Explain why or why not, giving one example of each type of wave.
**Answer**: The statement is incorrect. Waves transfer energy only, not matter. For example, in a water wave, the water particles oscillate about their positions but do not travel with the wave. Similarly, light transfers energy without carrying any material.
**Explanation**: The defining feature of waves is energy transfer without matter transport. A common error is observing water waves and thinking the water itself moves forward with the wave.
**Tags**: wave, energy-transfer, misconception, conceptual

### Question Q-4.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare and contrast transverse and longitudinal waves. Give two similarities and two differences, with an example of each type.
**Answer**: Similarities: (1) Both transfer energy without transferring matter. (2) Both have speed, frequency, and wavelength. Differences: (1) Transverse waves vibrate perpendicular to travel direction; longitudinal waves vibrate parallel. (2) Transverse waves can be polarised; longitudinal cannot. Examples: Transverse - light; Longitudinal - sound.
**Explanation**: A structured comparison requires both similarities and differences. A common error is listing only differences or providing incorrect examples.
**Tags**: wave-types, transverse, longitudinal, comparison, extended-response
