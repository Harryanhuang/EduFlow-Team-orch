# Items: Cross-topic — Nuclear & Space final top-up

### Question Q-6.1-07
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Why is the mass of a nucleus slightly less than the sum of its individual nucleons?
**Answer**: The "missing mass" (mass defect) has been converted into binding energy that holds the nucleus together, according to E = mc². This energy must be supplied to break the nucleus apart.
**Explanation**: Mass-energy equivalence. A common error is saying the mass "disappears."
**Tags**: mass-defect, binding-energy, E=mc²

### Question Q-6.2-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A nucleus emits a gamma ray. What happens to its mass number and atomic number?
**Answer**: Neither changes. Gamma emission occurs when an excited nucleus releases excess energy as a photon. The number of protons and neutrons remains the same.
**Explanation**: Gamma = energy release, no particle change. A common error is decreasing the mass number.
**Tags**: gamma-emission, nuclear-equation, no-change

### Question Q-6.3-07
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A Geiger counter reads 320 counts/min near a source and 40 counts/min with no source present. After one half-life, what will the corrected reading be if the initial corrected activity was 200 counts/min?
**Answer**: 100 counts/min (corrected), or 140 counts/min (including background).
**Explanation**: After one half-life, activity halves: 200 → 100. Raw reading = 100 + 40 = 140. A common error is halving the raw reading (320 → 160).
**Tags**: half-life, background-correction, calculation

### Question Q-7.1-07
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the difference between the Solar System, a galaxy, and the universe?
**Answer**: The Solar System is the Sun and its orbiting bodies. A galaxy is a system of billions of stars (the Milky Way contains our Solar System). The universe contains all galaxies, all matter, and all space.
**Explanation**: Nested scale: Solar System < galaxy < universe. A common error is equating galaxy with universe.
**Tags**: Solar-System, galaxy, universe, scale

### Question Q-7.2-07
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: How do astronomers determine the chemical composition of a distant star?
**Answer**: By analysing the star's spectrum. Each element produces characteristic absorption lines (dark lines) at specific wavelengths. Matching these lines to known element spectra reveals the star's composition.
**Explanation**: Spectral fingerprinting. A common error is saying "by the colour of the star."
**Tags**: spectroscopy, stellar-composition, spectral-lines
