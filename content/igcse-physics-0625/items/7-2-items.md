# Items: 7.2 — Stars, life cycle and the Sun

### Question Q-7.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Describe the life cycle of a star similar in mass to the Sun, listing all stages in order from formation to end stage.
**Answer**: Nebula → protostar → main-sequence star → red giant → planetary nebula → white dwarf → black dwarf.
**Explanation**: A star begins as a collapsing cloud of gas and dust (nebula), heats up as it contracts (protostar), stabilises with hydrogen fusion (main sequence), expands when hydrogen runs out (red giant), sheds its outer layers (planetary nebula), leaves a hot dense core (white dwarf), which eventually cools completely (black dwarf). A common error is missing stages or getting the order wrong.
**Tags**: stellar-evolution, sun, life-cycle, nebula, white-dwarf

### Question Q-7.2-02
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What happens during the main-sequence stage of a star? Explain why this stage is stable.
**Answer**: During the main-sequence stage, hydrogen nuclei fuse into helium in the core, releasing enormous amounts of energy. This stage is stable because the outward pressure from the hot gases produced by fusion exactly balances the inward gravitational pull trying to collapse the star. This equilibrium can last billions of years.
**Explanation**: The balance between fusion pressure pushing outward and gravity pulling inward maintains stability. A common error is saying the star is "burning like fire" — fusion is a nuclear process, not combustion.
**Tags**: main-sequence, fusion, equilibrium, hydrogen, stability

### Question Q-7.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What property of a star determines whether it ends its life as a white dwarf, neutron star, or black hole? Describe the endpoint for each mass range.
**Answer**: The initial mass of the star determines its fate. Low to medium mass stars (like the Sun, up to about 8 solar masses) end as white dwarfs after shedding their outer layers as a planetary nebula. High mass stars (8 to about 25 solar masses) undergo a supernova explosion and leave behind a neutron star. Very high mass stars (above about 25 solar masses) also go supernova but collapse further to form a black hole.
**Explanation**: Mass is the determining factor. More massive stars have stronger gravity and can compress matter further. A common error is thinking all stars become black holes or that the Sun will explode as a supernova (it is not massive enough).
**Tags**: stellar-mass, white-dwarf, neutron-star, black-hole, supernova

### Question Q-7.2-04
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a light-year? Calculate how far light travels in one year. (Speed of light c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s)
**Answer**: A light-year is the distance light travels in one year through vacuum. Distance = speed × time = 3 × 10⁸ m/s × 365 × 24 × 3600 s ≈ 9.46 × 10¹⁵ m (about 9.5 trillion kilometres or 9.5 million million km).
**Explanation**: It is a unit of distance, not time. The calculation multiplies the speed of light by the number of seconds in a year. A common error is confusing a light-year with a measure of time or getting the calculation wrong by using incorrect time units.
**Tags**: light-year, distance, calculation, speed-of-light, astronomy

### Question Q-7.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why does a star appear red when it becomes a red giant?
**Answer**: As a star expands into a red giant, its surface area increases dramatically but its surface temperature decreases (to about 3000-4000 K). Cooler objects emit more light at longer wavelengths, which appear redder. The lower surface temperature shifts the peak emission towards the red end of the spectrum, giving the star a reddish appearance.
**Explanation**: Surface temperature determines colour: hotter = bluer, cooler = redder. A common error is saying the star is "cooling down to die" without connecting temperature to colour through Wien's law.
**Tags**: red-giant, temperature, colour, wien's-law, stellar-evolution

### Question Q-7.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a nebula? Give two types of nebula and state how each is formed.
**Answer**: A nebula is a vast cloud of gas (mainly hydrogen and helium) and dust in space. Two types: (1) Star-forming nebulae (emission nebulae) — clouds of gas where new stars are born through gravitational collapse (e.g., Orion Nebula). (2) Planetary nebulae — shells of gas ejected by dying red giant stars when they shed their outer layers (e.g., Ring Nebula).
**Explanation**: Nebulae can be sites of star birth or remnants of stellar death. A common error is thinking all nebulae are the same type or confusing planetary nebulae with planets.
**Tags**: nebula, star-formation, planetary-nebula, gas-cloud, dust

### Question Q-7.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Describe the process of a supernova explosion. What conditions must be met for a supernova to occur, and what remains afterwards?
**Answer**: A supernova occurs in massive stars when nuclear fusion in the core produces iron. Iron cannot fuse to release energy, so fusion stops. Without outward fusion pressure, gravity causes the core to collapse catastrophically in milliseconds. The outer layers rebound off the dense core and explode outward in a tremendous burst of energy, briefly outshining an entire galaxy. Afterwards, the core remnant becomes either a neutron star (if the remaining mass is about 1.4 to 3 solar masses) or a black hole (if above about 3 solar masses).
**Explanation**: The key trigger is iron production halting fusion. A common error is saying the star "runs out of fuel" generally rather than specifically reaching iron in the fusion chain.
**Tags**: supernova, iron-core, collapse, explosion, remnant

### Question Q-7.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Two stars appear equally bright in the night sky. Star A is 10 light-years away and Star B is 100 light-years away. Which star is actually more luminous (intrinsically brighter)? Explain your reasoning.
**Answer**: Star B is actually more luminous. Apparent brightness decreases with the square of distance (inverse square law: brightness ∝ 1/d²). Since Star B is 10 times further away but appears equally bright, it must be 10² = 100 times more luminous than Star A to compensate for its greater distance. If both appeared equally bright from Earth, the more distant one must emit far more light.
**Explanation**: The inverse square law means doubling distance reduces apparent brightness to 1/4. At 10× distance, Star B needs 100× the luminosity to appear equally bright. A common error is thinking the closer star is more luminous or not applying the inverse square relationship.
**Tags**: luminosity, apparent-brightness, inverse-square-law, distance, comparison

### Question Q-7.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: On a Hertzsprung-Russell (H-R) diagram, stars are plotted according to their surface temperature and luminosity. Describe where the following would be found on an H-R diagram: (a) main-sequence stars, (b) red giants, (c) white dwarfs. Sketch the general shape.
**Answer**: (a) Main-sequence stars form a diagonal band from top-left (hot, luminous) to bottom-right (cool, dim). The Sun sits roughly in the middle. (b) Red giants are found in the top-right region: cool (red) but very luminous due to large size. (c) White dwarfs occupy the bottom-left: hot (white/blue) but dim due to small size. The diagram shows distinct groupings, not random distribution.
**Explanation**: The H-R diagram reveals stellar evolution pathways. Position depends on both temperature and size. A common error is placing red giants in the bottom-right (confusing low luminosity) or white dwarfs in the top-left (confusing high temperature with high luminosity).
**Tags**: h-r-diagram, main-sequence, red-giant, white-dwarf, luminosity, temperature
