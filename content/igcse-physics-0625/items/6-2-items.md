# Items: 6.2 — Radioactivity: alpha, beta and gamma

### Question Q-6.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Complete the table: State the charge, mass, and identity of alpha, beta, and gamma radiation.
**Answer**: Alpha: charge +2, mass 4, helium nucleus (⁴₂He). Beta: charge -1, negligible mass, high-speed electron. Gamma: charge 0, mass 0, electromagnetic wave (photon).
**Explanation**: Each type has distinct properties. Alpha is a heavy positively charged particle, beta is a light negatively charged particle, and gamma is uncharged electromagnetic radiation. A common error is giving gamma a mass or charge.
**Tags**: alpha, beta, gamma, properties, radiation

### Question Q-6.2-02
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A radioactive source emits radiation that passes through paper but is stopped by 3 mm of aluminium. What type of radiation is this?
**Answer**: Beta radiation. Alpha is stopped by paper; gamma passes through aluminium. Beta passes through paper but is stopped by a few mm of aluminium.
**Explanation**: Penetrating power identifies the type. Paper stops alpha, a few mm of aluminium stops beta, and only thick lead or concrete significantly reduces gamma. A common error is saying gamma radiation.
**Tags**: radiation-identification, penetrating-power, beta, aluminium

### Question Q-6.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Uranium-238 (²³⁸₉₂U) decays by alpha emission to thorium. Write the nuclear equation.
**Answer**: ²³⁸₉₂U → ²³⁴₉₀Th + ⁴₂α
**Explanation**: In alpha decay, the mass number decreases by 4 (238 - 4 = 234) and the atomic number decreases by 2 (92 - 2 = 90). Element 90 is thorium. A common error is not adjusting both the mass and atomic numbers correctly.
**Tags**: alpha-decay, nuclear-equation, uranium, thorium

### Question Q-6.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Carbon-14 (¹⁴₆C) decays by beta emission. Write the nuclear equation and identify the product.
**Answer**: ¹⁴₆C → ¹⁴₇N + ⁰₋₁β (or ⁰₋₁e). The product is nitrogen-14. In beta decay, a neutron becomes a proton + electron, so the atomic number increases by 1 but the mass number stays the same.
**Explanation**: Beta decay increases the atomic number by 1 (6 → 7) while the mass number remains 14. Element 7 is nitrogen. A common error is decreasing the mass number instead of keeping it constant.
**Tags**: beta-decay, nuclear-equation, carbon-14, nitrogen

### Question Q-6.2-05
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: In an electric field, alpha particles deflect towards the negative plate and beta particles towards the positive plate. Explain why gamma rays are not deflected.
**Answer**: Gamma rays are electromagnetic radiation and carry no electric charge. Electric fields only exert forces on charged particles, so gamma rays pass through unaffected.
**Explanation**: Alpha (+2 charge) and beta (-1 charge) are deflected in opposite directions due to their opposite charges. Gamma has zero charge, so no electric force acts on it. A common error is saying gamma rays are "too heavy" or "too fast" to deflect.
**Tags**: electric-field, deflection, gamma, charge, radiation

### Question Q-6.2-06
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Arrange alpha, beta, and gamma radiation in order of increasing penetrating power. State what material stops each type.
**Answer**: Increasing penetrating power: alpha < beta < gamma. Alpha is stopped by a sheet of paper. Beta is stopped by a few mm of aluminium. Gamma is reduced by thick lead or concrete but never completely stopped.
**Explanation**: Alpha has the lowest penetrating power due to its large mass and charge. Beta penetrates further due to smaller mass and charge. Gamma has the highest penetrating power as it has no mass or charge. A common error is reversing the order or stating incorrect stopping materials.
**Tags**: penetrating-power, alpha, beta, gamma, shielding

### Question Q-6.2-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Which type of radiation (alpha, beta, or gamma) has the greatest ionising power? Explain why, and state one practical consequence of this property.
**Answer**: Alpha radiation has the greatest ionising power. This is because alpha particles are massive, have a +2 charge, and move relatively slowly, allowing them to collide with and ionise many atoms along their short path. A practical consequence is that alpha sources are dangerous if ingested or inhaled but safe externally (skin stops them).
**Explanation**: High ionising power means alpha particles lose energy quickly and travel only a few cm in air. A common error is confusing ionising power with penetrating power — they are inversely related.
**Tags**: ionising-power, alpha, radiation-safety, properties

### Question Q-6.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A beam of radioactive particles passes through a magnetic field. Alpha particles curve in one direction, beta particles curve in the opposite direction, and gamma rays do not curve. Explain these observations.
**Answer**: Alpha particles are positively charged (+2) and beta particles are negatively charged (-1), so the magnetic field exerts a force on both in opposite directions (Fleming's left-hand rule). Gamma rays carry no charge, so no magnetic force acts on them and they travel in a straight line.
**Explanation**: Charged particles moving through a magnetic field experience a force perpendicular to both their velocity and the field direction. The force direction depends on the sign of the charge. A common error is forgetting that the direction of deflection depends on the charge sign.
**Tags**: magnetic-field, deflection, alpha, beta, gamma, charge

### Question Q-6.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Complete the following nuclear equation: ²²⁶₈₈Ra → ? + ⁴₂α. Identify the daughter element and determine how many protons and neutrons it contains.
**Answer**: ²²⁶₈₈Ra → ²²²₈₆Rn + ⁴₂α. The daughter element is radon-222 (Rn). It contains 86 protons and 222 - 86 = 136 neutrons.
**Explanation**: Conservation of mass number: 226 = A + 4, so A = 222. Conservation of atomic number: 88 = Z + 2, so Z = 86. Element 86 is radon. A common error is miscalculating the neutron number by subtracting incorrectly.
**Tags**: nuclear-equation, alpha-decay, radium, radon, conservation
