# Items: 6.5 — Safety, background radiation and uses

### Question Q-6.5-01
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: State three safety precautions that should be taken when handling radioactive materials in a school laboratory.
**Answer**: (1) Minimise time of exposure — handle sources quickly. (2) Maximise distance — use tongs or forceps, never touch directly. (3) Use shielding — store sources in lead-lined containers and place lead shields between the source and people.
**Explanation**: The three principles of radiation protection are time, distance, and shielding. Reducing any one factor reduces the total dose received. A common error is saying "wear a lab coat" — this does not protect against penetrating radiation.
**Tags**: radiation-safety, precautions, handling, laboratory

### Question Q-6.5-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A radioactive isotope is used as a tracer to detect a leak in an underground water pipe. What type of radiation should the isotope emit and why?
**Answer**: Gamma radiation. It has high penetrating power and can pass through soil and pipe walls to be detected above ground with a Geiger counter. Alpha and beta radiation would be absorbed by the pipe and surrounding soil, making detection impossible.
**Explanation**: Penetrating power is essential for underground detection. Gamma rays can travel through metres of soil. A common error is suggesting beta radiation, which has insufficient penetrating power for this application.
**Tags**: radioactive-tracer, gamma, leak-detection, penetrating-power

### Question Q-6.5-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Iodine-131 (half-life 8 days) is used to treat thyroid conditions. Why is an isotope with a relatively short half-life preferred for medical diagnosis and treatment?
**Answer**: A short half-life means the radioactivity decreases rapidly inside the patient's body, minimising the total radiation dose received and reducing the risk of long-term damage. However, it must be long enough to allow the isotope to reach the target organ and complete the diagnostic scan or treatment before decaying significantly.
**Explanation**: There is a balance: short enough to minimise dose but long enough to be useful. A common error is simply stating "short half-life is always safer" without considering the required functional duration.
**Tags**: medical-isotope, half-life, iodine-131, safety, diagnosis

### Question Q-6.5-04
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Explain how gamma radiation is used to sterilise medical instruments such as syringes and surgical gloves.
**Answer**: Medical instruments are sealed in plastic packaging and then exposed to a controlled dose of gamma radiation from a source such as cobalt-60. The gamma rays kill bacteria, viruses, and other microorganisms by damaging their DNA, effectively sterilising the instruments without heat damage. This allows single-use items to be sterilised in their final packaging.
**Explanation**: Gamma radiation penetrates packaging and kills microbes throughout. Unlike heat sterilisation, it does not damage heat-sensitive plastics. A common error is saying it "cleans" the instruments — sterilisation is not the same as cleaning.
**Tags**: gamma, sterilisation, medical-instruments, cobalt-60, disinfection

### Question Q-6.5-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How is the thickness of paper monitored during manufacture using radiation? Describe the setup and explain which type of radiation is used.
**Answer**: A beta radiation source is placed on one side of the moving paper sheet and a Geiger-Müller detector on the other. Beta particles pass through the paper; if the thickness increases, fewer particles reach the detector and the count rate drops. This triggers an automatic adjustment to reduce paper thickness. Beta is used because it is partially absorbed by paper — alpha would be completely stopped, and gamma would pass through almost unaffected.
**Explanation**: The system relies on the partial absorption of beta radiation by the paper. Changes in thickness produce measurable changes in count rate. A common error is selecting alpha (completely absorbed) or gamma (barely affected).
**Tags**: radiation, thickness-monitoring, beta, paper-manufacture, quality-control

### Question Q-6.5-06
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Carbon-14 dating is used to estimate the age of archaeological samples. Explain the principle behind this technique and state one assumption it makes.
**Answer**: Living organisms maintain a constant ratio of carbon-14 to carbon-12 through exchange with the atmosphere. When they die, carbon-14 intake stops and the existing C-14 decays (half-life 5730 years). By measuring the remaining C-14 activity and comparing it to that of a living sample, the age can be calculated. One assumption: the atmospheric C-14/C-12 ratio has remained constant over time.
**Explanation**: The technique assumes equilibrium between production and decay of C-14 in the atmosphere. A common error is not stating any assumption, or assuming the decay rate changes over time (it is constant).
**Tags**: carbon-dating, carbon-14, archaeology, radiocarbon, assumptions

### Question Q-6.5-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Many household smoke detectors contain americium-241, an alpha emitter. Explain how the detector works and why alpha radiation is specifically suitable.
**Answer**: Americium-241 emits alpha particles that ionise air molecules in the detector chamber, creating a small electric current. When smoke enters the chamber, it absorbs the alpha particles, reducing ionisation and causing the current to drop. This triggers the alarm. Alpha radiation is suitable because it has high ionising power (producing a detectable current) and low penetrating power (easily blocked by smoke and safely contained within the detector housing).
**Explanation**: The key is alpha's high ionising power creating a measurable current, and its easy absorption by smoke particles. A common error is not explaining why alpha rather than beta or gamma is chosen.
**Tags**: smoke-detector, americium-241, alpha, ionisation, application

### Question Q-6.5-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Spent nuclear fuel from fission reactors contains isotopes with very long half-lives. Explain why long-term storage of this waste is challenging and describe one method used to store it safely.
**Answer**: Long half-life isotopes remain radioactive for thousands to millions of years, requiring secure containment over geological timescales. Challenges include: preventing leakage into groundwater, resisting corrosion of containment vessels, and ensuring security against theft or sabotage. One storage method: vitrification — the waste is encased in glass blocks, sealed in stainless steel canisters, and buried deep underground in stable geological formations (e.g., granite or salt beds) far from population centres.
**Explanation**: The extremely long timescales involved make material degradation and geological stability critical concerns. A common error is not addressing the multi-millennial timeframe or proposing surface storage without considering long-term risks.
**Tags**: nuclear-waste, long-term-storage, half-life, vitrification, disposal

### Question Q-6.5-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Gamma irradiation is used to preserve food by killing bacteria and insects. State two advantages of this method compared to chemical preservatives or heat treatment, and identify one concern consumers might have.
**Answer**: Advantages: (1) Food can be treated in its final packaging, preventing recontamination. (2) No residual chemicals are added, and nutritional value is largely preserved (unlike heat treatment which can destroy vitamins). Concern: Consumers may worry that irradiated food becomes radioactive itself, though this is incorrect — the gamma rays pass through without making the food radioactive. Another concern is potential formation of breakdown products whose health effects are not fully known.
**Explanation**: Irradiation kills pathogens without making food radioactive, similar to how X-rays pass through patients without leaving radiation behind. A common error is claiming the food becomes radioactive, which demonstrates a misunderstanding of the process.
**Tags**: food-irradiation, gamma, preservation, advantages, consumer-concerns
