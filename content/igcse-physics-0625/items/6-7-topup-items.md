# Items: 6.1–7.4 — Nuclear & Space physics (top-up)

### Question Q-6.2-06
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A radioactive source is placed near a Geiger-Müller tube. A sheet of paper is placed between them and the reading drops from 500 to 300 counts/min. What does this tell you about the radiation emitted?
**Answer**: The source emits alpha radiation (stopped by paper, accounting for the 200 count drop) and another type that passes through paper (the remaining 300 counts). After subtracting background, the source emits a mix of alpha and either beta or gamma radiation.
**Explanation**: Partial absorption indicates mixed radiation. A common error is saying it's "only alpha."
**Tags**: radiation-identification, Geiger-counter, mixed-source

### Question Q-6.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A radioactive sample has a half-life of 3 days. How long until less than 1% of the original activity remains?
**Answer**: 21 days
**Explanation**: (1/2)⁷ = 1/128 ≈ 0.78% (< 1%). 7 half-lives = 7 × 3 = 21 days. After 6 HL: (1/2)⁶ = 1/64 ≈ 1.56% (still > 1%). A common error is saying 100 half-lives.
**Tags**: half-life, percentage, calculation

### Question Q-6.4-06
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the moderator in a nuclear reactor and what does it do?
**Answer**: The moderator (usually graphite or water) slows down fast neutrons produced by fission to speeds where they are more likely to cause further fission in uranium-235 nuclei.
**Explanation**: Slows neutrons, doesn't absorb them. A common error is confusing it with control rods.
**Tags**: moderator, nuclear-reactor, neutrons

### Question Q-6.5-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Carbon-14 dating: a sample of ancient wood has 25% of the carbon-14 activity of living wood. If the half-life of C-14 is 5730 years, how old is the sample?
**Answer**: 11460 years
**Explanation**: 100% → 50% → 25% = 2 half-lives. Age = 2 × 5730 = 11460 years. A common error is saying 5730 years.
**Tags**: carbon-dating, half-life, archaeology

### Question Q-7.1-06
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is an astronomical unit (AU)? Give its approximate value.
**Answer**: An AU is the average distance from the Earth to the Sun, approximately 150 million km (1.5 × 10¹¹ m). It is used to measure distances within the Solar System.
**Explanation**: It's a unit of distance. A common error is confusing it with a light-year.
**Tags**: astronomical-unit, distance, Solar-System

### Question Q-7.2-06
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a supernova?
**Answer**: A supernova is the explosive death of a massive star, releasing enormous energy and briefly outshining an entire galaxy. It scatters heavy elements into space, which can form new stars and planets.
**Explanation**: Explosive end of massive stars. A common error is saying all stars go supernova.
**Tags**: supernova, massive-star, explosion

### Question Q-7.3-06
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is Hubble's Law? Write the equation and define each term.
**Answer**: Hubble's Law: v = H₀d, where v is the recessional velocity of a galaxy (km/s), H₀ is the Hubble constant (km/s/Mpc), and d is the distance to the galaxy (Mpc). It shows that more distant galaxies recede faster.
**Explanation**: Linear relationship between speed and distance. A common error is swapping v and d.
**Tags**: Hubble's-law, equation, expansion

### Question Q-7.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why is the gravitational field strength at the surface of the Earth different at the equator compared to the poles?
**Answer**: The Earth is not a perfect sphere — it bulges at the equator, making the equatorial radius larger. Since g ∝ 1/r², the greater distance from the centre at the equator means g is slightly weaker there.
**Explanation**: Shape affects r. A common error is saying it's due to temperature.
**Tags**: gravity, Earth-shape, equator, poles
