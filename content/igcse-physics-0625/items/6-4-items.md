# Items: 6.4 — Nuclear fission and fusion

### Question Q-6.4-01
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is nuclear fission?
**Answer**: Nuclear fission is the splitting of a heavy nucleus (such as uranium-235 or plutonium-239) into two lighter nuclei of roughly equal mass, releasing a large amount of energy and usually 2 or 3 neutrons, when struck by a slow-moving neutron.
**Explanation**: Key features: a heavy nucleus splits, energy is released, and neutrons are produced. The process requires a neutron to initiate it. A common error is describing nuclear fusion instead, or omitting that neutrons are released.
**Tags**: nuclear-fission, definition, uranium, energy

### Question Q-6.4-02
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain what is meant by a chain reaction in nuclear fission.
**Answer**: When a uranium nucleus undergoes fission, it releases 2 or 3 neutrons. Each of these neutrons can be absorbed by another uranium nucleus, causing it to fission and release more neutrons. This self-sustaining process, where one fission event triggers further events, is called a chain reaction.
**Explanation**: The neutrons produced in one fission cause additional fissions, creating a cascading effect. A common error is stating that the energy released causes further fission rather than the neutrons.
**Tags**: chain-reaction, fission, neutrons, explanation

### Question Q-6.4-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the role of control rods in a nuclear reactor?
**Answer**: Control rods (made of boron or cadmium) absorb neutrons. By inserting them further into the reactor core or withdrawing them, the number of neutrons available to cause further fission is controlled, thereby regulating the rate of the chain reaction and the power output.
**Explanation**: Control rods absorb neutrons to slow down or speed up the reaction. Inserting them deeper reduces the reaction rate; withdrawing them increases it. A common error is saying they "slow down neutrons" — that is the function of the moderator.
**Tags**: control-rods, nuclear-reactor, neutrons, regulation

### Question Q-6.4-04
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is nuclear fusion? Where does it occur naturally?
**Answer**: Nuclear fusion is the joining together of two light nuclei (such as hydrogen isotopes) to form a heavier nucleus, releasing a large amount of energy. It occurs naturally in stars, including our Sun, where hydrogen nuclei fuse to form helium at extremely high temperatures and pressures.
**Explanation**: Fusion is the opposite of fission — nuclei combine rather than split. The extreme conditions in stellar cores are necessary to overcome electrostatic repulsion between nuclei. A common error is confusing fusion with fission.
**Tags**: nuclear-fusion, stars, sun, definition, energy

### Question Q-6.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why is nuclear fusion difficult to achieve and sustain on Earth?
**Answer**: Fusion requires extremely high temperatures (millions of degrees Celsius) to give nuclei enough kinetic energy to overcome the electrostatic repulsion between their positive charges. Containing matter at these temperatures requires powerful magnetic fields (in a tokamak reactor), which is technically very challenging and energy-intensive.
**Explanation**: The positively charged nuclei repel each other strongly. Only at extreme temperatures do they move fast enough to get close enough for the strong nuclear force to bind them. A common error is saying "we don't have enough fuel" — hydrogen is abundant.
**Tags**: nuclear-fusion, challenges, temperature, magnetic-confinement, tokamak

### Question Q-6.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the purpose of a moderator in a nuclear reactor? Name a suitable moderator material.
**Answer**: A moderator slows down fast-moving neutrons produced in fission to thermal (slow) speeds, making them more likely to be absorbed by uranium nuclei and cause further fission. Suitable moderators include graphite (carbon), heavy water (D₂O), and ordinary water (H₂O).
**Explanation**: Slow neutrons are more effective at inducing fission in uranium-235 than fast neutrons. The moderator must be light enough to slow neutrons through collisions without absorbing them. A common error is confusing the moderator with control rods.
**Tags**: moderator, nuclear-reactor, neutrons, graphite, fission

### Question Q-6.4-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare nuclear fission and nuclear fusion. State two similarities and two differences.
**Answer**: Similarities: (1) Both release large amounts of energy. (2) Both involve changes in atomic nuclei (nuclear reactions, not chemical). Differences: (1) Fission splits a heavy nucleus into lighter ones; fusion joins light nuclei to form a heavier one. (2) Fission can occur at room temperature (with neutrons); fusion requires millions of degrees. (3) Fission produces radioactive waste; fusion produces minimal long-lived radioactive waste.
**Explanation**: Both processes convert mass to energy via E = mc². However, they differ fundamentally in mechanism, conditions required, and byproducts. A common error is listing only superficial differences without addressing the nuclear processes.
**Tags**: nuclear-fission, nuclear-fusion, comparison, similarities, differences

### Question Q-6.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: In a fission reaction, the total mass of the products is 0.1% less than the mass of the reactants. Calculate the energy released when 1 kg of uranium undergoes fission. (c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s)
**Answer**: Mass defect = 0.1% of 1 kg = 0.001 kg. Energy released E = mc² = 0.001 × (3 × 10⁸)² = 0.001 × 9 × 10¹⁶ = 9 × 10¹³ J.
**Explanation**: Using Einstein's mass-energy equivalence equation E = mc², even a tiny mass loss produces enormous energy. The 0.1% mass defect is typical for fission. A common error is forgetting to square c or using the wrong percentage calculation.
**Tags**: mass-energy-equivalence, einstein, energy-calculation, fission, nuclear

### Question Q-6.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: What is meant by the term "critical mass" in relation to nuclear fission? Explain why a sub-critical mass cannot sustain a chain reaction.
**Answer**: Critical mass is the minimum mass of fissile material (such as uranium-235) needed to sustain a chain reaction. Below this mass (sub-critical), too many neutrons escape from the surface of the material without hitting other nuclei, so the chain reaction dies out. At or above critical mass, enough neutrons are retained to keep the reaction going.
**Explanation**: The surface-area-to-volume ratio matters. Smaller masses have proportionally more surface area, allowing more neutrons to escape. A common error is thinking critical mass depends only on the total number of atoms rather than the geometry.
**Tags**: critical-mass, fission, chain-reaction, criticality, explanation
