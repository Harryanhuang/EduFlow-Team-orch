# Items: 7.4 — Gravitational field strength

### Question Q-7.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: An astronaut has a mass of 70 kg. Calculate her weight on Earth (g = 10 N/kg) and on Mars (g = 3.7 N/kg). Explain the difference.
**Answer**: On Earth: W = mg = 70 × 10 = 700 N. On Mars: W = mg = 70 × 3.7 = 259 N. The astronaut weighs less on Mars because Mars has a weaker gravitational field strength (lower g) due to its smaller mass compared to Earth. Her mass remains 70 kg on both planets.
**Explanation**: Weight depends on the local gravitational field; mass is intrinsic. A common error is using Earth's g for both calculations or confusing mass with weight.
**Tags**: weight, gravity, mars, earth, calculation, mass

### Question Q-7.4-02
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: An object weighs 30 N on the Moon where g = 1.6 N/kg. Calculate its mass. Would this object weigh more or less on Earth?
**Answer**: Mass m = W/g = 30/1.6 = 18.75 kg. This mass is the same everywhere in the universe. On Earth (g = 10 N/kg), the object would weigh W = 18.75 × 10 = 187.5 N, which is significantly more than 30 N. So it weighs more on Earth.
**Explanation**: Mass is constant; weight varies with gravitational field strength. A common error is dividing by Earth's g (10) instead of the Moon's g (1.6) when calculating mass from the Moon weight.
**Tags**: mass, weight, moon, earth, calculation

### Question Q-7.4-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Planet X has twice the mass of Earth and twice the radius. Using the formula g = GM/r², calculate how the surface gravitational field strength on Planet X compares with that on Earth (g_Earth = 10 N/kg).
**Answer**: For Planet X: g_X = G(2M)/(2r)² = 2GM/4r² = (1/2)(GM/r²) = (1/2)g_Earth = 5 N/kg. The surface gravity on Planet X is half that of Earth. Although the doubled mass would increase gravity by a factor of 2, the doubled radius decreases it by a factor of 4 (since r² appears in the denominator). The radius effect dominates, giving a net factor of 1/2.
**Explanation**: Gravitational field strength depends on both mass and radius squared. A common error is saying g doubles because mass doubles, forgetting that radius has a squared inverse effect.
**Tags**: gravitational-field, mass, radius, formula, comparison, proportionality

### Question Q-7.4-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Astronauts orbiting Earth in the International Space Station appear to be "weightless." Explain why this is the case. Is there gravity at the ISS's altitude?
**Answer**: Astronauts feel weightless because they are in continuous free fall around Earth. Both the spacecraft and everything inside it are falling at the same rate towards Earth's centre, but their high orbital velocity means they keep "missing" the Earth. There is no contact force (normal force) pushing up on them, so they experience the sensation of weightlessness. Yes, there is still significant gravity at the ISS altitude (about 90% of surface gravity) — without it, the station could not stay in orbit.
**Explanation**: Weightlessness does not mean zero gravity. It means free fall with no supporting force. A common error is claiming "there is no gravity in space" or "the ISS is too far for Earth's gravity to reach it."
**Tags**: weightlessness, orbit, free-fall, iss, gravity, explanation

### Question Q-7.4-05
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: The gravitational field strength on Jupiter is approximately 24 N/kg, much greater than Earth's 10 N/kg. Explain why Jupiter's surface gravity is so strong. If you stood on Jupiter (assuming it had a solid surface), how would your weight compare to your weight on Earth?
**Answer**: Jupiter's surface gravity is strong primarily because Jupiter has an enormous mass (about 318 times Earth's mass). Although Jupiter also has a much larger radius, the mass effect dominates, resulting in g ≈ 24 N/kg. If you stood on Jupiter, your weight would be W = m × 24, which is 2.4 times your weight on Earth (W = m × 10). Your mass would remain unchanged.
**Explanation**: Mass is the primary determinant of gravitational field strength. A common error is attributing Jupiter's strong gravity to its density or composition rather than its total mass.
**Tags**: jupiter, gravity, mass, weight-comparison, gas-giant

### Question Q-7.4-06
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain how the value of g (gravitational field strength) changes as you move from the surface of the Earth towards its centre. Assume Earth has uniform density for simplicity.
**Answer**: Inside a uniform spherical body, g decreases linearly with depth from the surface to the centre. At the surface, g = GM/R² (maximum). As you go deeper, only the mass beneath you contributes to the gravitational pull (the shell above exerts no net force). At the centre, g = 0 because mass surrounds you equally in all directions. For uniform density, g(r) = g_surface × (r/R), where r is distance from centre. So halfway to the centre, g is halved.
**Explanation**: This differs from the outside behaviour where g ∝ 1/r². Inside, the effective attracting mass decreases faster than r². A common error is thinking g increases towards the centre or stays constant underground.
**Tags**: gravity, earth-interior, depth-variation, uniform-density, centre

### Question Q-7.4-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Derive an expression for the orbital speed of a satellite in circular orbit around Earth at height h above the surface. Then calculate the orbital speed for a satellite at h = 400 km (ISS altitude). Use: Earth radius R = 6400 km, Earth mass M = 6.0 × 10²⁴ kg, G = 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg².
**Answer**: For circular orbit, gravitational force provides centripetal force: GMm/r² = mv²/r, where r = R + h. Solving: v = √(GM/r) = √(GM/(R+h)). Substituting: r = 6400 + 400 = 6800 km = 6.8 × 10⁶ m. v = √(6.67×10⁻¹¹ × 6.0×10²⁴ / 6.8×10⁶) = √(5.885×10⁷) ≈ 7670 m/s ≈ 7.7 km/s.
**Explanation**: Orbital speed depends only on the central mass and orbital radius, not on the satellite's mass. A common error is using the surface radius R instead of R + h, or getting units wrong (km vs m).
**Tags**: orbital-velocity, satellite, iss, derivation, calculation, centripetal-force

### Question Q-7.4-08
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Calculate the gravitational field strength on the surface of Mars. Given: Mars mass = 6.4 × 10²³ kg, Mars radius = 3400 km. (G = 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg²)
**Answer**: g = GM/r² = (6.67 × 10⁻¹¹)(6.4 × 10²³) / (3.4 × 10⁶)² = 4.27 × 10¹³ / 1.156 × 10¹³ ≈ 3.7 N/kg.
**Explanation**: First convert radius to metres: 3400 km = 3.4 × 10⁶ m. Then substitute into the formula. A common error is forgetting to square the radius or not converting km to m, giving an answer off by orders of magnitude.
**Tags**: mars, gravitational-field, calculation, planetary-data, formula

### Question Q-7.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Define gravitational potential at a point. Explain why gravitational potential is negative near a planet and what happens to it as you move further away. State the value at infinity.
**Answer**: Gravitational potential at a point is the work done per unit mass to bring a small test mass from infinity to that point. It is negative near a planet because gravity is attractive — work must be done against gravity to move a mass away (the system has negative potential energy relative to infinity). As you move further from the planet, gravitational potential becomes less negative (increases towards zero). At infinity, gravitational potential is defined as zero.
**Explanation**: Negative potential indicates a bound state — energy must be added to escape. A common error is thinking negative potential means "negative energy exists" rather than understanding it as a reference convention where infinity = zero.
**Tags**: gravitational-potential, negative, infinity, work-done, definition
