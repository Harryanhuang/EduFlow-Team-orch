# Items: 5.1–5.11 — Electricity & magnetism (round 2)

### Question Q-5.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the function of a relay? Give one practical use.
**Answer**: A relay is an electromagnetic switch that uses a small current to control a larger current. Uses: car starter circuits, washing machines, industrial control systems.
**Explanation**: Small current → electromagnet → closes contacts → large current flows. A common error is confusing it with a resistor.
**Tags**: relay, electromagnetic-switch

### Question Q-5.3-01
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A coil is connected to a sensitive ammeter. A bar magnet is pushed into the coil. Describe what happens to the ammeter reading and explain why.
**Answer**: The ammeter deflects, showing a current is induced. The changing magnetic field through the coil induces an e.m.f. (electromagnetic induction). When the magnet stops moving, the reading returns to zero.
**Explanation**: Changing magnetic flux → induced e.m.f. A common error is saying the magnet itself creates the current.
**Tags**: electromagnetic-induction, Faraday's-law

### Question Q-5.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: State the three factors that affect the size of the induced e.m.f. in a coil.
**Answer**: (1) Strength of the magnetic field. (2) Speed of relative motion between the coil and magnet. (3) Number of turns on the coil.
**Explanation**: These are from Faraday's law. A common error is including the size of the coil.
**Tags**: induced-emf, factors, Faraday's-law

### Question Q-5.5-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the difference between a.c. and d.c.?
**Answer**: D.c. (direct current) flows in one direction only. A.c. (alternating current) repeatedly reverses direction. Mains electricity is a.c.; batteries produce d.c.
**Explanation**: Direction of flow is key. A common error is saying a.c. has "more power."
**Tags**: AC, DC, comparison

### Question Q-5.7-01
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why is electrical power transmitted at high voltage and low current?
**Answer**: Power loss in cables is P = I²R. Transmitting at high voltage means the current is lower for the same power, reducing heat loss in the transmission lines significantly.
**Explanation**: I²R loss is the key. A common error is saying "to make the voltage higher at the user's end."
**Tags**: power-transmission, high-voltage, I²R-loss
