# Items: 7.3 — Galaxies, universe expansion and the Big Bang

### Question Q-7.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a galaxy? Name the galaxy in which our Solar System is located.
**Answer**: A galaxy is a vast system containing billions of stars, gas clouds, dust, and dark matter, all held together by gravitational attraction. Our Solar System is located in the Milky Way galaxy.
**Explanation**: Galaxies are gravitationally bound collections of stars and interstellar material. They range from dwarf galaxies with millions of stars to giant galaxies with trillions. A common error is calling the Solar System a galaxy — it is just one tiny part of the much larger Milky Way.
**Tags**: galaxy, milky-way, definition, stars, gravitation

### Question Q-7.3-02
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is red-shift? Explain how it provides evidence for the expansion of the universe.
**Answer**: Red-shift is the displacement of spectral lines from distant galaxies towards longer wavelengths (the red end of the spectrum). It occurs because the light waves are stretched as space itself expands while the light travels through it. The observation that almost all galaxies show red-shift, and that more distant galaxies show greater red-shift, indicates they are moving away from us — evidence that the universe is expanding uniformly in all directions.
**Explanation**: This is analogous to the Doppler effect for sound: a receding source has its waves stretched. In cosmology, it is space itself expanding rather than galaxies moving through static space. A common error is saying galaxies "look redder" due to dust or ageing rather than wavelength stretching.
**Tags**: redshift, universe-expansion, doppler-effect, hubble's-law, spectral-lines

### Question Q-7.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: State two independent pieces of observational evidence that support the Big Bang theory.
**Answer**: (1) Red-shift of galaxies (Hubble's Law): galaxies are moving away from us, indicating the universe is expanding, implying it was once concentrated at a single point. (2) Cosmic microwave background radiation (CMB): uniform low-energy microwave radiation detected from all directions in space, representing the cooled remnant of the intense heat from the early universe about 380 000 years after the Big Bang.
**Explanation**: These are two distinct observations supporting the same theory. A common error is listing "red-shift" and "galaxies moving away" as separate pieces of evidence — they are the same observation described differently.
**Tags**: big-bang, evidence, redshift, cmb, cosmic-microwave-background

### Question Q-7.3-04
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the cosmic microwave background radiation (CMB)? Explain why it is found throughout all of space.
**Answer**: The CMB is low-energy electromagnetic radiation in the microwave region that fills all of space uniformly in every direction. It originated as high-energy gamma radiation from the extremely hot, dense early universe about 380 000 years after the Big Bang. As the universe expanded over billions of years, this radiation was stretched (red-shifted) to longer microwave wavelengths. It is found everywhere because it was emitted when the entire universe was hot and dense, not from a single point or explosion in space.
**Explanation**: The CMB is the "afterglow" of the Big Bang, detectable as a faint hiss of microwaves in any direction. A common error is thinking it comes from stars, the Sun, or human-made sources like microwave ovens.
**Tags**: cmb, cosmic-microwave-background, big-bang, microwave, early-universe

### Question Q-7.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Galaxy A has a greater red-shift than Galaxy B. Compare their distances from Earth and their recessional velocities. State the relationship discovered by Edwin Hubble.
**Answer**: Galaxy A is further away from Earth than Galaxy B, and it is moving away faster (has a greater recessional velocity). Hubble's Law states that the recessional velocity of a galaxy is proportional to its distance from us: v ∝ d, or v = H₀d, where H₀ is the Hubble constant (approximately 70 km/s/Mpc). This means doubling the distance doubles the recession speed.
**Explanation**: Hubble's Law quantifies the observation that more distant galaxies recede faster. A common error is reversing the relationship (saying closer galaxies move faster) or not recognising that red-shift is a measure of velocity.
**Tags**: hubble's-law, redshift, distance, velocity, proportionality

### Question Q-7.3-06
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Before the Big Bang theory was widely accepted, the Steady State theory proposed that the universe has always existed and is continuously creating matter as it expands. Explain why the Steady State theory was rejected in favour of the Big Bang theory.
**Answer**: The Steady State theory could not explain the cosmic microwave background radiation. The Big Bang theory predicted that radiation from the hot, dense early universe should still be detectable today, red-shifted to microwave wavelengths — and this was confirmed by observation in 1965. The Steady State theory had no mechanism to produce such uniform background radiation. Additionally, the observed evolution of galaxies over cosmic time contradicts the "steady state" assumption of an unchanging universe.
**Explanation**: The CMB was a definitive prediction of the Big Bang that the Steady State theory could not match. Observational evidence decided between competing theories. A common error is thinking the theories were rejected based on philosophical preference rather than empirical evidence.
**Tags**: steady-state-theory, big-bang, cmb, theory-rejection, scientific-method

### Question Q-7.3-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe two possible scenarios for the future evolution of the universe. What factor determines which scenario will occur?
**Answer**: (1) Open universe / Big Freeze: if the average density of matter is below the critical density, gravity cannot halt expansion. The universe expands forever, galaxies move apart, stars eventually burn out, and the universe approaches absolute zero temperature. (2) Closed universe / Big Crunch: if the density exceeds the critical value, gravity eventually stops expansion and reverses it. The universe contracts back to a hot, dense singularity. The determining factor is the average density of matter (including dark matter) relative to the critical density. Current observations suggest we live in an accelerating expanding universe (Big Freeze scenario, driven by dark energy).
**Explanation**: The fate depends on whether there is enough mass for gravity to overcome expansion. A common error is only knowing one scenario or not identifying density as the key factor.
**Tags**: universe-fate, big-freeze, big-crunch, critical-density, dark-energy

### Question Q-7.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: What is dark matter? State one piece of observational evidence that suggests it exists.
**Answer**: Dark matter is invisible matter that does not emit, absorb, or reflect electromagnetic radiation but exerts gravitational influence. It makes up approximately 27% of the universe's total mass-energy (compared to only 5% normal matter). Evidence: galaxies rotate faster than can be explained by visible matter alone — outer stars orbit at nearly constant speed regardless of distance from the centre, indicating additional unseen mass providing extra gravitational force. Other evidence includes gravitational lensing being stronger than visible mass accounts for, and the motion of galaxy clusters.
**Explanation**: Dark matter is inferred from its gravitational effects on visible matter. A common error is confusing dark matter with black holes or antimatter. It is not simply "matter we haven't discovered" but matter that interacts gravitationally but not electromagnetically.
**Tags**: dark-matter, galaxy-rotation, gravitational-lensing, evidence, cosmology

### Question Q-7.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Estimate the age of the universe using the Hubble constant H₀ = 70 km/s/Mpc. State the assumption made in this calculation and explain why the actual age may differ.
**Answer**: Converting units: H₀ = 70 km/s/Mpc ≈ 2.27 × 10⁻¹⁸ s⁻¹. Age ≈ 1/H₀ ≈ 4.4 × 10¹⁷ s ≈ 14 billion years. Assumption: the expansion rate has been constant since the Big Bang (uniform expansion). The actual age may differ because the expansion rate has not been constant — gravity from matter slowed expansion in the early universe, while dark energy is now accelerating it. More precise measurements give an age of about 13.8 billion years.
**Explanation**: The simple calculation assumes constant expansion, but the real universe has experienced changing expansion rates. A common error is not converting Mpc to consistent units or getting an order-of-magnitude wrong answer.
**Tags**: age-of-universe, hubble-constant, calculation, assumptions, precision
