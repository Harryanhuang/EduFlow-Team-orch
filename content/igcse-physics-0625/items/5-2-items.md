# Items: 5.2 — Electrical quantities

### Question Q-5.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A current of 2 A flows through a lamp for 30 seconds. Calculate the charge that has passed through the lamp.
**Answer**: 60 C
**Explanation**: Q = It = 2 × 30 = 60 C. A common error is dividing instead of multiplying.
**Tags**: charge, current, Q=It, calculation

### Question Q-5.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: 120 C of charge passes through a wire in 40 seconds. Calculate the current.
**Answer**: 3 A
**Explanation**: I = Q/t = 120/40 = 3 A. A common error is multiplying instead of dividing.
**Tags**: current, charge, I=Q/t, calculation

### Question Q-5.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the difference between the emf of a cell and the potential difference across a component?
**Answer**: Emf is the energy supplied per unit charge by the source. Potential difference is the energy transferred per unit charge by a component.
**Explanation**: Emf is about the source; p.d. is about energy transfer in a component. A common error is treating the terms as exactly identical.
**Tags**: emf, potential-difference, conceptual

### Question Q-5.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: In which direction does conventional current flow in a circuit? How does this compare with the actual electron flow?
**Answer**: Conventional current flows from the positive terminal to the negative terminal. Electron flow is in the opposite direction, from negative to positive.
**Explanation**: Conventional current was defined before electrons were discovered. A common error is saying conventional current and electron flow are the same direction.
**Tags**: conventional-current, electron-flow, direction

### Question Q-5.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A battery supplies 180 J of energy to 60 C of charge. Calculate the emf of the battery.
**Answer**: 3 V
**Explanation**: emf = W/Q = 180/60 = 3 V. A common error is using the power formula instead.
**Tags**: emf, energy, charge, V=W/Q

### Question Q-5.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How should an ammeter and a voltmeter be connected in a circuit to measure the current through and voltage across a lamp?
**Answer**: The ammeter must be connected in series with the lamp. The voltmeter must be connected in parallel across the lamp.
**Explanation**: Ammeters have very low resistance and voltmeters have very high resistance. A common error is swapping the connections.
**Tags**: ammeter, voltmeter, circuit-connection

### Question Q-5.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A resistor transfers 240 J of energy when 80 C of charge passes through it. The current is 4 A. Calculate (a) the potential difference across the resistor, (b) the time for which the current flows.
**Answer**: (a) V = W/Q = 240/80 = 3 V. (b) t = Q/I = 80/4 = 20 s.
**Explanation**: This combines V = W/Q and I = Q/t. A common error is mixing up energy and charge in the first formula.
**Tags**: potential-difference, current, charge, energy, multi-step

### Question Q-5.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student says, "Current is used up as it passes through a lamp, so less current leaves the lamp than enters it." Explain why this is wrong.
**Answer**: Current is the rate of flow of charge. In a complete series circuit, the same current enters and leaves the lamp because charge is conserved. The lamp transfers electrical energy to light and thermal energy; it does not use up charge.
**Explanation**: Energy is transferred in the lamp, but charge is not consumed. A common error is confusing current with energy.
**Tags**: current, conservation-of-charge, misconception

### Question Q-5.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A 6 V battery is connected to two resistors in series. The potential difference across the first resistor is 2 V. Explain the potential difference across the second resistor and what this shows about energy transfer in a series circuit.
**Answer**: The potential difference across the second resistor is 4 V because the p.d.s across series components add to the supply voltage: 2 V + 4 V = 6 V. This shows the energy supplied per coulomb by the battery is shared between the components.
**Explanation**: Series p.d.s add to the supply. A common error is saying each resistor must have the full 6 V across it.
**Tags**: series-circuit, potential-difference, energy-transfer
