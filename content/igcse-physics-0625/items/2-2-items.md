# Items: 2.2 — Hooke's law, spring constant, force-extension graph

### Question Q-2.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A spring extends by 4 cm when a force of 8 N is applied. Calculate the spring constant.
**Answer**: 2 N/cm (or 200 N/m)
**Explanation**: k = F/x = 8/4 = 2 N/cm. In SI units: k = 8/0.04 = 200 N/m. A common error is not converting cm to m when using SI units.
**Tags**: Hooke's-law, spring-constant, calculation

### Question Q-2.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A spring has a spring constant of 50 N/m. Calculate the force needed to extend it by 0.1 m.
**Answer**: 5 N
**Explanation**: F = kx = 50 × 0.1 = 5 N. A common error is dividing instead of multiplying.
**Tags**: Hooke's-law, force, calculation

### Question Q-2.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe how you could verify that a spring obeys Hooke's law using standard lab equipment.
**Answer**: Hang the spring vertically. Add known masses one at a time and measure the extension each time. Plot force against extension. If the graph is a straight line through the origin, the spring obeys Hooke's law.
**Explanation**: The key is the linear relationship. A common error is only measuring one or two points and claiming Hooke's law.
**Tags**: Hooke's-law, experiment, graph

### Question Q-2.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is meant by the "limit of proportionality" of a spring?
**Answer**: The maximum force up to which the extension is directly proportional to the force. Beyond this point, Hooke's law no longer applies.
**Explanation**: A common error is confusing it with the "breaking point" — the limit of proportionality is where linearity ends, not where the spring breaks.
**Tags**: limit-of-proportionality, Hooke's-law, definition

### Question Q-2.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A force-extension graph for a spring shows a straight line from the origin up to 12 N, then curves. What does the curved section indicate?
**Answer**: The spring has exceeded its limit of proportionality and no longer obeys Hooke's law. The extension is no longer proportional to the force.
**Explanation**: The straight section represents Hooke's law; the curve shows non-linear behaviour. A common error is saying the spring "breaks" in the curved section.
**Tags**: force-extension-graph, Hooke's-law, limit-of-proportionality

### Question Q-2.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Two identical springs are connected in parallel. A force of 10 N extends the combination by 2 cm. What is the spring constant of each individual spring?
**Answer**: 2.5 N/cm
**Explanation**: For springs in parallel, k_total = k₁ + k₂ = 2k. k_total = F/x = 10/2 = 5 N/cm. So k = 5/2 = 2.5 N/cm. A common error is treating them as if they were in series.
**Tags**: Hooke's-law, parallel-springs, spring-constant

### Question Q-2.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A spring of natural length 15 cm extends to 20 cm when a 4 N load is attached. Calculate: (a) the spring constant, (b) the extension when a 6 N load is attached, (c) the total length with the 6 N load, (d) the energy stored in the spring with the 4 N load.
**Answer**: (a) 0.8 N/cm. (b) 7.5 cm. (c) 22.5 cm. (d) 0.1 J.
**Explanation**: (a) k = 4/5 = 0.8 N/cm. (b) x = 6/0.8 = 7.5 cm. (c) 15 + 7.5 = 22.5 cm. (d) Energy = ½ × 4 × 0.05 = 0.1 J (using metres). A common error is using cm for the energy calculation.
**Tags**: Hooke's-law, spring-constant, energy, multi-step, challenge

### Question Q-2.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Two springs are connected in series. Spring A has k = 10 N/cm and Spring B has k = 5 N/cm. A force of 20 N is applied. Calculate: (a) the extension of each spring, (b) the total extension, (c) the effective spring constant of the combination.
**Answer**: (a) A: 2 cm, B: 4 cm. (b) 6 cm. (c) 3.33 N/cm.
**Explanation**: In series, each spring feels the full force. k_eff: 1/k_eff = 1/10 + 1/5 = 3/10, k_eff = 10/3 = 3.33 N/cm. A common error is adding the spring constants (as for parallel).
**Tags**: Hooke's-law, series-springs, effective-constant, challenge

### Question Q-2.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student plots a force-extension graph for a rubber band. The graph is curved throughout and does not pass through the origin when the load is removed (hysteresis). Explain what this means and why rubber does not obey Hooke's law.
**Answer**: Rubber's polymer chains uncoil rather than stretch bonds, giving non-linear behaviour. Hysteresis means energy is dissipated as heat — the area between loading and unloading curves represents energy lost.
**Explanation**: Hooke's law applies where extension is proportional to force. Rubber's non-linear behaviour is due to its polymer structure. A common error is saying rubber "doesn't work" — it stores energy, just not linearly.
**Tags**: Hooke's-law, rubber, hysteresis, molecular-explanation, challenge
