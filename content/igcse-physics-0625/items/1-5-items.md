# Items: 1.5 — Density, measuring density

### Question Q-1.5-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A rectangular block has a mass of 360 g and dimensions 6 cm × 5 cm × 4 cm. Calculate its density in g/cm³.
**Answer**: 3 g/cm³
**Explanation**: Volume = 6 × 5 × 4 = 120 cm³. Density = mass / volume = 360 / 120 = 3 g/cm³. A common error is adding the dimensions (6 + 5 + 4 = 15) instead of multiplying them to find volume.
**Tags**: density, volume, calculation, rho=m/V

### Question Q-1.5-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: The density of aluminium is 2.7 g/cm³. Convert this value into kg/m³.
**Answer**: 2700 kg/m³
**Explanation**: 1 g/cm³ = 1000 kg/m³. Therefore 2.7 g/cm³ = 2.7 × 1000 = 2700 kg/m³. To convert, multiply by 1000 because there are 1 000 000 cm³ in 1 m³ but only 1000 g in 1 kg, giving a factor of 1000. A common error is multiplying by 100 instead of 1000.
**Tags**: density, unit-conversion, g/cm3-to-kg/m3

### Question Q-1.5-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe how you would use the displacement method to determine the density of an irregularly shaped stone that does not dissolve in water. Include all measurements you need to take.
**Answer**: Step 1: Measure the mass of the stone using an electronic balance. Step 2: Partially fill a measuring cylinder with water and record the initial volume V₁. Step 3: Gently lower the stone into the water so it is fully submerged. Step 4: Record the new water level V₂. Step 5: Volume of stone = V₂ − V₁. Step 6: Density = mass / (V₂ − V₁).
**Explanation**: The displacement method works because the volume of water displaced equals the volume of the submerged object. A common error is forgetting to subtract the initial volume from the final volume, or dropping the stone in so quickly that water splashes out, reducing accuracy.
**Tags**: density, displacement-method, irregular-solid, experiment

### Question Q-1.5-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A student wants to measure the density of a liquid. She measures the mass of an empty measuring cylinder as 45 g. She pours 50 cm³ of the liquid into the cylinder and the total mass is now 85 g. Calculate the density of the liquid.
**Answer**: 0.8 g/cm³
**Explanation**: Mass of liquid = total mass − mass of empty cylinder = 85 − 45 = 40 g. Density = mass / volume = 40 / 50 = 0.8 g/cm³. A common error is using the total mass (85 g) instead of subtracting the cylinder mass first, which would give an incorrect density of 1.7 g/cm³.
**Tags**: density, liquid-density, measurement, calculation

### Question Q-1.5-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: An object has a density of 850 kg/m³. Water has a density of 1000 kg/m³. (a) Will the object float or sink in water? (b) What fraction of the object's volume will be submerged when it floats?
**Answer**: (a) It will float because its density (850 kg/m³) is less than water (1000 kg/m³). (b) Fraction submerged = 850 / 1000 = 0.85, so 85% of its volume is submerged.
**Explanation**: An object floats if its density is less than the fluid's density, and sinks if greater. For a floating object, the fraction submerged equals the ratio of the object's density to the fluid's density. A common error is predicting sink when the object's density is lower, or inverting the fraction (saying 15% submerged instead of 85%).
**Tags**: density, floating, sinking, Archimedes, prediction

### Question Q-1.5-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A mixture is made by pouring 200 cm³ of liquid A (density 1.2 g/cm³) and 300 cm³ of liquid B (density 0.8 g/cm³) into a container. Assuming the liquids mix completely and the total volume is 500 cm³, calculate the density of the mixture.
**Answer**: 0.96 g/cm³
**Explanation**: Mass of A = 200 × 1.2 = 240 g. Mass of B = 300 × 0.8 = 240 g. Total mass = 240 + 240 = 480 g. Total volume = 500 cm³. Density of mixture = 480 / 500 = 0.96 g/cm³. A common error is averaging the two densities: (1.2 + 0.8) / 2 = 1.0 g/cm³ — this ignores the different volumes of each liquid.
**Tags**: density, mixture, combined-materials, calculation

### Question Q-1.5-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: An alloy is made by melting together 600 g of copper (density 8.9 g/cm³) and 400 g of zinc (density 7.1 g/cm³). Assuming the total volume of the alloy equals the sum of the individual metal volumes, calculate the density of the alloy. Give your answer to 2 significant figures.
**Answer**: 8.1 g/cm³ (to 2 s.f.)
**Explanation**: Volume of copper = 600 / 8.9 = 67.4 cm³. Volume of zinc = 400 / 7.1 = 56.3 cm³. Total volume = 67.4 + 56.3 = 123.7 cm³. Total mass = 600 + 400 = 1000 g. Density = 1000 / 123.7 = 8.08 ≈ 8.1 g/cm³. A common error is simply averaging the two densities weighted by mass (e.g. (600×8.9 + 400×7.1)/1000 = 8.18) — this gives a weighted average of density, not the true alloy density. The correct method is to find each volume separately, sum them, then divide total mass by total volume.
**Tags**: density, alloy, mixed-material, multi-step, challenge

### Question Q-1.5-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A density bottle has a mass of 22.0 g when empty. When completely filled with water, its total mass is 72.0 g. When filled with an unknown liquid, its total mass is 62.5 g. (a) Find the volume of the bottle. (b) Calculate the density of the unknown liquid. (c) If the liquid is then heated and expands by 2%, what is its new density? (Water density = 1.00 g/cm³)
**Answer**: (a) Volume = 50.0 cm³. (b) Density = 0.81 g/cm³. (c) New density = 0.794 g/cm³ (to 3 s.f.).
**Explanation**: (a) Mass of water = 72.0 − 22.0 = 50.0 g. Since water density = 1.00 g/cm³, volume of bottle = 50.0 cm³. (b) Mass of unknown liquid = 62.5 − 22.0 = 40.5 g. Density = 40.5 / 50.0 = 0.81 g/cm³. (c) New volume = 50.0 × 1.02 = 51.0 cm³. Mass stays 40.5 g. New density = 40.5 / 51.0 = 0.794 g/cm³. Common errors include using total mass instead of liquid mass alone, and forgetting that thermal expansion increases volume while mass stays constant, thus decreasing density.
**Tags**: density, density-bottle, relative-density, thermal-expansion, multi-step, challenge

### Question Q-1.5-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student is asked to measure the density of a small metal sphere. She uses a balance (precision ±0.1 g) and a measuring cylinder (precision ±1 cm³). Her results: mass = 28.3 g, initial water volume = 50 cm³, final water volume = 54 cm³. (a) Calculate her measured density. (b) Calculate the maximum and minimum possible density values given the instrument uncertainties. (c) The accepted density is 7.8 g/cm³. State whether her result is consistent with the accepted value and suggest one improvement to her method.
**Answer**: (a) Density = 7.1 g/cm³. (b) Maximum density = 14.2 g/cm³, minimum density = 4.7 g/cm³. (c) Yes, 7.8 g/cm³ falls within the range 4.7–14.2 g/cm³, so the result is technically consistent but extremely imprecise. Improvement: use a more precise measuring cylinder (e.g. ±0.5 cm³) or use several identical spheres to increase the displaced volume and reduce the percentage uncertainty.
**Explanation**: (a) Volume = 54 − 50 = 4 cm³. Density = 28.3 / 4 = 7.075 ≈ 7.1 g/cm³. (b) Each volume reading has ±1 cm³ uncertainty, so the volume uncertainty is ±2 cm³ total. Minimum volume = (54 − 1) − (50 + 1) = 53 − 51 = 2 cm³. Maximum volume = (54 + 1) − (50 − 1) = 55 − 49 = 6 cm³. Mass range: 28.2 g to 28.4 g. Maximum density = max mass / min volume = 28.4 / 2 = 14.2 g/cm³. Minimum density = min mass / max volume = 28.2 / 6 = 4.7 g/cm³. (c) The accepted value falls within the range, but the huge uncertainty range (4.7–14.2) makes the measurement essentially useless for identifying the metal. The core problem is that a 4 cm³ displacement measured with ±2 cm³ uncertainty gives a 50% relative uncertainty in volume. A common error is treating the volume uncertainty as ±1 cm³ (from a single reading) rather than ±2 cm³ (since volume is the difference of two readings, each with its own ±1 cm³ uncertainty).
**Tags**: density, experimental-design, error-analysis, uncertainty, percentage-error, challenge
