# Items: 7.1 — Earth, solar system and orbits

### Question Q-7.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: List the eight planets of the Solar System in order from closest to the Sun to furthest away.
**Answer**: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune.
**Explanation**: The planets are ordered by increasing distance from the Sun. Pluto was reclassified as a dwarf planet in 2006 and is no longer counted among the major planets. A common error is including Pluto or listing the planets in the wrong order.
**Tags**: planets, solar-system, ordering, mercury, venus, earth, mars, jupiter, saturn, uranus, neptune

### Question Q-7.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What force keeps the planets in orbit around the Sun? Describe the direction of this force.
**Answer**: Gravitational force (gravity) from the Sun keeps the planets in orbit. This force acts towards the centre of the Sun and provides the centripetal force required for circular (or elliptical) motion.
**Explanation**: Gravity is the attractive force between masses. For orbital motion, gravity always points towards the central body. A common error is saying "centrifugal force" — this is not a real force but an apparent effect of inertia.
**Tags**: gravity, orbit, centripetal-force, sun, planets

### Question Q-7.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Distinguish between a planet and a natural satellite (moon).
**Answer**: A planet is a celestial body that orbits a star (such as the Sun), is massive enough to be rounded by its own gravity, and has cleared its orbital path. A moon (natural satellite) is a body that orbits a planet rather than a star directly. Both are held in their orbits by gravitational attraction.
**Explanation**: The key distinction is what they orbit: planets orbit stars, moons orbit planets. A common error is defining moons as "smaller than planets" — this is not always true (e.g., Ganymede is larger than Mercury).
**Tags**: planet, moon, satellite, definition, orbit

### Question Q-7.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a comet? Describe its composition, orbit, and the direction of its tail relative to the Sun.
**Answer**: A comet is a small body made of ice, dust, and rock that orbits the Sun in a highly elliptical orbit. When close to the Sun, the ice sublimates, creating a glowing coma and a tail of gas and dust. The tail always points away from the Sun due to the solar wind and radiation pressure, regardless of the comet's direction of travel.
**Explanation**: The tail direction is determined by solar wind pushing particles away, not by the comet's motion. A common error is saying the tail trails behind the comet — it can actually point ahead of the comet when it is moving away from the Sun.
**Tags**: comet, solar-system, tail, orbit, composition

### Question Q-7.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Where is our Solar System located within the Milky Way galaxy?
**Answer**: Our Solar System is located in one of the spiral arms of the Milky Way (called the Orion Arm or Orion Spur), approximately two-thirds of the way from the galactic centre to the outer edge, about 26 000 light-years from the centre.
**Explanation**: We are not at the centre of the galaxy nor at the very edge. A common error is stating that we are "at the centre of the Milky Way" or confusing the Solar System with the galaxy itself.
**Tags**: milky-way, solar-system, location, orion-arm, galaxy

### Question Q-7.1-06
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain why planets closer to the Sun orbit faster than planets further away.
**Answer**: The gravitational force from the Sun is stronger at shorter distances (F ∝ 1/r²). To maintain a stable orbit, a planet closer to the Sun must travel faster to balance the stronger gravitational pull. The orbital speed v = √(GM/r), so as r decreases, v increases. Mercury orbits fastest; Neptune slowest.
**Explanation**: Orbital speed depends on the balance between gravitational attraction and the centrifugal tendency of the orbiting body. A common error is thinking all planets orbit at the same speed or that outer planets are faster.
**Tags**: orbital-speed, gravity, planets, distance, kepler

### Question Q-7.1-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: An artificial satellite is placed in a geostationary orbit above the Earth. State two features of such an orbit and give one practical use.
**Answer**: Features: (1) The satellite orbits once every 24 hours, matching Earth's rotation period. (2) It orbits above the equator in the same direction as Earth's rotation (west to east). Practical use: communications satellites (TV, radio, telephone) because ground antennas do not need to track the satellite — it appears fixed in the sky.
**Explanation**: Geostationary means "stationary relative to Earth." A common error is confusing geostationary with low-Earth orbit satellites, which pass overhead many times per day.
**Tags**: artificial-satellite, geostationary, orbit, communications, earth

### Question Q-7.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: In 2006, Pluto was reclassified from a planet to a dwarf planet. State the three criteria for a body to be classified as a full planet, and explain which criterion Pluto fails to meet.
**Answer**: A planet must: (1) orbit the Sun, (2) be massive enough for its own gravity to make it nearly spherical, and (3) have "cleared its neighbourhood" — i.e., removed other objects of comparable size from its orbital path. Pluto meets criteria 1 and 2 but fails criterion 3: its orbit crosses through the Kuiper Belt, sharing its orbital region with many other icy bodies.
**Explanation**: The "clearing the neighbourhood" requirement distinguishes planets from dwarf planets. A common error is thinking Pluto was "demoted because it is small" — size alone does not determine planetary status.
**Tags**: pluto, dwarf-planet, classification, kuiper-belt, criteria

### Question Q-7.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare asteroids and comets. State two differences in their composition and orbits, and identify where each is predominantly found in the Solar System.
**Answer**: Differences: (1) Composition: asteroids are made of rock and metal; comets are made of ice, dust, and rock ("dirty snowballs"). (2) Orbits: asteroids have relatively circular orbits in a narrow band; comets have highly elliptical orbits taking them far beyond the planets. Location: most asteroids are in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter; comets originate from the Kuiper Belt beyond Neptune and the distant Oort Cloud.
**Explanation**: The fundamental difference is ice content: comets contain significant ice that sublimates near the Sun, creating tails. Asteroids are dry rocky bodies. A common error is saying asteroids also develop tails or that comets are found mainly between Mars and Jupiter.
**Tags**: asteroid, comet, composition, orbit, comparison, asteroid-belt, kuiper-belt
