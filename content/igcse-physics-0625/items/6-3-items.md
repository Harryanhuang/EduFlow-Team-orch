# Items: 6.3 — Half-life and decay curves

### Question Q-6.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A radioactive isotope has a half-life of 6 hours. What fraction of the original sample remains after 24 hours?
**Answer**: 1/16.
**Explanation**: Number of half-lives = 24 ÷ 6 = 4. Fraction remaining = (1/2)⁴ = 1/16. Each half-life halves the remaining amount. A common error is saying 1/4 (dividing by 4 instead of halving 4 times).
**Tags**: half-life, fraction, calculation, decay

### Question Q-6.3-02
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: The activity of a radioactive sample is 800 Bq. After 10 days, it is 200 Bq. Calculate the half-life.
**Answer**: 5 days.
**Explanation**: 800 → 400 → 200 represents two half-lives. Since 2 half-lives = 10 days, one half-life = 10 ÷ 2 = 5 days. Activity halves with each half-life. A common error is saying 10 days is one half-life without counting the steps.
**Tags**: half-life, activity, calculation, decay

### Question Q-6.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A Geiger counter reads 260 counts/min near a radioactive source. The background radiation is 20 counts/min. After 12 hours, the reading is 85 counts/min. Calculate the half-life.
**Answer**: Approximately 6 hours.
**Explanation**: Corrected initial activity = 260 - 20 = 240 counts/min. Corrected final activity = 85 - 20 = 65 counts/min. 240 → 120 → 60 (approximately 65) shows about 2 half-lives. 12 hours ÷ 2 ≈ 6 hours per half-life. Always subtract background radiation before calculating. A common error is using the uncorrected readings directly.
**Tags**: half-life, background-radiation, correction, geiger-counter

### Question Q-6.3-04
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: From a decay curve, the count rate drops from 400 to 100 in 14 days. What is the half-life?
**Answer**: 7 days.
**Explanation**: 400 → 200 → 100 represents 2 half-lives. Since 2 half-lives = 14 days, one half-life = 14 ÷ 2 = 7 days. Reading a decay curve requires identifying the time for the count rate to halve. A common error is saying 14 days is one half-life.
**Tags**: half-life, decay-curve, calculation, graph

### Question Q-6.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why is background radiation always present? Give two sources.
**Answer**: Background radiation is always present because radioactive materials occur naturally in the environment and cosmic rays constantly bombard Earth. Two sources: radon gas from rocks and soil, and cosmic rays from space. Other sources include naturally occurring isotopes in food and medical procedures.
**Explanation**: Background radiation is unavoidable and comes from multiple natural and artificial sources. A common error is attributing it solely to nuclear power stations or man-made sources.
**Tags**: background-radiation, sources, natural-radioactivity

### Question Q-6.3-06
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A sample of a radioactive isotope initially contains 8.0 g. After 15 days, only 1.0 g remains. Calculate the half-life and determine how much will remain after a further 10 days.
**Answer**: Half-life = 5 days. After a further 10 days, 0.25 g will remain.
**Explanation**: 8.0 → 4.0 → 2.0 → 1.0 shows 3 half-lives in 15 days, so half-life = 15 ÷ 3 = 5 days. After a further 10 days (= 2 more half-lives): 1.0 → 0.5 → 0.25 g. A common error is miscalculating the number of half-lives or not continuing the decay for the additional time.
**Tags**: half-life, mass, calculation, multi-step

### Question Q-6.3-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A radioactive isotope has a half-life of 3 years. What percentage of a sample will have decayed after 9 years?
**Answer**: 87.5% will have decayed.
**Explanation**: Number of half-lives = 9 ÷ 3 = 3. Fraction remaining = (1/2)³ = 1/8 = 12.5%. Percentage decayed = 100% - 12.5% = 87.5%. A common error is giving the percentage remaining (12.5%) instead of the percentage decayed.
**Tags**: half-life, percentage, decayed, calculation

### Question Q-6.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: An archaeological sample has an activity of 50 Bq. A similar living sample has an activity of 400 Bq. If the half-life of the isotope is 5730 years, estimate the age of the archaeological sample.
**Answer**: Approximately 17 190 years.
**Explanation**: 400 → 200 → 100 → 50 shows 3 half-lives. Age = 3 × 5730 = 17 190 years. This method (radiocarbon dating) compares current activity to the expected original activity. A common error is miscounting the number of half-lives or multiplying incorrectly.
**Tags**: half-life, radiocarbon-dating, age, archaeology, calculation

### Question Q-6.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Sketch a typical decay curve showing count rate against time for a radioactive source. Label the axes and indicate the half-life on your graph.
**Answer**: The graph shows a curved line starting at a high count rate on the y-axis and decreasing exponentially towards zero as time increases on the x-axis. The curve never touches the x-axis. The half-life is marked as equal time intervals along the x-axis corresponding to the count rate halving on the y-axis (e.g., 400 → 200 at t₁, 200 → 100 at t₂, where t₂ - t₁ = half-life).
**Explanation**: Radioactive decay follows an exponential decay curve. The key features are: smooth curve, constant half-life intervals, and the curve approaching but never reaching zero. A common error is drawing a straight line or inconsistent half-life intervals.
**Tags**: half-life, decay-curve, graph, sketch, exponential
