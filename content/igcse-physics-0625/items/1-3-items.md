# Items: 1.3 — Free fall, g, air resistance

### Question Q-1.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A stone is dropped from rest at the edge of a cliff. Calculate its speed after 5.0 seconds of free fall. (g = 10 m/s²)
**Answer**: 50 m/s
**Explanation**: Using v = u + gt, where u = 0 (dropped from rest): v = 0 + 10 × 5.0 = 50 m/s. The object accelerates at 10 m/s², gaining 10 m/s of speed every second. A common error is using s = ½gt² when the question asks for speed rather than distance, or forgetting to state that the initial speed u = 0.
**Tags**: free-fall, speed-calculation, gravity, v-equals-gt

### Question Q-1.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: An object is dropped from rest and reaches a speed of 35 m/s during free fall. Calculate how long it has been falling. (g = 10 m/s²)
**Answer**: 3.5 s
**Explanation**: Using v = gt (since u = 0), rearrange to t = v / g = 35 / 10 = 3.5 s. The object gains 10 m/s every second, so reaching 35 m/s takes 3.5 seconds. A common error is multiplying v × g instead of dividing, or rearranging the formula incorrectly.
**Tags**: free-fall, speed-calculation, gravity, time-calculation

### Question Q-1.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A ball is dropped from the top of a tall building and takes 6.0 seconds to reach the ground. Calculate: (a) the height of the building, (b) the speed of the ball just before it hits the ground. (g = 10 m/s²)
**Answer**: (a) 180 m. (b) 60 m/s.
**Explanation**: (a) s = ½gt² = ½ × 10 × 6.0² = 5 × 36 = 180 m. (b) v = gt = 10 × 6.0 = 60 m/s. A common error is forgetting the factor of ½ in the distance formula, giving 360 m instead of 180 m. Another common mistake is using s = vt with the final speed (60 × 6 = 360 m) rather than the correct kinematic equation — the speed is not constant during the fall.
**Tags**: free-fall, distance-calculation, speed-calculation, kinematics

### Question Q-1.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A speed-time graph for an object in free fall (no air resistance) is a straight line through the origin with a constant gradient of 10 m/s². (a) State what the gradient of this graph represents. (b) Use the graph to calculate the distance fallen in the first 4.0 seconds. (c) Explain how the graph would change if air resistance were significant.
**Answer**: (a) The gradient represents the acceleration due to gravity, g = 10 m/s². (b) At t = 4.0 s, v = 10 × 4.0 = 40 m/s. Distance = area under graph = ½ × base × height = ½ × 4.0 × 40 = 80 m. (c) With air resistance, the graph would curve and gradually level off (gradient decreases) as the object approaches terminal velocity, rather than remaining a straight line.
**Explanation**: The gradient of a speed-time graph equals acceleration. In free fall without air resistance this is constant at g. The area under the graph gives distance. A common error is calculating the area as a rectangle (40 × 4 = 160 m) instead of a triangle, forgetting that the object started from rest. For part (c), students often incorrectly draw a line that goes downward, confusing decreasing acceleration with decreasing speed.
**Tags**: speed-time-graph, free-fall, area-under-graph, air-resistance, gradient

### Question Q-1.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Define terminal velocity and explain, in terms of forces, why a falling object reaches terminal velocity. Include in your answer what happens to the acceleration at terminal velocity.
**Answer**: Terminal velocity is the constant maximum speed reached by a falling object when the upward air resistance (drag force) equals the downward weight (gravitational force), resulting in a net force of zero. As an object falls, it accelerates due to gravity, and its speed increases. As speed increases, air resistance increases. Eventually air resistance grows large enough to equal the object's weight. At this point the resultant force is zero, so acceleration becomes zero, and the object continues to fall at a constant speed — its terminal velocity.
**Explanation**: The key concept is the force balance: weight (downwards) = air resistance (upwards) at terminal velocity. Because F_net = 0, by Newton's second law a = 0, so speed is constant. A common error is stating that the object "stops falling" at terminal velocity — it continues to fall, just at a constant speed. Another error is saying air resistance exceeds weight at terminal velocity; they are exactly equal.
**Tags**: terminal-velocity, definition, forces, air-resistance, equilibrium

### Question Q-1.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A skydiver of weight 750 N is falling through the air. At one instant during the fall, the air resistance acting on the skydiver is 450 N. (a) Calculate the resultant force on the skydiver and state its direction. (b) Describe and explain how the skydiver's acceleration at this instant compares with the acceleration at the moment of jumping. (c) Explain what will happen as the skydiver continues to fall.
**Answer**: (a) Resultant force = weight − air resistance = 750 − 450 = 300 N downwards. (b) At the moment of jumping, air resistance was zero so acceleration was g = 10 m/s². Now, with a resultant force of 300 N (less than the weight of 750 N), the acceleration is less than g. The skydiver is still accelerating, but at a reduced rate. (c) As the skydiver continues to fall, speed increases, so air resistance increases, reducing the resultant force further. Eventually air resistance equals weight (750 N), the resultant force becomes zero, and terminal velocity is reached.
**Explanation**: Acceleration depends on resultant force, not just weight. Since the resultant force is decreasing (air resistance is increasing), the acceleration decreases even though the speed is still increasing. A common error is saying the skydiver is "slowing down" when air resistance is less than weight — the skydiver is still speeding up, just at a decreasing rate of acceleration. Another error is confusing decreased acceleration with deceleration.
**Tags**: resultant-force, air-resistance, free-fall, acceleration, terminal-velocity

### Question Q-1.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A package is dropped from a helicopter and falls freely (no air resistance) for 3.0 seconds. A parachute then opens and the package decelerates uniformly, reducing its speed from its current value to 5.0 m/s over the next 2.0 seconds. Calculate: (a) the speed of the package just before the parachute opens, (b) the distance fallen during the first 3.0 seconds (free fall), (c) the deceleration during the 2.0 seconds after the parachute opens, (d) the distance fallen during the 2.0 seconds after the parachute opens, (e) the total distance fallen in the first 5.0 seconds. (g = 10 m/s²)
**Answer**: (a) v = gt = 10 × 3.0 = 30 m/s. (b) s = ½gt² = ½ × 10 × 3.0² = 45 m. (c) Deceleration = (30 − 5.0) / 2.0 = 25 / 2.0 = 12.5 m/s². (d) s = average speed × time = ½(30 + 5.0) × 2.0 = 17.5 × 2.0 = 35 m. (e) Total distance = 45 + 35 = 80 m.
**Explanation**: This is a two-stage problem requiring separate analysis of each phase. Stage 1 is standard free fall. Stage 2 is uniform deceleration (not free fall) — use average speed × time or s = ut + ½at². A common error is treating the entire 5 seconds as one motion and applying a single formula. Another error in part (c) is getting a negative deceleration by subtracting in the wrong order. For part (d), students sometimes use s = ½gt² for the parachute phase, which is incorrect because g does not apply during deceleration.
**Tags**: multi-stage-motion, free-fall, deceleration, parachute, kinematics, challenge

### Question Q-1.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Two identical steel spheres, A and B, are dropped simultaneously from the top of a 180 m tower. Sphere A falls through a vacuum tube and sphere B falls through the air. (a) Calculate the time taken for sphere A to reach the ground and its speed on impact. (b) Sphere B reaches a terminal velocity of 50 m/s after falling for 8.0 seconds. Describe the motion of sphere B during the first 8.0 seconds and after 8.0 seconds. (c) Explain, with reference to forces, why sphere B takes longer to reach the ground than sphere A. (d) Calculate the distance fallen by sphere B in the first 8.0 seconds if its average speed during this period is 30 m/s, and then calculate the remaining distance to the ground. (g = 10 m/s²)
**Answer**: (a) For sphere A: s = ½gt² → 180 = 5t² → t² = 36 → t = 6.0 s. Impact speed v = gt = 10 × 6.0 = 60 m/s. (b) During the first 8.0 s: sphere B accelerates but at a decreasing rate as air resistance increases. After 8.0 s: sphere B falls at a constant terminal velocity of 50 m/s (acceleration = 0). (c) Air resistance acts upwards on sphere B, reducing the resultant downward force (resultant = weight − air resistance). This gives sphere B a smaller acceleration than sphere A at every speed, so sphere B takes longer to reach the ground. (d) Distance in first 8.0 s = average speed × time = 30 × 8.0 = 240 m. However, the tower is only 180 m tall, so sphere B reaches the ground during the acceleration phase (before reaching terminal velocity). The remaining distance concept does not apply — sphere B hits the ground before 8.0 s at this average speed.
**Explanation**: Part (a) is straightforward free fall. Part (b) requires understanding the changing force balance: initially weight >> air resistance so acceleration is near g; as speed increases, air resistance increases, reducing acceleration; at terminal velocity the forces balance. Part (c) tests force reasoning — air resistance reduces the net force, not gravity itself. Part (d) is a trap: 30 × 8.0 = 240 m exceeds the 180 m tower height, meaning sphere B must reach the ground before attaining terminal velocity. A common error is blindly calculating 240 m without checking against the tower height, or assuming both spheres can be compared over the full 180 m using terminal velocity.
**Tags**: vacuum, air-resistance, terminal-velocity, comparison, free-fall, forces, challenge

### Question Q-1.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A skydiver jumps from a stationary helicopter and her motion has three stages: Stage 1 — she accelerates from rest and reaches a terminal velocity of 60 m/s after 12 s (assume uniform acceleration for simplicity); Stage 2 — she falls at 60 m/s for 30 s; Stage 3 — she opens her parachute and decelerates uniformly to a new terminal velocity of 5.0 m/s in 4.0 s, then falls at 5.0 m/s until she lands. (a) Calculate the distance fallen in Stage 1. (b) Calculate the distance fallen in Stage 2. (c) Calculate the deceleration in Stage 3 during the first 4.0 s after opening the parachute. (d) Calculate the distance fallen during the 4.0 s deceleration in Stage 3. (e) If the skydiver jumped from a height of 3000 m, calculate the distance she must fall at 5.0 m/s and the time this final part takes. (f) Calculate the total time from jumping to landing. (g) Sketch the speed-time graph for the entire motion, labelling each stage. (g = 10 m/s²)
**Answer**: (a) Stage 1 (uniform acceleration): s = ½(u + v)t = ½(0 + 60) × 12 = 360 m. (b) Stage 2 (constant speed): s = vt = 60 × 30 = 1800 m. (c) Deceleration = (60 − 5.0) / 4.0 = 55 / 4.0 = 13.75 m/s² ≈ 14 m/s². (d) s = ½(u + v)t = ½(60 + 5.0) × 4.0 = ½ × 65 × 4.0 = 130 m. (e) Distance so far = 360 + 1800 + 130 = 2290 m. Remaining distance = 3000 − 2290 = 710 m. Time at 5.0 m/s = 710 / 5.0 = 142 s. (f) Total time = 12 + 30 + 4.0 + 142 = 188 s (3 minutes 8 seconds). (g) The speed-time graph has: a straight line from (0, 0) to (12, 60) labelled Stage 1; a horizontal line from (12, 60) to (42, 60) labelled Stage 2; a straight line sloping down from (42, 60) to (46, 5) labelled Stage 3 deceleration; a horizontal line from (46, 5) to (188, 5) labelled Stage 3 constant speed.
**Explanation**: This comprehensive problem requires applying different kinematic equations to each stage. Stage 1 uses average speed for uniform acceleration. Stage 2 uses s = vt for constant speed. Stage 3 deceleration uses the definition of acceleration and average speed. The final part is simple constant-speed calculation. A common error is applying s = ½gt² to Stage 1 — this would give ½ × 10 × 144 = 720 m, which is incorrect because the actual acceleration is not g (air resistance is present and terminal velocity is reached). The problem states to assume uniform acceleration, so s = ½(u+v)t is appropriate. Another error is adding the deceleration distance incorrectly or forgetting to include it in the total when calculating the remaining distance.
**Tags**: multi-stage-motion, terminal-velocity, speed-time-graph, deceleration, comprehensive, parachute, kinematics, challenge
