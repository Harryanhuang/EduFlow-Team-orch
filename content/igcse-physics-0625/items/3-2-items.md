# Items: 3.2 — Temperature scales, thermometers, thermal equilibrium

### Question Q-3.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Convert the following temperatures to Kelvin: (a) 0°C, (b) 25°C, (c) -40°C.
**Answer**: (a) 273 K, (b) 298 K, (c) 233 K
**Explanation**: T(K) = θ(°C) + 273. For -40°C: -40 + 273 = 233 K. A common error is subtracting 273 instead of adding.
**Tags**: temperature, Kelvin, conversion

### Question Q-3.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A thermometer is calibrated using the ice point and steam point. The distance between these marks is 20 cm. If the mercury rises 5 cm above the ice point, what is the temperature?
**Answer**: 25°C
**Explanation**: Temperature = (5/20) × 100 = 25°C. The scale is linear between the two fixed points. A common error is not using the proportional method.
**Tags**: thermometer, calibration, linear-scale

### Question Q-3.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is meant by thermal equilibrium?
**Answer**: Two objects in contact are in thermal equilibrium when they are at the same temperature and there is no net flow of thermal energy between them.
**Explanation**: The key is "no net" flow — energy still transfers both ways equally. A common error is saying "no energy transfer at all."
**Tags**: thermal-equilibrium, definition

### Question Q-3.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why a clinical thermometer has a constriction in the capillary tube.
**Answer**: The constriction prevents the mercury from flowing back into the bulb when removed from the patient, so the reading can be taken after removal. The mercury must be shaken back down for the next use.
**Explanation**: The constriction breaks the mercury column. A common error is saying it "makes the thermometer more accurate."
**Tags**: thermometer, clinical, constriction

### Question Q-3.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: State two advantages of a thermocouple thermometer over a liquid-in-glass thermometer.
**Answer**: (1) Can measure very high or very low temperatures (wider range). (2) Responds quickly to temperature changes (small thermal capacity).
**Explanation**: Thermocouples use voltage, not liquid expansion. A common error is saying they are "more accurate" — they may not be, but they have other advantages.
**Tags**: thermocouple, thermometer, comparison

### Question Q-3.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A liquid-in-glass thermometer has a very narrow bore. How does this affect its sensitivity?
**Answer**: A narrower bore means a greater change in the length of the liquid column for the same temperature change, making the thermometer more sensitive.
**Explanation**: The same volume expansion produces a longer column change in a narrower tube. A common error is saying it makes it "more accurate" — sensitivity and accuracy are different.
**Tags**: thermometer, sensitivity, design

### Question Q-3.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A resistance thermometer gives a resistance of 20 Ω at 0°C and 28 Ω at 100°C. When placed in a warm liquid, the resistance is 24 Ω. Calculate the temperature of the liquid.
**Answer**: 50°C
**Explanation**: The change in resistance is proportional to temperature change. Total resistance change = 28 - 20 = 8 Ω for 100°C. Measured change = 24 - 20 = 4 Ω. Temperature = (4/8) × 100 = 50°C. A common error is not recognising the linear relationship between resistance and temperature.
**Tags**: resistance-thermometer, calculation, linear-scale

### Question Q-3.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Two metal blocks X and Y are in thermal contact. Block X is initially at 80°C and block Y is at 20°C. Describe what happens to the temperatures of both blocks until they reach thermal equilibrium.
**Answer**: Thermal energy flows from the hotter block X to the cooler block Y. Block X cools down (loses energy) and block Y warms up (gains energy). This continues until both blocks reach the same intermediate temperature, at which point they are in thermal equilibrium and there is no net energy transfer. The final temperature depends on the masses and specific heat capacities of the blocks.
**Explanation**: Energy transfer continues until temperatures equalise. A common error is thinking that the final temperature will be exactly the average (50°C) — it depends on the thermal properties of each block, not just the initial temperatures.
**Tags**: thermal-equilibrium, energy-transfer, temperature-change

### Question Q-3.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A mercury-in-glass thermometer is inaccurate because the bore is slightly wider than intended. Will this thermometer read higher or lower than the true temperature? Explain your reasoning.
**Answer**: It will read lower than the true temperature. For a given temperature increase, the mercury expands by a fixed volume. In a wider bore, this same volume produces a smaller rise in the column length. Since the scale is calibrated assuming a narrower bore, the thermometer will indicate a smaller temperature change than actually occurred, giving a reading that is too low.
**Explanation**: A wider bore means less column movement per degree. A common error is confusing bore width with overall accuracy — the issue is about sensitivity, not whether the thermometer works at all.
**Tags**: thermometer, error-analysis, design, bore-width
