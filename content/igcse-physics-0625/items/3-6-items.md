# Items: 3.6 — Melting, boiling, latent heat, heating/cooling curves

### Question Q-3.6-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the difference between evaporation and boiling?
**Answer**: Evaporation occurs only at the surface of a liquid and can happen at any temperature. Boiling occurs throughout the liquid and happens only at the boiling point.
**Explanation**: Both location and temperature conditions differ. A common error is saying they are the same process.
**Tags**: evaporation, boiling, comparison

### Question Q-3.6-02
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Calculate the energy needed to melt 500 g of ice at 0°C. (Specific latent heat of fusion of ice = 334 000 J/kg)
**Answer**: 167 000 J
**Explanation**: Q = mL = 0.5 × 334 000 = 167 000 J. A common error is using Q = mcΔT (which gives zero since ΔT = 0).
**Tags**: latent-heat, melting, Q=mL, calculation

### Question Q-3.6-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A heating curve for water shows a flat section at 100°C. Explain why the temperature does not rise during this section even though heating continues.
**Answer**: The energy supplied is used to break the bonds between water molecules (change of state from liquid to gas), not to increase the kinetic energy (temperature) of the molecules.
**Explanation**: Energy goes into potential energy (bond breaking), not kinetic energy. A common error is saying "no energy is being absorbed."
**Tags**: heating-curve, boiling, plateau, energy-transfer

### Question Q-3.6-04
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Why does steam at 100°C cause a more severe burn than water at 100°C?
**Answer**: Steam has additional latent heat of vaporisation. When steam condenses on the skin, it releases this latent heat as well as cooling down, transferring more energy than water at the same temperature.
**Explanation**: The extra latent heat makes steam more dangerous. A common error is saying steam is "hotter than 100°C."
**Tags**: latent-heat, steam, burns, application

### Question Q-3.6-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: 2000 J of energy is supplied to 0.1 kg of a substance and it completely melts at its melting point. Calculate the specific latent heat of fusion.
**Answer**: 20 000 J/kg
**Explanation**: L = Q/m = 2000/0.1 = 20 000 J/kg. A common error is multiplying instead of dividing.
**Tags**: latent-heat, calculation, L=Q/m

### Question Q-3.6-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe the shape of a cooling curve for molten lead as it cools from 400°C to 250°C. (Melting point of lead = 327°C)
**Answer**: The temperature decreases steadily from 400°C to 327°C, then remains constant at 327°C while lead solidifies (plateau), then decreases again from 327°C to 250°C as solid lead cools.
**Explanation**: The plateau occurs at the melting/freezing point. A common error is missing the plateau or placing it at the wrong temperature.
**Tags**: cooling-curve, freezing, plateau, description

### Question Q-3.6-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student places 0.2 kg of ice at 0°C in a beaker and supplies 80 000 J of energy. After heating, some ice remains. Calculate how much ice has melted. (Specific latent heat of fusion of ice = 334 000 J/kg)
**Answer**: All 0.2 kg of ice melts; the statement that some ice remains is inconsistent with the data.
**Explanation**: Energy to melt all the ice is Q = mL = 0.2 × 334 000 = 66 800 J. Since 80 000 J is greater than this, all the ice melts and energy remains to warm the water. A common error is calculating 80 000/334 000 = 0.24 kg without checking the available mass.
**Tags**: latent-heat, melting, calculation, critical-thinking

### Question Q-3.6-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain why sweating is an effective cooling mechanism for the human body. Refer to the physics of phase change.
**Answer**: As sweat evaporates, it absorbs latent heat of vaporisation from the skin and body. This energy changes liquid water into vapour, removing thermal energy from the body. Air movement helps by removing water vapour, while high humidity reduces the evaporation rate.
**Explanation**: Evaporation requires energy, and that energy comes from the body. A common error is saying liquid sweat itself cools directly; the phase change is the key process.
**Tags**: evaporation, latent-heat, cooling, biological-application

### Question Q-3.6-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: An electric kettle boils 0.5 kg of water at 100°C. Calculate the energy needed to convert all the water to steam at 100°C. If the kettle is rated at 2400 W, calculate the minimum time required. (Specific latent heat of vaporisation of water = 2.26 × 10^6 J/kg)
**Answer**: Energy = 1.13 × 10^6 J; time = 471 s, approximately 7.9 min.
**Explanation**: Q = mL = 0.5 × 2.26 × 10^6 = 1.13 × 10^6 J. Then t = Q/P = 1 130 000/2400 = 471 s. A common error is using specific heat capacity when the temperature is constant at 100°C.
**Tags**: latent-heat, vaporisation, calculation, time
