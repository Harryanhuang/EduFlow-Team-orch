# Items: 5.10 — Force on a current-carrying conductor and d.c. motor

### Question Q-5.10-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: State what happens to a current-carrying wire placed at right angles to a magnetic field.
**Answer**: It experiences a force. The direction of the force depends on the directions of the current and magnetic field.
**Explanation**: The force is greatest when the wire is perpendicular to the field.
**Tags**: motor-effect, magnetic-field, current

### Question Q-5.10-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Name the rule used to find the direction of the force on a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field.
**Answer**: Fleming's left-hand rule.
**Explanation**: The thumb gives force/motion, first finger field, and second finger current.
**Tags**: Fleming-left-hand-rule, motor-effect

### Question Q-5.10-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: State three ways to increase the force on a current-carrying wire in a magnetic field.
**Answer**: Increase the current, use a stronger magnetic field, or increase the length of wire inside the field.
**Explanation**: These are the factors in F = BIL for a perpendicular wire.
**Tags**: motor-effect, force, factors

### Question Q-5.10-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A wire of length 0.20 m carries a current of 3.0 A at right angles to a magnetic field of flux density 0.50 T. Calculate the force on the wire.
**Answer**: F = BIL = 0.50 × 3.0 × 0.20 = 0.30 N.
**Explanation**: This formula applies when the wire is perpendicular to the magnetic field.
**Tags**: BIL, force, calculation

### Question Q-5.10-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why a current-carrying coil in a magnetic field turns.
**Answer**: The two sides of the coil carry current in opposite directions, so they experience forces in opposite directions. These forces form a turning effect on the coil.
**Explanation**: The pair of forces creates a moment.
**Tags**: dc-motor, coil, turning-effect

### Question Q-5.10-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the function of the split-ring commutator in a simple d.c. motor?
**Answer**: It reverses the current in the coil every half-turn so the forces keep the coil rotating in the same direction.
**Explanation**: Without the commutator, the coil would stop or reverse after half a turn.
**Tags**: dc-motor, split-ring-commutator

### Question Q-5.10-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Use Fleming's left-hand rule to explain what happens if the current in a motor coil is reversed.
**Answer**: Reversing the current reverses the force on each side of the coil, so the direction of rotation reverses.
**Explanation**: In Fleming's rule, changing the second finger direction changes the thumb direction.
**Tags**: Fleming-left-hand-rule, motor, current-reversal

### Question Q-5.10-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student says increasing the number of turns on the coil does not affect a d.c. motor because the current is unchanged. Explain why this is wrong.
**Answer**: More turns mean more lengths of wire experience force in the magnetic field, increasing the total turning effect on the coil, even if current in each turn is unchanged.
**Explanation**: Motor torque depends on current, field strength, coil area, and number of turns.
**Tags**: dc-motor, turns, torque, misconception

### Question Q-5.10-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the motor effect and electromagnetic induction.
**Answer**: In the motor effect, a current in a magnetic field produces a force/motion. In electromagnetic induction, motion or changing magnetic field produces an emf/current. They are related but reverse processes.
**Explanation**: One converts electrical energy to mechanical motion; the other converts mechanical/change energy to electrical energy.
**Tags**: motor-effect, induction, comparison
