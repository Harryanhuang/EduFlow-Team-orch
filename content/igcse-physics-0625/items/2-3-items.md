# Items: 2.3 — Work, energy, conservation

### Question Q-2.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A boy pushes a crate with a constant horizontal force of 30 N over a distance of 8 m. Calculate the work done by the boy.
**Answer**: 240 J. W = Fs = 30 × 8 = 240 J.
**Explanation**: Work is calculated using W = Fs, where F is the force in the direction of motion and s is the distance moved. A common error is dividing instead of multiplying (W = F/s), or giving the answer in newtons rather than joules.
**Tags**: work, force, distance, W=Fs, calculation

### Question Q-2.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Calculate the kinetic energy of a 1200 kg car travelling at 15 m/s.
**Answer**: 135 000 J (or 135 kJ). Ek = ½mv² = ½ × 1200 × 15² = 600 × 225 = 135 000 J.
**Explanation**: Kinetic energy uses the formula Ek = ½mv². A common error is forgetting the ½ factor, or forgetting to square the velocity (getting ½ × 1200 × 15 = 9000 J instead).
**Tags**: kinetic-energy, Ek=½mv², calculation

### Question Q-2.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A 3 kg ball is held at rest on top of a 12 m high building. It is released and falls freely. Calculate: (a) the gravitational potential energy of the ball at the top, (b) the kinetic energy of the ball just before it hits the ground. Assume no air resistance. (g = 10 N/kg)
**Answer**: (a) Ep = mgh = 3 × 10 × 12 = 360 J. (b) By conservation of energy, all Ep is converted to Ek, so Ek = 360 J.
**Explanation**: At the top, all energy is gravitational potential energy. As the ball falls, Ep is converted to Ek. With no air resistance, the total mechanical energy is conserved, so Ek at the bottom equals Ep at the top. A common error is thinking the ball "loses" energy during the fall, or trying to use Ek = ½mv² without first finding the speed.
**Tags**: potential-energy, energy-conservation, Ep-to-Ek, Ep=mgh, calculation

### Question Q-2.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe the main energy transfers that take place in each of the following situations: (a) A cyclist applies her brakes and slows down on a flat road. (b) A battery-powered torch is switched on in a dark room.
**Answer**: (a) Kinetic energy of the cyclist is transferred to thermal energy (due to friction between brake pads and wheel) and a small amount of sound energy. (b) Chemical energy stored in the battery is transferred to electrical energy, then to light energy and thermal energy in the bulb.
**Explanation**: Energy transfers must be described using correct energy types and the sequence in which they occur. A common error is saying energy is "used up" or "lost" — energy is always transferred, never destroyed. Another common error is failing to identify the initial energy store (e.g., not mentioning chemical energy for the battery).
**Tags**: energy-transfer, energy-types, thermal-energy, chemical-energy, description

### Question Q-2.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A Sankey diagram for an electric motor shows 200 J of electrical energy as the input. The diagram splits into two output arrows: one labelled "useful kinetic energy" with a width representing 140 J, and one labelled "wasted thermal energy". (a) How much energy is wasted as thermal energy? (b) State what the width of each arrow in a Sankey diagram represents. (c) Calculate the efficiency of the motor.
**Answer**: (a) Wasted energy = 200 − 140 = 60 J. (b) The width of each arrow represents the amount of energy flowing in that pathway. (c) Efficiency = useful output energy / total input energy × 100% = 140 / 200 × 100% = 70%.
**Explanation**: A Sankey diagram visually shows energy inputs and outputs, where arrow width is proportional to the quantity of energy. A common error is adding the outputs instead of subtracting from the input for part (a). For efficiency, a common error is dividing the wrong way round (input/useful instead of useful/input) or forgetting to multiply by 100 to give a percentage.
**Tags**: Sankey-diagram, efficiency, energy-transfer, wasted-energy, interpretation

### Question Q-2.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: In each situation below, state whether work is being done by the force mentioned and explain your answer. (a) A waiter holds a heavy tray stationary above his hand while standing still. Is work done by the waiter's upward supporting force? (b) A girl pushes a trolley along a horizontal corridor. Is work done by the girl's horizontal pushing force? (c) A crane lifts a steel beam vertically upwards. Is work done by the crane's upward cable tension?
**Answer**: (a) No work is done — although there is a force, the tray does not move, so the distance moved is zero and W = F × 0 = 0. (b) Yes, work is done — the horizontal pushing force and the horizontal displacement are in the same direction. (c) Yes, work is done — the upward tension force and the upward displacement are in the same direction.
**Explanation**: Work is done only when a force causes a displacement in the direction of the force (W = Fs). A common error is thinking that holding something heavy "must" involve work because it feels tiring — biologically the muscles expend energy, but in physics no work is done on the object if it does not move. Another error is confusing perpendicular force and displacement (e.g., a waiter carrying a tray horizontally does no work with the vertical supporting force).
**Tags**: work, force, displacement, work-is-done, conceptual, W=Fs

### Question Q-2.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A stone of mass 0.8 kg is dropped from rest from the top of a cliff that is 45 m high. Ignoring air resistance, calculate: (a) the gravitational potential energy of the stone at the top of the cliff, (b) the kinetic energy of the stone just before it hits the ground, (c) the speed of the stone just before it hits the ground, (d) the speed of the stone when it has fallen halfway (i.e. when it is 22.5 m above the ground). (g = 10 m/s²)
**Answer**: (a) Ep = mgh = 0.8 × 10 × 45 = 360 J. (b) By conservation of energy, all Ep converts to Ek, so Ek = 360 J. (c) Ek = ½mv², so 360 = ½ × 0.8 × v², 360 = 0.4v², v² = 900, v = 30 m/s. (d) At 22.5 m above ground, Ep remaining = 0.8 × 10 × 22.5 = 180 J. So Ek = 360 − 180 = 180 J. Then 180 = 0.4v², v² = 450, v = √450 ≈ 21.2 m/s.
**Explanation**: This is a multi-step conservation of energy problem. The key insight is that at any point during the fall, Ep + Ek = 360 J (the total mechanical energy is conserved). For part (d), a common error is assuming the speed is half of the final speed (15 m/s) — in fact, because Ek is proportional to v², the speed at half height is about 71% of the final speed, not 50%. Another common error is forgetting to subtract the remaining Ep from the total to find Ek at the halfway point.
**Tags**: energy-conservation, free-fall, speed-from-height, multi-step, Ep-to-Ek, calculation, challenge

### Question Q-2.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A crane lifts a load of mass 600 kg through a vertical height of 20 m in 30 seconds. The electrical power input to the crane's motor is 5000 W. Calculate: (a) the useful energy output (the gravitational potential energy gained by the load), (b) the total energy input to the motor during the 30 seconds, (c) the efficiency of the crane system, (d) the useful power output of the crane, (e) the amount of energy wasted and describe where it goes. (g = 10 N/kg)
**Answer**: (a) Useful output = Ep = mgh = 600 × 10 × 20 = 120 000 J. (b) Total input = Power × time = 5000 × 30 = 150 000 J. (c) Efficiency = useful output / total input × 100% = 120 000 / 150 000 × 100% = 80%. (d) Useful power output = 120 000 / 30 = 4000 W. (e) Wasted energy = 150 000 − 120 000 = 30 000 J. This is transferred to thermal energy (heating the motor, cables, and surroundings due to electrical resistance and friction in moving parts) and sound energy.
**Explanation**: This problem combines energy, power, and efficiency. For part (b), a common error is confusing power with energy — power must be multiplied by time to get energy. For part (c), efficiency must be calculated as useful/total (not total/useful). For part (d), a common error is using the input power rather than calculating the useful output power. Students sometimes think 80% efficiency means 80% of the power is wasted — in fact 80% is useful and 20% is wasted.
**Tags**: efficiency, power, energy, multi-step, crane, wasted-energy, calculation, challenge

### Question Q-2.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A hydroelectric power station allows water to fall from a reservoir 80 m above a turbine. Each second, 500 kg of water passes through the turbine. (a) Calculate the gravitational potential energy lost by the water each second. (b) The turbine converts 75% of this energy into kinetic energy of rotation. Calculate the useful kinetic energy produced per second. (c) The generator then converts 90% of the kinetic energy into electrical energy. Calculate the electrical power output of the station. (d) Calculate the overall efficiency of the power station (from gravitational potential energy to electrical energy). (e) Describe what happens to the energy that is not converted to electrical energy at each stage. (g = 10 N/kg)
**Answer**: (a) Ep = mgh = 500 × 10 × 80 = 400 000 J per second (i.e. 400 kW of input power). (b) Kinetic energy = 75% of 400 000 = 0.75 × 400 000 = 300 000 J per second. (c) Electrical energy = 90% of 300 000 = 0.90 × 300 000 = 270 000 J per second = 270 kW electrical power output. (d) Overall efficiency = useful output / total input × 100% = 270 000 / 400 000 × 100% = 67.5%. (Alternatively: 0.75 × 0.90 = 0.675 = 67.5%.) (e) In the turbine stage, 25% (100 000 J/s) is lost as thermal energy (friction in bearings, water turbulence, heating of turbine components) and sound energy. In the generator stage, 10% of 300 000 J = 30 000 J/s is lost as thermal energy (heating of generator coils due to electrical resistance) and sound energy. Total wasted = 100 000 + 30 000 = 130 000 J/s.
**Explanation**: This is a comprehensive multi-conversion problem with two stages of energy transfer. The overall efficiency is found by multiplying the individual stage efficiencies (0.75 × 0.90 = 0.675), not by adding them. A common error is adding the two percentages (75% + 90% = 165%) which is physically meaningless. Another error is applying both percentages to the original input (e.g., 75% + 90% of 400 000). The 90% applies to the kinetic energy output of the turbine, not to the original Ep. For part (e), students often write that wasted energy "disappears" — it is always transferred to other forms (mainly thermal energy and sound).
**Tags**: hydroelectric, energy-conservation, efficiency, multi-step, multi-conversion, power, comprehensive-analysis, challenge
