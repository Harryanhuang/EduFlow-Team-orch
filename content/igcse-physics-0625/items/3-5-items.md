# Items: 3.5 — Specific heat capacity

### Question Q-3.5-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Calculate the energy needed to raise the temperature of 2 kg of water from 20°C to 80°C. (c_water = 4200 J/(kg·°C))
**Answer**: 504 000 J
**Explanation**: Q = mcΔT = 2 × 4200 × (80 - 20) = 2 × 4200 × 60 = 504 000 J. A common error is using the final temperature (80) instead of the temperature change (60).
**Tags**: specific-heat-capacity, calculation, Q=mcΔT

### Question Q-3.5-02
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A 0.5 kg block of aluminium is heated with a 50 W heater for 5 minutes. The temperature rises by 18°C. Calculate the specific heat capacity of aluminium.
**Answer**: 1667 J/(kg·°C)
**Explanation**: Energy supplied = Pt = 50 × 300 = 15 000 J. c = Q/(mΔT) = 15 000/(0.5 × 18) = 15 000/9 = 1667 J/(kg·°C). A common error is using 5 minutes instead of 300 seconds.
**Tags**: specific-heat-capacity, electrical-heating, calculation

### Question Q-3.5-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Why is water used as a coolant in car engines? Refer to its specific heat capacity.
**Answer**: Water has a very high specific heat capacity (4200 J/(kg·°C)), meaning it can absorb a lot of thermal energy with only a small temperature rise, making it an effective coolant.
**Explanation**: High c = absorbs lots of heat without getting too hot. A common error is saying water is "cheap" without mentioning specific heat capacity.
**Tags**: specific-heat-capacity, coolant, application

### Question Q-3.5-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: In an experiment to measure the specific heat capacity of a metal, the measured value is higher than the accepted value. Suggest two possible reasons.
**Answer**: (1) Heat loss to the surroundings means more energy was needed than calculated, giving an apparently higher c. (2) The heater was not fully in contact with the block, so some energy was lost to the air.
**Explanation**: Systematic errors that cause overestimation. A common error is saying "reading error" without specifying the direction of the effect.
**Tags**: specific-heat-capacity, experiment, error-analysis

### Question Q-3.5-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Two blocks of equal mass, one of copper (c = 385 J/(kg·°C)) and one of aluminium (c = 900 J/(kg·°C)), are given the same amount of heat energy. Which block will have the greater temperature rise? Explain.
**Answer**: The copper block. Since Q = mcΔT and Q and m are the same, ΔT = Q/(mc). Copper has a lower c, so it has a greater ΔT.
**Explanation**: Lower c means easier to heat up. A common error is saying aluminium heats more because it "conducts better."
**Tags**: specific-heat-capacity, comparison, temperature-rise

### Question Q-3.5-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A 200 g sample of oil at 25°C is heated with 8400 J of energy. The specific heat capacity of oil is 2100 J/(kg·°C). Calculate the final temperature of the oil.
**Answer**: 45°C
**Explanation**: Q = mcΔT, so ΔT = Q/(mc) = 8400/(0.2 × 2100) = 20°C. Final temperature = 25 + 20 = 45°C. A common error is forgetting to convert grams to kilograms.
**Tags**: specific-heat-capacity, calculation, final-temperature

### Question Q-3.5-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Describe an experiment to determine the specific heat capacity of a metal block. Include the apparatus needed, measurements taken, and one precaution to improve accuracy.
**Answer**: Use a metal block, heater of known power, thermometer, stopwatch, insulation, power supply and balance. Measure the block's mass, record the initial temperature, heat for time t, then record the final temperature. Calculate energy using Q = Pt and then c = Pt/(mΔT). Insulate the block to reduce heat loss.
**Explanation**: The key measurements are mass, temperature change, power and time. A common error is not mentioning insulation, which causes a systematic error.
**Tags**: specific-heat-capacity, experiment, method, apparatus

### Question Q-3.5-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student claims that a substance with a higher specific heat capacity takes longer to heat up. Is this always true? Explain your answer, considering all relevant factors.
**Answer**: Not always. Heating time depends on specific heat capacity, mass and heater power. For the same mass and same heater, a higher specific heat capacity means a longer heating time. But different masses or powers can change the comparison.
**Explanation**: The relationship follows Q = mcΔT and P = Q/t. A common error is considering only c while ignoring mass and power.
**Tags**: specific-heat-capacity, heating-time, factors, critical-thinking

### Question Q-3.5-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: An electric kettle rated at 2400 W contains 1.5 kg of water at 20°C. Calculate the minimum time needed to bring the water to boiling point. State any assumption you make. (c_water = 4200 J/(kg·°C))
**Answer**: 210 s, or 3.5 min, assuming all electrical energy is transferred to the water.
**Explanation**: Q = mcΔT = 1.5 × 4200 × 80 = 504 000 J. Time = Q/P = 504 000/2400 = 210 s. In reality, heat loss means the actual time is longer.
**Tags**: specific-heat-capacity, calculation, time, efficiency-assumption
