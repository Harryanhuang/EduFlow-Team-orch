# Items: 3.1 — Kinetic particle model

### Question Q-3.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Describe the arrangement and motion of particles in a solid.
**Answer**: Particles are closely packed in a regular arrangement. They vibrate about fixed positions but do not move from place to place.
**Explanation**: Both arrangement and motion must be described. A common error is saying particles are "stationary" — they vibrate.
**Tags**: kinetic-model, solid, particle-arrangement

### Question Q-3.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Use the kinetic particle model to explain why a gas exerts pressure on the walls of its container.
**Answer**: Gas particles move rapidly and collide with the container walls. Each collision exerts a tiny force. The sum of all these collisions per unit area produces pressure.
**Explanation**: Pressure is caused by collisions, not by particles "pushing" statically. A common error is saying particles "press against" the walls.
**Tags**: kinetic-model, gas-pressure, collisions

### Question Q-3.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why does a liquid take the shape of its container but a solid does not?
**Answer**: In a liquid, particles can slide past each other (they are close but not in fixed positions), allowing the liquid to flow. In a solid, particles are in fixed positions and can only vibrate.
**Explanation**: The key difference is freedom of movement. A common error is saying liquid particles are "far apart" — they are close, like solids.
**Tags**: kinetic-model, liquid, solid, comparison

### Question Q-3.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A student observes smoke particles under a microscope and sees them moving randomly. Name this effect and explain what it proves.
**Answer**: Brownian motion. It proves that air (or gas) particles are in constant random motion, colliding with the visible smoke particles from different directions.
**Explanation**: The smoke particles are not moving themselves — they are being pushed by invisible gas molecules. A common error is saying the smoke particles themselves prove particle motion directly.
**Tags**: Brownian-motion, evidence, kinetic-model

### Question Q-3.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why a balloon expands when placed in warm water.
**Answer**: The air particles inside the balloon gain kinetic energy and move faster. They collide with the balloon walls more frequently and with greater force, causing the balloon to expand.
**Explanation**: Temperature increase → faster particles → more forceful collisions. A common error is saying "air expands" without particle-level explanation.
**Tags**: kinetic-model, temperature, expansion

### Question Q-3.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Compare the density of a substance in its solid, liquid, and gaseous states. Explain your answer using the particle model.
**Answer**: Solid has the highest density (particles closest together), liquid has slightly lower density (particles slightly further apart), gas has the lowest density (particles very far apart). Density = mass/volume, and for the same mass, the gas occupies much more volume.
**Explanation**: The spacing between particles determines density. A common error is saying liquid is always less dense than solid (water is an exception at 0-4°C).
**Tags**: density, kinetic-model, states-of-matter, comparison

### Question Q-3.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Use the kinetic particle model to describe what happens to the particles in ice as it melts into water.
**Answer**: As ice is heated, the particles gain kinetic energy and vibrate more vigorously. At the melting point, the vibrations become strong enough to overcome the forces holding particles in fixed positions. The particles break free from their regular arrangement and can now slide past each other, forming a liquid. The temperature remains constant during melting because the energy is used to break bonds, not increase kinetic energy.
**Explanation**: Melting involves breaking intermolecular forces, not just increasing vibration. A common error is saying particles "speed up" during the actual melting process — at the phase change, energy goes into bond-breaking, not temperature rise.
**Tags**: kinetic-model, melting, state-change, bonds

### Question Q-3.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A drop of perfume is placed in one corner of a room. After several minutes, the scent can be detected throughout the room. (a) Name this process. (b) Explain why it happens more quickly on a warm day than on a cold day.
**Answer**: (a) Diffusion. (b) On a warm day, the air particles have higher kinetic energy and move faster. This means the perfume particles are carried and spread more rapidly through the air. Higher temperature increases the rate of diffusion because particles have greater average speed.
**Explanation**: Diffusion rate depends on temperature because particle speed is temperature-dependent. A common error is saying "heat makes particles bigger" — particle size doesn't change, only their speed and spacing.
**Tags**: diffusion, temperature, kinetic-model, rate-factors

### Question Q-3.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Use the kinetic particle model to explain why evaporation causes cooling. Refer to the energies of the particles involved.
**Answer**: In a liquid, particles have a range of kinetic energies. The most energetic particles near the surface can escape into the air, becoming vapour. When these high-energy particles leave, the average kinetic energy of the remaining particles decreases. Since temperature is proportional to average kinetic energy, the temperature of the liquid falls. This is why evaporation causes cooling.
**Explanation**: Only the fastest particles escape, lowering the average KE of those left behind. A common error is saying "all particles leave" or that evaporation happens at a specific temperature (it occurs at any temperature, unlike boiling).
**Tags**: evaporation, cooling, kinetic-model, energy-distribution
