# Items: 5.1 — Magnetic phenomena, magnetic fields

### Question Q-5.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: State the law of magnetic poles.
**Answer**: Like poles repel and unlike poles attract.
**Explanation**: North-North and South-South repel; North-South attract. A common error is getting attraction and repulsion backwards.
**Tags**: magnetic-poles, law, definition

### Question Q-5.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Name three magnetic materials.
**Answer**: Iron, steel, nickel, and cobalt are magnetic materials. Any three are acceptable.
**Explanation**: Magnetic materials can be attracted by a magnet and can usually be magnetised. A common error is listing copper or aluminium.
**Tags**: magnetic-materials, examples

### Question Q-5.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Draw the magnetic field pattern around a bar magnet, showing the direction of the field lines.
**Answer**: Field lines leave the N pole, curve around, and enter the S pole outside the magnet. They are closest together near the poles and never cross. Inside the magnet, field lines go from S to N.
**Explanation**: The direction outside the magnet is N to S. A common error is drawing field lines from S to N outside the magnet.
**Tags**: magnetic-field, field-lines, drawing

### Question Q-5.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A steel needle is stroked from end to end with the N pole of a bar magnet, always moving in the same direction. What pole is created at the end where the magnet finishes?
**Answer**: A south pole. The end where the stroking magnet finishes becomes the opposite pole to the stroking pole.
**Explanation**: This is the single-stroke method of magnetisation. A common error is thinking the finishing end becomes the same pole.
**Tags**: magnetisation, stroking-method, poles

### Question Q-5.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does a plotting compass help map a magnetic field?
**Answer**: The compass needle aligns with the magnetic field at each point. By marking the direction of the needle at several points and joining the marks, field lines can be drawn.
**Explanation**: The north-seeking pole of the compass points along the magnetic field direction. A common error is not showing direction arrows.
**Tags**: plotting-compass, field-mapping, experiment

### Question Q-5.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe how you could demagnetise a bar magnet.
**Answer**: Heat it strongly, hammer it repeatedly, or place it inside a solenoid connected to an a.c. supply and slowly withdraw it.
**Explanation**: These methods disrupt the aligned magnetic domains. A common error is saying a magnet can be demagnetised simply by putting it near another magnet.
**Tags**: demagnetisation, methods

### Question Q-5.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student says that if a bar magnet is cut in half, one half will be a north pole only and the other half will be a south pole only. Explain why this is wrong.
**Answer**: Cutting a bar magnet produces two smaller magnets. Each piece has both a north pole and a south pole. Magnetic poles always occur in pairs in ordinary magnets; isolated north or south poles are not produced by cutting.
**Explanation**: The magnetic domains remain arranged so each piece has two poles. A common error is treating poles like separable electric charges.
**Tags**: magnetic-poles, misconception, explanation

### Question Q-5.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare soft iron and steel for making (a) a temporary electromagnet core, (b) a permanent magnet. Explain your choices.
**Answer**: Soft iron is best for an electromagnet core because it is easily magnetised and demagnetised. Steel is best for a permanent magnet because it is harder to magnetise but retains its magnetism well.
**Explanation**: The key distinction is magnetic softness versus retention. A common error is choosing steel for an electromagnet because it sounds stronger.
**Tags**: soft-iron, steel, permanent-magnet, electromagnet-core

### Question Q-5.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain how the Earth's magnetic field allows a compass to show direction. Include why the north-seeking end of a compass points roughly towards geographic north.
**Answer**: A compass needle is a small magnet that aligns with Earth's magnetic field. The north-seeking end points along the field direction towards geographic north. This happens because the region near geographic north acts like a magnetic south pole, attracting the north-seeking end of the compass.
**Explanation**: Geographic north and magnetic pole naming can be confusing. A common error is saying Earth's magnetic north pole attracts the compass north pole.
**Tags**: compass, earth-magnetic-field, direction, explanation
