# Items: 4.8 — Sound

### Question Q-4.8-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A person shouts towards a cliff and hears the echo 3 seconds later. Calculate the distance to the cliff. (Speed of sound = 340 m/s)
**Answer**: 510 m
**Explanation**: Total distance = 340 × 3 = 1020 m. Distance to cliff = 1020 / 2 = 510 m because the sound travels there and back.
**Tags**: echo, sound-speed, distance, calculation

### Question Q-4.8-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What property of a sound wave determines (a) pitch, (b) loudness?
**Answer**: (a) Pitch is determined by frequency: higher frequency means higher pitch. (b) Loudness is determined by amplitude: larger amplitude means louder sound.
**Explanation**: These are independent properties. A common error is confusing pitch with loudness.
**Tags**: sound, pitch, loudness, frequency, amplitude

### Question Q-4.8-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe an experiment to measure the speed of sound in air.
**Answer**: Two people stand a known distance apart, for example 500 m. One person makes a loud sound and a visible signal at the same time, such as a starting pistol. The other starts timing when they see the flash and stops when they hear the sound. Speed = distance / time.
**Explanation**: Light arrives almost instantly compared with sound. A common error is starting the timer when the sound is heard.
**Tags**: sound-speed, experiment, measurement

### Question Q-4.8-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Sound cannot travel through a vacuum. Describe an experiment to demonstrate this.
**Answer**: Place a ringing electric bell inside a bell jar connected to a vacuum pump. As air is removed, the sound becomes fainter until it cannot be heard, even though the bell can still be seen ringing. This shows sound needs a medium.
**Explanation**: The visible ringing confirms the source still works. A common error is not explaining how the experiment proves the need for a medium.
**Tags**: sound, vacuum, experiment

### Question Q-4.8-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: On an oscilloscope display, how would the trace change if (a) the sound is louder, (b) the pitch is higher?
**Answer**: (a) Louder sound: the amplitude of the trace increases. (b) Higher pitch: more waves fit in the same horizontal space because the frequency increases.
**Explanation**: Amplitude links to loudness; frequency links to pitch. A common error is saying louder sound makes more waves.
**Tags**: oscilloscope, sound, trace, amplitude, frequency

### Question Q-4.8-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A drum is struck harder. Describe the changes to the sound produced in terms of amplitude and frequency.
**Answer**: Striking the drum harder increases the amplitude of vibration, making the sound louder. The frequency remains approximately the same because the pitch is mainly set by the drum's size and tension.
**Explanation**: Force affects amplitude much more than frequency. A common error is thinking hitting harder also increases pitch.
**Tags**: sound, amplitude, force, drum

### Question Q-4.8-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain why astronauts on the Moon cannot communicate by speaking directly to each other, even though they can see each other clearly.
**Answer**: The Moon has no atmosphere, so there is no material medium for sound waves to travel through. Light can travel through vacuum, so the astronauts can see each other. They must use radio communication, which uses electromagnetic waves that can travel through vacuum.
**Explanation**: The key distinction is mechanical wave versus electromagnetic wave. A common error is saying only "there is no air" without explaining the wave requirement.
**Tags**: sound, vacuum, moon, astronaut, explanation

### Question Q-4.8-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student stands 165 m from a large wall and claps her hands. She hears the echo 1 second later. Calculate the speed of sound in air from this data.
**Answer**: 330 m/s
**Explanation**: The sound travels to the wall and back, so total distance = 2 × 165 = 330 m. Speed = distance / time = 330 / 1 = 330 m/s. A common error is using 165 m instead of the round-trip distance.
**Tags**: echo, speed-calculation, practical, measurement

### Question Q-4.8-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare how sound travels through solids, liquids, and gases. Explain why the speed differs in each medium.
**Answer**: Sound usually travels fastest in solids, slower in liquids, and slowest in gases. In solids, particles are close together and strongly bonded, so vibrations transfer quickly. In liquids particles are less strongly held, and in gases particles are far apart, so energy transfer is slower.
**Explanation**: Particle spacing and bonding determine the speed. A common error is thinking sound travels fastest in gases because they are less dense.
**Tags**: sound, medium, particle-model, comparison, extended-response
