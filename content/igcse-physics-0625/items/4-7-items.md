# Items: 4.7 — Electromagnetic spectrum

### Question Q-4.7-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: List the regions of the electromagnetic spectrum in order of increasing frequency.
**Answer**: Radio waves, microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X-rays, gamma rays.
**Explanation**: Remember "R-M-I-V-U-X-G." A common error is swapping infrared and microwaves.
**Tags**: EM-spectrum, ordering, frequency

### Question Q-4.7-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: State two properties that all electromagnetic waves have in common.
**Answer**: (1) All travel at the speed of light (3 × 10^8 m/s) in a vacuum. (2) All are transverse waves. Also: all can travel through a vacuum, all transfer energy.
**Explanation**: Speed in vacuum and transverse nature are key. A common error is saying they all need a medium.
**Tags**: EM-waves, common-properties

### Question Q-4.7-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Give one use each for: (a) infrared, (b) ultraviolet, (c) X-rays.
**Answer**: (a) Infrared: remote controls, thermal imaging, heaters. (b) Ultraviolet: sterilisation, fluorescent lamps, security marking. (c) X-rays: medical imaging, security scanning.
**Explanation**: Each use must match the correct region. A common error is swapping infrared and ultraviolet uses.
**Tags**: EM-spectrum, applications, matching

### Question Q-4.7-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Calculate the wavelength of a radio wave with a frequency of 100 MHz. (c = 3 × 10^8 m/s)
**Answer**: 3 m
**Explanation**: λ = c/f = 3 × 10^8 / (100 × 10^6) = 3 × 10^8 / 10^8 = 3 m. A common error is not converting MHz to Hz.
**Tags**: wavelength, EM-waves, v=fλ, calculation

### Question Q-4.7-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Which region of the electromagnetic spectrum has the shortest wavelength and the highest energy?
**Answer**: Gamma rays. They have the shortest wavelength, highest frequency, and therefore highest energy (E ∝ f).
**Explanation**: Short wavelength means high frequency and high photon energy. A common error is saying X-rays.
**Tags**: gamma-rays, wavelength, energy

### Question Q-4.7-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Microwaves have a wavelength of about 3 cm. Calculate their frequency. (c = 3 × 10^8 m/s)
**Answer**: 10 GHz (or 10 × 10^9 Hz)
**Explanation**: f = c/λ = 3 × 10^8 / 0.03 = 10^10 Hz = 10 GHz. A common error is not converting cm to metres.
**Tags**: microwave, frequency, calculation, unit-conversion

### Question Q-4.7-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain why UV radiation is more dangerous to living cells than visible light, even though both are electromagnetic waves travelling at the same speed.
**Answer**: UV radiation has a higher frequency than visible light, so each photon carries more energy (E ∝ f). This higher energy can damage or destroy cells, causing sunburn or skin cancer. Visible light photons have insufficient energy to cause this damage. Both travel at c in vacuum, but the energy per photon differs.
**Explanation**: The danger comes from photon energy, not speed. A common error is saying UV travels faster.
**Tags**: UV-radiation, photon-energy, safety, explanation

### Question Q-4.7-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: An FM radio station broadcasts at 96 MHz and an AM station at 1000 kHz. Calculate the wavelengths of both and explain which would diffract more around hills. (c = 3 × 10^8 m/s)
**Answer**: FM: λ = c/f = 3 × 10^8 / (96 × 10^6) = 3.125 m. AM: λ = 3 × 10^8 / (1000 × 10^3) = 300 m. The AM wave has a much longer wavelength, so it diffracts more around obstacles like hills.
**Explanation**: Diffraction increases with wavelength. A common error is thinking higher frequency means more diffraction.
**Tags**: radio-waves, diffraction, wavelength-comparison, calculation

### Question Q-4.7-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Describe how you could demonstrate that infrared radiation can travel through a vacuum. Include a labelled diagram and explain what observations would confirm this.
**Answer**: Place an electric heater or infrared source inside a bell jar connected to a vacuum pump. Put an infrared detector on the other side of the jar and connect it to a voltmeter. First observe a detector reading with air present. Then pump out the air. If the detector still gives a reading when the air is removed, infrared radiation has travelled through the vacuum.
**Explanation**: Electromagnetic waves do not need a material medium. A common error is not including an observation before and after the air is removed.
**Tags**: infrared, vacuum, experiment, extended-response, practical
