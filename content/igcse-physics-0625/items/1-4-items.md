# Items: 1.4 — Mass, weight, centre of gravity

### Question Q-1.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: State the difference between mass and weight. Include the units for each quantity in your answer.
**Answer**: Mass is the amount of matter in an object and is measured in kilograms (kg). It does not change with location. Weight is the gravitational force acting on an object and is measured in newtons (N). It changes depending on the gravitational field strength at the object's location.
**Explanation**: Mass is a scalar property of the object itself, while weight is a force (W = mg) that depends on the local value of g. A common error is using "mass" and "weight" interchangeably or stating that mass is measured in newtons.
**Tags**: mass, weight, definition, units, conceptual

### Question Q-1.4-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A bag of shopping has a mass of 6 kg. Calculate its weight on Earth. (g = 9.8 N/kg)
**Answer**: W = mg = 6 × 9.8 = 58.8 N
**Explanation**: Weight is calculated using W = mg, where m is in kg and g is in N/kg. A common error is stating the weight is "6 kg" — kilograms measure mass, not weight. Another common error is using g = 10 N/kg when the question specifies 9.8 N/kg.
**Tags**: weight, mass, W=mg, calculation, Earth

### Question Q-1.4-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A rover has a mass of 900 kg. Calculate its weight on Earth (g = 9.8 N/kg) and on Mars (g = 3.7 N/kg). Explain why the rover's mass stays the same but its weight changes.
**Answer**: Weight on Earth = 900 × 9.8 = 8820 N. Weight on Mars = 900 × 3.7 = 3330 N. Mass is a measure of the amount of matter in an object and does not depend on gravity, so it remains 900 kg everywhere. Weight is the gravitational force on the object (W = mg), so it changes when g changes.
**Explanation**: The key distinction is that mass is an intrinsic property of the object while weight depends on the local gravitational field strength. A common error is stating that mass changes on different planets, or failing to explain why weight changes even though mass does not.
**Tags**: mass, weight, different-gravity, Mars, W=mg, conceptual

### Question Q-1.4-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe how to find the centre of gravity of an irregularly shaped piece of thin cardboard using a plumb line. Explain why the method works.
**Answer**: (1) Make a small hole near one edge of the card and suspend it from a pin so it can swing freely. (2) Hang a plumb line from the same pin. (3) When the card is stationary, draw a line on the card along the plumb line. (4) Repeat steps 1–3 from a different suspension point. (5) The centre of gravity is where the two drawn lines intersect. The method works because when the card hangs freely, its centre of gravity must lie vertically below the point of suspension — the plumb line marks this vertical line. Using two suspension points gives two lines whose intersection locates the centre of gravity.
**Explanation**: The plumb line method relies on the principle that a freely suspended object will come to rest with its centre of gravity directly below the pivot. A common error is describing only one suspension point — at least two are needed to find the intersection. Another error is failing to explain why the plumb line passes through the centre of gravity.
**Tags**: centre-of-gravity, plumb-line, experiment, method

### Question Q-1.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why a double-decker bus is less stable than a single-decker bus, and state two design features that could improve the stability of a double-decker bus.
**Answer**: A double-decker bus has a higher centre of gravity than a single-decker bus, so it tilts more easily before the line of action of its weight passes outside the base, making it less stable. Two design features to improve stability: (1) widen the wheelbase (track width) so the bus can tilt further before toppling, and (2) place heavy components such as the engine and fuel tank as low as possible to lower the centre of gravity.
**Explanation**: Stability depends on two factors: the height of the centre of gravity and the width of the base. An object topples when the vertical line through the centre of gravity falls outside the base area. A common error is mentioning only one factor (e.g., only the height) rather than both. Another error is suggesting adding weight to the top to "balance it."
**Tags**: stability, centre-of-gravity, design, application

### Question Q-1.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: An astronaut has a mass of 75 kg. She stands on the surface of Jupiter's moon Europa, where g = 1.3 N/kg. (a) Calculate her weight on Europa. (b) Calculate her weight on Earth (g = 9.8 N/kg). (c) A balance scale (two-pan balance) is used to measure her mass on Europa. What reading does it give? Explain why.
**Answer**: (a) W = 75 × 1.3 = 97.5 N. (b) W = 75 × 9.8 = 735 N. (c) The balance scale reads 75 kg. A two-pan balance compares masses by balancing moments on each side — since both the astronaut's mass and the standard masses are affected equally by the local value of g, the scale gives the correct mass regardless of location.
**Explanation**: A balance scale measures mass (not weight) because it compares gravitational forces on both sides, and g cancels out. This contrasts with a spring balance (Newton meter), which measures force and would give a different reading. A common error is thinking a balance scale would give a different reading on Europa, or confusing a balance scale with a spring balance.
**Tags**: mass, weight, measurement, balance-scale, Europa, W=mg

### Question Q-1.4-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A probe of mass 50 kg is sent on a mission. Calculate its weight in each of the following locations: (a) on Earth (g = 9.8 N/kg), (b) on the Moon (g = 1.6 N/kg), (c) on Mars (g = 3.7 N/kg). The probe is designed with its centre of gravity 0.4 m above the ground and has a base width of 1.2 m. On Mars, a sandstorm exerts a horizontal force of 80 N at the top of the probe, which is 1.5 m above the ground. Determine whether the probe will topple. Show all working.
**Answer**: (a) W = 50 × 9.8 = 490 N. (b) W = 50 × 1.6 = 80 N. (c) W = 50 × 3.7 = 185 N. Topple check on Mars: The probe topples when the overturning moment exceeds the restoring moment about the edge of the base. Taking moments about the edge of the base (pivot point at 0.6 m from centre): Overturning moment = 80 × 1.5 = 120 N·m. Restoring moment = weight × half base width = 185 × 0.6 = 111 N·m. Since 120 > 111, the overturning moment is greater and the probe will topple.
**Explanation**: This question combines W = mg across multiple locations with a stability analysis using moments. The critical insight is identifying the correct pivot point (edge of the base) and using the perpendicular distance from the centre of gravity to this edge. A common error is using the full base width instead of half, or comparing forces instead of moments. Another error is using the Earth weight for the restoring moment calculation instead of the Martian weight.
**Tags**: weight, mass, W=mg, stability, moments, topple, multi-step, Mars, Moon

### Question Q-1.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A tall cylindrical container has a base diameter of 20 cm and a height of 80 cm. When empty, its mass is 2 kg and its centre of gravity is at its geometric centre (40 cm from the base). Water (density 1000 kg/m³) is poured into the container to a depth of h cm. (a) Derive an expression for the total mass of the container plus water in terms of h. (b) Given that the centre of gravity of the system is at height y = (80 + 0.5h²) / (40 + πh) cm from the base (where h is in cm and using the base area = π × 10² cm²), find the height of the centre of gravity when h = 20 cm. (c) Explain qualitatively what happens to the centre of gravity as the container is filled from empty to full, and why the container becomes harder to topple as water is first added. (Use g = 9.8 N/kg where needed.)
**Answer**: (a) Volume of water = π × 10² × h = 100πh cm³ = 100πh × 10⁻⁶ m³ = πh × 10⁻⁴ m³. Mass of water = 1000 × πh × 10⁻⁴ = 0.1πh kg ≈ 0.314h kg. Total mass = 2 + 0.1πh kg. (b) When h = 20 cm: y = (80 + 0.5 × 20²) / (40 + π × 20) = (80 + 200) / (40 + 62.83) = 280 / 102.83 ≈ 27.2 cm from the base. (c) As water is first added, the centre of gravity moves downward from 40 cm because the water's mass is concentrated below the original centre of gravity. The total weight increases while the centre of gravity drops, both of which increase stability (greater restoring moment and the line of weight stays within the base for a larger tilt angle). Eventually, as more water is added, the centre of gravity begins to rise again because the water level itself rises.
**Explanation**: This question integrates mass, weight, centre of gravity, and stability in a single system. Part (a) requires unit conversion from cm³ to m³. Part (b) is substitution. Part (c) requires understanding that stability increases when the centre of gravity lowers and weight increases. A common error in (c) is saying the centre of gravity always rises as water is added — it first falls, reaches a minimum, then rises. Another error is failing to link the lowered centre of gravity to increased stability.
**Tags**: centre-of-gravity, stability, mass, weight, derivation, multi-step, challenge

### Question Q-1.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A uniform wooden plank of mass 12 kg and length 3 m rests horizontally on two supports, A and B, which are 0.5 m and 2.5 m from the left end respectively. (a) Calculate the reaction force at each support. (b) A person of mass 50 kg stands at the left end of the plank. Calculate the new reaction forces at A and B. (c) The person now walks along the plank. How far from the left end can the person walk before the plank begins to tip? (Use g = 9.8 N/kg.)
**Answer**: (a) The plank is uniform so its weight (12 × 9.8 = 117.6 N) acts at the centre (1.5 m from the left end). Taking moments about B (at 2.5 m from left end): Clockwise moment of weight = 117.6 × (2.5 − 1.5) = 117.6 × 1.0 = 117.6 N·m. Anticlockwise moment of R_A = R_A × (2.5 − 0.5) = R_A × 2.0. So R_A = 117.6 / 2.0 = 58.8 N. By vertical equilibrium: R_A + R_B = 117.6 N, so R_B = 117.6 − 58.8 = 58.8 N. (b) Person's weight = 50 × 9.8 = 490 N at the left end (x = 0). Taking moments about B: Clockwise moments = 117.6 × 1.0 (plank) + 490 × 2.5 (person) = 117.6 + 1225 = 1342.6 N·m. Anticlockwise moment = R_A × 2.0. So R_A = 1342.6 / 2.0 = 671.3 N. R_B = 117.6 + 490 − 671.3 = −63.7 N. Since R_B is negative, support B would need to pull down — the plank lifts off support B when the person stands at the extreme left end. (c) As the person walks rightward from A towards B and beyond, the plank tips about B when R_A falls to zero. Taking moments about B with the person at distance x from the left end (x > 2.5): Plank weight creates anticlockwise moment = 117.6 × 1.0 = 117.6 N·m. Person's weight creates clockwise moment = 490 × (x − 2.5) N·m. At tipping (R_A = 0): 490(x − 2.5) = 117.6, so x − 2.5 = 0.24 m, giving x = 2.74 m from the left end. The person can walk up to 2.74 m from the left end before the plank tips.
**Explanation**: This problem requires careful application of equilibrium conditions (sum of forces = 0, sum of moments = 0) and identifying the correct tipping condition. In part (b), the person at the extreme left end causes the plank to lift off support B (R_B < 0). In part (c), as the person walks to the right past support B, the plank tips about B when R_A drops to zero. The key is choosing B as the pivot and equating the clockwise moment of the person to the anticlockwise restoring moment of the plank's weight. A common error is using the wrong pivot point, measuring distances from the wrong reference, or forgetting that the plank's own weight contributes to the restoring moment. Another error is not checking whether the plank might also tip to the left when the person stands near the left end.
**Tags**: equilibrium, moments, reaction-forces, stability, multi-step, centre-of-gravity, challenge
