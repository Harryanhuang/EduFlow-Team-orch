# Items: 5.3 — Electrical circuits: series and parallel

### Question Q-5.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Two resistors of 4 ohm and 6 ohm are connected in series. Calculate the total resistance.
**Answer**: 10 ohm
**Explanation**: In series, R_total = R1 + R2 = 4 + 6 = 10 ohm. A common error is using the parallel formula.
**Tags**: series, resistance, calculation

### Question Q-5.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: In a series circuit with two lamps, one lamp blows. What happens to the other lamp? Explain.
**Answer**: The other lamp also goes out because a series circuit has only one path. If one lamp breaks, the circuit is open and no current flows anywhere.
**Explanation**: Series circuits have only one current path. A common error is saying the other lamp stays on.
**Tags**: series-circuit, open-circuit, conceptual

### Question Q-5.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Two resistors of 4 ohm and 6 ohm are connected in parallel. Calculate the total resistance.
**Answer**: 2.4 ohm
**Explanation**: 1/R = 1/4 + 1/6 = 3/12 + 2/12 = 5/12, so R = 12/5 = 2.4 ohm. Parallel resistance is less than either branch resistance. A common error is adding the resistors.
**Tags**: parallel, resistance, calculation

### Question Q-5.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: In a parallel circuit with two lamps, one lamp blows. What happens to the other lamp? Explain.
**Answer**: The other lamp stays on because each parallel branch has its own path for current.
**Explanation**: Parallel branches can work independently. A common error is treating the circuit as one series path.
**Tags**: parallel-circuit, independence, conceptual

### Question Q-5.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A 12 V battery is connected to two resistors in series: 3 ohm and 9 ohm. Calculate the current and the p.d. across each resistor.
**Answer**: Total R = 12 ohm. I = V/R = 12/12 = 1 A. p.d. across 3 ohm = 3 V; p.d. across 9 ohm = 9 V.
**Explanation**: In series, current is the same and voltage divides in proportion to resistance.
**Tags**: series, Ohm-law, voltage-division

### Question Q-5.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Three identical 6 ohm resistors are connected in parallel to a 12 V battery. Calculate the total current from the battery.
**Answer**: 6 A
**Explanation**: Each branch current is I = 12/6 = 2 A. Total current = 2 + 2 + 2 = 6 A. Each parallel branch has the full supply voltage. A common error is calculating only one branch.
**Tags**: parallel, total-current, Ohm-law

### Question Q-5.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A 12 V battery is connected to a 4 ohm resistor in series with a parallel pair of 6 ohm and 3 ohm resistors. Calculate the total resistance and total current.
**Answer**: The parallel pair has 1/R = 1/6 + 1/3 = 3/6, so R = 2 ohm. Total resistance = 4 + 2 = 6 ohm. Total current = 12/6 = 2 A.
**Explanation**: Reduce the parallel section first, then add the series resistor.
**Tags**: series-parallel, equivalent-resistance, current

### Question Q-5.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student says, "In a parallel circuit the current is the same in every branch." Explain why this is not always true.
**Answer**: The p.d. is the same across each parallel branch, but branch current depends on resistance. Lower resistance branches take larger currents. Total current is the sum of branch currents.
**Explanation**: Parallel circuits share voltage, not necessarily current.
**Tags**: parallel-circuit, current, misconception

### Question Q-5.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Design a circuit with two lamps that can be switched on and off independently from the same battery. Explain where the switches should be placed.
**Answer**: Put the lamps in parallel, with one switch in series with each lamp branch. Each switch controls only its own branch.
**Explanation**: Independent control requires separate branches. A common error is placing one switch before the junction.
**Tags**: parallel, switches, circuit-design
