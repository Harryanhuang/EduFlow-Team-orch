# Items: 5.8 — Household wiring and electrical safety

### Question Q-5.8-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: State the colour and function of the live wire in a mains plug.
**Answer**: The live wire is brown. It carries the alternating potential difference from the supply to the appliance.
**Explanation**: The live wire can be dangerous even when an appliance is switched off if the switch is wired incorrectly.
**Tags**: household-wiring, live-wire, safety

### Question Q-5.8-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: State the colour and function of the earth wire in a mains plug.
**Answer**: The earth wire is green-and-yellow. It provides a low-resistance path to ground if the metal case becomes live.
**Explanation**: The earth wire is a safety wire and normally carries no current.
**Tags**: household-wiring, earth-wire, safety

### Question Q-5.8-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how a fuse protects an appliance and user.
**Answer**: A fuse contains a thin wire that melts if the current exceeds its rated value. This breaks the circuit and stops current flowing before wires overheat or the appliance case remains dangerous.
**Explanation**: The fuse is connected in the live wire so it disconnects the appliance from the dangerous supply.
**Tags**: fuse, safety, overcurrent

### Question Q-5.8-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A 720 W appliance is connected to a 240 V supply. Calculate the normal current and choose a suitable fuse rating from 1 A, 3 A, 5 A, and 13 A.
**Answer**: I = P/V = 720/240 = 3 A. Choose a 5 A fuse because it is just above the normal current.
**Explanation**: A fuse rating should be above normal operating current but low enough to protect the appliance.
**Tags**: fuse-rating, power-current, mains

### Question Q-5.8-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why switches and fuses should be connected in the live wire rather than the neutral wire.
**Answer**: Disconnecting the live wire removes the dangerous potential from the appliance. If only the neutral wire is disconnected, internal parts may still be live and could give an electric shock.
**Explanation**: The safety aim is to isolate the appliance from live voltage.
**Tags**: live-wire, switch, fuse, safety

### Question Q-5.8-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe how double insulation protects a user.
**Answer**: A double-insulated appliance has two layers of insulating material between live parts and the user, so the external case cannot become live. It does not need an earth wire.
**Explanation**: Double insulation is used especially for appliances with plastic cases.
**Tags**: double-insulation, safety, earth-wire

### Question Q-5.8-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: An appliance with a metal case develops a fault where the live wire touches the case. Explain how the earth wire and fuse protect the user.
**Answer**: The metal case becomes connected to earth through the earth wire, giving a low-resistance path for a large fault current. This large current melts the fuse and disconnects the live supply, so the case does not remain live.
**Explanation**: The protection depends on both the earth wire and fuse working together.
**Tags**: earth-wire, fuse, fault-current, metal-case

### Question Q-5.8-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student replaces a 3 A fuse with a 13 A fuse because the appliance keeps cutting out. Explain why this is dangerous.
**Answer**: The 13 A fuse may not melt when an unsafe current flows. The cable or appliance could overheat, causing fire or electric shock risk. The fault should be repaired instead of using a larger fuse.
**Explanation**: Oversized fuses defeat the protection.
**Tags**: fuse-rating, safety, overheating

### Question Q-5.8-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare a fuse and a circuit breaker as safety devices in household circuits.
**Answer**: Both disconnect a circuit when current is too large. A fuse melts and must be replaced; a circuit breaker trips electromagnetically or thermally and can be reset. Circuit breakers can act faster and are easier to restore.
**Explanation**: Both protect against overcurrent, but their reset method differs.
**Tags**: fuse, circuit-breaker, comparison
