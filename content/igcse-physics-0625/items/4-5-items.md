# Items: 4.5 — Refraction, refractive index, critical angle, TIR

### Question Q-4.5-01
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Light enters glass (n = 1.5) from air at an angle of incidence of 45°. Calculate the angle of refraction.
**Answer**: 28.1°
**Explanation**: n = sin i / sin r. sin r = sin 45° / 1.5 = 0.7071 / 1.5 = 0.4714. r = sin⁻¹(0.4714) = 28.1°. A common error is finding sin r = 0.4714 and stopping without using sin⁻¹.
**Tags**: refraction, refractive-index, calculation

### Question Q-4.5-02
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Calculate the critical angle for glass with refractive index 1.5.
**Answer**: 41.8°
**Explanation**: sin c = 1/n = 1/1.5 = 0.6667. c = sin⁻¹(0.6667) = 41.8°. A common error is using n = sin c instead of sin c = 1/n.
**Tags**: critical-angle, refractive-index, calculation

### Question Q-4.5-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: State the two conditions for total internal reflection to occur.
**Answer**: (1) Light must travel from a more dense (optically denser) medium to a less dense medium. (2) The angle of incidence must be greater than the critical angle.
**Explanation**: Both conditions are essential. A common error is stating only one condition or getting the density direction wrong.
**Tags**: TIR, conditions, definition

### Question Q-4.5-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Light travels from water (n = 1.33) into air. The angle of incidence is 50°. Will total internal reflection occur? (Critical angle for water ≈ 48.8°)
**Answer**: Yes. The light travels from a denser to a less dense medium and the angle of incidence (50°) is greater than the critical angle (48.8°), so total internal reflection occurs.
**Explanation**: Both conditions are met. A common error is comparing 50° to 90° instead of the critical angle.
**Tags**: TIR, calculation, condition-check

### Question Q-4.5-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how optical fibres use total internal reflection to transmit light.
**Answer**: Light enters the fibre at an angle greater than the critical angle. It undergoes total internal reflection at the boundary between the core and cladding, bouncing along the fibre without escaping.
**Explanation**: The cladding has a lower refractive index than the core. A common error is saying light "travels straight through" the fibre.
**Tags**: optical-fibre, TIR, application

### Question Q-4.5-06
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A ray of light passes from air into water. Does it bend towards or away from the normal? Explain.
**Answer**: Towards the normal. Water is optically denser than air, so light slows down and bends towards the normal.
**Explanation**: Denser medium -> slower speed -> bend towards normal. A common error is getting the direction wrong.
**Tags**: refraction, bending-direction, explanation

### Question Q-4.5-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A diamond has a refractive index of 2.42. Calculate the speed of light in diamond. (Speed of light in vacuum = 3.0 × 10⁸ m/s)
**Answer**: 1.24 × 10⁸ m/s
**Explanation**: n = c/v, so v = c/n = 3.0 × 10⁸ / 2.42 = 1.24 × 10⁸ m/s. A common error is multiplying instead of dividing, or forgetting that n > 1 means v < c.
**Tags**: refractive-index, speed-of-light, calculation, diamond

### Question Q-4.5-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A ray of light passes from glass (n = 1.5) into water (n = 1.33). The angle of incidence in glass is 30°. Calculate the angle of refraction in water.
**Answer**: 34.3°
**Explanation**: Using Snell's law: n₁ sin i₁ = n₂ sin i₂. 1.5 × sin 30° = 1.33 × sin i₂. sin i₂ = (1.5 × 0.5) / 1.33 = 0.75 / 1.33 = 0.5639. i₂ = sin⁻¹(0.5639) = 34.3°. A common error is using n = sin i / sin r directly without accounting for both media.
**Tags**: snells-law, refraction, multi-medium, calculation

### Question Q-4.5-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain why a fish in a pond appears closer to the surface than it actually is when viewed from above. Include a labelled ray diagram in your explanation.
**Answer**: Light from the fish travels from water (denser) to air (less dense). As it exits the water, it refracts away from the normal. To the observer's eye, the rays appear to come from a point shallower than the actual position of the fish. This is called apparent depth. The ray diagram should show rays from the fish bending at the surface and extending backwards to form a virtual image above the real position.
**Explanation**: Apparent depth is caused by refraction at the water-air boundary. A common error is drawing rays bending in the wrong direction or not extending them backwards to find the virtual image.
**Tags**: apparent-depth, refraction, ray-diagram, explanation, extended-response
