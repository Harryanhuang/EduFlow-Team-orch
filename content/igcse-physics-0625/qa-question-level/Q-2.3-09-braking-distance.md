# Question Q-2.3-09

**Difficulty**: Challenge

**Question**: A 1500 kg car travelling at 20 m/s brakes to a stop over a distance of 50 m. Calculate: (a) the initial kinetic energy, (b) the work done by the brakes, (c) the average braking force, (d) if the same car was travelling at 40 m/s, what distance would it need to stop (assuming same braking force)?

**Answer**: (a) Ek = ½ × 1500 × 20² = 300 000 J. (b) Work done by brakes = 300 000 J (all KE dissipated). (c) F = W/d = 300 000/50 = 6000 N. (d) Ek at 40 m/s = ½ × 1500 × 40² = 1 200 000 J. Distance = W/F = 1 200 000/6000 = 200 m.

**Explanation**: Doubling the speed quadruples the kinetic energy (v² relationship), so stopping distance is 4× longer. A common error is assuming stopping distance doubles when speed doubles.
