# Question Q-7.2-08

**Difficulty**: Challenge

**Question**: Two stars appear equally bright in the night sky. Star A is 10 light-years away and Star B is 100 light-years away. Which star is actually more luminous (intrinsically brighter)? Explain your reasoning.

**Answer**: Star B is actually more luminous. Apparent brightness decreases with the square of distance (inverse square law: brightness ∝ 1/d²). Since Star B is 10 times further away but appears equally bright, it must be 10² = 100 times more luminous than Star A to compensate for its greater distance. If both appeared equally bright from Earth, the more distant one must emit far more light.

**Explanation**: The inverse square law means doubling distance reduces apparent brightness to 1/4. At 10× distance, Star B needs 100× the luminosity to appear equally bright. A common error is thinking the closer star is more luminous or not applying the inverse square relationship.
