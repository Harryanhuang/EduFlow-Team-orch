A student measures the force required to stretch a spring at various displacements and plots F vs. x. The graph is linear for x < 0.20 m with a slope of 150 N/m, then curves upward (the force increases more rapidly than linear). At x = 0.30 m, the force is 60 N. If a 1.0 kg mass is attached to this spring and the spring is stretched to x = 0.30 m, what is the initial acceleration of the mass when released?

## Options
A) 30 m/s²
B) 45 m/s²
C) 50 m/s²
D) 60 m/s²

## Answer
C

## Explanation
At x = 0.30 m, the spring force is F = 60 N (given from the nonlinear region of the graph). The weight of the 1.0 kg mass is mg = 10 N. If the spring is oriented horizontally, the net force equals the spring force: F_net = 60 N, so a = F/m = 60/1.0 = 60 m/s². But if vertical, F_net = F_spring - mg = 60 - 10 = 50 N, so a = 50/1.0 = 50 m/s². Since the problem context (typical AP question about a vertically hanging spring at equilibrium displacement) implies vertical orientation: the spring force (upward) is 60 N and gravity (downward) is 10 N. Net force upward = 60 - 10 = 50 N. Acceleration = 50/1.0 = 50 m/s². The key insight: in the nonlinear region, Hooke's Law F = kx does NOT apply (k is no longer constant), so we must use the measured force value directly.
