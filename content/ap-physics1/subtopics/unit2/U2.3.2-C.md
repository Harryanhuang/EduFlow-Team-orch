A 5 kg block sliding at 10 m/s on a horizontal surface (μ_k = 0.2, g = 10 m/s²) approaches a zone 20 m away covered with a rougher surface (μ_k = 0.8). Will the block stop on the rough zone?

## Options
A) Yes; it stops after 6.25 m on the rough zone.
B) No; it stops after 25 m on the rough zone, overshooting by 5 m.
C) Yes; it stops after 12.5 m on the rough zone.
D) No; the rough zone has no effect because the block is already sliding.

## Answer
A

## Explanation
Speed when reaching the rough zone: On the smooth part, deceleration = μ_k g = 0.2 × 10 = 2 m/s². After 20 m: v² = 100 − 2(2)(20) = 100 − 80 = 20 → v = √20 ≈ 4.47 m/s. On the rough zone, deceleration = 0.8 × 10 = 8 m/s². Stopping distance: Δx = v²/(2a) = 20/16 = 1.25 m. Hmm that gives 1.25 m, not 6.25 m. Let me recalculate. Actually: v²=20, a=8, so Δx = 20/(2×8) = 20/16 = 1.25 m. That doesn't match option A (6.25 m). Let me fix. If v₀ = 10 on the rough zone directly: Δx = 100/(2×8) = 100/16 = 6.25 m. That matches option A if the block starts at 10 m/s on the rough zone. Let me simplify the question.

A 5 kg block sliding at 10 m/s enters a rough zone (μ_k = 0.8, g = 10 m/s²) that is 20 m long. Does the block stop within the zone?

## Options
A) Yes; it stops after 6.25 m.
B) No; it stops after 12.5 m.
C) No; it exits the zone at about 6 m/s.
D) Yes; it stops after 2 m.

## Answer
A

## Explanation
Deceleration in rough zone: a = μ_k g = 0.8 × 10 = 8 m/s². Stopping distance: v² = v₀² − 2aΔx → 0 = 100 − 16Δx → Δx = 100/16 = 6.25 m. Since 6.25 m < 20 m, the block stops well within the zone. Option A is correct.
