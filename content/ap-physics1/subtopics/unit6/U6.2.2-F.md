A simple pendulum has a period of 2.0 s on Earth. If the mass of the bob is doubled, what is the new period? (Assume small-angle oscillations.)

## Options
A) 1.0 s
B) 2.0 s
C) 2.8 s
D) 4.0 s

## Answer
B

## Explanation
The period of a simple pendulum is T = 2π√(L/g). Notice that mass does NOT appear in this formula. The period depends only on the length L and gravitational acceleration g, not on the mass of the bob. Doubling the mass has no effect on the period — it remains 2.0 s. This is analogous to how all objects fall at the same rate in a vacuum (gravitational and inertial mass cancel). Option A (1.0) incorrectly halves the period. Option C (2.8 ≈ 2√2) might come from T_new = T × √2. Option D (4.0) doubles the period. All of these incorrectly assume mass matters.
