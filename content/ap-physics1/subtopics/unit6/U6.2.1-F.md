Why does a simple pendulum approximate simple harmonic motion only for small swing angles?

## Options
A) Because friction is negligible only at small angles.
B) Because the restoring force (mg sin θ) is only proportional to displacement when sin θ ≈ θ at small angles.
C) Because gravity is weaker at larger angles.
D) Because the string becomes slack at large angles.

## Answer
B

## Explanation
The restoring force for a pendulum is the tangential component of gravity: F = -mg sin θ. For SHM, we need F ∝ displacement (F = -kx). The arc displacement is s = Lθ, so we need F ∝ θ. However, F = -mg sin θ, and sin θ ≠ θ in general. For small angles (θ < ~10°), the small-angle approximation sin θ ≈ θ (in radians) holds, making F ≈ -mgθ = -(mg/L)s, which IS proportional to displacement. At larger angles, sin θ deviates significantly from θ, and the motion is periodic but not simple harmonic. Option A is wrong — friction exists at all angles. Option C is wrong — gravity is constant. Option D is wrong — the string stays taut.
