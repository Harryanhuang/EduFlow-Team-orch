An object oscillates in simple harmonic motion. At which point in its motion is the magnitude of its acceleration greatest?

## Options
A) At the equilibrium position.
B) At the point of maximum displacement (amplitude).
C) At the point where its speed is greatest.
D) The acceleration is constant throughout the motion.

## Answer
B

## Explanation
In SHM, acceleration a = -(k/m)x. The magnitude of acceleration is |a| = (k/m)|x|, which is directly proportional to the displacement from equilibrium. At maximum displacement (x = ±A, the amplitude), the acceleration magnitude is greatest: |a_max| = kA/m. At equilibrium (x = 0), acceleration is zero. Option A is wrong — acceleration is zero at equilibrium. Option C is wrong — speed is greatest at equilibrium, where acceleration is zero (they are out of phase). Option D is wrong — acceleration varies with position. Key insight: acceleration and velocity reach their maxima at different points in the cycle.
