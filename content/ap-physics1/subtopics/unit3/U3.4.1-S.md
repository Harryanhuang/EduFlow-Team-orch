A satellite orbits a planet of mass M in a circular orbit of radius r with period T. If the satellite's mass is doubled (but M and r stay the same), what happens to the period?

## Options
A) The period doubles.
B) The period halves.
C) The period stays the same.
D) The period quadruples.

## Answer
C

## Explanation
From Newton's Second Law for circular orbit: GMm/r² = mv²/r = m(2πr/T)²/r. The satellite mass m cancels: GM/r² = (2πr/T)²/r = 4π²r/T². Solving: T² = 4π²r³/(GM). The period depends only on r and M, not on the satellite mass. Doubling the satellite mass has no effect on the period. This is analogous to how all objects fall at the same rate regardless of mass — the gravitational force increases proportionally with mass, but so does the inertia, and the effects cancel.
