A satellite orbits Earth at a constant radius $r = 7.00 \times 10^6\,\text{m}$ from Earth's center. Earth's mass is $M_E = 5.97 \times 10^{24}\,\text{kg}$. What is the orbital period $T$?

$G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11}\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{kg}^2$

## Options
A) $1.52 \times 10^3\,\text{s}$
B) $5.20 \times 10^3\,\text{s}$
C) $5.85 \times 10^3\,\text{s}$
D) $2.76 \times 10^3\,\text{s}$

## Answer
C

## Explanation
For a circular orbit, the gravitational force provides the centripetal acceleration:

$$\frac{GM_E m}{r^2} = m\frac{v^2}{r}$$

The orbital speed is $v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}$. Substituting:

$$\frac{GM_E}{r^2} = \frac{1}{r}\left(\frac{2\pi r}{T}\right)^2 = \frac{4\pi^2 r}{T^2}$$

Solving for $T$:

$$T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2 r^3}{GM_E}$$

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{r^3}{GM_E}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{(7.00 \times 10^6)^3}{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(5.97 \times 10^{24})}}$$

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{3.43 \times 10^{20}}{3.98 \times 10^{14}}} = 2\pi\sqrt{8.62 \times 10^5} = 2\pi \times 928 = 5.83 \times 10^3\,\text{s}$$

Option A (1.52 × 10³ s) uses the satellite mass instead of Earth's mass or a related error. Option B (5.20 × 10³ s) results from a calculation error in $r^3$. Option D (2.76 × 10³ s) results from using $r^2$ instead of $r^3$ in Kepler's law.

The answer is **C**.
