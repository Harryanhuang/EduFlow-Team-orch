A thin spherical shell of radius $R = 5.00 \times 10^6\,\text{m}$ and total mass $M = 3.00 \times 10^{24}\,\text{kg}$ is centered at the origin. What is the magnitude of the gravitational field at $r = 3.00 \times 10^6\,\text{m}$ from the center?

## Options
A) $13.3\,\text{N/kg}$
B) $8.00\,\text{N/kg}$
C) $0\,\text{N/kg}$
D) $29.6\,\text{N/kg}$

## Answer
C

## Explanation
The point $r = 3.00 \times 10^6\,\text{m}$ lies inside the spherical shell ($r < R = 5.00 \times 10^6\,\text{m}$). By Gauss's law for gravity:

$$\oint \mathbf{g} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = -4\pi G M_{\text{enc}}$$

Choosing a spherical Gaussian surface of radius $r = 3.00 \times 10^6\,\text{m}$: the enclosed mass is $M_{\text{enc}} = 0$ because the shell's mass lies outside this surface.

Since $M_{\text{enc}} = 0$, the flux is zero, and by spherical symmetry, $g \cdot 4\pi r^2 = 0$, so $g = 0$ everywhere inside the shell.

This is the classic shell theorem result: a uniform spherical shell exerts no net gravitational force on any particle inside it. The gravitational field at all interior points is zero, regardless of the shell's mass.

Options A, B, and D all give nonzero values, which would be obtained if one incorrectly applied $g = GM/r^2$ without checking whether the point is inside or outside the mass distribution.

The answer is **C**.
