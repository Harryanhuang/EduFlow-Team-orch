common_mistake: Applying the inverse-square law inside a sphere without accounting for the enclosed mass fraction.
explanation_context: By Gauss's law: ∮g·dA = -4πGM_enc. For a point outside a sphere, M_enc = M_total, giving g(r) = -GM/r². For a point inside a uniform sphere of radius R, M_enc = M(r/R)³, giving g(r) = -(GM/R³)r, proportional to r.

A uniform solid sphere has mass $M = 6.00 \times 10^{24}\,\text{kg}$ and radius $R = 6.40 \times 10^6\,\text{m}$. What is the magnitude of the gravitational field at a distance $r = R/2$ from the center (inside the sphere)?

## Options
A) $9.77\,\text{N/kg}$
B) $19.5\,\text{N/kg}$
C) $4.89\,\text{N/kg}$
D) $0\,\text{N/kg}$

## Answer
C

## Explanation
**Surface field:** $g_{\text{surface}} = \frac{GM}{R^2} = \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11})(6.00 \times 10^{24})}{(6.40 \times 10^6)^2}$

$$g_{\text{surface}} = \frac{4.002 \times 10^{14}}{4.096 \times 10^{13}} = 9.77\,\text{N/kg}$$

**Inside the sphere:** By Gauss's law, the enclosed mass at $r = R/2$ is:

$$M_{\text{enc}} = M\left(\frac{r}{R}\right)^3 = M\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^3 = \frac{M}{8}$$

The flux is $\oint g \cdot dA = -4\pi G M_{\text{enc}}$, so:

$$g(4\pi r^2) = -4\pi G \cdot \frac{M}{8}$$

$$g = -\frac{GM}{8}\cdot\frac{1}{r^2} = -\frac{GM}{8}\cdot\frac{1}{(R/2)^2} = -\frac{GM}{8}\cdot\frac{4}{R^2} = -\frac{GM}{2R^2}$$

Thus $|g(r = R/2)| = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 9.77 = 4.89\,\text{N/kg}$.

Option A (9.77) is the surface field, not accounting for the reduced enclosed mass. Option B (19.5) is double the surface value, which would be the value at $r = 2R$ if one forgot to divide by $r^2$ correctly inside. Option D (0) would only be true at $r = 0$.

The answer is **C**.
