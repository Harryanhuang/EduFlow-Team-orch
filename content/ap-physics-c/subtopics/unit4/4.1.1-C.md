A solid hemisphere of radius $R = 0.500\ \text{m}$ has its flat face in the $yz$-plane, with the curved surface extending in the positive $x$-direction from $x = 0$ to $x = R$. The density varies linearly with $x$:

$$\rho(x) = \rho_0\,\frac{x}{R}, \qquad \rho_0 = 800\ \text{kg/m}^3.$$

What is the $x$-coordinate of the hemisphere's center of mass?

## Options
A) $0.188\ \text{m}$
B) $0.267\ \text{m}$
C) $0.300\ \text{m}$
D) $0.375\ \text{m}$

## Answer
B

## Explanation
The $x$-coordinate of the center of mass for a three-dimensional body is

$$x_{\text{cm}} = \frac{\displaystyle\iiint x\,\rho\,dV}{\displaystyle\iiint \rho\,dV}.$$

Use the disk method: at a given $x$, a cross-section perpendicular to the $x$-axis is a disk of radius $r(x) = \sqrt{R^2 - x^2}$. The volume element for a thin disk of thickness $dx$ is $dV = \pi r^2\,dx = \pi (R^2 - x^2)\,dx$.

**Denominator (total mass):**

$$M = \iiint \rho\,dV = \int_{x=0}^{R} \rho(x)\,\pi (R^2 - x^2)\,dx
= \pi \int_0^R \rho_0\frac{x}{R}\,(R^2 - x^2)\,dx
= \frac{\pi\rho_0}{R} \int_0^R x(R^2 - x^2)\,dx.$$

$$\int_0^R x(R^2 - x^2)\,dx = \int_0^R (R^2 x - x^3)\,dx
= \left[\frac{R^2 x^2}{2} - \frac{x^4}{4}\right]_0^R
= \frac{R^4}{2} - \frac{R^4}{4} = \frac{R^4}{4}.$$

Thus

$$M = \frac{\pi\rho_0}{R} \cdot \frac{R^4}{4} = \frac{\pi\rho_0 R^3}{4}.$$

**Numerator:**

$$\iiint x\,\rho\,dV = \int_{x=0}^{R} x\cdot\rho(x)\,\pi (R^2 - x^2)\,dx
= \pi \int_0^R x\left(\rho_0\frac{x}{R}\right)(R^2 - x^2)\,dx
= \frac{\pi\rho_0}{R} \int_0^R x^2 (R^2 - x^2)\,dx.$$

$$\int_0^R x^2(R^2 - x^2)\,dx = \int_0^R (R^2 x^2 - x^4)\,dx
= \left[\frac{R^2 x^3}{3} - \frac{x^5}{5}\right]_0^R
= \frac{R^5}{3} - \frac{R^5}{5}
= R^5\left(\frac{5}{15} - \frac{3}{15}\right)
= \frac{2R^5}{15}.$$

Thus

$$\text{Numerator} = \frac{\pi\rho_0}{R} \cdot \frac{2R^5}{15} = \frac{2\pi\rho_0 R^4}{15}.$$

**Center of mass:**

$$x_{\text{cm}} = \frac{2\pi\rho_0 R^4 / 15}{\pi\rho_0 R^3 / 4}
= \frac{2}{15} \cdot \frac{4}{1}\,R
= \frac{8R}{15}.$$

Substituting $R = 0.500\ \text{m}$:

$$x_{\text{cm}} = \frac{8(0.500\ \text{m})}{15} = \frac{4.00}{15}\ \text{m} \approx 0.267\ \text{m}.$$

This is significantly offset from the uniform-hemisphere result $x_{\text{cm}} = 3R/8 = 0.188\ \text{m}$ (option A), because the linearly increasing density places more mass near the curved surface, shifting the center of mass toward larger $x$.
