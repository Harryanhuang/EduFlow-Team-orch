A thin rod of length $L = 2.00\ \text{m}$ lies along the $x$-axis from $x = 0$ to $x = L$. The linear mass density of the rod varies with position according to $\lambda(x) = \lambda_0 \left(\frac{x}{L}\right)$, where $\lambda_0 = 3.00\ \text{kg/m}$.

What is the $x$-coordinate of the rod's center of mass?

## Options
A) $1.33\ \text{m}$
B) $1.00\ \text{m}$
C) $1.50\ \text{m}$
D) $0.667\ \text{m}$

## Answer
A

## Explanation
The center of mass for a continuous one-dimensional distribution is

$$x_{\text{cm}} = \frac{\displaystyle\int x\,dm}{\displaystyle\int dm} = \frac{\displaystyle\int_0^L x\,\lambda(x)\,dx}{\displaystyle\int_0^L \lambda(x)\,dx}.$$

Substitute $\lambda(x) = \lambda_0 \frac{x}{L}$:

$$x_{\text{cm}} = \frac{\displaystyle\int_0^L x\left(\lambda_0\frac{x}{L}\right)dx}{\displaystyle\int_0^L \lambda_0\frac{x}{L}\,dx}
= \frac{\displaystyle\frac{\lambda_0}{L}\int_0^L x^2\,dx}{\displaystyle\frac{\lambda_0}{L}\int_0^L x\,dx}
= \frac{\displaystyle\int_0^L x^2\,dx}{\displaystyle\int_0^L x\,dx}.$$

Evaluating the integrals:

$$\int_0^L x^2\,dx = \left[\frac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^L = \frac{L^3}{3}, \qquad
\int_0^L x\,dx = \left[\frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^L = \frac{L^2}{2}.$$

Thus

$$x_{\text{cm}} = \frac{L^3/3}{L^2/2} = \frac{2L}{3}.$$

With $L = 2.00\ \text{m}$:

$$x_{\text{cm}} = \frac{2(2.00\ \text{m})}{3} = \frac{4.00}{3}\ \text{m} \approx 1.33\ \text{m}.$$

The result $x_{\text{cm}} = 2L/3$ is independent of $\lambda_0$ because $\lambda_0$ cancels in the ratio — the linear dependence $\lambda \propto x$ shifts the center of mass toward the heavier end of the rod.
