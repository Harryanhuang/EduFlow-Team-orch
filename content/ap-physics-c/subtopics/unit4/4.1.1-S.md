A rectangular lamina occupies the region $0 \le x \le a$, $0 \le y \le b$ in the $xy$-plane, where $a = 0.600\ \text{m}$ and $b = 0.400\ \text{m}$. The surface mass density varies linearly with $x$:

$$\sigma(x) = \sigma_0\,\frac{x}{a}, \qquad \sigma_0 = 8.00\ \text{kg/m}^2.$$

What is the $x$-coordinate of the lamina's center of mass?

## Options
A) $0.400\ \text{m}$
B) $0.300\ \text{m}$
C) $0.267\ \text{m}$
D) $0.200\ \text{m}$

## Answer
A

## Explanation
For a planar mass distribution, the $x$-coordinate of the center of mass is

$$x_{\text{cm}} = \frac{\displaystyle\iint x\,\sigma(x)\,dA}{\displaystyle\iint \sigma(x)\,dA},$$

where $dA = dx\,dy$. Since $\sigma$ depends only on $x$, the $y$-integral in both numerator and denominator factors out as the width $b$.

**Denominator (total mass):**

$$M = \iint \sigma(x)\,dA = \int_{x=0}^{a} \int_{y=0}^{b} \sigma_0\frac{x}{a}\,dy\,dx
= \frac{\sigma_0}{a} \int_0^a x \left(\int_0^b dy\right) dx
= \frac{\sigma_0}{a} \int_0^a x\,(b)\,dx
= \frac{\sigma_0 b}{a} \int_0^a x\,dx.$$

$$\int_0^a x\,dx = \frac{a^2}{2} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
M = \frac{\sigma_0 b}{a} \cdot \frac{a^2}{2} = \frac{\sigma_0 a b}{2}.$$

**Numerator:**

$$\iint x\,\sigma(x)\,dA = \int_0^a \int_0^b x\left(\sigma_0\frac{x}{a}\right)dy\,dx
= \frac{\sigma_0}{a} \int_0^a x^2 \left(\int_0^b dy\right) dx
= \frac{\sigma_0}{a} \int_0^a x^2\,(b)\,dx
= \frac{\sigma_0 b}{a} \int_0^a x^2\,dx.$$

$$\int_0^a x^2\,dx = \frac{a^3}{3} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\text{Numerator} = \frac{\sigma_0 b}{a} \cdot \frac{a^3}{3} = \frac{\sigma_0 a^2 b}{3}.$$

**Center of mass:**

$$x_{\text{cm}} = \frac{\sigma_0 a^2 b / 3}{\sigma_0 a b / 2} = \frac{2a}{3}.$$

Substituting $a = 0.600\ \text{m}$:

$$x_{\text{cm}} = \frac{2(0.600\ \text{m})}{3} = \frac{1.20}{3}\ \text{m} = 0.400\ \text{m}.$$

Note that $x_{\text{cm}} = 2a/3$ is independent of $b$ and $\sigma_0$. The $y$-coordinate can be found similarly (for this density, $y_{\text{cm}} = b/2$, the midpoint in $y$, because $\sigma$ does not depend on $y$).
