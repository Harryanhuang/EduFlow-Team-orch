A thin uniform rod of length $L = 2.00$ m and mass $M = 4.00$ kg rotates about an axis perpendicular to the rod through a point located $L/4$ from one end (i.e., $0.500$ m from the near end). What is its moment of inertia?

## Options
A) $3.00\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$
B) $2.33\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$
C) $1.33\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$
D) $4.00\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$

## Answer
B

## Explanation
**Method 1: Direct integration (calculus-based)**

Set the rotation axis at $r = 0$. The rod extends from $r = -L/4$ (near end) to $r = 3L/4$ (far end). With linear density $\lambda = M/L$:

$$I = \int r^2\,dm = \int_{-L/4}^{3L/4} r^2 \cdot \frac{M}{L}\,dr = \frac{M}{L} \left[ \frac{r^3}{3} \right]_{-L/4}^{3L/4}$$

Evaluate:

$$\left[ \frac{r^3}{3} \right]_{-L/4}^{3L/4} = \frac{1}{3}\left[ \left(\frac{3L}{4}\right)^3 - \left(-\frac{L}{4}\right)^3 \right] = \frac{1}{3}\left[ \frac{27L^3}{64} + \frac{L^3}{64} \right] = \frac{1}{3}\cdot\frac{28L^3}{64} = \frac{7L^3}{48}$$

Therefore:

$$I = \frac{M}{L} \cdot \frac{7L^3}{48} = \frac{7}{48}ML^2$$

**Method 2: Parallel-axis theorem (verification)**

The axis is $d = L/4$ from the rod's center. Using $I = I_{\text{cm}} + Md^2$:

$$I = \frac{1}{12}ML^2 + M\left(\frac{L}{4}\right)^2 = \frac{1}{12}ML^2 + \frac{1}{16}ML^2 = \left(\frac{4}{48} + \frac{3}{48}\right)ML^2 = \frac{7}{48}ML^2 \checkmark$$

**Numerical substitution:**

$$I = \frac{7}{48}(4.00\ \text{kg})(2.00\ \text{m})^2 = \frac{7 \cdot 16.0}{48} = \frac{112.0}{48} = 2.33\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$$

The moment of inertia is $2.33\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$, corresponding to choice **B**.

Note: A common mistake is using only $I_{\text{cm}} = \frac{1}{12}ML^2 = \frac{1}{12}(4.00)(4.00) = 1.33\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$ (choice C), which neglects the parallel-axis contribution $Md^2 = 4.00 \cdot (0.50)^2 = 1.00\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$.
