A uniform rod of length $L = 1.50$ m and mass $m = 3.00$ kg is hinged at one end on a vertical wall. A horizontal string attached to the rod at $x = 1.00$ m from the hinge holds the rod at $45^\circ$ above the horizontal. What is the tension $T$ in the string?

## Options

A) $T = 15.6$ N
B) $T = 22.1$ N
C) $T = 31.2$ N
D) $T = 44.1$ N

## Answer

B

## Explanation

**Step 1: Torque from the distributed weight (calculus approach)**

Each infinitesimal segment $dr$ at distance $r$ from the hinge has mass $dm = \lambda\,dr = (m/L)\,dr$. Its weight $dW = g\,dm$ acts vertically downward. Only the component perpendicular to the rod produces torque about the hinge. Since the rod is at $45^\circ$ above horizontal, the perpendicular component of each segment's weight is $dW_\perp = dW \cos 45^\circ$.

The torque contribution about the hinge from this segment is $d\tau = r \cdot dW_\perp$.

$$\tau_W = \int_0^L r \,\lambda\, g \cos 45^\circ\, dr
        = \frac{mg\cos 45^\circ}{L} \int_0^L r\, dr
        = \frac{mg\cos 45^\circ}{L} \cdot \frac{L^2}{2}
        = \frac{1}{2}\,mgL\cos 45^\circ$$

Numerically:

$$\tau_W = \frac{1}{2}(3.00)(9.80)(1.50)\cos 45^\circ
        = 22.05 \times 0.7071
        = 15.59 \text{ N·m}$$

**Step 2: Torque from the horizontal string tension**

The string pulls horizontally at $x = 1.00$ m from the hinge. The rod is at $45^\circ$ above the horizontal, so the angle between the horizontal string and the rod is $45^\circ$. The perpendicular component of the tension is $T\sin 45^\circ$.

$$\tau_T = T \cdot x \cdot \sin 45^\circ = T(1.00)(0.7071)$$

**Step 3: Equilibrium condition**

Setting $\tau_T = \tau_W$:

$$T(1.00)(0.7071) = 15.59$$

$$T = \frac{15.59}{0.7071} = 22.05 \text{ N} \approx 22.1 \text{ N}$$

**Key insight:** For a uniform rod, the torque integral $\int_0^L r\,\lambda\,g\cos\theta\,dr$ evaluates to $\frac{1}{2}mgL\cos\theta$. At $\theta = 45^\circ$, the $\cos 45^\circ$ and $\sin 45^\circ$ factors are equal, so they cancel and the answer simplifies to $T = (mgL)/(2x)$. Substituting gives $T = (3.00 \times 9.80 \times 1.50)/(2 \times 1.00) = 22.1$ N.

A common mistake is to omit the $\cos 45^\circ$ factor from the weight torque (yielding option C) or to omit the $\sin 45^\circ$ factor from the string torque (yielding option A).
