A solid hemisphere of radius $R = 0.300$ m and mass $M = 2.50$ kg rotates about an axis passing through the **center of its flat face**, perpendicular to the flat face. What is its moment of inertia?

## Options
A) $0.0450\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$
B) $0.0900\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$
C) $0.1125\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$
D) $0.225\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$

## Answer
B

## Explanation
**Setup in cylindrical coordinates**

Place the flat face of the hemisphere in the $xy$-plane with its center at the origin. The hemisphere occupies $0 \le z \le R$, and at each height $z$ the cross-section is a disk of radius:

$$r(z) = \sqrt{R^2 - z^2}$$

Slice the hemisphere into infinitesimal disks of thickness $dz$ at height $z$.

**Disk contribution to moment of inertia**

Each infinitesimal disk has:
- Mass: $dm = \rho \cdot \pi r(z)^2\,dz = \rho\pi(R^2 - z^2)\,dz$
- Moment of inertia about its central axis (perpendicular to the disk face): $dI = \frac{1}{2}r(z)^2\,dm = \frac{1}{2}r(z)^2 \cdot \rho\pi r(z)^2\,dz = \frac{1}{2}\pi\rho\,(R^2 - z^2)^2\,dz$

**Integrate over the hemisphere:**

$$I = \int_0^R \frac{\pi\rho}{2}(R^2 - z^2)^2\,dz = \frac{\pi\rho}{2}\int_0^R (R^4 - 2R^2z^2 + z^4)\,dz$$

Evaluate the integral:

$$\int_0^R (R^4 - 2R^2z^2 + z^4)\,dz = \left[ R^4z - \frac{2R^2z^3}{3} + \frac{z^5}{5} \right]_0^R$$

$$= R^5 - \frac{2R^5}{3} + \frac{R^5}{5} = R^5\left(1 - \frac{2}{3} + \frac{1}{5}\right) = R^5\left(\frac{15 - 10 + 3}{15}\right) = \frac{8R^5}{15}$$

Therefore:

$$I = \frac{\pi\rho}{2} \cdot \frac{8R^5}{15} = \frac{4\pi\rho R^5}{15}$$

**Express $\rho$ in terms of $M$:**

The mass of the hemisphere is $M = \rho V = \rho \cdot \frac{2}{3}\pi R^3$, so:

$$\rho = \frac{3M}{2\pi R^3}$$

Substitute into $I$:

$$I = \frac{4\pi}{15} \cdot \frac{3M}{2\pi R^3} \cdot R^5 = \frac{12\pi M R^5}{30\pi R^3} = \frac{2}{5}MR^2$$

**Numerical calculation:**

$$I = \frac{2}{5}(2.50\ \text{kg})(0.300\ \text{m})^2 = \frac{2}{5}(2.50)(0.0900) = \frac{2}{5}(0.225) = 0.0900\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$$

The moment of inertia is $0.0900\ \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$, corresponding to choice **B**.

Note: Remarkably, $I = (2/5)MR^2$ is the same formula as for a full solid sphere about any diameter. This is not a coincidence — the full sphere's mass is distributed symmetrically, and the hemisphere's contribution about this specific axis yields the same functional form. Do not be tempted to simply apply the result without the calculus derivation.
