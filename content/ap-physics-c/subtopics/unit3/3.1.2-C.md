A $2$ kg block on a horizontal surface with kinetic friction coefficient $\mu_k = 0.5$ is pushed by a variable force $F(x) = 8x$ N. The block starts at rest at $x = 0$. What is the block's speed at $x = 4$ m? (Use $g = 9.8$ m/s².)

## Options
A) $v_f \approx 4.00$ m/s
B) $v_f \approx 5.74$ m/s
C) $v_f \approx 5.00$ m/s
D) $v_f \approx 6.16$ m/s

## Answer
C

## Explanation
The normal force is $F_N = mg = 2(9.8) = 19.6$ N.

The kinetic friction force (opposing motion) is:

$$f_k = \mu_k F_N = 0.5(19.6) = 9.8 \text{ N}$$

The net force in the direction of motion is $F(x) - f_k = 8x - 9.8$.

The net work done as the block moves from $x = 0$ to $x = 4$ m:

$$W_{net} = \int_0^4 (8x - 9.8)\, dx = \left[4x^2 - 9.8x\right]_0^4 = (64 - 39.2) - 0 = 24.8 \text{ J}$$

Applying the work-energy theorem with $v_i = 0$:

$$W_{net} = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - 0 \implies v_f = \sqrt{\frac{2W_{net}}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(24.8)}{2}} = \sqrt{24.8} \approx 4.98 \text{ m/s}$$

The block reaches a speed of approximately $5.00$ m/s at $x = 4$ m. The correct answer is **C**.

Key insight: the work done by friction is path-dependent and is $f_k \cdot d = 9.8 \cdot 4 = 39.2$ J (the area under the constant friction-force curve), while the work done by $F(x) = 8x$ is $64$ J. The difference gives the net work that becomes kinetic energy.
