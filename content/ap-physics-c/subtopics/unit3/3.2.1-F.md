A conservative force $F(x) = -6x$ N acts on a particle of mass $m = 2$ kg. The particle is released from rest at $x = 2$ m. What is the speed when it reaches $x = 0$?

## Options
A) $v \approx 2.45$ m/s
B) $v \approx 3.46$ m/s
C) $v \approx 4.90$ m/s
D) $v \approx 6.93$ m/s

## Answer
B

## Explanation
The potential energy is:

$$U(x) = -\int F(x)\, dx = -\int (-6x)\, dx = 3x^2 + C$$

Choosing the reference $U(0) = 0$ (so $C = 0$):

$$U(x) = 3x^2 \text{ J}$$

At $x = 2$ m: $U(2) = 3(4) = 12$ J. The particle starts at rest, so $KE = 0$.

By conservation of energy:

$$E_{initial} = KE_i + U_i = 0 + 12 = 12 \text{ J}$$
$$E_{final} = KE_f + U_f = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 + 0 = 12 \text{ J}$$
$$v_f = \sqrt{\frac{2(12)}{2}} = \sqrt{12} \approx 3.46 \text{ m/s}$$

The correct answer is **B**.

This is also known as Hooke's law potential: $U(x) = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$ where $k = 6$ N/m. Energy conservation between two positions gives the same result as computing the work integral directly.
