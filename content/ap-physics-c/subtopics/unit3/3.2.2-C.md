A particle has velocity $v(t) = 6 - 3t$ m/s and is subject to a time-varying force $F(t) = 4t$ N. At what times is the instantaneous power delivered by the force equal to zero? At what time is it at a maximum?

## Options
A) $P = 0$ at $t = 0$ s and $t = 2$ s; max at $t = 1$ s
B) $P = 0$ at $t = 0$ s; max at $t = 2$ s
C) $P = 0$ at $t = 1$ s; max at $t = 2$ s
D) $P$ is never zero; max at $t = 1$ s

## Answer
A

## Explanation
Instantaneous power is the product of force and velocity:

$$P(t) = F(t) \cdot v(t) = (4t)(6 - 3t) = 24t - 12t^2 \text{ W}$$

**Zeros of P(t)**: Factor:

$$P(t) = 12t(2 - t) = 0 \implies t = 0 \text{ s or } t = 2 \text{ s}$$

At $t = 0$, the velocity is non-zero ($v = 6$ m/s) but the force is zero, so $P = 0$. At $t = 2$ s, the velocity is zero ($v = 0$) but the force is non-zero, so $P = 0$.

**Maximum of P(t)**: Differentiate using the product rule:

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}(24t - 12t^2) = 24 - 24t$$

Set to zero: $24 - 24t = 0 \implies t = 1$ s.

Verify it's a maximum: $\frac{d^2P}{dt^2} = -24 < 0$, so $P$ is concave down at $t = 1$.

$$P(1) = 24(1) - 12(1)^2 = 24 - 12 = 12 \text{ W}$$

The instantaneous power is zero at $t = 0$ and $t = 2$ s, and reaches a maximum of $12$ W at $t = 1$ s. The correct answer is **A**.

The key insight is that power is zero whenever force or velocity is zero, and the maximum power occurs at the equilibrium between increasing force and decreasing velocity.
