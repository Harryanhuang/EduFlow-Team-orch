### Question Q-4.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: To what extent should commercial banks be allowed to create credit without government restrictions?
**Answer**: Allowing banks freedom to create credit promotes economic growth by enabling businesses to invest and consumers to spend. Competition among banks can lead to lower interest rates and more innovative financial products. However, unrestricted credit creation can lead to excessive lending, creating asset bubbles (such as housing booms) and financial instability. The 2008 financial crisis demonstrated how excessive credit creation in mortgage markets can trigger a global economic downturn. Banks may prioritise short-term profit over long-term stability, lending to risky borrowers. Government restrictions such as reserve requirements, capital adequacy ratios, and lending limits are necessary to prevent systemic risk. In conclusion, some degree of regulation is essential — completely unrestricted credit creation is dangerous, but over-regulation may stifle economic growth. A balanced approach with prudential regulation is most effective.
**Explanation**: Weigh economic growth benefits against financial stability risks. Reference to real-world examples (2008 crisis) strengthens the evaluation. A common error is arguing for either complete freedom or complete control without considering the trade-off.
