### Question Q-3.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: To what extent can a government use knowledge of PED when setting indirect taxes on demerit goods such as cigarettes?
**Answer**: If demand is price inelastic (as with addictive goods like cigarettes), an indirect tax will raise significant tax revenue while only slightly reducing consumption. This is effective for revenue but less effective at changing behaviour. If demand were more elastic, the tax would reduce quantity demanded more substantially. However, inelastic demand means the tax is regressive, disproportionately affecting low-income consumers. The government must weigh revenue generation against equity concerns and consider complementary policies such as education campaigns and advertising restrictions.
**Explanation**: Inelastic demand means tax raises revenue but has limited impact on quantity. A common error is assuming a tax always significantly reduces consumption regardless of elasticity.
