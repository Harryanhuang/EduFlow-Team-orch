### Question Q-5.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Analyse why two countries with similar levels of GNI per capita may have very different HDI rankings.
**Answer**: The HDI incorporates health and education alongside income. Two countries may have similar GNI per capita but differ significantly in life expectancy and schooling. For example, a country that invests heavily in public healthcare and universal education (such as Sri Lanka) can achieve a higher HDI than a country with similar income but weak social services. Differences in government spending priorities, the efficiency of healthcare systems, cultural attitudes to education (especially for girls), and the prevalence of diseases all affect health and education outcomes independently of income. Additionally, GNI per capita is an average — if income is more equally distributed, more people can access health and education services, boosting the HDI.
**Explanation**: GNI per capita is only one component of the HDI; health and education outcomes depend on policy choices, not just income. A common error is assuming GNI alone determines HDI ranking.
