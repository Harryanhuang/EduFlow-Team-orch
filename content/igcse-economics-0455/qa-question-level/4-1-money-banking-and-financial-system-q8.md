### Question Q-4.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Discuss whether a move to a completely cashless society would benefit all members of an economy.
**Answer**: A cashless society could reduce the costs of printing, storing, and transporting physical currency, make transactions faster and more traceable (reducing tax evasion and illegal activity), and improve convenience for consumers through digital payments. However, it would disadvantage groups without access to bank accounts or smartphones, particularly the elderly, low-income earners, and rural populations. Small vendors who rely on cash transactions may face higher costs from payment processing fees. Privacy concerns arise as all transactions become electronically recorded. The loss of cash as a backup during system outages or cyberattacks creates vulnerability. Overall, while a cashless society offers efficiency gains, it would not benefit all members equally — policy measures such as financial inclusion programmes and maintaining some cash infrastructure would be needed to protect vulnerable groups.
**Explanation**: Consider efficiency gains against exclusion, privacy loss, and vulnerability. A common error is assuming everyone has equal access to digital payment technology or ignoring the impact on specific groups such as the elderly or informal sector workers.
