### Question Q-5.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: To what extent does the HDI provide an accurate and fair measure of development for all countries? Discuss with reference to its strengths and weaknesses.
**Answer**: The HDI has important strengths: it moves beyond income alone by including health and education, it enables straightforward international comparison, and it highlights that development is about expanding human capabilities. However, its accuracy and fairness are limited in several ways. It does not capture inequality within countries — the Inequality-adjusted HDI (IHDI) was created to address this. It ignores environmental sustainability, so a country depleting its natural resources may still score highly. It does not measure political freedom, safety, or happiness. The education component (years of schooling) does not capture quality of learning. For the least developed countries, data reliability is often poor, reducing accuracy. Furthermore, the HDI may not reflect the priorities of all cultures equally. In conclusion, the HDI is a valuable starting point for measuring development but should be supplemented with other indicators such as the Gini coefficient, environmental metrics, and the Multidimensional Poverty Index for a fairer, more accurate assessment.
**Explanation**: The HDI is a useful but incomplete measure — its accuracy depends on data quality and its fairness depends on what dimensions are valued. A common error is either fully endorsing or fully dismissing the HDI without balanced analysis.
