### Question Q-3.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What characterises a perfectly competitive market?
**Answer**: Many buyers and sellers, identical (homogeneous) products, free entry and exit from the market, and perfect information. No single firm can influence the market price.
**Explanation**: Many firms, identical products, free entry. A common error is saying firms in perfect competition can set their own prices.
**Tags**: perfect-competition, characteristics

### Question Q-3.4-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a monopoly?
**Answer**: A monopoly exists when a single firm is the only supplier of a product with no close substitutes. The firm has significant market power and can influence the market price.
**Explanation**: Single supplier, no close substitutes. A common error is saying any large firm is a monopoly.
**Tags**: monopoly, definition

### Question Q-3.4-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Compare prices and output in perfect competition versus monopoly.
**Answer**: Perfect competition leads to lower prices and higher output because many firms compete and are allocatively efficient (P = MC). Monopoly leads to higher prices and lower output because the single firm restricts supply to maximise profit (P > MC).
**Explanation**: PC = low price, high output. Monopoly = high price, low output. A common error is reversing this comparison.
**Tags**: perfect-competition, monopoly, comparison

### Question Q-3.4-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is an oligopoly and why are firms in an oligopoly interdependent?
**Answer**: An oligopoly is a market dominated by a small number of large firms with high barriers to entry. Firms are interdependent because each firm's pricing and output decisions directly affect its rivals, leading to strategic behaviour where firms must anticipate competitors' reactions.
**Explanation**: Few large firms, interdependence due to market concentration. A common error is confusing oligopoly with monopolistic competition.
**Tags**: oligopoly, definition, interdependence

### Question Q-3.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is monopolistic competition and how does it differ from perfect competition?
**Answer**: Monopolistic competition has many firms selling differentiated (similar but not identical) products, giving each firm some price-setting power. It differs from perfect competition because products are not identical, firms face downward-sloping demand curves, and there is non-price competition through branding and advertising.
**Explanation**: Many firms, differentiated products. A common error is confusing monopolistic competition with monopoly or perfect competition.
**Tags**: monopolistic-competition, perfect-competition, distinction

### Question Q-3.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What are barriers to entry? Give two examples and explain how they protect existing firms.
**Answer**: Barriers to entry are obstacles that prevent or make it difficult for new firms to enter a market. Examples: (1) Patents give legal protection that prevents competitors from copying a product. (2) High start-up costs (e.g. building factories) deter new entrants who lack capital. These protect existing firms by limiting competition, allowing them to maintain higher prices and profits.
**Explanation**: Obstacles to new entrants. A common error is confusing barriers to entry with barriers to exit.
**Tags**: barriers-to-entry, examples, market-power

### Question Q-3.4-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether monopoly is always bad for consumers.
**Answer**: Monopolies can harm consumers by charging higher prices, restricting output, and having less incentive to be efficient due to lack of competition. However, monopolies are not always bad: they may benefit from economies of scale that lower costs and prices, they may invest more in research and development using supernormal profits, and natural monopolies (such as utilities) may be more efficient as a single provider than with duplicated infrastructure. The impact on consumers depends on whether the monopoly uses its market power exploitatively or invests in innovation and efficiency.
**Explanation**: Trade-off between higher prices and potential benefits. A common error is saying monopoly is always bad without considering economies of scale and innovation.
**Tags**: monopoly, evaluation, natural-monopoly, consumer-welfare

### Question Q-3.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the efficiency of perfect competition and monopoly in terms of allocative and productive efficiency.
**Answer**: Perfect competition achieves allocative efficiency (P = MC, meaning resources are allocated to what consumers value most) and productive efficiency (producing at minimum average cost). Monopoly fails both tests: price exceeds marginal cost (P > MC), creating deadweight loss, and the monopolist has no competitive pressure to minimise costs. However, monopoly may achieve dynamic efficiency through innovation funded by supernormal profits, potentially benefiting consumers in the long run. The comparison depends on the time horizon: perfect competition is statically more efficient, but monopoly may deliver dynamic gains.
**Explanation**: PC = allocatively and productively efficient. Monopoly = inefficient statically but possibly innovative. A common error is ignoring dynamic efficiency.
**Tags**: efficiency, perfect-competition, monopoly, allocative, productive, dynamic

### Question Q-3.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Discuss whether oligopoly is always worse for consumers than monopolistic competition.
**Answer**: Oligopoly may be worse because fewer firms dominate, leading to higher prices, potential collusion (explicit or tacit), and reduced consumer choice. Firms may engage in non-price competition that wastes resources. However, oligopolists may achieve significant economies of scale, invest heavily in R&D, and offer high-quality products due to reputational concerns. Monopolistic competition offers more choice and lower barriers to entry, but firms operate with excess capacity (not productively efficient) and differentiation may be superficial. The answer depends on whether oligopolists collude or compete: a competitive oligopoly may offer better outcomes than monopolistic competition in capital-intensive industries.
**Explanation**: Both structures have trade-offs for consumers. A common error is assuming fewer firms always means worse outcomes without considering scale and innovation.
**Tags**: oligopoly, monopolistic-competition, consumer-welfare, evaluation, comparison
