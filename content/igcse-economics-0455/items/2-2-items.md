### Question Q-2.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define demand.
**Answer**: Demand is the quantity of a good that consumers are willing and able to buy at a given price, during a given time period.
**Explanation**: Willing AND able. A common error is only wanting something (without ability to pay).
**Tags**: demand, definition

### Question Q-2.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: State the law of demand.
**Answer**: As the price of a good rises, the quantity demanded falls, ceteris paribus (all other things being equal). There is an inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded.
**Explanation**: Inverse relationship. A common error is saying demand falls when price falls.
**Tags**: law-of-demand, inverse-relationship

### Question Q-2.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Distinguish between a change in demand and a change in quantity demanded.
**Answer**: Change in quantity demanded: movement along the demand curve caused by a price change. Change in demand: a shift of the entire demand curve caused by non-price factors (income, tastes, etc.).
**Explanation**: Movement along vs shift of curve. A common error is confusing the two.
**Tags**: demand, quantity-demanded, shift

### Question Q-2.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does an increase in consumer income affect the demand for a normal good?
**Answer**: Demand for a normal good increases when income rises — the demand curve shifts to the right.
**Explanation**: Normal good: income up → demand up. A common error is saying demand decreases.
**Tags**: demand, income, normal-good

### Question Q-2.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: If the price of coffee rises, what happens to the demand for tea? Explain.
**Answer**: Demand for tea increases — coffee and tea are substitutes. When coffee becomes more expensive, consumers switch to tea.
**Explanation**: Substitutes: price of one up → demand for the other up. A common error is saying demand for tea falls.
**Tags**: demand, substitutes, cross-price

### Question Q-2.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why does the demand curve slope downward?
**Answer**: 1) Substitution effect: as price falls, consumers switch to this good from alternatives. 2) Income effect: as price falls, consumers can afford more with the same income.
**Explanation**: Substitution and income effects. A common error is only giving one reason.
**Tags**: demand, downward-slope, substitution-effect, income-effect

### Question Q-2.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Analyse how advertising affects the demand for a product.
**Answer**: Successful advertising increases consumer awareness and desire for the product, shifting the demand curve to the right. It may also make demand more inelastic (less responsive to price changes) by building brand loyalty.
**Explanation**: Advertising shifts demand right and may affect elasticity. A common error is only saying 'more people buy it.'
**Tags**: demand, advertising, shift, elasticity

### Question Q-2.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether an increase in consumer income always leads to an increase in demand.
**Answer**: For normal goods, higher income increases demand (rightward shift) as consumers buy more at every price. However, for inferior goods, higher income reduces demand as consumers switch to superior alternatives — e.g. switching from bus travel to car ownership, or from generic brands to premium brands. The proportion of income spent also matters: as incomes rise, the share spent on basic necessities may fall (Engel's law). Additionally, higher income may increase savings rather than spending if consumer confidence is low. Evaluation: the effect of income on demand depends on the type of good, consumer preferences, and the broader economic context.
**Explanation**: Requires distinguishing normal and inferior goods and considering broader factors. A common error is assuming all goods see higher demand when income rises.
**Tags**: demand, income, normal-good, inferior-good, evaluation

### Question Q-2.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: To what extent are non-price determinants of demand more important than price in influencing consumer purchasing decisions?
**Answer**: Price is fundamental: the law of demand states that lower prices increase quantity demanded, and price changes cause movements along the demand curve. However, non-price determinants can be equally or more influential: income changes shift demand significantly (e.g. rising incomes drive demand for electronics); changes in tastes and fashion can dramatically increase or destroy demand regardless of price (e.g. smartphone adoption); prices of related goods matter (e.g. petrol price affects car demand); population changes alter market size; consumer expectations about future prices can trigger panic buying or deferred purchases. Evaluation: for everyday goods, price tends to dominate short-term decisions. For durable goods and luxury items, non-price factors such as income, trends, and expectations often have a greater impact. Both sets of determinants interact in practice.
**Explanation**: Requires comparing price and non-price factors with examples and reaching a reasoned judgment. A common error is listing factors without evaluating their relative importance.
**Tags**: demand, determinants, price, non-price-factors, evaluation
