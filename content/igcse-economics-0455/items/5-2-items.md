### Question Q-5.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the Human Development Index (HDI)?
**Answer**: The HDI is a composite measure of development based on three dimensions: life expectancy at birth (health), education (mean years of schooling and expected years of schooling), and GNI per capita adjusted for purchasing power parity (standard of living).
**Explanation**: Health + education + income are the three dimensions. A common error is thinking the HDI measures only income.
**Tags**: hdi, definition, dimensions

### Question Q-5.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What does a high HDI value indicate about a country?
**Answer**: A high HDI value (close to 1.0) indicates that a country has high levels of health (long life expectancy), education (many years of schooling), and income (high GNI per capita). A low HDI value (close to 0) indicates poor development across these dimensions.
**Explanation**: The HDI scale runs from 0 to 1. A common error is interpreting HDI as a percentage rather than an index value.
**Tags**: hdi, interpretation, scale

### Question Q-5.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is GNI per capita and how does it differ from GDP per capita?
**Answer**: GNI (Gross National Income) per capita is total income earned by a country's residents divided by the population. It includes income from abroad (such as remittances and profits from overseas investments). GDP per capita measures the value of goods and services produced within a country's borders regardless of who owns the factors of production. GNI is used in the HDI because it better reflects the income actually available to a country's citizens.
**Explanation**: GNI includes net income from abroad; GDP does not. A common error is using GDP and GNI interchangeably without recognising the distinction.
**Tags**: gni-per-capita, gdp, definition

### Question Q-5.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why the HDI is considered a better measure of development than GDP per capita alone.
**Answer**: The HDI is multi-dimensional — it includes health (life expectancy) and education (schooling) alongside income (GNI per capita). GDP per capita only measures average economic output and ignores how income is distributed and whether people are healthy or educated. Two countries with the same GDP per capita can have very different HDI scores if one invests more in healthcare and schools, making the HDI a more complete picture of human well-being.
**Explanation**: Multi-dimensional vs single dimension. A common error is claiming GDP per capita fully captures development.
**Tags**: hdi, gdp, comparison, multi-dimensional

### Question Q-5.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Identify four limitations of the HDI as a measure of development.
**Answer**: (1) It does not measure inequality within a country — averages may hide large disparities between rich and poor. (2) It ignores environmental quality and sustainability. (3) It does not account for political freedom, human rights, or gender equality. (4) It excludes non-market activities such as unpaid care work and subsistence farming, which are important in many developing countries.
**Explanation**: The HDI is useful but incomplete. A common error is treating the HDI as a perfect or comprehensive measure of all aspects of development.
**Tags**: hdi, limitations, inequality, environment

### Question Q-5.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how life expectancy is used as a development indicator and what it reveals about a country.
**Answer**: Life expectancy at birth measures the average number of years a newborn is expected to live. It serves as a proxy for the health dimension of the HDI. A higher life expectancy indicates better healthcare, nutrition, clean water, sanitation, and overall living conditions. Countries with low life expectancy typically face problems such as high infant mortality, infectious diseases, and poor access to medical services.
**Explanation**: Life expectancy is a proxy for overall health and living conditions. A common error is assuming life expectancy measures only medical quality.
**Tags**: life-expectancy, health, indicator

### Question Q-5.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the usefulness of composite development indices such as the HDI compared to single indicators such as literacy rate or infant mortality rate.
**Answer**: Composite indices like the HDI provide a broader, multi-dimensional picture of development by combining health, education, and income into a single figure, making cross-country comparisons straightforward. However, they may mask important details — a moderate HDI could result from good health but poor education, which a single indicator would reveal more clearly. Single indicators such as literacy rate or infant mortality rate are specific and highlight particular problems, but alone they give an incomplete picture. In practice, the best approach is to use composite indices for an overall ranking alongside single indicators to identify specific areas of concern, such as gender gaps or regional disparities.
**Explanation**: Composite indices offer breadth; single indicators offer depth. A common error is arguing one approach is definitively superior without recognising their complementary strengths.
**Tags**: development-indicators, composite, evaluation, comparison

### Question Q-5.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Analyse why two countries with similar levels of GNI per capita may have very different HDI rankings.
**Answer**: The HDI incorporates health and education alongside income. Two countries may have similar GNI per capita but differ significantly in life expectancy and schooling. For example, a country that invests heavily in public healthcare and universal education (such as Sri Lanka) can achieve a higher HDI than a country with similar income but weak social services. Differences in government spending priorities, the efficiency of healthcare systems, cultural attitudes to education (especially for girls), and the prevalence of diseases all affect health and education outcomes independently of income. Additionally, GNI per capita is an average — if income is more equally distributed, more people can access health and education services, boosting the HDI.
**Explanation**: GNI per capita is only one component of the HDI; health and education outcomes depend on policy choices, not just income. A common error is assuming GNI alone determines HDI ranking.
**Tags**: gni, hdi, analysis, policy

### Question Q-5.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: To what extent does the HDI provide an accurate and fair measure of development for all countries? Discuss with reference to its strengths and weaknesses.
**Answer**: The HDI has important strengths: it moves beyond income alone by including health and education, it enables straightforward international comparison, and it highlights that development is about expanding human capabilities. However, its accuracy and fairness are limited in several ways. It does not capture inequality within countries — the Inequality-adjusted HDI (IHDI) was created to address this. It ignores environmental sustainability, so a country depleting its natural resources may still score highly. It does not measure political freedom, safety, or happiness. The education component (years of schooling) does not capture quality of learning. For the least developed countries, data reliability is often poor, reducing accuracy. Furthermore, the HDI may not reflect the priorities of all cultures equally. In conclusion, the HDI is a valuable starting point for measuring development but should be supplemented with other indicators such as the Gini coefficient, environmental metrics, and the Multidimensional Poverty Index for a fairer, more accurate assessment.
**Explanation**: The HDI is a useful but incomplete measure — its accuracy depends on data quality and its fairness depends on what dimensions are valued. A common error is either fully endorsing or fully dismissing the HDI without balanced analysis.
**Tags**: hdi, evaluation, accuracy, development-measurement
