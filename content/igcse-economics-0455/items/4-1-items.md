### Question Q-4.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What are the three main functions of money?
**Answer**: Medium of exchange (used to buy and sell goods and services), unit of account (provides a common measure of value), and store of value (can be saved and used for future purchases).
**Explanation**: These three functions distinguish money from other assets. A common error is listing 'power' or 'wealth' as a function of money, or omitting one of the three core functions.
**Tags**: money, functions, medium-of-exchange

### Question Q-4.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is fiat money and why does it have value?
**Answer**: Fiat money is currency that has value because the government declares it to be legal tender. It has no intrinsic value (unlike gold or silver coins) and is not backed by a commodity. Its value depends on public confidence in the government and the central bank.
**Explanation**: Fiat money is government-decreed, not commodity-backed. A common error is saying fiat money is backed by gold reserves or has value because of the material it is made from.
**Tags**: fiat-money, legal-tender, definition

### Question Q-4.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain the main roles of a central bank in an economy.
**Answer**: The central bank manages the country's monetary policy by controlling the money supply and setting interest rates. It acts as the government's bank (managing government accounts and debt), serves as the lender of last resort to commercial banks to prevent bank failures, issues banknotes and coins, and regulates the banking system to maintain financial stability.
**Explanation**: The central bank has multiple roles: monetary policy, government's bank, lender of last resort, currency issuer, and regulator. A common error is confusing the central bank with a commercial bank or only mentioning one of its functions.
**Tags**: central-bank, monetary-policy, lender-of-last-resort

### Question Q-4.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how commercial banks create credit and how this affects the money supply.
**Answer**: When a commercial bank makes a loan, it credits the borrower's account with new deposits. The borrower spends this money, and the recipient deposits it in another bank. That bank keeps a fraction as reserves and lends the rest, creating further deposits. This money multiplier process means the total money supply expands beyond the original deposit. For example, with a 10% reserve ratio, an initial deposit of $100 can theoretically create up to $1,000 in total deposits.
**Explanation**: Lending creates new deposits, which are re-lent, multiplying the money supply. A common error is thinking banks simply lend out existing deposits without creating new money in the process.
**Tags**: credit-creation, money-multiplier, commercial-bank

### Question Q-4.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Distinguish between a commercial bank and a central bank.
**Answer**: A commercial bank is a profit-seeking institution that accepts deposits from and provides loans to individuals and businesses. It offers services such as current accounts, savings accounts, mortgages, and credit cards. A central bank is a government institution that manages monetary policy, regulates the banking system, issues currency, acts as the government's bank, and serves as the lender of last resort. The central bank does not deal directly with individual customers.
**Explanation**: Commercial banks are profit-oriented and serve the public; the central bank is policy-oriented and serves the government and other banks. A common error is saying both types of bank serve individual customers or both aim to make profit.
**Tags**: commercial-bank, central-bank, distinction

### Question Q-4.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why confidence is essential to the stability of a banking system.
**Answer**: Commercial banks operate a fractional reserve system, meaning they keep only a small proportion of deposits as cash reserves and lend out the rest. If depositors lose confidence and attempt to withdraw their money simultaneously (a bank run), the bank cannot meet all withdrawal demands because the funds are tied up in loans. This can cause the bank to collapse and may spread to other banks through a contagion effect, threatening the entire financial system.
**Explanation**: Fractional reserve banking means banks are inherently vulnerable to loss of confidence. A common error is saying banks keep all depositors' money in reserve and can always meet withdrawal demands.
**Tags**: banking, confidence, bank-run, fractional-reserve

### Question Q-4.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the impact of digital banking on the financial system.
**Answer**: Digital banking has significantly increased access to financial services, especially in developing countries where mobile banking reaches unbanked populations. It reduces operating costs for banks (fewer branches needed), speeds up transactions, and provides consumers with 24/7 convenience. However, it creates cybersecurity risks such as hacking and fraud, may exclude elderly or low-income groups who lack internet access or digital literacy, and reduces personal relationships between banks and customers, potentially weakening trust. Additionally, digital banking enables faster bank runs as deposits can be withdrawn instantly online. On balance, digital banking has improved efficiency and inclusion, but regulators must ensure adequate consumer protection and cybersecurity standards.
**Explanation**: Weigh benefits (access, cost, speed, inclusion) against risks (cyber threats, digital exclusion, faster bank runs, reduced trust). A common error is focusing only on convenience while ignoring systemic risks or excluding evaluation of who loses out.
**Tags**: digital-banking, financial-system, evaluation, cybersecurity

### Question Q-4.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Discuss whether a move to a completely cashless society would benefit all members of an economy.
**Answer**: A cashless society could reduce the costs of printing, storing, and transporting physical currency, make transactions faster and more traceable (reducing tax evasion and illegal activity), and improve convenience for consumers through digital payments. However, it would disadvantage groups without access to bank accounts or smartphones, particularly the elderly, low-income earners, and rural populations. Small vendors who rely on cash transactions may face higher costs from payment processing fees. Privacy concerns arise as all transactions become electronically recorded. The loss of cash as a backup during system outages or cyberattacks creates vulnerability. Overall, while a cashless society offers efficiency gains, it would not benefit all members equally — policy measures such as financial inclusion programmes and maintaining some cash infrastructure would be needed to protect vulnerable groups.
**Explanation**: Consider efficiency gains against exclusion, privacy loss, and vulnerability. A common error is assuming everyone has equal access to digital payment technology or ignoring the impact on specific groups such as the elderly or informal sector workers.
**Tags**: cashless-society, financial-inclusion, evaluation, digital-payments

### Question Q-4.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: To what extent should commercial banks be allowed to create credit without government restrictions?
**Answer**: Allowing banks freedom to create credit promotes economic growth by enabling businesses to invest and consumers to spend. Competition among banks can lead to lower interest rates and more innovative financial products. However, unrestricted credit creation can lead to excessive lending, creating asset bubbles (such as housing booms) and financial instability. The 2008 financial crisis demonstrated how excessive credit creation in mortgage markets can trigger a global economic downturn. Banks may prioritise short-term profit over long-term stability, lending to risky borrowers. Government restrictions such as reserve requirements, capital adequacy ratios, and lending limits are necessary to prevent systemic risk. In conclusion, some degree of regulation is essential — completely unrestricted credit creation is dangerous, but over-regulation may stifle economic growth. A balanced approach with prudential regulation is most effective.
**Explanation**: Weigh economic growth benefits against financial stability risks. Reference to real-world examples (2008 crisis) strengthens the evaluation. A common error is arguing for either complete freedom or complete control without considering the trade-off.
**Tags**: credit-creation, banking-regulation, evaluation, financial-stability
