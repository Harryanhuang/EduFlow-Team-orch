### Question Q-6.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define globalisation.
**Answer**: Globalisation is the increasing integration and interdependence of national economies through cross-border trade, foreign direct investment, movement of labour and capital, and the transfer of technology and ideas.
**Explanation**: Increasing economic integration across countries. A common error is defining globalisation as only international trade.
**Tags**: globalisation, definition

### Question Q-6.4-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What role do multinational corporations (MNCs) play in globalisation?
**Answer**: MNCs operate in multiple countries, investing, producing, and trading across borders. They drive globalisation by transferring technology and management practices, creating jobs in host countries, linking economies through global supply chains, and increasing cross-border flows of goods, services, and capital.
**Explanation**: MNCs are key agents of economic integration. A common error is assuming MNCs only exploit host countries without providing any benefits.
**Tags**: mncs, globalisation, role

### Question Q-6.4-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the World Trade Organization (WTO) and what is its role in the global economy?
**Answer**: The WTO is an international organisation that promotes free trade among its member countries. Its main roles include: negotiating multilateral trade agreements to reduce tariffs and other trade barriers; providing a forum for resolving trade disputes between member countries; monitoring national trade policies for compliance with WTO rules; and offering technical assistance to developing countries to help them participate in global trade.
**Explanation**: The WTO facilitates and regulates international trade. A common error is saying the WTO sets exchange rates or controls trade volumes.
**Tags**: wto, role, free-trade

### Question Q-6.4-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How has technology contributed to the acceleration of globalisation?
**Answer**: Advances in technology have dramatically reduced the cost and increased the speed of international economic activity. The internet and digital communication enable instant coordination across global supply chains and support e-commerce across borders. Container shipping and improved logistics have slashed transport costs for goods. Financial technology allows rapid cross-border capital flows and investment. These innovations have made it feasible for firms of all sizes to operate internationally, not just large MNCs.
**Explanation**: Technology reduces costs and increases speed of trade, investment, and information flow. A common error is only mentioning the internet without considering transport and financial technology.
**Tags**: technology, globalisation, communication, transport

### Question Q-6.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a trade bloc? Give one example and explain how it promotes trade among its members.
**Answer**: A trade bloc is a group of countries that agree to reduce or remove trade barriers (tariffs and quotas) on goods and services traded among themselves. Example: the European Union (EU) is a single market where goods, services, capital, and labour move freely between member states. Trade blocs promote trade by lowering costs for intra-bloc trade, creating larger markets that attract investment, and encouraging specialisation based on comparative advantage within the bloc.
**Explanation**: Regional trade agreements reduce internal barriers. A common error is confusing a trade bloc with the WTO, which operates at the global level.
**Tags**: trade-bloc, example, regional-trade

### Question Q-6.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What are the main benefits of globalisation for developing countries?
**Answer**: Key benefits include: access to larger export markets, which allows developing countries to grow beyond the limits of their domestic demand; foreign direct investment that brings capital, technology, and management skills; job creation as MNCs establish operations; technology transfer that accelerates industrial development; lower prices for consumers due to increased import competition; and the opportunity to learn from more advanced economies. Countries such as China and Vietnam have used globalisation to lift millions out of poverty.
**Explanation**: Markets, investment, technology, and jobs flow into developing economies. A common error is focusing only on the costs of globalisation while ignoring its benefits.
**Tags**: globalisation, benefits, developing-countries

### Question Q-6.4-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether globalisation has reduced or increased global inequality.
**Answer**: Between countries: globalisation has reduced inequality, as developing countries (particularly China, India, and Vietnam) have grown faster than developed countries by integrating into the global economy, narrowing the income gap. Within countries: globalisation may have increased inequality, as skilled workers and capital owners benefit disproportionately from expanded trade and investment, while unskilled workers in developed countries face job losses from import competition and in developing countries may face low wages and poor conditions. The overall effect is mixed: global inequality measured across all individuals has likely declined due to rapid growth in large developing countries, but inequality within many individual nations has risen. The extent of inequality also depends on government policies such as education, taxation, and social safety nets that can redistribute the gains from globalisation.
**Explanation**: Between-country inequality has fallen but within-country inequality may have risen. A common error is taking a one-sided view without distinguishing between different measures of inequality.
**Tags**: globalisation, inequality, evaluation

### Question Q-6.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Discuss whether the growth of multinational corporations has been more beneficial or harmful to developing countries.
**Answer**: Benefits: MNCs bring foreign direct investment, create employment, transfer technology and management expertise, develop local supply chains, and increase host countries' export capacity. They may also raise labour standards and introduce environmental practices that exceed local norms. Examples include technology transfer by firms like Samsung in Southeast Asia. Harms: MNCs may exploit low wages and weak labour laws, repatriate profits rather than reinvest locally, crowd out domestic firms that cannot compete with their scale and resources, and influence government policy in their favour. Environmental damage from MNC operations (such as oil extraction in the Niger Delta) can impose costs on local communities. Some MNCs use transfer pricing to minimise tax payments in host countries. On balance, the net impact depends on the host country's regulatory framework, the bargaining power of its government, and whether it has policies to maximise spillover benefits while limiting exploitation.
**Explanation**: MNCs bring investment and jobs but may exploit and crowd out local firms. A common error is viewing MNCs as purely beneficial or purely harmful.
**Tags**: mncs, developing-countries, evaluation, globalisation

### Question Q-6.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the effectiveness of the WTO in promoting free trade among its member countries.
**Answer**: Achievements: the WTO has successfully reduced average tariff levels worldwide through successive rounds of negotiation; its dispute settlement mechanism has resolved hundreds of trade conflicts, providing a rules-based alternative to unilateral trade action; it has extended trade rules to new areas such as services and intellectual property; and its special provisions help developing countries participate in global trade. Limitations: negotiations have stalled on key issues (the Doha Round has made little progress since 2001), reflecting disagreements between developed and developing countries over agricultural subsidies and market access; enforcement relies on member compliance, and powerful countries sometimes ignore WTO rulings; the organisation has been criticised for favouring developed countries' interests; and the rise of bilateral and regional trade agreements undermines the multilateral system. Overall, the WTO has been effective in establishing a framework for rules-based trade but faces growing challenges from protectionist sentiment, geopolitical tensions, and the difficulty of reaching consensus among 160+ diverse member countries.
**Explanation**: The WTO has significant achievements but faces structural and political limitations. A common error is dismissing the WTO entirely or overstating its power without acknowledging constraints.
**Tags**: wto, evaluation, free-trade, effectiveness
