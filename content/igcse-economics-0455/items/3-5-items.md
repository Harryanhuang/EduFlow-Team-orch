### Question Q-3.5-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define market failure.
**Answer**: Market failure occurs when the free market mechanism fails to allocate resources efficiently, resulting in a net welfare loss to society. It means the market produces too much or too little of a good.
**Explanation**: Inefficient resource allocation by the market. A common error is saying market failure means the market has collapsed or ceased to function.
**Tags**: market-failure, definition

### Question Q-3.5-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is an externality? Give one example.
**Answer**: An externality is a cost or benefit of an economic transaction that affects a third party who is not directly involved in the transaction. Example: air pollution from a factory is a negative externality affecting nearby residents.
**Explanation**: Third-party effect outside the transaction. A common error is confusing an externality with a cost or benefit experienced by the buyer or seller themselves.
**Tags**: externality, definition, third-party

### Question Q-3.5-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why a negative externality leads to market failure.
**Answer**: When a negative externality exists, the social cost of production exceeds the private cost. Producers only consider their private costs, so the market produces more than the socially optimal quantity. This overproduction creates a welfare loss because the additional units produced impose costs on third parties that exceed the benefits to consumers.
**Explanation**: Social cost > private cost leads to overproduction and welfare loss. A common error is saying negative externalities cause underproduction.
**Tags**: negative-externality, market-failure, overproduction, welfare-loss

### Question Q-3.5-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a public good? Give an example and explain why the free market will not provide it.
**Answer**: A public good is non-excludable (you cannot prevent people from using it) and non-rivalrous (one person's use does not reduce availability for others). Example: street lighting. The free market will not provide it because of the free rider problem: people can benefit without paying, so no private firm can charge users and make a profit.
**Explanation**: Non-excludable + non-rivalrous creates the free rider problem. A common error is calling any government-provided good a public good.
**Tags**: public-good, non-excludable, non-rivalrous, free-rider

### Question Q-3.5-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the difference between a public good and a merit good?
**Answer**: A public good is non-excludable and non-rivalrous, meaning the private market will not provide it at all. A merit good (such as education or healthcare) is a good that is underconsumed in a free market because consumers undervalue its long-term benefits and it generates positive externalities. Merit goods can be provided by the private sector but are consumed below the socially optimal level.
**Explanation**: Public = non-excludable + non-rivalrous (market fails to provide). Merit = underconsumed due to information failure (market underprovides). A common error is treating them as the same thing.
**Tags**: public-good, merit-good, distinction

### Question Q-3.5-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain the tragedy of the commons with an example.
**Answer**: The tragedy of the commons occurs when a shared resource is overused because each individual acts in their own self-interest, ignoring the negative externality imposed on others. Example: overfishing in international waters. Each fisher has an incentive to catch as many fish as possible, but collectively this depletes the fish stock for everyone, leading to long-term resource destruction.
**Explanation**: Shared resource leads to overuse and depletion. A common error is thinking that private ownership causes the tragedy rather than the absence of property rights.
**Tags**: tragedy-of-the-commons, shared-resources, overexploitation

### Question Q-3.5-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Analyse how pollution from a factory creates market failure and evaluate whether a tax on the factory is the best solution.
**Answer**: Pollution is a negative externality where the social cost of production exceeds the private cost, leading to overproduction and a deadweight welfare loss. A tax on the factory (Pigouvian tax) can internalise the externality by raising the firm's private costs to equal social costs, reducing output to the socially optimal level. However, a tax has limitations: it is difficult to set at the correct level (requires knowing the exact external cost), it may not reduce pollution if demand is very inelastic, and it may make the firm uncompetitive internationally. Alternatives include regulation (setting emission limits), tradable permits (capping total pollution), or Coase-style bargaining. The best solution depends on the accuracy of information available and the administrative capacity of the government.
**Explanation**: External cost leads to overproduction. Tax internalises the externality but is hard to set correctly. A common error is assuming a tax completely eliminates the externality.
**Tags**: pollution, externality, tax, evaluation, market-failure

### Question Q-3.5-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether education should be provided free by the government.
**Answer**: Education is a merit good with significant positive externalities: a more educated workforce increases productivity, reduces crime, and promotes innovation. Free provision increases consumption, especially among low-income groups, improving equity and social mobility. However, free government provision requires substantial tax funding, may be less efficient than private provision due to lack of competition, and may not respond quickly to changing labour market needs. A mixed approach, where the government funds education but allows both public and private providers, may achieve better outcomes. The case for free provision is strongest at primary and secondary levels where the external benefits are greatest.
**Explanation**: Merit good with positive externalities supports government provision, but costs and efficiency concerns remain. A common error is saying education should always be free without considering the trade-offs.
**Tags**: education, merit-good, government-provision, evaluation, externalities

### Question Q-3.5-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: To what extent is the free market able to solve the problem of negative externalities without government intervention?
**Answer**: The free market can partially address negative externalities through the Coase theorem, where affected parties negotiate compensation if property rights are clearly defined and transaction costs are low. Firms may also self-regulate to protect their reputation or avoid future regulation. Consumer pressure and ethical behaviour can encourage firms to reduce externalities voluntarily. However, the free market is generally unable to fully solve the problem because property rights are often unclear (e.g. air and water), transaction costs are high when many parties are affected, and firms prioritise profit over social welfare. Free riding undermines voluntary agreements. In most cases, some form of government intervention (taxes, regulation, or permits) is necessary to achieve the socially optimal outcome.
**Explanation**: Market solutions have limited effectiveness for negative externalities. A common error is claiming the market can fully solve externality problems through negotiation alone.
**Tags**: negative-externality, free-market, coase-theorem, government-intervention, evaluation
