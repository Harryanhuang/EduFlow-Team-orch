### Question Q-1.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define labour as a factor of production.
**Answer**: Labour is the physical and mental effort of people used in the production of goods and services. It includes all workers from manual labourers to professionals such as doctors and engineers.
**Explanation**: Labour = human effort, both physical and mental. A common error is restricting the definition to physical/manual work only.
**Tags**: labour, definition, factors-of-production

### Question Q-1.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Give three examples of natural resources classified as land in economics.
**Answer**: Oil reserves, fertile soil, and forests are examples of land. Other examples include rivers, mineral deposits, and fishing grounds. In economics, land refers to all natural resources available for production, not just the physical area of ground.
**Explanation**: Land includes all natural resources, not just physical land area. A common error is thinking land means only the surface area of a country.
**Tags**: land, natural-resources, factors-of-production

### Question Q-1.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Distinguish between physical capital and human capital.
**Answer**: Physical capital refers to man-made aids to production such as machinery, buildings, tools, and equipment. Human capital refers to the skills, knowledge, and experience of workers, gained through education, training, and on-the-job experience. Both increase productivity, but physical capital is tangible while human capital is embodied in people.
**Explanation**: Physical = machines/buildings. Human = skills/knowledge. A common error is treating education spending as physical capital investment rather than human capital investment.
**Tags**: capital, physical-capital, human-capital, distinction

### Question Q-1.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why is enterprise considered a special factor of production?
**Answer**: Enterprise organises the other three factors (land, labour, and capital), takes risks, and makes strategic decisions about production. Without enterprise, land, labour, and capital would not be combined effectively to produce goods and services. The entrepreneur bears the risk of business failure and is rewarded with profit if successful.
**Explanation**: Enterprise is the organising and risk-bearing factor. A common error is treating it as just another type of labour rather than recognising its unique coordinating role.
**Tags**: enterprise, organisation, risk, factors-of-production

### Question Q-1.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how investment in education increases the quality of labour.
**Answer**: Education improves workers' skills, knowledge, and productivity. Better-educated workers can perform more complex tasks, adapt to new technologies more quickly, and contribute more to economic growth. This increases the quality (not quantity) of labour — each worker becomes more productive, which is an investment in human capital.
**Explanation**: Education improves skills, which raises productivity and human capital. A common error is saying education increases the quantity of labour rather than its quality.
**Tags**: education, human-capital, productivity, labour-quality

### Question Q-1.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why is capital not the same as money in economics?
**Answer**: Capital refers to physical assets used in the production of goods and services (machinery, factories, tools, equipment). Money is a medium of exchange used to buy goods and services. Money itself does not produce anything — it is financial capital used to purchase physical capital. A bank deposit is financial capital; the factory it finances is physical capital.
**Explanation**: Capital = productive physical assets. Money = financial instrument/medium of exchange. A common error is treating bank accounts or cash as capital in the economic sense.
**Tags**: capital, money, distinction, financial-capital

### Question Q-1.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether investment in human capital or physical capital is more important for a developing country's economic growth.
**Answer**: Physical capital (machinery, infrastructure) provides immediate productive capacity and is essential for industrialisation. However, without skilled workers (human capital), machines may be underutilised or poorly maintained. Human capital generates innovation, adapts technology, and has spillover effects — educated workers train others and drive entrepreneurship. Physical capital depreciates and requires replacement, while human capital accumulates over time. For a developing country, a balanced approach is needed, but human capital may be more critical long-term because skilled workers can attract foreign investment and create technology, whereas machines without skilled operators have limited value.
**Explanation**: Both types of capital are important and complementary. A common error is arguing one is universally superior without considering how they interact, or ignoring depreciation and maintenance needs.
**Tags**: human-capital, physical-capital, developing-countries, evaluation, economic-growth

### Question Q-1.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate which factor of production is most important for economic development in a low-income country.
**Answer**: Enterprise is often considered most important because it organises the other factors, drives innovation, and creates employment. However, all four factors are interdependent — without sufficient capital, enterprise cannot scale production; without skilled labour, technology cannot be adopted; without natural resources (land), many industries cannot start. In practice, the most critical factor varies by country: resource-rich countries may need enterprise and capital most, while labour-abundant countries may need capital and enterprise to employ their workforce productively. No single factor is sufficient — development requires balanced improvement across all four.
**Explanation**: Enterprise drives organisation and innovation but is interdependent with other factors. A common error is saying one factor alone is sufficient for development without acknowledging the complementary nature of all four factors.
**Tags**: enterprise, development, evaluation, low-income-countries, interdependence

### Question Q-1.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: To what extent can technological advancement replace the need for enterprise as a factor of production? Justify your answer.
**Answer**: Technology can automate routine decision-making (AI-driven supply chains, automated quality control) and reduce the need for human judgement in some production processes. However, enterprise involves more than operational management — it includes identifying market opportunities, taking risks on new ventures, and innovating new products. Technology itself is created and deployed through entrepreneurial decisions. The initial vision to use technology, the risk of investing in unproven systems, and the strategic direction of a business all require enterprise. While technology can supplement enterprise in routine functions, it cannot replace the creative, risk-bearing, and strategic roles of the entrepreneur.
**Explanation**: Technology automates tasks but enterprise provides vision, risk-taking, and strategic direction. A common error is assuming that because technology handles many decisions, enterprise is no longer needed.
**Tags**: technology, enterprise, automation, evaluation, innovation
