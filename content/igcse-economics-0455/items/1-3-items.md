### Question Q-1.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define opportunity cost.
**Answer**: Opportunity cost is the value of the next best alternative forgone when a choice is made. It is what you give up to get something else — specifically the single most valuable alternative that was not chosen.
**Explanation**: Only the next best alternative counts, not all alternatives combined. A common error is adding up all alternatives instead of identifying just the next best one.
**Tags**: opportunity-cost, definition

### Question Q-1.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A student chooses to study economics instead of history. What is the opportunity cost?
**Answer**: The opportunity cost is the benefit the student would have gained from studying history — the next best alternative. It is NOT the combined benefit of all other subjects the student could have studied.
**Explanation**: Only the next best alternative counts as the opportunity cost. A common error is listing all subjects not chosen and adding their benefits together.
**Tags**: opportunity-cost, education, next-best-alternative

### Question Q-1.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A government spends $5 billion on building a new highway. What is the opportunity cost?
**Answer**: The opportunity cost is the next best use of that $5 billion — for example, building hospitals or schools that could have been funded instead. It is NOT all other possible uses combined, only the single most valuable alternative that would have been chosen if the highway had not been built.
**Explanation**: Only the next best alternative is the opportunity cost. A common error is listing all possible spending options and summing their values.
**Tags**: opportunity-cost, government-spending, trade-off

### Question Q-1.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why does a free concert have an opportunity cost even though no money is charged for entry?
**Answer**: The time spent attending the concert could have been used for something else (working, studying, resting). The opportunity cost is the value of the next best use of that time. Even though no money is paid, scarce resources (time, the venue, performers' effort) are used and could have been deployed elsewhere.
**Explanation**: Time is a scarce resource with opportunity cost. A common error is saying there is no cost because no money is paid — confusing monetary cost with opportunity cost.
**Tags**: opportunity-cost, time, implicit-cost, scarce-resources

### Question Q-1.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A farmer can grow wheat or barley on the same land. If the price of wheat rises, what happens to the opportunity cost of growing barley?
**Answer**: The opportunity cost of growing barley increases. By choosing barley, the farmer is now giving up more revenue from wheat (which is more valuable after the price rise). The opportunity cost is measured in terms of the value of the forgone alternative, so when that alternative becomes more valuable, the opportunity cost rises.
**Explanation**: Higher wheat price means higher revenue forgone when choosing barley instead. A common error is saying the opportunity cost falls or stays the same because the physical output has not changed.
**Tags**: opportunity-cost, price-change, agriculture, trade-off

### Question Q-1.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Country A can produce 10 tonnes of rice or 5 tonnes of wheat on the same land. Calculate the opportunity cost of each good.
**Answer**: Opportunity cost of 1 tonne of rice = 5/10 = 0.5 tonnes of wheat. Opportunity cost of 1 tonne of wheat = 10/5 = 2 tonnes of rice. To find opportunity cost, divide what is given up by what is gained.
**Explanation**: OC of rice = wheat forgone / rice gained = 5/10 = 0.5. OC of wheat = rice forgone / wheat gained = 10/5 = 2. A common error is mixing up which quantity goes in the numerator and denominator.
**Tags**: opportunity-cost, calculation, production, trade-off

### Question Q-1.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A company has $1 million to invest. Option A gives 8% return, Option B gives 12%, and Option C gives 6%. If the company chooses Option B, identify the opportunity cost and evaluate whether financial return should be the only factor considered in this investment decision.
**Answer**: The opportunity cost is Option A's 8% return — the next best alternative after B's 12%. It is NOT A + C (14%). However, financial return should not be the only consideration. The company should also evaluate risk (Option B may be riskier), time horizon (returns may vary over time), strategic fit (some options may align better with long-term goals), and non-financial factors such as environmental impact or regulatory requirements. A lower-return but lower-risk option might be preferable in uncertain economic conditions.
**Explanation**: Only the next best alternative counts as opportunity cost (8%, not 14%). A common error is adding up all other returns. Additionally, focusing only on returns ignores risk, timing, and strategic considerations.
**Tags**: opportunity-cost, investment, next-best-alternative, evaluation, risk

### Question Q-1.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the usefulness of opportunity cost analysis when a government must decide between spending on healthcare and defence.
**Answer**: Opportunity cost analysis forces the government to explicitly identify what is sacrificed with each choice — spending on defence means fewer hospitals and doctors, while spending on healthcare means weaker military capability. This helps justify decisions to the public and promotes transparent resource allocation. However, opportunity cost analysis has limitations: some benefits are hard to measure (national security vs. lives saved), the time horizons differ (defence provides immediate security, healthcare has long-term benefits), and political considerations may override economic logic. Additionally, governments may be able to borrow to fund both, temporarily reducing the apparent opportunity cost. Despite these limitations, the concept remains valuable for structured decision-making.
**Explanation**: Opportunity cost clarifies trade-offs but has measurement and political limitations. A common error is ignoring implicit costs like environmental damage, or assuming opportunity cost analysis always leads to the correct decision.
**Tags**: opportunity-cost, government, policy, evaluation, trade-off

### Question Q-1.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the opportunity costs faced by a student choosing between university and full-time work, and a government choosing between building infrastructure and funding welfare programmes. Discuss which decision-maker faces the greater opportunity cost.
**Answer**: The student's opportunity cost of attending university includes forgone wages (potentially 3-4 years of income), plus tuition costs that could have been saved or invested. The benefit is higher future earning potential. The government's opportunity cost of building infrastructure is the immediate welfare support not provided to vulnerable populations — potentially affecting health and living standards now. The student's decision primarily affects one individual's future, while the government's decision affects millions of citizens. The government arguably faces greater opportunity cost because the scale of impact is larger and the consequences of wrong choices (poverty, social unrest) are more severe. However, the student's opportunity cost may represent a larger proportion of their lifetime resources.
**Explanation**: Both face significant trade-offs but at different scales. A common error is comparing only monetary costs without considering the breadth of impact, time horizons, and non-monetary consequences.
**Tags**: opportunity-cost, comparison, individual, government, evaluation
