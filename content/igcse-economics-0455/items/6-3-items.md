### Question Q-6.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is an exchange rate?
**Answer**: An exchange rate is the price of one currency expressed in terms of another currency. For example, if $1 = \u00a30.80, it means one US dollar can be exchanged for 80 British pence.
**Explanation**: Price of one currency in terms of another. A common error is confusing the exchange rate with the interest rate.
**Tags**: exchange-rate, definition

### Question Q-6.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a trade deficit?
**Answer**: A trade deficit occurs when the value of a country's imports of goods and services exceeds the value of its exports over a given period. It is a component of the current account in the balance of payments.
**Explanation**: Imports exceed exports in value. A common error is confusing a trade deficit with a government budget deficit.
**Tags**: trade-deficit, definition, current-account

### Question Q-6.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What causes a currency to appreciate in a floating exchange rate system?
**Answer**: A currency appreciates when demand for it increases or its supply decreases. Key causes include: rising exports (foreigners need the currency to pay for them), higher interest rates attracting foreign investment, increased foreign direct investment, improved economic confidence, and falling imports (less of the currency is sold on foreign exchange markets).
**Explanation**: Higher demand or lower supply for the currency drives up its price. A common error is confusing appreciation with depreciation.
**Tags**: exchange-rate, appreciation, causes

### Question Q-6.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does a currency appreciation affect a country's exports and imports?
**Answer**: Appreciation makes the country's exports more expensive for foreign buyers, so export volumes tend to fall. At the same time, imports become cheaper for domestic consumers, so import volumes tend to rise. This generally worsens the trade balance (current account). Domestic exporters may also see reduced profits as they must either raise foreign prices or absorb the exchange rate loss.
**Explanation**: Exports become dearer abroad, imports become cheaper at home. A common error is reversing these effects.
**Tags**: exchange-rate, appreciation, exports, imports

### Question Q-6.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How can a country correct a persistent balance of payments deficit?
**Answer**: Methods include: (1) Devaluation or depreciation of the currency, which makes exports cheaper and imports more expensive, improving the trade balance. (2) Contractionary fiscal or monetary policy to reduce national income and therefore reduce import spending. (3) Supply-side policies such as education, infrastructure, and R&D investment to improve the competitiveness of domestic industries and boost exports. (4) Trade protection measures, though these may provoke retaliation and are limited by WTO rules.
**Explanation**: Reduce imports or increase exports through exchange rate, demand, or supply-side measures. A common error is suggesting the government simply print more money.
**Tags**: balance-of-payments, deficit, correction

### Question Q-6.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How do changes in interest rates affect the exchange rate?
**Answer**: When a country raises its interest rates relative to other countries, foreign investors seeking higher returns move their savings into that country's banks and financial assets. This increases demand for the domestic currency, causing it to appreciate. Conversely, lower interest rates may lead to capital outflows as investors seek better returns abroad, increasing supply of the currency and causing depreciation.
**Explanation**: Higher interest rates attract foreign capital and increase currency demand. A common error is predicting depreciation when interest rates rise.
**Tags**: interest-rates, exchange-rate, capital-flows

### Question Q-6.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether a strong (appreciated) currency is always beneficial for a country.
**Answer**: Benefits of a strong currency: imports become cheaper, benefiting consumers and firms that rely on imported raw materials; inflation is reduced as import prices fall; the cost of servicing foreign currency debt decreases; and consumers travelling abroad benefit. Drawbacks: exports become more expensive and less competitive in world markets, potentially reducing export volumes and causing job losses in export industries; the current account may worsen if the Marshall-Lerner condition is met; and domestic firms competing with cheap imports may be forced to cut output. Whether a strong currency is beneficial depends on the structure of the economy: countries that are net importers or rely on imported inputs may benefit more, while export-dependent economies may suffer. The time period also matters, as short-term effects may differ from long-term adjustments.
**Explanation**: Trade-off between cheaper imports and less competitive exports. A common error is assuming a strong currency is always advantageous.
**Tags**: exchange-rate, strong-currency, evaluation

### Question Q-6.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Analyse the effects of a sustained depreciation of the currency on a country's balance of payments and domestic economy.
**Answer**: A sustained depreciation makes exports cheaper for foreign buyers and imports more expensive for domestic consumers. In the short run, the current account may initially worsen (the J-curve effect) because import and export volumes are slow to adjust to price changes. Over time, as demand becomes more price elastic, export volumes rise and import volumes fall, improving the current account (Marshall-Lerner condition). Domestically, depreciation raises the cost of imported raw materials and consumer goods, contributing to cost-push inflation. This may force the central bank to raise interest rates, potentially slowing economic growth. However, domestic industries that compete with imports may expand as their products become relatively cheaper, boosting output and employment. The net effect depends on the elasticity of demand for exports and imports, the degree of dependence on imported inputs, and the economy's spare capacity to increase production.
**Explanation**: Depreciation has complex, time-dependent effects on both external and domestic balances. A common error is assuming depreciation always and immediately improves the trade balance.
**Tags**: depreciation, balance-of-payments, J-curve, analysis

### Question Q-6.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Discuss whether a fixed or a floating exchange rate system is more suitable for a developing country.
**Answer**: A fixed exchange rate provides certainty for trade and investment, helps control inflation by anchoring prices, and prevents speculative attacks if the peg is credible. For a developing country with limited financial markets, a fixed rate can provide stability that encourages foreign investment. However, maintaining a fixed rate requires large foreign exchange reserves, limits the use of monetary policy for domestic objectives, and may lead to crises if the peg becomes unsustainable. A floating exchange rate allows the currency to adjust automatically to external shocks, enables independent monetary policy, and does not require large reserves. However, floating rates can be highly volatile in developing countries with thin financial markets, creating uncertainty for trade and investment, and may lead to inflationary spirals if the currency depreciates sharply. Many developing countries adopt managed floats or currency pegs as a compromise, gaining some stability while retaining limited policy flexibility. The best choice depends on the country's trade structure, institutional capacity, and vulnerability to external shocks.
**Explanation**: Each system has distinct trade-offs for developing economies. A common error is recommending one system without considering the specific characteristics and vulnerabilities of the country.
**Tags**: fixed-exchange-rate, floating-exchange-rate, developing-country, evaluation
