### Question Q-3.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define price elasticity of demand (PED).
**Answer**: PED measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in price. PED = % change in quantity demanded / % change in price.
**Explanation**: Responsiveness to price. A common error is using absolute changes instead of percentages.
**Tags**: ped, definition, formula

### Question Q-3.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: If PED = 0.5, is demand elastic or inelastic?
**Answer**: Inelastic (PED < 1). Quantity demanded changes by a smaller percentage than the price change.
**Explanation**: < 1 = inelastic. A common error is saying elastic because 0.5 is a 'large' number.
**Tags**: ped, elastic, inelastic

### Question Q-3.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Calculate PED: price rises from $10 to $12 and quantity demanded falls from 100 to 80.
**Answer**: % change in Q = (80-100)/100 = -20%. % change in P = (12-10)/10 = +20%. PED = -20%/20% = -1 (unit elastic).
**Explanation**: Use percentage changes. A common error is using absolute values: -20/2 = -10.
**Tags**: ped, calculation, unit-elastic

### Question Q-3.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What factors determine whether demand is elastic or inelastic?
**Answer**: 1) Availability of substitutes (more substitutes = more elastic). 2) Necessity vs luxury (necessities = inelastic). 3) Proportion of income spent (large share = more elastic). 4) Time period (longer = more elastic).
**Explanation**: Substitutes, necessity, income share, time. A common error is listing only one factor.
**Tags**: ped, determinants, factors

### Question Q-3.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does PED affect total revenue when price rises?
**Answer**: If demand is inelastic (PED < 1), total revenue increases when price rises. If demand is elastic (PED > 1), total revenue decreases when price rises.
**Explanation**: Inelastic demand + price up = revenue up. Elastic demand + price up = revenue down. A common error is always assuming revenue rises with price.
**Tags**: ped, total-revenue, price-change

### Question Q-3.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: If XED between coffee and tea is positive, what is the relationship between these goods?
**Answer**: They are substitutes. When the price of coffee rises, demand for tea increases. Positive XED indicates substitute goods.
**Explanation**: Positive XED = substitutes. A common error is confusing with complements (which have negative XED).
**Tags**: xed, substitutes, sign

### Question Q-3.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A firm with inelastic demand for its product is considering raising prices. Advise the firm.
**Answer**: Raising prices will increase total revenue because quantity demanded falls by a smaller percentage. However, the firm should consider long-run effects as consumers may find substitutes over time, making demand more elastic. The firm should also assess competitor responses and whether the price rise might damage brand reputation or customer loyalty.
**Explanation**: Price increase leads to revenue increase for inelastic demand, but consider long-run elasticity changes. A common error is ignoring long-run elasticity and competitor reactions.
**Tags**: ped, pricing-strategy, advice, evaluation

### Question Q-3.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: To what extent can a government use knowledge of PED when setting indirect taxes on demerit goods such as cigarettes?
**Answer**: If demand is price inelastic (as with addictive goods like cigarettes), an indirect tax will raise significant tax revenue while only slightly reducing consumption. This is effective for revenue but less effective at changing behaviour. If demand were more elastic, the tax would reduce quantity demanded more substantially. However, inelastic demand means the tax is regressive, disproportionately affecting low-income consumers. The government must weigh revenue generation against equity concerns and consider complementary policies such as education campaigns and advertising restrictions.
**Explanation**: Inelastic demand means tax raises revenue but has limited impact on quantity. A common error is assuming a tax always significantly reduces consumption regardless of elasticity.
**Tags**: ped, indirect-tax, demerit-goods, government-policy, evaluation

### Question Q-3.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether income elasticity of demand (YED) is more useful than cross elasticity of demand (XED) for a retailer deciding on its product range.
**Answer**: YED helps a retailer predict how demand for products will change as consumer incomes rise or fall. Normal goods (positive YED) will see higher demand during economic growth, while inferior goods (negative YED) will see lower demand. XED helps the retailer understand relationships between products, identifying substitutes and complements to optimise pricing and promotions. For a retailer, XED is particularly useful for short-term pricing strategies and shelf placement, while YED is more useful for long-term product planning. Both are valuable, but YED may be more important for strategic decisions about which product categories to expand or reduce, especially during economic cycles.
**Explanation**: Both measures have distinct uses. YED is better for long-term planning linked to economic conditions; XED is better for pricing and promotion strategies. A common error is stating one is always more useful without contextual justification.
**Tags**: yed, xed, retail-strategy, evaluation, comparison
