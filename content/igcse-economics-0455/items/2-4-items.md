### Question Q-2.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is market equilibrium?
**Answer**: Market equilibrium occurs where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. The market clears — there is no excess supply or demand.
**Explanation**: Demand = supply. A common error is saying price = cost.
**Tags**: equilibrium, definition, market-clearing

### Question Q-2.4-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the price mechanism?
**Answer**: The price mechanism is the system where prices are determined by supply and demand, and these prices signal to producers and consumers how to allocate resources.
**Explanation**: Prices coordinate buyers and sellers. A common error is saying the government sets prices.
**Tags**: price-mechanism, definition

### Question Q-2.4-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What happens when demand increases in a market?
**Answer**: The demand curve shifts right. At the original price, there is excess demand. Price rises and quantity traded increases until a new equilibrium is reached.
**Explanation**: Demand up → price up, quantity up. A common error is price down.
**Tags**: equilibrium, demand-increase, price-change

### Question Q-2.4-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What happens when supply decreases in a market?
**Answer**: The supply curve shifts left. At the original price, there is excess demand. Price rises and quantity traded falls until a new equilibrium is reached.
**Explanation**: Supply down → price up, quantity down. A common error is quantity up.
**Tags**: equilibrium, supply-decrease, price-change

### Question Q-2.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does a price ceiling (maximum price) below equilibrium affect the market?
**Answer**: It creates excess demand (shortage). At the ceiling price, quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied. A black market may develop.
**Explanation**: Price ceiling below equilibrium → shortage. A common error is saying it creates surplus.
**Tags**: price-ceiling, shortage, government-intervention

### Question Q-2.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does a price floor (minimum price) above equilibrium affect the market?
**Answer**: It creates excess supply (surplus). At the floor price, quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded. The government may need to buy the surplus.
**Explanation**: Price floor above equilibrium → surplus. A common error is shortage.
**Tags**: price-floor, surplus, government-intervention

### Question Q-2.4-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Analyse the effect of a government subsidy on market equilibrium.
**Answer**: A subsidy shifts the supply curve right. Equilibrium price falls and quantity rises. Consumers benefit from lower prices; producers receive the market price plus the subsidy.
**Explanation**: Subsidy → supply right → lower price, higher quantity. A common error is price rises.
**Tags**: equilibrium, subsidy, analysis

### Question Q-2.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the effectiveness of a maximum price policy for essential food items.
**Answer**: Pros: makes food affordable for poor consumers. Cons: creates shortage, reduces producer incentive, may lead to black markets. The government may need to subsidise producers or ration supply.
**Explanation**: Affordability vs shortage trade-off. A common error is only listing pros or only cons.
**Tags**: price-ceiling, evaluation, food-policy

### Question Q-2.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether the price mechanism always leads to an efficient allocation of resources.
**Answer**: Arguments for efficiency: the price mechanism responds to consumer preferences (consumer sovereignty), encourages producers to minimise costs through competition, and adjusts quickly to changes in supply and demand without government intervention. Prices act as signals, incentives, and rationing devices. Arguments against: the price mechanism fails in cases of market failure — it underprovides public goods (free-rider problem), overproduces goods with negative externalities (pollution), underproduces merit goods (education), and can create significant inequality as resources flow to those with highest ability to pay rather than greatest need. Monopoly power can also distort price signals. Evaluation: the price mechanism is efficient for private goods in competitive markets but requires government intervention to address market failures, provide public goods, and ensure equitable distribution.
**Explanation**: Requires balanced evaluation of the price mechanism's strengths and limitations with reference to market failure. A common error is assuming free markets are always efficient or always inefficient.
**Tags**: price-mechanism, efficiency, market-failure, evaluation
