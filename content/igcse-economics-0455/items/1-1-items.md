### Question Q-1.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define scarcity in economics.
**Answer**: Scarcity means that resources are limited but human wants are unlimited. This creates the need to make choices about how resources are allocated.
**Explanation**: Scarcity is the fundamental economic problem. A common error is confusing scarcity with poverty — scarcity exists even in rich countries because wants always exceed available resources.
**Tags**: scarcity, definition, fundamental-problem

### Question Q-1.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Why do individuals have to make economic choices?
**Answer**: Because resources (income, time) are limited while wants are unlimited. People must decide which goods and services to buy and which to go without.
**Explanation**: Limited resources + unlimited wants = choice. A common error is saying choices are only about money — time and effort are also scarce resources.
**Tags**: choice, limited-resources, unlimited-wants

### Question Q-1.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A government has a budget of $10 billion. It can spend it on healthcare, education, or defence. Explain the economic problem this illustrates.
**Answer**: This illustrates scarcity (limited budget) and choice (must decide allocation). Spending on healthcare means less for education or defence — the opportunity cost of healthcare is the next best alternative forgone.
**Explanation**: Budget constraint forces trade-offs. A common error is saying the government can afford all three equally or ignoring the concept of opportunity cost in the analysis.
**Tags**: government-budget, scarcity, choice, opportunity-cost

### Question Q-1.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Distinguish between renewable and non-renewable resources with examples.
**Answer**: Renewable resources can be replenished naturally over time (solar energy, wind, forests if managed sustainably). Non-renewable resources are finite and cannot be replaced once used (oil, coal, natural gas). Overuse of renewable resources beyond their replenishment rate can make them effectively non-renewable.
**Explanation**: Renewable = replenished naturally. Non-renewable = finite supply. A common error is not recognising that water can be scarce locally, or classifying all natural resources as renewable.
**Tags**: resources, renewable, non-renewable, sustainability

### Question Q-1.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why air is a free good but bottled water is an economic good.
**Answer**: Air is abundant and freely available — using it does not reduce availability for others (zero opportunity cost). Bottled water requires resources to produce, bottle, and transport — it has an opportunity cost. An economic good uses scarce resources and has an opportunity cost; a free good does not.
**Explanation**: Free good = zero opportunity cost. Economic good = uses scarce resources. A common error is saying air is not free because of pollution, or confusing "free of charge" with "free good."
**Tags**: free-good, economic-good, opportunity-cost, distinction

### Question Q-1.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A student has 3 hours to study for two exams. Explain the economic concepts involved in this situation.
**Answer**: Time is a scarce resource. The student faces a trade-off: more time spent on Exam A means less time for Exam B. The opportunity cost of studying one subject is the benefit forgone from not studying the other. The student must make a choice about how to allocate limited time to maximise overall results.
**Explanation**: Time is scarce, creating a trade-off and opportunity cost. A common error is saying the student can study both equally without acknowledging the trade-off involved.
**Tags**: time, scarcity, trade-off, opportunity-cost

### Question Q-1.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A country discovers large new oil reserves. Analyse whether this eliminates the problem of scarcity for that country.
**Answer**: No, scarcity is not eliminated. While the country has more of one resource, other resources (labour, capital, land for agriculture) remain limited, and human wants are still unlimited. The new wealth from oil may even create new wants (better infrastructure, higher living standards). Additionally, oil extraction uses other scarce resources and has environmental costs. Scarcity is a universal and permanent economic condition — having more of one resource does not resolve the fundamental mismatch between limited resources and unlimited wants.
**Explanation**: Scarcity is fundamental — more of one resource does not eliminate it because wants grow and other resources remain limited. A common error is assuming that resource wealth ends scarcity entirely.
**Tags**: scarcity, resources, analysis, oil-reserves

### Question Q-1.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether technological advances reduce or increase the problem of scarcity.
**Answer**: Technology can increase productive efficiency (more output from same inputs), reducing scarcity for some goods — for example, mechanised farming increases food supply. However, technology also creates new wants (smartphones, internet services, electric vehicles) that did not previously exist, generating fresh demands on resources. Technology may also deplete resources faster (more efficient mining exhausts minerals sooner). Overall, while technology eases scarcity in specific areas, it cannot eliminate the fundamental economic problem because wants grow alongside capabilities. Scarcity persists regardless of technological progress.
**Explanation**: Technology increases supply but also creates new wants and can accelerate resource depletion. A common error is saying technology eliminates scarcity entirely, or that it only has positive effects.
**Tags**: technology, scarcity, evaluation, efficiency

### Question Q-1.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Discuss whether a planned economy is more effective than a free-market economy in solving the basic economic problems of what to produce, how to produce, and for whom to produce.
**Answer**: In a planned economy, the government decides resource allocation, which can prioritise merit goods (healthcare, education) and reduce inequality. However, central planners lack the price signals that communicate consumer preferences, often leading to shortages or surpluses. A free-market economy uses price mechanisms to allocate resources efficiently — high prices signal scarcity and incentivise production. However, markets may under-provide public goods and create significant inequality. Most real-world economies are mixed, combining market efficiency with government intervention to address market failures. The effectiveness depends on the quality of institutions, information, and governance rather than the system alone.
**Explanation**: Both systems have strengths and weaknesses in addressing the three basic economic questions. A common error is arguing one system is universally better without considering context, institutional quality, and the role of mixed economies.
**Tags**: planned-economy, free-market, resource-allocation, evaluation, economic-systems
