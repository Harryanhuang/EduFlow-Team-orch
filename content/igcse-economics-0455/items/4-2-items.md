### Question Q-4.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define fiscal policy.
**Answer**: Fiscal policy is the use of government spending and taxation to influence the level of economic activity, including aggregate demand, employment, and inflation.
**Explanation**: Fiscal policy involves two main tools: government spending and taxation. A common error is confusing fiscal policy (government) with monetary policy (central bank).
**Tags**: fiscal-policy, definition, government-spending, taxation

### Question Q-4.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a budget deficit and how does it differ from the national debt?
**Answer**: A budget deficit occurs when government spending exceeds government revenue (mainly from taxation) in a given year. The national debt is the total accumulated amount of money the government owes from all past budget deficits. The deficit is a flow (annual), while the national debt is a stock (accumulated total).
**Explanation**: Budget deficit is annual overspending; national debt is the accumulated total of past deficits. A common error is treating the two terms as interchangeable.
**Tags**: budget-deficit, national-debt, distinction

### Question Q-4.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how expansionary fiscal policy is used to stimulate economic growth.
**Answer**: Expansionary fiscal policy involves increasing government spending and/or reducing taxation. Higher government spending directly increases aggregate demand (e.g., building schools, hospitals). Tax cuts leave consumers and businesses with more disposable income, increasing consumption and investment. This multiplier effect boosts output and employment. It is typically used during recessions to close a deflationary gap.
**Explanation**: Spend more and/or tax less to boost aggregate demand. A common error is saying expansionary policy involves cutting government spending.
**Tags**: expansionary-fiscal-policy, aggregate-demand, recession

### Question Q-4.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how contractionary fiscal policy can be used to reduce inflation.
**Answer**: Contractionary fiscal policy involves reducing government spending and/or increasing taxation. Lower government spending directly reduces aggregate demand. Higher taxes reduce consumers’ disposable income and business profits, decreasing consumption and investment. This reduces demand-pull inflation by closing an inflationary gap. However, it may also slow economic growth and increase unemployment.
**Explanation**: Spend less and/or tax more to reduce aggregate demand and inflation. A common error is suggesting that increasing government spending reduces inflation.
**Tags**: contractionary-fiscal-policy, inflation, aggregate-demand

### Question Q-4.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Distinguish between direct and indirect taxes, giving one example of each.
**Answer**: Direct taxes are levied on income or wealth and are paid directly by the person or firm on whom they are imposed. They cannot be passed on to someone else. Example: income tax. Indirect taxes are levied on goods and services and can be passed on to consumers in the form of higher prices. Example: value-added tax (VAT) or excise duty on cigarettes.
**Explanation**: Direct taxes are on income/wealth and cannot be shifted; indirect taxes are on spending and can be passed to consumers. A common error is classifying income tax as indirect or VAT as direct.
**Tags**: direct-tax, indirect-tax, distinction, examples

### Question Q-4.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how government spending on infrastructure can affect economic growth in both the short run and the long run.
**Answer**: In the short run, infrastructure spending creates jobs directly (construction workers, engineers) and increases aggregate demand through the multiplier effect as workers spend their incomes. In the long run, better infrastructure (roads, ports, broadband) reduces transport costs, improves productivity, and makes the economy more competitive, increasing the economy’s productive capacity (potential output). Both effects contribute to economic growth, though the long-run supply-side benefits are more significant for sustained growth.
**Explanation**: Short-run: demand-side stimulus through jobs and multiplier. Long-run: supply-side improvement through productivity. A common error is only considering one time period or one effect.
**Tags**: government-spending, infrastructure, economic-growth, short-run-long-run

### Question Q-4.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether a government should always aim to balance its budget.
**Answer**: A balanced budget avoids accumulating debt and maintains investor confidence in government finances. It prevents future generations from bearing the burden of debt repayment and avoids crowding out private investment through high interest rates. However, insisting on a balanced budget at all times is inappropriate. During recessions, tax revenues fall and welfare spending rises automatically, creating a deficit. Forcing spending cuts or tax rises during a downturn would worsen the recession and increase unemployment (pro-cyclical policy). Keynesian economists argue that deficit spending during recessions is essential to stimulate recovery. During economic booms, surpluses should be run to repay debt. Therefore, a cyclical approach — deficits in downturns, surpluses in booms — is more effective than always balancing the budget.
**Explanation**: Balance vs stimulus trade-off across the economic cycle. A common error is arguing that the budget should always be balanced without considering the stage of the economic cycle.
**Tags**: budget-balance, evaluation, economic-cycle, fiscal-policy

### Question Q-4.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: To what extent is expansionary fiscal policy the most effective way to reduce unemployment?
**Answer**: Expansionary fiscal policy can reduce cyclical (demand-deficient) unemployment effectively by increasing aggregate demand. Government spending on public works creates jobs directly, while tax cuts boost consumer spending and business investment, leading firms to hire more workers. The multiplier effect amplifies the impact. However, its effectiveness depends on the type of unemployment. If unemployment is structural (skills mismatch), increased demand will not create jobs for workers with the wrong skills — supply-side policies such as retraining are needed. If unemployment is frictional, better job information services are more appropriate. There are also limitations: expansionary policy may cause inflation if the economy is near full capacity, increase government debt, and suffer from time lags in implementation. In conclusion, expansionary fiscal policy is most effective for cyclical unemployment but less effective for structural or frictional unemployment, where targeted supply-side policies are preferable.
**Explanation**: Effectiveness depends on the type of unemployment. Consider demand-side vs supply-side approaches and limitations such as inflation risk, debt, and time lags. A common error is assuming fiscal policy works equally well for all types of unemployment.
**Tags**: expansionary-fiscal-policy, unemployment, evaluation, policy-effectiveness

### Question Q-4.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Analyse the effectiveness of progressive taxation as a tool for reducing income inequality in an economy.
**Answer**: Progressive taxation reduces income inequality by taking a larger proportion of income from high earners. This revenue can fund transfer payments (welfare, pensions), healthcare, and education for lower-income groups, redistributing wealth. Countries with highly progressive tax systems (e.g., Nordic countries) tend to have lower Gini coefficients. However, there are limitations. If tax rates are too high, they may discourage work effort, reduce entrepreneurship, and cause high earners to emigrate (brain drain) or engage in tax avoidance. Indirect taxes (VAT) may offset the redistributive effect as they are regressive. Tax revenue alone does not reduce inequality — it must be spent effectively on programmes that benefit the poor. Additionally, wealth inequality (distinct from income inequality) is harder to address through income tax alone. In conclusion, progressive taxation is a necessary but insufficient tool — it works best when combined with targeted government spending and policies addressing wealth accumulation.
**Explanation**: Consider redistributive benefits against disincentive effects, tax avoidance, and the need for effective spending. A common error is assuming progressive taxation alone can solve inequality without considering how the revenue is spent.
**Tags**: progressive-tax, income-inequality, evaluation, redistribution
