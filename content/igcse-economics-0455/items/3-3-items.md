### Question Q-3.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define fixed costs and give one example.
**Answer**: Fixed costs are costs that do not change with the level of output. They must be paid even if output is zero. Examples include rent, insurance, and salaries of permanent staff.
**Explanation**: Do not vary with output. A common error is saying fixed costs never change at all (they can change for reasons other than output).
**Tags**: fixed-costs, definition

### Question Q-3.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define variable costs and give one example.
**Answer**: Variable costs are costs that change directly with the level of output. When output increases, variable costs increase. Examples include raw materials, hourly wages, and energy for production.
**Explanation**: Vary with output. A common error is confusing variable costs with fixed costs.
**Tags**: variable-costs, definition

### Question Q-3.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Calculate total cost if fixed costs are $5000, variable cost per unit is $10, and output is 300 units.
**Answer**: Total cost = Fixed costs + (Variable cost per unit x output) = 5000 + (10 x 300) = 5000 + 3000 = $8000.
**Explanation**: TC = FC + (VC per unit x output). A common error is adding FC and VC per unit without multiplying by output: 5000 + 10 = 5010.
**Tags**: total-cost, calculation

### Question Q-3.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Calculate profit if total revenue is $12,000 and total cost is $9,000.
**Answer**: Profit = Total revenue - Total cost = $12,000 - $9,000 = $3,000.
**Explanation**: Profit = TR - TC. A common error is dividing TR by TC instead of subtracting.
**Tags**: profit, calculation

### Question Q-3.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the difference between accounting profit and economic profit?
**Answer**: Accounting profit = total revenue minus explicit costs only. Economic profit = total revenue minus both explicit costs and implicit (opportunity) costs. Economic profit is therefore lower than accounting profit because it accounts for the value of the next best alternative foregone.
**Explanation**: Economic profit includes opportunity costs that accounting profit ignores. A common error is treating the two as identical.
**Tags**: accounting-profit, economic-profit, opportunity-cost

### Question Q-3.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What are economies of scale? Give two examples.
**Answer**: Economies of scale are cost advantages that arise when a firm increases its scale of production, leading to a fall in average cost per unit. Examples: (1) Purchasing economies through bulk buying discounts. (2) Technical economies from using specialised machinery that is more efficient at large scale.
**Explanation**: Bigger scale = lower average cost. A common error is confusing economies of scale with diseconomies of scale.
**Tags**: economies-of-scale, definition, examples

### Question Q-3.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A firm has fixed costs of $10,000, variable cost per unit of $5, and sells at $15 per unit. Analyse how a 20% increase in fixed costs would affect the break-even quantity.
**Answer**: Original break-even: 15Q = 10,000 + 5Q, so 10Q = 10,000, Q = 1,000 units. With 20% higher fixed costs ($12,000): 15Q = 12,000 + 5Q, so 10Q = 12,000, Q = 1,200 units. The break-even quantity increases by 200 units (20%). The firm must sell 20% more just to cover higher fixed costs. This highlights the risk of high fixed costs: they increase the break-even point and make the firm more vulnerable to falls in demand.
**Explanation**: Set TR = TC and solve for Q. A 20% increase in FC leads to a 20% increase in break-even output. A common error is forgetting to adjust fixed costs before recalculating.
**Tags**: break-even, calculation, analysis, fixed-costs

### Question Q-3.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether a firm should always aim to maximise its scale of production to reduce average costs.
**Answer**: Increasing scale can reduce average costs through purchasing, technical, financial, and managerial economies of scale. However, beyond a certain point, diseconomies of scale may set in as coordination becomes difficult, communication breaks down, and worker motivation falls. Additionally, a very large firm may face reduced flexibility to respond to market changes, potential diseconomies from bureaucratic decision-making, and the risk of overproduction if demand is limited. The optimal scale depends on the industry, the firm's management capability, and market conditions. A firm should aim for its minimum efficient scale rather than simply maximising size.
**Explanation**: Economies of scale have limits. A common error is assuming bigger is always better without considering diseconomies of scale.
**Tags**: economies-of-scale, diseconomies-of-scale, evaluation, average-cost

### Question Q-3.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: To what extent is the break-even analysis a reliable tool for business decision-making?
**Answer**: Break-even analysis is useful as it identifies the minimum output needed to avoid losses, helps set pricing strategies, and allows scenario planning for different cost structures. However, it has significant limitations: it assumes all output is sold, assumes costs and revenue are linear (which may not be true with bulk discounts or changing prices), ignores the time value of money, and does not account for uncertainty or changing market conditions. It also treats costs as either purely fixed or purely variable, when many costs are semi-variable. Break-even analysis is a useful starting point but should be combined with other tools such as cash flow forecasting and market research for reliable decision-making.
**Explanation**: Break-even is a useful but limited tool. A common error is treating break-even analysis as the only tool needed for business decisions.
**Tags**: break-even, evaluation, limitations, decision-making
