### Question Q-2.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a market economy?
**Answer**: A market economy is a system where resources are allocated by the price mechanism (supply and demand). Private owners make production decisions. The government plays a minimal role.
**Explanation**: Prices allocate resources. A common error is saying the government decides what to produce.
**Tags**: market-economy, definition

### Question Q-2.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a command (planned) economy?
**Answer**: A command economy is a system where the government owns resources and makes all production and distribution decisions through central planning.
**Explanation**: Government controls everything. A common error is confusing it with a mixed economy.
**Tags**: command-economy, definition, central-planning

### Question Q-2.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Compare how a market economy and a command economy answer 'for whom to produce'.
**Answer**: Market economy: goods go to those who can afford them (based on income/wealth). Command economy: goods are distributed according to government decisions (may aim for equality).
**Explanation**: Market = ability to pay. Command = government allocation. A common error is saying market economies distribute equally.
**Tags**: market-economy, command-economy, distribution

### Question Q-2.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What are two advantages of a market economy?
**Answer**: 1) Efficiency — competition drives firms to minimise costs. 2) Consumer choice — firms respond to consumer demand through the price mechanism.
**Explanation**: Efficiency and choice are key advantages. A common error is ignoring inequality as a disadvantage.
**Tags**: market-economy, advantages

### Question Q-2.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a mixed economy?
**Answer**: A mixed economy combines elements of both market and command systems. Private firms operate in most sectors, but the government provides public goods and regulates certain industries.
**Explanation**: Mix of private and public. A common error is saying it's half market, half command.
**Tags**: mixed-economy, definition

### Question Q-2.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why do most countries have mixed economies?
**Answer**: Pure market economies fail to provide public goods and can create inequality. Pure command economies are inefficient. Mixed economies balance efficiency with social welfare.
**Explanation**: Balance between efficiency and equity. A common error is saying mixed economies are the same everywhere.
**Tags**: mixed-economy, why, balance

### Question Q-2.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether a developing country should adopt a market or command economy.
**Answer**: Arguments for market: efficiency, innovation, foreign investment. Arguments for command: rapid industrialisation, addressing inequality. In practice, most developing countries use mixed economies.
**Explanation**: Both have pros and cons. Mixed is most common. A common error is advocating one without acknowledging trade-offs.
**Tags**: economic-systems, developing-country, evaluation

### Question Q-2.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Discuss whether a command economy can achieve greater economic equality than a market economy.
**Answer**: Command economy arguments: government controls wages, provides free healthcare/education, eliminates private profit, redistributes income — reducing inequality. Market economy arguments: higher wages reward skills and effort, competition creates jobs and growth, entrepreneurship allows wealth creation. Evaluation: command economies may reduce income inequality but often at the cost of lower living standards, shortages, and lack of incentives. Market economies generate higher average incomes but with wider income gaps. Most evidence suggests mixed economies achieve the best balance of equality and prosperity.
**Explanation**: Requires balanced discussion of both systems with a reasoned judgment. A common error is only discussing one system or failing to reach a conclusion.
**Tags**: command-economy, market-economy, inequality, evaluation

### Question Q-2.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the view that the profit motive always leads to better resource allocation than government planning.
**Answer**: Arguments for profit motive: drives efficiency as firms minimise costs, encourages innovation and new products, responds to consumer demand through the price mechanism, rewards productive use of resources. Arguments against: profit motive can lead to overproduction of demerit goods (e.g. tobacco), underprovision of public goods (e.g. street lighting), environmental damage from ignoring externalities, and growing inequality as resources flow to profitable markets rather than needed ones. Government planning can direct resources to socially desirable outcomes such as healthcare and infrastructure. Evaluation: the profit motive is effective for private goods but fails for public goods and merit goods — most economies need a combination of both mechanisms.
**Explanation**: Requires weighing the strengths and limitations of both allocation methods with examples. A common error is assuming the profit motive is always efficient or always wasteful.
**Tags**: profit-motive, government-planning, resource-allocation, evaluation
