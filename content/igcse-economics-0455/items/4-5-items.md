### Question Q-4.5-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define unemployment.
**Answer**: Unemployment occurs when people who are willing and able to work at the current wage rate are actively seeking employment but cannot find jobs. To be counted as unemployed, a person must be of working age, available to start work, and actively looking for a job.
**Explanation**: Unemployment requires willingness, ability, and active job search. A common error is counting all non-working people (e.g., students, retired people, those not seeking work) as unemployed.
**Tags**: unemployment, definition, labour-market

### Question Q-4.5-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: How is the unemployment rate calculated?
**Answer**: The unemployment rate is calculated as: (number of unemployed people ÷ labour force) × 100. The labour force consists of all employed people plus unemployed people actively seeking work. It excludes people not in the labour force (students, retirees, homemakers, discouraged workers who have stopped looking for jobs).
**Explanation**: The denominator is the labour force (employed + unemployed seeking work), not the total population. A common error is dividing by the total population or including non-working people who are not seeking jobs.
**Tags**: unemployment-rate, calculation, labour-force

### Question Q-4.5-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain what frictional unemployment is and why it exists.
**Answer**: Frictional unemployment is short-term unemployment that occurs when workers are between jobs or are entering the labour market for the first time. It exists because it takes time for workers to find suitable jobs and for employers to find suitable workers. Causes include imperfect information about job vacancies, geographical immobility (workers cannot instantly move to where jobs are), and the time needed for interviews and hiring processes. A certain level of frictional unemployment is natural and unavoidable in any dynamic economy where people change jobs regularly.
**Explanation**: Frictional unemployment is caused by search time and imperfect information. A common error is confusing it with structural unemployment or suggesting it can be completely eliminated.
**Tags**: frictional-unemployment, job-search, labour-market

### Question Q-4.5-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain what structural unemployment is and what causes it.
**Answer**: Structural unemployment is long-term unemployment caused by a fundamental mismatch between the skills workers possess and the skills demanded by employers. It results from permanent changes in the structure of the economy, such as the decline of traditional industries (e.g., coal mining, shipbuilding), technological change making certain skills obsolete, globalisation shifting production to other countries, or changes in consumer demand patterns. Unlike frictional unemployment, structural unemployment cannot be solved simply by matching workers to existing vacancies — workers need to retrain, relocate, or acquire new skills, which takes significant time and investment.
**Explanation**: Structural unemployment is caused by a skills mismatch due to economic change. A common error is confusing it with cyclical unemployment or suggesting that more job advertisements will solve it.
**Tags**: structural-unemployment, skills-mismatch, economic-change

### Question Q-4.5-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain what cyclical (demand-deficient) unemployment is and how it relates to the business cycle.
**Answer**: Cyclical unemployment (also called demand-deficient or Keynesian unemployment) is caused by a fall in aggregate demand, typically during a recession or economic downturn. When consumer spending, investment, and exports decline, firms experience falling demand for their products and lay off workers to reduce costs. The unemployed workers then have less income, which further reduces demand, creating a downward spiral. Cyclical unemployment rises during recessions and falls during economic booms, following the business cycle. Unlike structural unemployment, cyclical unemployment can be reduced by stimulating aggregate demand through expansionary fiscal or monetary policy.
**Explanation**: Cyclical unemployment is driven by insufficient aggregate demand during downturns. A common error is confusing it with structural unemployment or suggesting supply-side policies will solve it.
**Tags**: cyclical-unemployment, aggregate-demand, recession, business-cycle

### Question Q-4.5-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Analyse the economic and social costs of unemployment.
**Answer**: Economic costs include: lost output and GDP (unemployed workers are not producing goods and services), wasted resources (human capital is underutilised), lower tax revenue for the government (fewer people paying income tax), higher government spending on unemployment benefits and welfare, and a potential loss of skills as workers become deskilled during long-term unemployment (hysteresis). Social costs include: increased poverty and financial hardship for unemployed workers and their families, deteriorating physical and mental health, increased crime rates and social unrest, loss of self-esteem and social status, and intergenerational effects (children of unemployed parents may have poorer educational outcomes). The costs are greater for long-term and structural unemployment than for short-term frictional unemployment.
**Explanation**: Consider both economic (output, fiscal) and social (health, crime, poverty) costs. A common error is only listing personal costs to the individual or only economic costs to the government.
**Tags**: unemployment, costs, economic-social, lost-output

### Question Q-4.5-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the view that some unemployment is necessary for a healthy economy.
**Answer**: Frictional unemployment can be considered beneficial: it reflects workers voluntarily leaving jobs to find better matches, which improves productivity and job satisfaction over time. It indicates a dynamic, flexible labour market where people have the freedom to change careers. Zero unemployment is unrealistic and may indicate a rigid labour market where workers are trapped in unsuitable jobs with no mobility. However, this argument only applies to low levels of frictional unemployment. Structural unemployment represents wasted human potential and requires costly retraining. Cyclical unemployment causes significant economic damage, poverty, and social problems during recessions. Long-term unemployment of any type leads to deskilling (hysteresis), making it even harder for workers to re-enter employment. Youth unemployment is particularly damaging as it affects lifetime earnings and career prospects. In conclusion, a small amount of frictional unemployment (around the natural rate) is consistent with a healthy economy, but any unemployment above this level represents economic waste and social harm that governments should actively address.
**Explanation**: Distinguish between frictional (arguably necessary) and structural/cyclical (harmful) unemployment. Consider hysteresis and youth unemployment. A common error is accepting all unemployment as necessary or rejecting any unemployment as harmful.
**Tags**: unemployment, evaluation, natural-rate, frictional-unemployment, hysteresis

### Question Q-4.5-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the effectiveness of supply-side policies and demand-side policies in reducing structural unemployment.
**Answer**: Demand-side policies (expansionary fiscal and monetary policy) increase aggregate demand and create jobs, but they do not address the root cause of structural unemployment: the skills mismatch. Even with high demand, workers without the right skills cannot fill available vacancies. Attempting to reduce structural unemployment purely through demand stimulus risks causing inflation without significantly lowering unemployment (as seen in the Phillips curve breakdown). Supply-side policies directly address the mismatch: education and retraining programmes equip workers with skills demanded by growing industries; subsidies for firms that hire and train long-term unemployed; investment in infrastructure to improve labour mobility; and better job information services to reduce search time. However, supply-side policies take years to show results and require significant government spending. Education outcomes are uncertain, and retrained workers may still not find jobs if demand is weak. In conclusion, supply-side policies are more appropriate for structural unemployment, but they are most effective when combined with adequate aggregate demand — a dual approach using both supply-side and demand-side measures is optimal.
**Explanation**: Demand-side creates jobs but does not fix skills mismatch; supply-side addresses root cause but takes time. A common error is recommending only demand stimulus for structural unemployment or ignoring the need for adequate aggregate demand.
**Tags**: structural-unemployment, supply-side, demand-side, comparison, evaluation

### Question Q-4.5-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: To what extent is technological change a major cause of unemployment in developed economies? Discuss.
**Answer**: Technological change (automation, artificial intelligence, robotics) can displace workers in specific industries, causing structural unemployment. Manufacturing has seen significant job losses as machines replace manual labour, and service sectors (banking, retail) are increasingly affected by self-service technologies and AI. This can create concentrated pockets of high unemployment in regions dependent on affected industries. However, technology also creates new jobs in emerging industries (software development, data analysis, renewable energy) and increases productivity, which can boost overall demand and employment. Historically, the Industrial Revolution and subsequent technological waves created more jobs than they destroyed. The net effect depends on the pace of change (rapid automation leaves less time for workers to adapt), the availability of retraining programmes, and whether new industries emerge domestically or abroad. Education systems must adapt to prepare workers for technology-complementary skills. In developed economies, technology is a significant cause of structural unemployment in specific sectors, but it is not the sole cause — globalisation, deindustrialisation, and demand fluctuations also play major roles. The long-term solution is continuous investment in education and flexible labour markets rather than resisting technological progress.
**Explanation**: Consider both job destruction and job creation effects of technology. Reference historical evidence and current trends. A common error is arguing that technology only destroys jobs or ignoring the role of other factors like globalisation.
**Tags**: technological-unemployment, automation, structural-change, evaluation, developed-economies
