### Question Q-2.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define supply.
**Answer**: Supply is the quantity of a good that producers are willing and able to sell at a given price, during a given time period.
**Explanation**: Willing AND able to sell. A common error is only wanting to sell (without capacity).
**Tags**: supply, definition

### Question Q-2.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: State the law of supply.
**Answer**: As the price of a good rises, the quantity supplied rises, ceteris paribus. There is a direct relationship between price and quantity supplied.
**Explanation**: Direct relationship. A common error is inverse relationship.
**Tags**: law-of-supply, direct-relationship

### Question Q-2.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Distinguish between a change in supply and a change in quantity supplied.
**Answer**: Change in quantity supplied: movement along the supply curve caused by a price change. Change in supply: a shift of the entire supply curve caused by non-price factors (costs, technology, etc.).
**Explanation**: Movement along vs shift. A common error is confusing the two.
**Tags**: supply, quantity-supplied, shift

### Question Q-2.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does an increase in production costs affect supply?
**Answer**: Higher costs reduce profit margins, so firms supply less at each price. The supply curve shifts to the left.
**Explanation**: Costs up → supply down (left shift). A common error is movement along the curve.
**Tags**: supply, costs, shift

### Question Q-2.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does improved technology affect supply?
**Answer**: Better technology reduces production costs and increases efficiency, so firms can supply more at each price. The supply curve shifts to the right.
**Explanation**: Technology → lower costs → more supply. A common error is saying technology affects demand.
**Tags**: supply, technology, shift

### Question Q-2.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why does the supply curve slope upward?
**Answer**: Higher prices give producers an incentive to supply more — they can cover higher marginal costs and earn more profit. Each additional unit costs more to produce.
**Explanation**: Higher price → more profitable to produce more. A common error is saying suppliers just 'want to sell more.'
**Tags**: supply, upward-slope, profit

### Question Q-2.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Analyse how a drought affects the supply of agricultural products and the resulting market outcome.
**Answer**: Drought reduces crop yields → supply decreases (left shift). At the original price, there is excess demand. Price rises until a new equilibrium is reached. Quantity traded falls.
**Explanation**: Supply shock → higher price, lower quantity. A common error is saying demand changes.
**Tags**: supply, drought, market-outcome

### Question Q-2.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Analyse how simultaneous increases in production costs and improvements in technology might affect the supply of a manufactured good.
**Answer**: Rising production costs (e.g. higher wages or raw material prices) shift the supply curve to the left, reducing supply at every price. Simultaneously, technological improvements increase efficiency and lower unit costs, shifting the supply curve to the right. The net effect on supply depends on which force is stronger. If cost increases outweigh technology gains, supply falls and price rises. If technology gains are greater, supply rises and price falls. In practice, many manufacturing industries experience both pressures simultaneously — e.g. rising energy costs offset by automation. Evaluation: the final outcome depends on the magnitude of each change, the time period considered, and the specific industry.
**Explanation**: Requires analysing two opposing shifts and evaluating their net effect. A common error is only discussing one factor or assuming they cancel out exactly.
**Tags**: supply, costs, technology, simultaneous-shifts, analysis

### Question Q-2.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether government subsidies to producers always benefit consumers.
**Answer**: Arguments that subsidies benefit consumers: lower production costs shift supply right, reducing market price and increasing quantity available; subsidies can make essential goods more affordable (e.g. food, energy); they can support industries that create positive externalities (e.g. renewable energy). Arguments against: subsidies are funded by taxpayers, so consumers pay indirectly through taxation; firms may become dependent on subsidies and less efficient (moral hazard); subsidies may be passed to producers as higher profits rather than lower prices; government spending on subsidies has an opportunity cost (e.g. less spending on healthcare). Evaluation: the benefit to consumers depends on how much of the subsidy is passed through as lower prices, the efficiency of the subsidised industry, and whether the tax burden on consumers outweighs the price reduction.
**Explanation**: Requires evaluating both sides with economic reasoning. A common error is assuming all subsidies are fully passed to consumers as lower prices.
**Tags**: supply, subsidy, evaluation, consumer-benefit, government-intervention
