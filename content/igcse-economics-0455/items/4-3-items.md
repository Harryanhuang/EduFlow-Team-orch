### Question Q-4.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define monetary policy.
**Answer**: Monetary policy is the use of interest rates and the money supply by the central bank to influence the level of economic activity, including aggregate demand, inflation, and employment.
**Explanation**: Monetary policy is conducted by the central bank using interest rates and money supply. A common error is confusing monetary policy (central bank, interest rates) with fiscal policy (government, spending and taxation).
**Tags**: monetary-policy, definition, central-bank

### Question Q-4.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the money supply and why is it important for the economy?
**Answer**: The money supply is the total amount of money in circulation in an economy at a given time, including physical currency (notes and coins), bank deposits, and other liquid assets. It is important because changes in the money supply affect spending, inflation, and economic growth. Too much money growth can cause inflation; too little can lead to deflation and economic stagnation.
**Explanation**: Money supply includes cash plus bank deposits, not just physical cash. A common error is thinking the money supply only refers to notes and coins in circulation.
**Tags**: money-supply, definition, importance

### Question Q-4.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how an increase in interest rates affects consumer spending and business investment.
**Answer**: Higher interest rates increase the cost of borrowing, making loans for houses, cars, and other goods more expensive. This reduces consumer spending, especially on big-ticket items purchased on credit. Higher rates also increase the reward for saving, encouraging people to save rather than spend. For businesses, higher borrowing costs make investment projects less profitable, reducing spending on new equipment, factories, and expansion. Existing borrowers with variable-rate mortgages and loans face higher repayments, reducing their disposable income. Overall, higher interest rates reduce aggregate demand in the economy.
**Explanation**: Higher rates increase borrowing costs, reward saving, and reduce disposable income for variable-rate borrowers. A common error is saying higher interest rates increase spending or only affect one channel.
**Tags**: interest-rates, consumer-spending, investment, contractionary

### Question Q-4.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how a decrease in interest rates is intended to stimulate economic growth.
**Answer**: Lower interest rates reduce the cost of borrowing, making loans cheaper for consumers buying houses and cars, and for businesses investing in equipment and expansion. Lower rates also reduce the reward for saving, encouraging spending over saving. Existing borrowers with variable-rate mortgages face lower repayments, increasing their disposable income. This increase in consumption and investment raises aggregate demand, stimulating output and employment. Lower rates may also reduce the exchange rate, making exports cheaper and boosting net exports.
**Explanation**: Lower rates reduce borrowing costs, penalise saving, and increase disposable income. A common error is saying lower interest rates reduce economic growth or ignoring the exchange rate channel.
**Tags**: interest-rates, economic-growth, expansionary, aggregate-demand

### Question Q-4.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain what quantitative easing (QE) is and how it is intended to stimulate the economy.
**Answer**: Quantitative easing is an unconventional monetary policy where the central bank creates new money electronically to purchase government bonds and other financial assets from banks and institutions. This increases the money supply and pushes up bond prices, which lowers long-term interest rates. The selling institutions receive cash, increasing bank reserves and encouraging lending to businesses and consumers. QE is used when conventional interest rate cuts have been exhausted (rates near zero) to provide additional economic stimulus.
**Explanation**: Central bank creates electronic money to buy assets, lowering long-term rates and increasing bank reserves. A common error is saying QE involves printing physical cash or that it directly gives money to consumers.
**Tags**: quantitative-easing, money-supply, unconventional-monetary-policy

### Question Q-4.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Compare fiscal policy and monetary policy as tools for managing the economy.
**Answer**: Fiscal policy uses government spending and taxation to influence aggregate demand directly. It can target specific sectors or regions and has a strong multiplier effect, but is slow to implement due to political processes and may increase government debt. Monetary policy uses interest rates and money supply, controlled by the independent central bank. It can be implemented quickly (monthly meetings) and affects the whole economy indirectly through borrowing costs. However, it is less effective in a liquidity trap (when rates are near zero) and works with time lags (12–18 months for full effect). Fiscal policy is better for deep recessions and targeted interventions, while monetary policy is better for fine-tuning and controlling inflation.
**Explanation**: Fiscal: direct impact, slow implementation, targeted. Monetary: indirect impact, fast implementation, economy-wide. A common error is saying monetary policy has a direct impact on aggregate demand or ignoring the time lags of both tools.
**Tags**: fiscal-policy, monetary-policy, comparison, policy-tools

### Question Q-4.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the effectiveness of monetary policy during a deep recession.
**Answer**: In a deep recession, the central bank typically cuts interest rates to stimulate borrowing and spending. However, monetary policy may be ineffective for several reasons. First, a liquidity trap may occur: even at very low interest rates, businesses and consumers may be unwilling to borrow due to pessimism about the future, rendering rate cuts ineffective. Second, banks may be reluctant to lend due to bad debts and a desire to rebuild reserves, blocking the transmission mechanism. Third, if the recession is caused by a financial crisis (e.g., 2008), damaged confidence and deleveraging reduce the impact of cheap credit. Quantitative easing can provide additional stimulus but has limited reach if credit channels remain blocked. In contrast, fiscal policy (direct government spending) may be more effective as it injects demand directly. However, monetary policy still has merit — it prevents deflation, supports asset prices, and can weaken the currency to boost exports. On balance, monetary policy alone is insufficient in a deep recession and should be combined with fiscal stimulus.
**Explanation**: Consider liquidity trap, bank reluctance, confidence crisis, and QE limitations. Compare with fiscal policy. A common error is assuming monetary policy is always effective regardless of economic conditions.
**Tags**: monetary-policy, recession, liquidity-trap, evaluation

### Question Q-4.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Discuss whether a central bank should prioritise controlling inflation or promoting economic growth.
**Answer**: Prioritising inflation control (inflation targeting) provides price stability, which creates certainty for businesses to plan investment and for consumers to spend. Low and stable inflation prevents the erosion of savings and maintains international competitiveness. Many successful central banks (e.g., the ECB) have a primary inflation mandate. However, focusing solely on inflation may lead to overly tight monetary policy during downturns, increasing unemployment and slowing growth. The US Federal Reserve has a dual mandate (price stability and maximum employment), which allows it to respond to recessions more flexibly. In practice, the two goals are interconnected: stable inflation supports sustainable long-term growth, while excessive growth stimulus can create inflation that ultimately damages the economy. The optimal approach depends on the economic context — during high inflation periods, inflation control should dominate; during recessions, growth promotion is more urgent. A flexible inflation target with consideration for employment is generally the most balanced approach.
**Explanation**: Weigh price stability benefits against the risk of ignoring unemployment and growth. Consider dual mandate vs single mandate. A common error is arguing that only one objective matters without considering how they interact.
**Tags**: central-bank, inflation-targeting, economic-growth, dual-mandate, evaluation

### Question Q-4.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: To what extent is monetary policy more effective than fiscal policy in controlling inflation?
**Answer**: Monetary policy can control inflation by raising interest rates, which reduces borrowing, spending, and aggregate demand. Higher rates also attract foreign capital, appreciating the currency and making imports cheaper, reducing imported inflation. The central bank can act quickly and independently of political pressures, making monetary policy credible and timely. However, monetary policy has limitations: it mainly addresses demand-pull inflation and is less effective against cost-push inflation (where higher rates cannot reduce oil prices or wage demands). Raising rates to combat inflation may also cause a recession and increase unemployment. Fiscal policy can complement monetary policy by cutting government spending or raising taxes to reduce aggregate demand directly. However, fiscal policy is slower to implement (requires parliamentary approval), may be politically motivated, and tax rises are unpopular. In conclusion, monetary policy is generally more effective for controlling inflation due to its speed, independence, and flexibility, but for severe inflation, a coordinated approach using both monetary and fiscal tightening is most effective.
**Explanation**: Compare speed, independence, and channels of both policies. Consider demand-pull vs cost-push inflation. A common error is saying monetary policy works for all types of inflation or ignoring the role of fiscal policy.
**Tags**: monetary-policy, fiscal-policy, inflation-control, comparison, evaluation
