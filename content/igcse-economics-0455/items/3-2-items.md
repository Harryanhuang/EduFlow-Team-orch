### Question Q-3.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define price elasticity of supply (PES).
**Answer**: PES measures the responsiveness of quantity supplied to a change in price. PES = % change in quantity supplied / % change in price.
**Explanation**: Responsiveness of supply to price. A common error is confusing PES with PED.
**Tags**: pes, definition, formula

### Question Q-3.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: If PES = 2, is supply elastic or inelastic?
**Answer**: Elastic (PES > 1). Quantity supplied changes by a larger percentage than the price change.
**Explanation**: > 1 = elastic. A common error is saying inelastic.
**Tags**: pes, elastic, inelastic

### Question Q-3.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What factors determine price elasticity of supply?
**Answer**: 1) Time period (short run = inelastic, long run = elastic). 2) Spare capacity (more spare capacity = more elastic). 3) Stock levels (high stocks = more elastic). 4) Ease of switching production between goods.
**Explanation**: Time, capacity, stocks, flexibility. A common error is listing only time as a factor.
**Tags**: pes, determinants, factors

### Question Q-3.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why is the supply of agricultural products often price inelastic in the short run?
**Answer**: Crops take time to grow and farmers cannot quickly increase supply in response to a price increase. Production is constrained by biological growing seasons and land availability.
**Explanation**: Time lag in production. A common error is saying supply is perfectly inelastic.
**Tags**: pes, agriculture, short-run

### Question Q-3.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Calculate PES: price rises from $5 to $6 and quantity supplied increases from 200 to 260.
**Answer**: % change in Q = (260-200)/200 = 30%. % change in P = (6-5)/5 = 20%. PES = 30%/20% = 1.5 (elastic).
**Explanation**: Use percentage changes. A common error is calculating 60/1 = 60 using absolute values.
**Tags**: pes, calculation, elastic

### Question Q-3.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why is supply more elastic in the long run than in the short run?
**Answer**: In the long run, firms can build new factories, enter or exit the market, adopt new technologies, and adjust all factors of production. In the short run, at least one factor is fixed.
**Explanation**: Long run = more flexibility to adjust. A common error is saying supply is always more elastic in the short run.
**Tags**: pes, long-run, short-run

### Question Q-3.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate why the PES for manufactured goods is generally higher than for agricultural goods.
**Answer**: Manufactured goods can be produced more quickly and flexibly as factories can increase shifts, hire more workers, and use spare capacity. Agricultural goods are constrained by biological processes, seasons, and land availability. However, manufacturing may also face capacity constraints when operating at full capacity, and some manufactured goods require long lead times for specialised components. The difference in PES largely reflects the greater ability of manufacturers to adjust output in the short run.
**Explanation**: Manufacturing is more flexible than agriculture. A common error is saying manufacturing PES is always high regardless of capacity utilisation.
**Tags**: pes, manufacturing, agriculture, comparison, evaluation

### Question Q-3.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Analyse how knowledge of PES could help a government decide whether to subsidise housing construction to address a housing shortage.
**Answer**: If the supply of housing is price inelastic (PES < 1), a subsidy to builders will have a limited effect on the quantity of housing supplied in the short run because construction takes time, land is scarce, and planning permissions create delays. The subsidy may mainly increase builders' profits and land prices rather than output. In the long run, supply becomes more elastic as new developments are planned and construction capacity increases, making the subsidy more effective. The government should consider whether the subsidy is better targeted at increasing construction capacity (training workers, releasing land) rather than just reducing costs for existing builders.
**Explanation**: Inelastic supply limits the output effect of subsidies in the short run. A common error is assuming subsidies always increase supply significantly regardless of elasticity.
**Tags**: pes, subsidy, housing, policy-analysis, evaluation

### Question Q-3.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Discuss whether the concept of PES is equally useful for producers of primary commodities and producers of manufactured goods.
**Answer**: For primary commodity producers (e.g. farmers, miners), PES is crucial because supply is often inelastic due to biological constraints, weather dependence, and long extraction lead times. Understanding PES helps them plan investment in capacity and technology. For manufactured goods producers, PES tends to be higher and more predictable, making it useful for short-term output decisions. However, PES may be less useful for manufacturers facing rapid technological change or those producing customised goods with variable production processes. Overall, PES is more critical for primary producers who face greater supply-side constraints, while manufacturers may focus more on demand-side considerations.
**Explanation**: PES is more relevant for sectors with supply constraints. A common error is treating PES as equally important across all industries without justification.
**Tags**: pes, primary-commodities, manufacturing, comparison, discussion
