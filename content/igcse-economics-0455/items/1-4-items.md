### Question Q-1.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What does a production possibility frontier (PPF) show?
**Answer**: A PPF shows the maximum combinations of two goods (or categories of goods) that an economy can produce with its available resources and technology, assuming full and efficient use of all resources.
**Explanation**: Maximum output with given resources and technology. A common error is saying it shows what an economy wants to produce or what it actually produces — it shows what is possible at maximum capacity.
**Tags**: ppf, definition, maximum-output, production

### Question Q-1.4-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What does a point inside the PPF indicate about an economy?
**Answer**: A point inside the PPF indicates that resources are not being fully or efficiently used — there is unemployment, idle capital, or productive inefficiency. The economy could produce more of both goods without needing additional resources.
**Explanation**: Inside the curve = underutilisation of resources. A common error is saying it means the economy is saving resources for the future, or that it represents a desirable position.
**Tags**: ppf, inside, unemployment, inefficiency

### Question Q-1.4-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What causes the PPF to shift outward? Give at least three specific causes.
**Answer**: An outward shift of the PPF represents economic growth caused by: (1) an increase in the quantity of resources (more labour through population growth, discovery of new natural resources); (2) an improvement in the quality of resources (better education and training, improved healthcare); (3) advances in technology (automation, new production methods). Investment in capital goods also shifts the PPF outward over time.
**Explanation**: More or better resources shift the PPF outward. A common error is saying that a change in demand or prices shifts the PPF — only changes in productive capacity cause shifts.
**Tags**: ppf, shift, economic-growth, resources, technology

### Question Q-1.4-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why is the PPF usually drawn concave (bowed outwards) to the origin?
**Answer**: The bowed shape reflects increasing opportunity cost — as more of one good is produced, increasingly larger amounts of the other good must be given up. This occurs because resources are not equally suited to producing both goods. Workers and land specialised for one product are less efficient when redirected to another, so each additional unit costs more in terms of the forgone good.
**Explanation**: Increasing opportunity cost = bowed/concave shape. A common error is drawing a straight-line PPF (which assumes constant opportunity cost) or a convex shape.
**Tags**: ppf, shape, increasing-opportunity-cost, specialisation

### Question Q-1.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A natural disaster destroys a significant number of factories in a country. Explain the effect on the country's PPF.
**Answer**: The PPF shifts inward — the economy can now produce less of both goods with the reduced capital stock. The destruction of factories means fewer capital goods available for production, reducing the economy's productive capacity. This is a loss of economic growth, not merely a movement along the existing curve.
**Explanation**: Fewer resources (capital destroyed) cause an inward shift of the PPF. A common error is showing a movement along the curve instead of a shift, or confusing a demand change with a supply change.
**Tags**: ppf, inward-shift, disaster, capital-destruction

### Question Q-1.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: On a PPF showing guns and butter, what happens when the economy moves from producing more guns to producing more butter?
**Answer**: The economy moves along the PPF curve. As more butter is produced, fewer guns can be produced — the opportunity cost of more butter is the quantity of guns forgone. This illustrates the trade-off between the two goods when resources are reallocated. The economy remains on the frontier (efficient), but the composition of output changes.
**Explanation**: Movement along the curve = reallocation of resources between two goods. A common error is showing a shift of the curve rather than a movement along it.
**Tags**: ppf, movement-along, trade-off, opportunity-cost, reallocation

### Question Q-1.4-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the usefulness of the PPF model for analysing real-world economic decisions.
**Answer**: The PPF simplifies a complex economy to two goods, making trade-offs, scarcity, and opportunity cost visually clear. It effectively illustrates the concepts of efficiency (on the curve), unemployment (inside), and growth (outward shift). However, real economies produce thousands of goods, not two, and resources are not always fully employed or perfectly transferable. The model assumes a fixed level of technology and does not capture income distribution, externalities, or the role of international trade. Despite these limitations, the PPF remains a powerful teaching and analytical tool for understanding fundamental economic principles and the cost of resource allocation choices.
**Explanation**: Useful for illustrating core concepts but simplified. A common error is treating the PPF as a literal description of an economy rather than a simplified analytical model.
**Tags**: ppf, evaluation, limitations, model-usefulness

### Question Q-1.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the effects on a country's PPF of an increase in immigration versus an increase in automation technology. Justify which would have a greater long-term impact on productive capacity.
**Answer**: Immigration increases the quantity of labour, shifting the PPF outward — especially for labour-intensive goods. However, the shift depends on the skills of immigrants and may be uneven across sectors. Automation technology improves the productivity of existing workers, shifting the PPF outward — particularly for capital-intensive goods. Automation may cause a more pronounced shift in specific sectors. In the long term, automation may have greater impact because technological improvements compound over time (each innovation enables further innovation), while immigration increases are limited by population growth and policy. However, automation may increase structural unemployment, while immigration can fill labour shortages and support an ageing population. The greater long-term impact depends on the country's existing factor endowments and economic structure.
**Explanation**: Both shift the PPF outward but through different mechanisms and with different distributional effects. A common error is assuming both cause identical outward shifts without considering sectoral differences and long-term dynamics.
**Tags**: ppf, immigration, automation, comparison, long-run, evaluation

### Question Q-1.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Analyse whether an outward shift in a country's PPF always leads to an improvement in living standards.
**Answer**: An outward PPF shift indicates greater productive capacity, which could raise living standards through higher output and income. However, this is not guaranteed. The benefits may be distributed unequally — if growth concentrates among a wealthy elite, most citizens see no improvement. The shift may also involve negative externalities (pollution, resource depletion) that reduce quality of life. Additionally, if the shift is towards capital goods rather than consumer goods, current living standards may not improve even though future capacity increases. Population growth may absorb the gains, leaving per capita output unchanged. Therefore, while an outward PPF shift is a necessary condition for sustained improvement in living standards, it is not sufficient — distribution, environmental quality, and the composition of output also matter.
**Explanation**: Growth in productive capacity does not automatically translate to higher living standards. A common error is assuming that any outward PPF shift automatically improves welfare without considering distribution, externalities, and output composition.
**Tags**: ppf, economic-growth, living-standards, evaluation, inequality, externalities
