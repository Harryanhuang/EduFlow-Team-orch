### Question Q-6.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is trade protection?
**Answer**: Trade protection refers to government policies that restrict imports to protect domestic industries from foreign competition. Methods include tariffs, quotas, subsidies, and non-tariff barriers.
**Explanation**: Restricting imports to shield domestic producers. A common error is confusing trade protection with free trade.
**Tags**: trade-protection, definition

### Question Q-6.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a tariff?
**Answer**: A tariff is a tax imposed on imported goods. It raises the price of imports, making domestic goods more competitive and reducing the quantity of imports demanded.
**Explanation**: Tax on imports that raises their price. A common error is thinking a tariff is a tax on exports.
**Tags**: tariff, definition

### Question Q-6.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What are the effects of a tariff on consumers in the importing country?
**Answer**: Consumers face higher prices for both imported and domestically produced goods (domestic producers raise prices to match the tariff-inflated import price). This reduces consumer surplus, lowers real incomes, and restricts consumer choice. Consumers may also face reduced product variety as some foreign producers exit the market.
**Explanation**: Higher prices and less choice for consumers. A common error is saying consumers benefit from tariffs.
**Tags**: tariff, consumer-effects, prices

### Question Q-6.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What are the effects of a tariff on domestic producers in the importing country?
**Answer**: Domestic producers benefit from reduced foreign competition, allowing them to sell more output at higher prices and potentially expand production and employment. However, they may become less efficient over time due to lack of competitive pressure, and producers who rely on imported raw materials face higher input costs.
**Explanation**: Protected from competition in the short run but may become inefficient long-term. A common error is assuming domestic producers always benefit without drawbacks.
**Tags**: tariff, producer-effects, efficiency

### Question Q-6.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does a subsidy to domestic producers work as a form of trade protection?
**Answer**: A subsidy is a government payment to domestic producers that lowers their production costs. This allows them to reduce prices and compete with cheaper imports without cutting output. Unlike a tariff, a subsidy does not directly raise the price of imports for consumers. However, it is funded by taxpayers and may encourage producers to remain inefficient.
**Explanation**: Government payment lowers domestic costs. A common error is confusing a subsidy with a tariff or assuming it has no cost to the government.
**Tags**: subsidy, trade-protection, government-spending

### Question Q-6.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the impact of trade protection on employment in the economy as a whole?
**Answer**: Trade protection may save jobs in the protected industry by shielding it from foreign competition, but it costs jobs elsewhere. Downstream industries face higher input costs and may cut employment. Export industries may lose jobs if trading partners retaliate with their own protectionist measures. The net effect on total employment across the economy is often negative or negligible.
**Explanation**: Protects some jobs but destroys others through higher costs and retaliation. A common error is assuming protection always increases total employment.
**Tags**: trade-protection, employment, retaliation

### Question Q-6.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether trade protection is justified for infant industries.
**Answer**: Arguments for protection: new industries in developing countries need temporary shelter from established foreign competitors to grow, achieve economies of scale, and develop the skills and technology needed to compete globally. Without protection, infant industries may be crushed before they can mature. Arguments against: protection may become permanent due to political lobbying; the industry may never become efficient if it faces no competition; and consumers bear higher prices during the protection period. Historical evidence is mixed: countries like South Korea successfully used temporary protection, while others maintained it indefinitely without achieving competitiveness. The policy is most justified when protection is time-limited, targeted, and combined with efforts to build productive capacity.
**Explanation**: Temporary protection may allow growth but risks creating permanent dependency. A common error is arguing protection is always or never justified.
**Tags**: infant-industry, trade-protection, evaluation

### Question Q-6.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the effectiveness of tariffs and quotas as methods of trade protection.
**Answer**: Tariffs are taxes on imports that raise prices, while quotas are physical limits on import quantities. Both restrict imports and protect domestic producers, but they work differently. Tariffs generate government revenue and allow consumers to buy more if they are willing to pay the higher price. Quotas do not generate direct government revenue (unless licences are auctioned) and set a hard limit on quantity regardless of price. Quotas provide more certainty about import volumes but may lead to higher price increases than tariffs. Tariffs are more transparent and easier to negotiate in trade agreements, while quotas can be more restrictive but harder to administer. In practice, tariffs are generally preferred by the WTO as they are more transparent and allow market forces to determine quantities.
**Explanation**: Tariffs raise price and earn revenue; quotas cap quantity and may raise prices more. A common error is treating tariffs and quotas as identical in their effects.
**Tags**: tariff, quota, comparison, trade-protection

### Question Q-6.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: To what extent is trade protection more harmful than beneficial for an economy?
**Answer**: Arguments that protection is harmful: it raises prices for consumers, reduces choice and competition, encourages inefficiency in protected industries, invites retaliation from trading partners, and reduces overall economic welfare by distorting the allocation of resources. Protection also harms export industries that face higher input costs and retaliatory barriers. Arguments that protection can be beneficial: it can protect strategic industries essential for national security, shield infant industries while they develop, prevent dumping by foreign firms selling below cost, and protect employment during periods of structural adjustment. On balance, most economists argue that the costs of protection exceed the benefits because the efficiency losses and higher prices outweigh the short-term gains to specific industries. However, the extent of harm depends on the type of protection, its duration, and the economic context in which it is applied.
**Explanation**: Weighing broad economic costs against targeted benefits. A common error is taking a one-sided view without acknowledging legitimate arguments on both sides.
**Tags**: trade-protection, evaluation, welfare, costs-and-benefits
