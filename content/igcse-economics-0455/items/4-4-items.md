### Question Q-4.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define inflation.
**Answer**: Inflation is a sustained increase in the general price level of goods and services in an economy over time. As prices rise, the purchasing power of money falls — each unit of currency buys fewer goods and services than before.
**Explanation**: Inflation refers to the general price level rising continuously, not a one-off price increase or the rise in price of a single good. A common error is saying that any price increase is inflation.
**Tags**: inflation, definition, purchasing-power

### Question Q-4.4-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Explain how inflation is measured using the Consumer Price Index (CPI).
**Answer**: The CPI measures inflation by tracking the price changes of a representative basket of goods and services typically purchased by households (e.g., food, housing, transport, clothing, healthcare). Each item is given a weight based on its share of household spending. The inflation rate is calculated as the percentage change in the CPI from one period to the next. For example, if the CPI rises from 100 to 105 over a year, the annual inflation rate is 5%.
**Explanation**: CPI tracks a weighted basket of goods and services. The inflation rate is the percentage change in the CPI. A common error is confusing the price level (CPI) with the inflation rate (change in CPI).
**Tags**: inflation, measurement, cpi, consumer-price-index

### Question Q-4.4-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain what causes demand-pull inflation.
**Answer**: Demand-pull inflation occurs when aggregate demand exceeds the economy's productive capacity (aggregate supply), pushing prices upward. It is often described as 'too much money chasing too few goods.' Causes include increased consumer spending (e.g., rising wages, consumer confidence), higher government spending, increased money supply, lower interest rates stimulating borrowing, and a surge in exports. When the economy is operating near full employment, firms cannot increase output quickly enough to meet demand, so prices rise instead.
**Explanation**: Excess aggregate demand over supply drives prices up. A common error is confusing demand-pull inflation with cost-push inflation or failing to explain why output cannot simply increase to meet demand.
**Tags**: demand-pull-inflation, aggregate-demand, causes

### Question Q-4.4-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain what causes cost-push inflation.
**Answer**: Cost-push inflation occurs when production costs rise, causing firms to pass on higher costs to consumers in the form of increased prices. This shifts the aggregate supply curve to the left. Key causes include rising raw material prices (e.g., oil price shocks), increases in wages above productivity growth (wage-push inflation), higher indirect taxes (e.g., VAT increases), and depreciation of the currency making imported inputs more expensive. Unlike demand-pull inflation, cost-push inflation can occur even when the economy has spare capacity and unemployment is high (stagflation).
**Explanation**: Higher production costs force firms to raise prices, reducing supply. A common error is confusing cost-push with demand-pull inflation or not explaining the link between costs and prices.
**Tags**: cost-push-inflation, production-costs, aggregate-supply

### Question Q-4.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Analyse the effects of inflation on savers and borrowers.
**Answer**: Inflation harms savers because the real value of their savings falls if the interest rate earned on savings is lower than the inflation rate (negative real interest rate). For example, if a saver earns 3% interest but inflation is 6%, the real return is –3%, meaning their savings buy fewer goods over time. Fixed-income savers (e.g., bondholders) are especially affected. In contrast, borrowers benefit from inflation because they repay their loans with money that is worth less in real terms. If someone borrows $10,000 at 5% interest but inflation runs at 8%, the real value of repayments falls. This redistribution from savers to borrowers is one of the key distributional effects of inflation.
**Explanation**: Savers lose when inflation exceeds interest rates (negative real return); borrowers gain because debt becomes cheaper in real terms. A common error is saying both savers and borrowers lose from inflation.
**Tags**: inflation, savers, borrowers, real-interest-rate, redistribution

### Question Q-4.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain the main policies a government can use to reduce inflation.
**Answer**: Contractionary monetary policy: the central bank raises interest rates, making borrowing more expensive and saving more attractive, which reduces consumer spending, investment, and aggregate demand. Contractionary fiscal policy: the government cuts spending or raises taxes, directly reducing aggregate demand. Supply-side policies: investment in education, training, infrastructure, and deregulation can increase productive capacity and reduce cost-push inflation over the long term. Incomes policies: wage controls or guidelines can limit wage-push inflation, though these are difficult to enforce. Exchange rate appreciation (if managed) can reduce the price of imported goods, lowering imported inflation.
**Explanation**: Multiple policy tools target different causes of inflation. A common error is recommending expansionary policies (which would increase inflation) or only mentioning one policy tool.
**Tags**: inflation, anti-inflation-policy, monetary-policy, fiscal-policy, supply-side

### Question Q-4.4-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the view that inflation is always harmful to an economy.
**Answer**: High or unpredictable inflation is clearly harmful: it erodes the real value of savings and wages, creates uncertainty that discourages investment, redistributes income from fixed-income earners and savers to borrowers, and can reduce international competitiveness if a country’s prices rise faster than trading partners’. Hyperinflation can destroy an economy entirely (e.g., Zimbabwe, Venezuela). However, moderate inflation (around 2–3%) is generally considered beneficial. It encourages spending and investment (as holding cash loses value), allows real wages to adjust without nominal pay cuts (avoiding deflationary spirals), and provides a buffer for monetary policy (interest rates can be cut before hitting zero). Deflation is often considered more dangerous than moderate inflation because it encourages delayed spending, increases the real burden of debt, and is associated with economic stagnation (e.g., Japan). In conclusion, while high inflation is harmful, moderate inflation is not only tolerable but preferable to deflation or zero inflation.
**Explanation**: Distinguish between moderate (beneficial) and high (harmful) inflation. Compare with deflation. A common error is stating that all inflation is bad without recognising the benefits of moderate price increases.
**Tags**: inflation, evaluation, moderate-inflation, deflation, harmful-effects

### Question Q-4.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the economic problems caused by demand-pull inflation and cost-push inflation and evaluate which is more difficult for policymakers to address.
**Answer**: Demand-pull inflation is caused by excess aggregate demand. It typically occurs during economic booms when unemployment is low. Policymakers can address it by raising interest rates or cutting government spending to reduce demand. This is relatively straightforward: contractionary policy cools the economy without causing a major recession if applied carefully. Cost-push inflation is caused by rising production costs (e.g., oil price shocks, wage increases). It is more difficult to address because contractionary demand-side policies would reduce output and increase unemployment further, worsening the stagflation (rising prices plus falling output). Supply-side solutions (deregulation, investment in alternatives) take years to implement. For example, the 1970s oil crisis created cost-push inflation that central banks struggled to manage — raising rates caused deep recessions. In conclusion, cost-push inflation is generally more difficult to address because it involves a trade-off between inflation and unemployment that demand-side policies alone cannot resolve.
**Explanation**: Demand-pull can be addressed by reducing demand; cost-push creates a policy dilemma (stagflation). A common error is treating both types of inflation as equally easy to address or ignoring the stagflation problem.
**Tags**: demand-pull, cost-push, comparison, stagflation, policy-dilemma

### Question Q-4.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: To what extent does inflation create winners and losers in society? Justify your answer.
**Answer**: Inflation redistributes income and wealth in ways that create clear winners and losers. Losers include: savers whose real returns are negative when inflation exceeds interest rates; fixed-income earners (pensioners, public sector workers with frozen pay) whose real incomes fall; exporters who become less competitive if domestic inflation exceeds that of trading partners; and lenders who receive repayment in devalued currency. Winners include: borrowers who repay debts with money worth less than when they borrowed (especially those with fixed-rate mortgages); owners of real assets (property, commodities) whose values tend to rise with inflation; and workers in strong bargaining positions (e.g., unionised sectors) who can negotiate wage increases above inflation. The extent of redistribution depends on whether inflation is anticipated (allowing people to adjust contracts and wages) or unanticipated (causing sudden, arbitrary redistribution). Moderate, predictable inflation creates fewer extreme winners and losers, while high, unpredictable inflation causes severe and arbitrary redistribution. Overall, inflation does create winners and losers, but the net effect on society is generally negative because the losses to savers, the poor, and the economy’s efficiency outweigh the gains to borrowers.
**Explanation**: Identify specific winners (borrowers, asset owners) and losers (savers, fixed-income earners). Consider anticipated vs unanticipated inflation. A common error is only discussing losers or failing to explain why certain groups benefit.
**Tags**: inflation, redistribution, winners-losers, evaluation, real-income
