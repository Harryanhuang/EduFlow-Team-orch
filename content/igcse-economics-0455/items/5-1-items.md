### Question Q-5.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define economic growth.
**Answer**: Economic growth is an increase in a country's real GDP (gross domestic product) over time. It measures the increase in the total output of goods and services produced in an economy.
**Explanation**: Increase in real GDP. A common error is confusing real GDP with nominal GDP — real GDP is adjusted for inflation.
**Tags**: economic-growth, definition, gdp

### Question Q-5.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define economic development.
**Answer**: Economic development is an improvement in the living standards and well-being of a country's population. It includes improvements in health, education, and freedom, not just income.
**Explanation**: Broader than growth — includes quality of life dimensions. A common error is treating economic development as the same as economic growth.
**Tags**: economic-development, definition, living-standards

### Question Q-5.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Distinguish between economic growth and economic development.
**Answer**: Economic growth is quantitative — it measures the increase in real GDP. Economic development is qualitative — it measures improvement in living standards, health, education, and freedom. A country can experience growth without development if the gains are concentrated among a small section of the population.
**Explanation**: Quantitative vs qualitative distinction. A common error is treating them as synonyms or assuming growth automatically produces development.
**Tags**: growth, development, distinction

### Question Q-5.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is GDP per capita? Explain why it is a useful measure.
**Answer**: GDP per capita is calculated by dividing total GDP by the population. It measures average income per person and is useful because it allows comparison between countries of different population sizes, giving a better indication of average living standards than total GDP alone.
**Explanation**: Average output per person enables cross-country comparison. A common error is using total GDP to compare living standards without adjusting for population size.
**Tags**: gdp-per-capita, definition, usefulness

### Question Q-5.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why GDP per capita may be a misleading measure of living standards.
**Answer**: GDP per capita is an average that does not show how income is distributed — a country may have high GDP per capita but widespread poverty if income is concentrated in the hands of a few. It also ignores non-market activities such as subsistence farming, environmental degradation, health outcomes, education levels, and overall happiness.
**Explanation**: Averages hide inequality and non-income dimensions. A common error is assuming GDP per capita perfectly reflects the welfare of all citizens.
**Tags**: gdp-per-capita, limitations, inequality

### Question Q-5.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Identify and explain four factors that contribute to economic growth.
**Answer**: (1) Increases in physical capital — more machinery, infrastructure, and technology raise productivity. (2) Improvements in human capital — better education and skills increase worker output. (3) Technological progress — innovation leads to more efficient production methods. (4) Political stability — a stable environment encourages investment and long-term planning.
**Explanation**: Growth requires both more/better resources and an environment that supports productive activity. A common error is listing only one factor such as natural resources.
**Tags**: economic-growth, factors, capital, technology

### Question Q-5.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether economic growth always leads to economic development.
**Answer**: Economic growth provides the resources needed for development — higher tax revenue can fund healthcare, education, and infrastructure. However, growth does not guarantee development. If the benefits are concentrated among a wealthy elite, the majority may see no improvement in living standards. Growth may also come at the cost of environmental degradation, harming long-term well-being. Development requires deliberate policy action: investment in human capital, progressive taxation, and social programmes. Countries such as Equatorial Guinea have experienced rapid GDP growth without corresponding improvements in HDI, while others like Costa Rica have achieved high development with moderate growth through strong social policies.
**Explanation**: Growth enables but does not guarantee development — distribution and policy matter. A common error is assuming a direct, automatic link between the two.
**Tags**: growth, development, evaluation, distribution

### Question Q-5.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: To what extent can GDP per capita be used as a reliable measure of economic development? Justify your answer.
**Answer**: GDP per capita is useful as a quick indicator of average economic output and allows international comparisons. However, it has significant limitations as a measure of development. It ignores income inequality — a high average can coexist with mass poverty. It excludes non-market activities such as unpaid domestic work and subsistence farming, which are significant in developing countries. It does not account for environmental costs, leisure time, health outcomes, or education levels. For example, a country with high GDP per capita from oil extraction may have poor health and education indicators. Therefore, GDP per capita should be used alongside broader measures such as the HDI, which incorporate health and education dimensions, to give a more reliable picture of development.
**Explanation**: GDP per capita captures income but misses the multi-dimensional nature of development. A common error is treating GDP per capita as a complete proxy for development without acknowledging its blind spots.
**Tags**: gdp-per-capita, development, evaluation, limitations

### Question Q-5.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Discuss whether sustainable economic growth is possible in developing countries without causing significant environmental damage.
**Answer**: Sustainable growth is theoretically possible through investment in renewable energy, green technology, and circular economy practices. Countries that adopt environmental regulations early can avoid the costly clean-up experienced by industrialised nations. However, there are strong counterarguments. Developing countries often rely on resource-intensive industries such as mining and agriculture, making environmental damage difficult to avoid. Green technology is expensive and may not be affordable without international support. The pressure to grow quickly and create jobs may lead governments to prioritise short-term output over environmental protection. Additionally, developed countries historically caused most environmental damage during their own industrialisation, raising fairness concerns. In conclusion, while sustainable growth is possible with the right policies and international support, it requires difficult trade-offs that many developing countries find hard to make.
**Explanation**: Sustainability requires balancing economic goals with environmental protection — trade-offs are inevitable. A common error is arguing that growth and environment are always compatible or always in conflict, without acknowledging the nuance.
**Tags**: sustainable-development, environment, evaluation, trade-offs
