**Question**: Define specialisation.

### Question Q-6.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define absolute advantage.
**Answer**: A country has an absolute advantage in producing a good if it can produce more of it (or the same amount using fewer resources) than another country.
**Explanation**: Produces more with same resources. A common error is confusing with comparative advantage.
**Tags**: absolute-advantage, definition

### Question Q-6.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Country A can produce 10 cars or 20 tonnes of wheat. Country B can produce 5 cars or 15 tonnes of wheat. Which country has a comparative advantage in cars?
**Answer**: Country A: OC of 1 car = 2 tonnes wheat. Country B: OC of 1 car = 3 tonnes wheat. Country A has lower OC and therefore has a comparative advantage in cars.
**Explanation**: A: 20/10 = 2 wheat per car. B: 15/5 = 3 wheat per car. A has lower OC. A common error is using absolute advantage instead of opportunity cost.
**Tags**: comparative-advantage, calculation

### Question Q-6.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why does trade based on comparative advantage benefit both countries?
**Answer**: Each country specialises in the good it produces at a lower opportunity cost and trades for the other good. Both countries can then consume beyond their own production possibility frontier (PPF), gaining access to more of both goods than they could produce alone.
**Explanation**: Specialisation + trade allows consumption beyond PPF. A common error is assuming only one country benefits from trade.
**Tags**: comparative-advantage, trade, mutual-benefit

### Question Q-6.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What are the risks of over-specialisation for a country?
**Answer**: Dependence on a single product or market makes the economy vulnerable to price shocks, changes in demand, or natural disasters affecting that sector. Lack of economic diversification reduces resilience. For example, oil-dependent countries face severe recessions when global oil prices fall sharply.
**Explanation**: Concentration risk reduces economic stability. A common error is assuming specialisation has no drawbacks.
**Tags**: over-specialisation, risks, diversification

### Question Q-6.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Can a country have a comparative advantage in producing every good? Explain your reasoning.
**Answer**: No. If a country has a lower opportunity cost in producing one good, it must have a higher opportunity cost in producing another. Comparative advantage is relative: one country's advantage in a good necessarily means the other country has the advantage in the other good. It is mathematically impossible for one country to have the lowest opportunity cost in all goods.
**Explanation**: Opportunity costs are inversely related across goods. A common error is assuming a highly productive country can have a comparative advantage in everything.
**Tags**: comparative-advantage, reasoning, opportunity-cost

### Question Q-6.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether a developing country should specialise in primary products or manufactured goods.
**Answer**: Primary products: easier to begin producing using existing natural resources, but are subject to high price volatility, low income elasticity of demand, and declining terms of trade. Manufactured goods: offer higher value added, more employment, and greater export earnings, but require skilled labour, infrastructure, and capital investment that developing countries may lack. A gradual shift from primary to manufacturing specialisation, as seen in the development paths of South Korea and China, may be the most effective strategy. The best choice depends on the country's resource endowment, stage of development, and access to international markets.
**Explanation**: Weigh trade-offs between primary and manufacturing sectors. A common error is arguing one sector is always superior without considering the country's specific circumstances.
**Tags**: specialisation, developing-country, evaluation, primary-products

### Question Q-6.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the view that comparative advantage is the only determinant of international trade patterns.
**Answer**: Comparative advantage explains why countries specialise based on opportunity cost differences, and it predicts that countries will export goods where they have a lower opportunity cost. However, trade patterns are also shaped by other factors: government trade policies such as tariffs and quotas distort trade flows; transport costs may make it uneconomical to trade goods where a country has a comparative advantage; economies of scale allow countries to dominate industries regardless of initial opportunity costs; historical ties and trade agreements create preferential trading relationships; and differences in product quality and branding affect consumer choices. While comparative advantage provides the theoretical foundation for trade, real-world trade patterns reflect a combination of comparative advantage, government intervention, geography, and institutional factors.
**Explanation**: Comparative advantage is important but not the sole factor. A common error is ignoring non-economic influences on trade patterns such as politics, transport costs, and historical relationships.
**Tags**: comparative-advantage, trade-patterns, evaluation, determinants

### Question Q-6.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: To what extent does specialisation always lead to higher living standards?
**Answer**: Specialisation can raise living standards by increasing productivity, lowering costs through economies of scale, improving product quality, and generating higher incomes through trade. Workers gain expertise from repeating tasks, and countries can export surplus output to earn foreign income. However, excessive specialisation may reduce living standards: workers performing repetitive tasks may experience low job satisfaction and alienation; over-reliance on a narrow range of exports makes an economy vulnerable to external shocks such as falling commodity prices or loss of markets; and structural unemployment can result when specialised industries decline. Additionally, the gains from specialisation may not be distributed equally across society. Therefore, specialisation generally improves living standards when moderately diversified, but the extent of improvement depends on how broadly the gains are shared and whether the economy retains sufficient flexibility to adapt to changing conditions.
**Explanation**: Specialisation brings gains but with important caveats. A common error is assuming that any degree of specialisation automatically benefits everyone in society.
**Tags**: specialisation, living-standards, evaluation, distribution
