# Question: Question Q-16.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: In humans, haemophilia is caused by a recessive allele (h) carried on the X chromosome. A normal man marries a carrier woman. Use a genetic cross to determine the possible genotypes of their children and explain why haemophilia appears more frequently in males.
**Answer**: Father: XH Y (normal). Mother: XH Xh (carrier). Punnett square: XH XH (normal female), XH Xh (carrier female), XH Y (normal male), Xh Y (haemophiliac male). Offspring: 25% normal female, 25% carrier female, 25% normal male, 25% haemophiliac male. Haemophilia appears more in males because they only have one X chromosome (XY). If a male inherits the recessive allele on his single X, it is expressed. Females (XX) must inherit two recessive alleles to express it, so they are usually carriers.
**Explanation**: X-linked recessive, males more affected. A common error is saying haemophilia is "equally common in males and females."
