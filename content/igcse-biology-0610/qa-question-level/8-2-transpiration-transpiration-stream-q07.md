# Question: Question Q-8.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Why do some plants have adaptations to reduce transpiration, and what are the specific structural adaptations found in xerophytes?
**Answer**: Plants in dry environments (xerophytes) need to conserve water because water is scarce and transpiration rates are high (high temperature, low humidity, high wind). Adaptations: (1) small leaves or spines — reduce surface area for evaporation. (2) Thick waxy cuticle — impermeable barrier, water cannot pass through. (3) Sunken stomata in pits — trap humid air, reducing the water vapour concentration gradient. (4) Rolled leaves — create enclosed humid space, reduce air movement over stomata. (5) Hairy leaves — trap a layer of moist air near the leaf surface. (6) Stomata on lower epidermis only — shielded from direct sunlight. Some xerophytes also store water in succulent tissues (e.g., cacti).
**Explanation**: Xerophyte adaptations, reduce water loss, structural detail. A common error is saying these plants "don't transpire."
