# Question: Question Q-19.2-08
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain the role of zoos and botanical gardens in conservation. What are the arguments for and against using captive breeding as a conservation strategy?
**Answer**: Zoos and botanical gardens contribute to conservation through: (1) Captive breeding programmes — breeding endangered species to increase population numbers (e.g., Arabian oryx, California condor). (2) Reintroduction — releasing captive-bred individuals into the wild after habitat restoration. (3) Research — studying species biology to inform wild conservation. (4) Education — raising public awareness about threats to biodiversity. (5) Gene banking — storing genetic material (sperm, seeds) for future use. Arguments for captive breeding: prevents extinction of critically endangered species when wild habitat is destroyed; provides insurance populations; enables scientific research. Arguments against: resources diverted from habitat protection; animals may lose wild behaviours (genetic adaptation to captivity); reintroduction success rates are often low; high cost per species; ethical concerns about keeping animals in captivity.
**Explanation**: Breeding, reintroduction, education, but also resource diversion. A common error is saying captive breeding alone can "save" a species.
