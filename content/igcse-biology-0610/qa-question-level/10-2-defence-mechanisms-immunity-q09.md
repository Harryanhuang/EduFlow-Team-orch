# Question: Question Q-10.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A child has a severely weakened immune system. Evaluate whether it is safe to give the child the MMR (measles, mumps, rubella) vaccine, which is a live attenuated vaccine. Consider both the risk to the child and the public health implications.
**Answer**: For an individual immunocompromised child: live attenuated vaccines contain weakened but still replicating pathogens. In a child with a severely weakened immune system, even attenuated viruses may replicate uncontrollably, causing vaccine-associated disease. Administering the MMR to an immunocompromised child is contraindicated by the WHO. This child should NOT receive live vaccines. Public health implications: (1) The child relies on herd immunity for protection — a high vaccination rate in the surrounding population (>95% for measles) prevents virus circulation. (2) All close contacts (family, classmates) should be fully vaccinated to protect the child (cocooning strategy). (3) Inactivated vaccines may be safe alternatives depending on the specific immune deficiency. (4) The child's medical team should assess which vaccines are safe on a case-by-case basis. This scenario illustrates why high vaccination coverage matters — it protects those who cannot be vaccinated.
**Explanation**: Live vaccine + immunocompromised → contraindicated; herd immunity protects. A common error is saying "give a smaller dose."
