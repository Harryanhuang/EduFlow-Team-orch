# Question: Question Q-19.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain how habitat fragmentation affects biodiversity. Why is habitat connectivity important for conservation efforts?
**Answer**: Habitat fragmentation occurs when large, continuous habitats are divided into smaller, isolated patches (by roads, agriculture, urbanisation). Effects on biodiversity: (1) Edge effects — increased exposure to wind, light, and invasive species at habitat edges. (2) Reduced population sizes — isolated populations are more vulnerable to inbreeding and extinction. (3) Loss of specialist species — species requiring large territories or specific habitats cannot survive in small patches. (4) Reduced genetic diversity — isolation prevents gene flow between populations. (5) Increased extinction risk — small populations are vulnerable to random environmental events. Habitat connectivity is important because corridors (linear habitats connecting patches) allow: individuals to migrate between patches, gene flow between populations (preventing inbreeding), recolonisation after local extinctions, and access to larger feeding or breeding areas. Conservation efforts increasingly focus on habitat networks rather than isolated reserves.
**Explanation**: Fragmentation reduces patches; Connectivity allows migration and gene flow. A common error is saying fragmentation "only" affects large animals.
