# Question: Question Q-15.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain how meiosis produces genetically different gametes, and why this is important for sexual reproduction.
**Answer**: Meiosis produces four haploid cells (n = 23 chromosomes), each genetically different: (1) Crossing over in Prophase I — non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes exchange segments of DNA at chiasmata, creating new allele combinations. (2) Independent assortment in Metaphase I — each pair of homologous chromosomes lines up randomly at the equator; the orientation of each pair is independent of others, so maternal and paternal chromosomes can be distributed to daughter cells in many different combinations. (3) Each haploid gamete receives a unique mixture of the parent's alleles. Importance: each fertilisation event combines two genetically different gametes, producing offspring that are genetically different from each other and from their parents. This variation is the raw material for natural selection — in a changing environment, some individuals may have advantageous combinations that help them survive and reproduce.
**Explanation**: Crossing over + independent assortment → genetically different haploid gametes → variation. A common error is saying meiosis produces identical cells.
