# Question: Question Q-2.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student draws a diagram of a specialised cell and labels it "has no nucleus, therefore it is dead." Evaluate whether this statement is always correct, using two examples of specialised cells.
**Answer**: The statement is partially correct but oversimplified. Red blood cells lose their nucleus during development and are indeed "dead" in the sense they cannot repair themselves or divide — but they still carry out their function (oxygen transport) for ~120 days. Xylem vessels are dead cells with no contents (no nucleus, no cytoplasm) — they form empty tubes for water transport. However, sieve tube cells (phloem) are living but also have no nucleus — they are supported by companion cells. So "no nucleus = dead" is true for RBCs and xylem, but the lack of nucleus alone does not determine if a cell is alive.
**Explanation**: RBCs + xylem: no nucleus, dead. Phloem: no nucleus, alive + companion. A common error is assuming all enucleate cells are dead.
