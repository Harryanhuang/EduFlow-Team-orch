# Question: Question Q-16.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain how a mutation in a single gene can lead to a change in the structure of a protein. Why might this affect the organism's phenotype?
**Answer**: A mutation is a change in the DNA base sequence of a gene. This changes the mRNA codon during transcription. During translation, the ribosome reads the altered mRNA and may insert a different amino acid into the polypeptide chain. This changes the primary structure of the protein. If the new amino acid has different properties (size, charge, hydrophobicity), it alters the protein's folding, 3D shape, and active site. A non-functional or altered protein may lead to a changed phenotype — for example, sickle cell anaemia (glutamic acid replaced by valine changes haemoglobin shape and its oxygen-carrying ability).
**Explanation**: DNA base change → altered codon → different amino acid → changed protein structure → changed phenotype. A common error is saying mutations always produce "more" or "better" proteins.
