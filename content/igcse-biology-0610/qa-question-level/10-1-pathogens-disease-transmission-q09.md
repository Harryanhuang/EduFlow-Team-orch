# Question: Question Q-10.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A traveller returns from a tropical region with fever, chills, and diarrhoea. Two doctors disagree: one suspects malaria, the other suspects cholera. Discuss which pathogen type, mode of transmission, and symptoms support each diagnosis, and explain why accurate diagnosis matters for treatment.
**Answer**: Malaria — pathogen: Plasmodium (protoctist). Transmission: mosquito bite (not person-to-person). Symptoms: cyclical fever, chills, sweating (rupture of infected RBCs). Diarrhoea is less common. No person-to-person transmission risk. Treatment: antimalarial drugs (e.g., chloroquine, artemisinin). Cholera — pathogen: Vibrio cholerae (bacterium). Transmission: contaminated water/food (faecal-oral). Main symptom: profuse watery diarrhoea ("rice-water stool"), rapid dehydration. Person-to-person transmission possible via contaminated hands/surfaces. Treatment: oral rehydration therapy (ORT) + antibiotics. Accurate diagnosis matters because: (1) antimalarials are useless against cholera; antibiotics are useless against malaria (protoctist ≠ bacterium). (2) Cholera requires immediate rehydration to prevent death from dehydration within hours. (3) Malaria requires prompt treatment to prevent severe complications (cerebral malaria). (4) Public health response differs: cholera → sanitation/water; malaria → vector control. Laboratory blood smear (malaria) + stool culture (cholera) would confirm.
**Explanation**: Differential diagnosis — pathogen type, symptoms, treatment implications all differ. A common error is treating both as "tropical fever" without recognising the different management urgency.
