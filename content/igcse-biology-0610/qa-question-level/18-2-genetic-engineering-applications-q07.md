# Question: Question Q-18.2-07
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Explain how gene therapy could be used to treat a genetic disorder. What are the current challenges and ethical concerns associated with this technology?
**Answer**: Gene therapy aims to treat genetic disorders by introducing, removing, or altering genetic material in a patient's cells. Methods include: (1) In vivo — vectors (modified viruses) deliver functional genes directly into the patient's body. (2) Ex vivo — patient cells are removed, genetically modified in the lab, and returned to the patient. For example, treating severe combined immunodeficiency (SCID) by inserting the functional ADA gene into patient stem cells. Challenges: (1) Viruses used as vectors may cause immune reactions or become harmful. (2) Genes may be inserted in the wrong location, disrupting other genes ( mutagenesis). (3) Effects may be temporary, requiring repeated treatment. (4) High cost limits accessibility. Ethical concerns: germline therapy (modifying embryos) is widely prohibited because changes are heritable; questions of "designer babies" (enhancing traits rather than treating disease); consent issues for children; equitable access to expensive treatments.
**Explanation**: Viral vectors, ex vivo/in vivo, risks and ethics. A common error is saying gene therapy "fixes" all genetic diseases.
