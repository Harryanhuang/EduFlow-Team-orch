# Question: Question Q-12.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A baker notices bread dough rises faster at 35°C than at 20°C, but at 50°C no rising occurs at all. Explain these observations in terms of enzyme activity and fermentation.
**Answer**: 35°C: closer to yeast enzyme optimum (30-37°C). Enzymes (amylase, maltase, glycolytic enzymes) have higher kinetic energy → more frequent enzyme-substrate collisions → faster fermentation rate → more CO₂/min → dough rises faster. Q₁₀ ≈ 2. 50°C: above denaturation temperature. Hydrogen bonds in enzyme tertiary structures break → active sites permanently change shape → substrates no longer bind → enzymes inactive → fermentation stops → zero CO₂ → no rising. Yeast thermal death point ~45-50°C. Practical: bakers use warm water (~27-35°C), never hot water (>45°C) which would kill yeast.
**Explanation**: 35°C = closer to optimum, faster rate; 50°C = denaturation, zero activity. A common error is describing temperature effect without linking to fermentation enzymes.
