## Item 1
**Tags**: mutation | variation | gene | chromosome
**Difficulty**: S
**Question**: What is the difference between genetic variation and environmental variation?
**Answer**: Genetic variation is caused by differences in genes or alleles — it is inherited from parents. Environmental variation is caused by differences in the environment — diet, climate, etc. Most characteristics are influenced by both genetic and environmental factors.

## Item 3
**Tags**: mutation | variation | gene | chromosome
**Difficulty**: S
**Question**: What causes continuous and discontinuous variation?
**Answer**: Continuous variation: caused by many genes (polygenes) acting together plus environmental factors. Results in a bell-shaped distribution (e.g., human height). Discontinuous variation: caused by a single gene with a few distinct alleles. Results in distinct categories (e.g., blood groups, flower colour).
**Explanation**: Standard difficulty: requires applying the core definition to a specific biological context.

**Question**: Explain why siblings look similar but not identical.
**Answer**: Siblings share about 50% of their DNA (on average) because each receives a random 50% from each parent. The combination of alleles from each parent is different for each child. Environmental factors (nutrition, lifestyle) also contribute to differences.

## Item 5
**Tags**: mutation | variation | gene | chromosome
**Difficulty**: S
**Question**: What is a mutation?
**Answer**: A mutation is a change in the DNA base sequence. This can occur spontaneously during DNA replication or be caused by mutagens (chemicals, radiation). Mutations can be neutral, harmful, or beneficial. If they occur in body cells they cannot be passed on; if in gametes they can be inherited.

## Item 6
**Tags**: mutation | variation | gene | chromosome
**Difficulty**: C
**Question**: Compare continuous and discontinuous variation with examples.
**Answer**: Continuous variation: range of phenotypes with no distinct categories, influenced by many genes plus environment. Examples: height, weight, skin colour. Continuous data can take any value. Discontinuous variation: distinct categories, usually controlled by one or few genes. Examples: blood groups, flower colour (red/white/pink), ability to roll tongue. Categorical data.

## Item 7
**Tags**: mutation | variation | gene | chromosome
**Difficulty**: C
**Question**: Explain how natural selection can lead to evolution.
**Answer**: In a population, individuals with advantageous alleles are more likely to survive and reproduce, passing these alleles to offspring. Over many generations, the frequency of advantageous alleles increases. If populations become isolated, they may diverge, eventually forming new species. This is natural selection driving evolution.

## Item 8
**Tags**: mutation | variation | gene | chromosome
**Difficulty**: C
**Question**: What is the role of meiosis in producing genetic variation?
**Answer**: Meiosis produces gametes with half the chromosome number. During meiosis, crossing over in prophase I exchanges DNA between homologous chromosomes, creating new combinations of alleles. Independent assortment in metaphase I and II randomly distributes maternal and paternal chromosomes. Each gamete is genetically unique, maximising variation.
**Explanation**: Challenge level: requires connecting multiple genetic concepts to explain the biological mechanism.
