# Items: 7.2 — Digestion: mechanical and chemical, alimentary canal

### Question Q-7.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the difference between ingestion, digestion, absorption, and egestion?
**Answer**: Ingestion = taking food into the body (eating). Digestion = breaking down large insoluble food molecules into small soluble molecules (mechanical and chemical). Absorption = small soluble molecules passing into the blood. Egestion = removal of undigested waste (faeces) from the body.
**Explanation**: Four distinct processes. A common error is confusing egestion with excretion.
**Tags**: ingestion, digestion, absorption, egestion

### Question Q-7.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Where are the main sites of mechanical digestion in the alimentary canal?
**Answer**: Mouth: teeth break food into smaller pieces (chewing/mastication), increasing surface area for enzyme action. Stomach: muscular walls churn and mix food with gastric juice, further breaking it into a semi-liquid called chyme. Both are mechanical digestion — physical breakdown without chemical change.
**Explanation**: Mouth (teeth) + stomach (churning). A common error is saying the small intestine does mechanical digestion.
**Tags**: mechanical-digestion, mouth, stomach, mastication

### Question Q-7.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe the role of the stomach in digestion.
**Answer**: The stomach churns food (mechanical digestion) and secretes hydrochloric acid and the enzyme pepsin (chemical digestion). HCl kills bacteria, provides the optimum acidic pH (pH 2) for pepsin, and begins protein breakdown. Pepsin breaks proteins into smaller peptides. The stomach wall is protected by mucus.
**Explanation**: Churning, HCl, pepsin, mucus. A common error is saying the stomach "absorbs nutrients."
**Tags**: stomach, pepsin, HCl, protein-digestion

### Question Q-7.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why is the small intestine adapted for absorption?
**Answer**: The small intestine has villi — finger-like projections that greatly increase the surface area for absorption. Each villus has a thin wall (one cell thick) for short diffusion distance, a network of blood capillaries to absorb glucose and amino acids, and a lacteal to absorb fatty acids and glycerol.
**Explanation**: Villi, surface area, thin wall, blood supply, lacteal. A common error is saying villi are in the large intestine.
**Tags**: small-intestine, villi, absorption, surface-area

### Question Q-7.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the role of bile in digestion?
**Answer**: Bile is produced by the liver and stored in the gall bladder. It neutralises stomach acid (providing alkaline conditions for intestinal enzymes) and emulsifies fats — breaking large fat drops into tiny droplets, increasing surface area for lipase to act on. Bile does not contain enzymes.
**Explanation**: Neutralises acid + emulsifies fats. A common error is saying bile "digests" fats (it emulsifies, lipase digests).
**Tags**: bile, emulsification, liver, lipase

### Question Q-7.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What enzymes are involved in digestion and what do they break down?
**Answer**: Amylase breaks down starch into maltose (produced by salivary glands and pancreas). Protease (e.g., pepsin, trypsin) breaks down proteins into amino acids (stomach and pancreas). Lipase breaks down fats (lipids) into fatty acids and glycerol (pancreas). Maltase breaks maltose into glucose.
**Explanation**: Amylase → starch, protease → protein, lipase → fats. A common error is confusing which enzyme breaks down which substrate.
**Tags**: enzymes, digestion, amylase, protease, lipase

### Question Q-7.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain how the muscular action of the alimentary canal moves food, using peristalsis and its coordination mechanism.
**Answer**: Peristalsis is the wave-like contraction and relaxation of circular and longitudinal muscles in the walls of the alimentary canal. Circular muscles contract behind the food bolus (pushing it forward) while longitudinal muscles relax ahead, widening the lumen to receive it. This coordinated wave pushes food from the oesophagus all the way through the stomach and intestines. It is involuntary — controlled by the autonomic nervous system. Without peristalsis, food would not move through the digestive tract.
**Explanation**: Circular/longitudinal coordination, autonomic control, whole-canal scope. A common error is saying peristalsis only occurs in the oesophagus.
**Tags**: peristalsis, circular-muscle, longitudinal-muscle, alimentary-canal

### Question Q-7.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain the role of the pancreas and liver in digestion, and how their secretions are delivered to the small intestine.
**Answer**: Pancreas: produces pancreatic amylase (starch → maltose), trypsin/protease (protein → amino acids), and lipase (fats → fatty acids + glycerol). Also secretes sodium hydrogen carbonate to neutralise stomach acid. These enzymes are delivered via the pancreatic duct into the duodenum (first section of small intestine). Liver: produces bile (stored in gall bladder), which is delivered via the bile duct to the duodenum. Bile emulsifies fats (increases surface area for lipase) and neutralises stomach acid, creating alkaline conditions for intestinal enzymes. Both organs are accessory organs — food does not pass through them.
**Explanation**: Pancreatic enzymes + bile via ducts, accessory organ concept. A common error is thinking food passes through the pancreas or confusing the pancreas's digestive role with its endocrine role (insulin).
**Tags**: pancreas, liver, pancreatic-duct, bile-duct, accessory-organs

### Question Q-7.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain the relationship between peristalsis and absorption in the small intestine.
**Answer**: Peristalsis moves the partially digested food (chyme) slowly through the small intestine, allowing enough time for absorption to occur. The churning action of peristalsis mixes chyme with digestive enzymes and bile for final chemical digestion, producing small soluble molecules that can pass through the villi wall. The slow, rhythmic contractions ensure maximum contact between digested nutrients and the villi surface. After absorption, peristalsis pushes the remaining undigested material into the large intestine. Movement and absorption are interdependent — without peristalsis, absorption efficiency drops drastically.
**Explanation**: Peristalsis → mixing + slow transit → max enzyme contact + absorption time. A common error is treating movement and absorption as separate when they are interdependent.
**Tags**: peristalsis, absorption, villi, small-intestine, integration
