# Items: 11.2 — Breathing mechanism and effects of exercise

### Question Q-11.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Describe what happens during exhalation (breathing out).
**Answer**: During exhalation (expiration): the diaphragm relaxes and domes upward. The external intercostal muscles relax — the ribcage moves down and in. Thoracic volume decreases → pressure inside the thorax increases above atmospheric pressure → air is forced out of the lungs. At rest, this is passive (no energy needed) — elastic recoil of stretched lung tissue does the work. In forced exhalation (exercise, coughing), internal intercostal and abdominal muscles contract (active).
**Explanation**: Diaphragm↑, ribs↓in → volume↓ → pressure↑ → air out. Rest = passive; forced = active. A common error is saying all exhalation is active.
**Tags**: exhalation, diaphragm, intercostal-muscles, elastic-recoil

### Question Q-11.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What are tidal volume and vital capacity?
**Answer**: Tidal volume is the volume of air breathed in and out during normal, resting breathing (~0.5 dm³ per breath). Vital capacity is the maximum volume of air that can be forcibly exhaled after the deepest possible inhalation (~3-5 dm³ in adults). Tidal volume is always smaller than vital capacity. Spirometer traces show tidal volume as the small regular wave amplitude; vital capacity as the maximum peak-to-trough height.
**Explanation**: Tidal = normal breath; vital capacity = max forced breath. A common error is confusing vital capacity with total lung capacity.
**Tags**: tidal-volume, vital-capacity, spirometer, lung-volumes

### Question Q-11.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does exercise affect breathing rate and depth? Explain the physiological trigger.
**Answer**: During exercise, both breathing rate (breaths per minute) and depth (tidal volume) increase. The **primary trigger** is rising CO₂ concentration in the blood (not falling O₂). Muscle cells respire more to release energy for contraction → produce more CO₂ → CO₂ dissolves in blood plasma forming carbonic acid → lowers blood pH → detected by chemoreceptors in the medulla oblongata (brainstem) → the medulla sends impulses to diaphragm and intercostal muscles to contract more frequently and more forcefully. This increases ventilation to exhale excess CO₂ and inhale more O₂. Adrenaline release during exercise also stimulates faster breathing.
**Explanation**: CO₂↑ → pH↓ → chemoreceptors → medulla → rate↑ + depth↑. A common error is saying the trigger is "need for oxygen" — CO₂ is the primary driver.
**Tags**: exercise, breathing-rate, CO2, chemoreceptors, medulla-oblongata

### Question Q-11.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What happens to the breathing rate and depth during recovery after exercise? Explain why it stays elevated.
**Answer**: After exercise stops, breathing rate and depth remain elevated for several minutes and gradually return to resting levels. Reasons: (1) Oxygen debt — the body must repay the O₂ that was "borrowed" during anaerobic respiration. O₂ is needed to convert accumulated lactic acid back to glucose in the liver (Cori cycle) and to replenish myoglobin O₂ stores in muscles. (2) Excess CO₂ removal — residual CO₂ accumulated in blood and tissues must be exhaled. (3) Elevated body temperature — higher temperature increases metabolic rate, requiring more O₂. The fitter the person, the faster recovery occurs (shorter oxygen debt period).
**Explanation**: Oxygen debt repayment + residual CO₂ removal + temperature effect. A common error is saying breathing rate returns to normal "immediately."
**Tags**: recovery, oxygen-debt, lactic-acid, breathing-rate

### Question Q-11.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Compare the roles of internal and external intercostal muscles during breathing.
**Answer**: External intercostals — contract during inhalation: pull ribs upward and outward, increasing thoracic volume. Relax during passive exhalation. Internal intercostals — contract during forced exhalation: pull ribs downward and inward, decreasing thoracic volume. Relax during inhalation. The two sets work antagonistically. At rest: external intercostals do the work (active inhalation), internal intercostals are relaxed. During exercise/coughing: both sets are actively used cyclically.
**Explanation**: External = inhalation (up/out), Internal = forced exhalation (down/in). A common error is saying both contract simultaneously during normal breathing.
**Tags**: intercostal-muscles, external, internal, antagonistic, inhalation, exhalation

### Question Q-11.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how anaerobic respiration relates to breathing changes during intense exercise.
**Answer**: During intense exercise, O₂ demand exceeds O₂ supply. Muscles switch to anaerobic respiration: glucose → lactic acid (+ small ATP). This produces energy without O₂ but is inefficient (2 ATP per glucose vs 38 ATP aerobically) and produces lactic acid as a toxic by-product. Breathing cannot supply enough O₂ fast enough to meet demand during the activity itself — the respiratory and circulatory systems have a lag. The accumulated lactic acid causes muscle fatigue and cramps. After exercise, the oxygen debt is repaid — extra O₂ is used to oxidise lactic acid back to pyruvate → CO₂ + H₂O (in liver) or convert it to glucose (gluconeogenesis). This is why breathing stays elevated during recovery.
**Explanation**: Anaerobic→lactic acid→O₂ debt→elevated post-exercise breathing. A common error is saying anaerobic respiration occurs "instead of" aerobic — they occur simultaneously during intense exercise.
**Tags**: anaerobic-respiration, lactic-acid, oxygen-debt, exercise

### Question Q-11.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student measures their breathing rate at rest and after running 400 metres. Explain the shape of the spirometer trace they would expect over a 15-minute period (2 min rest, 2 min run, 11 min recovery), and explain the physiological reasons for each phase.
**Answer**: Phase 1 (rest, 0-2 min): regular, low-amplitude trace — tidal volume ~0.5 dm³, rate ~12-15 breaths/min. Phase 2 (exercise, 2-4 min): dramatic increase in both rate (~30-40 breaths/min) and depth (~2-3 dm³ tidal volume). Amplitude and frequency both spike. Trace rises because the person is using O₂ from the spirometer. Phase 3 (early recovery, 4-8 min): rate and depth gradually decrease but remain above resting. Body is repaying oxygen debt — oxidising lactic acid, replenishing myoglobin O₂, removing excess CO₂. Phase 4 (late recovery, 8-15 min): trace returns to resting levels. If the person is fit, recovery is faster (e.g., 5-7 min); if unfit, recovery is slower (>10 min). The total volume of extra O₂ consumed during recovery equals the oxygen debt.
**Explanation**: Four-phase trace linked to CO₂ drive, O₂ debt, fitness. A common error is describing only rate changes without explaining the spirometer trace.
**Tags**: spirometer, exercise, oxygen-debt, recovery, breathing-rate

### Question Q-11.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain how the respiratory system and circulatory system work together to meet increased oxygen demand during exercise.
**Answer**: Coordination: (1) Medulla oblongata detects ↑CO₂/↓pH → signals respiratory muscles to increase ventilation rate and depth (respiratory response). (2) Also signals the sinoatrial node (SAN) to increase heart rate → cardiac output ↑ (circulatory response). (3) Increased ventilation brings more O₂ into alveoli and removes more CO₂. (4) Increased heart rate pumps blood faster through pulmonary capillaries → maintains steep O₂/CO₂ concentration gradients across alveolar walls. (5) During exercise, arterioles in muscles vasodilate (widen) → more blood flow to active muscles; arterioles in gut vasoconstrict → divert blood to muscles. (6) Haemoglobin releases O₂ more readily at higher temperature and lower pH (Bohr effect) → more O₂ delivered to respiring muscle cells. The two systems are not independent — they are co-regulated by the medulla oblongata to match O₂ supply to O₂ demand.
**Explanation**: Respiratory + circulatory co-regulation by medulla; Bohr effect. A common error is describing them as separate systems without showing how they coordinate.
**Tags**: respiratory-system, circulatory-system, coordination, medulla, Bohr-effect, exercise

### Question Q-11.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A trained athlete and an untrained person perform the same exercise. Predict and explain the differences in their breathing rate responses during and after exercise.
**Answer**: During exercise: (1) Athlete's breathing rate increases less for the same workload (more efficient lungs — greater vital capacity, stronger respiratory muscles). (2) Athlete's tidal volume is larger (more air per breath) so rate does not need to rise as much. (3) Athlete's heart stroke volume is larger → more O₂ delivered per heartbeat → less anaerobic respiration → less lactic acid. After exercise: (4) Athlete's recovery is faster — breathing rate returns to resting sooner. Reasons: athlete has more mitochondria per muscle cell (more aerobic capacity), greater myoglobin stores (more O₂ reservoir), more efficient cardiovascular system (clears lactate faster), and a higher lactate threshold (tolerates higher intensity before switching to anaerobic). The athlete's smaller oxygen debt means less post-exercise O₂ needed. Spirometer traces would show: athlete — smaller amplitude increase, faster return to baseline; untrained — larger amplitude spike, prolonged elevated recovery.
**Explanation**: Athlete = less rate increase, faster recovery; physiological basis. A common error is saying "athletes don't breathe hard" — they do, but more efficiently.
**Tags**: athlete, breathing-rate, recovery, oxygen-debt, fitness, ventilation
