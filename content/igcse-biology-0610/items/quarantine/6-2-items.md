# Items: 6.2 — Factors affecting photosynthesis, limiting factors

### Question Q-6.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Name three factors that can limit the rate of photosynthesis.
**Answer**: Light intensity, carbon dioxide concentration, and temperature. Chlorophyll amount can also be a limiting factor (e.g., in a magnesium-deficient plant where the plant cannot make enough chlorophyll).
**Explanation**: Light, CO₂, temperature. A common error is including "oxygen" — oxygen is a product, not a limiting factor.
**Tags**: photosynthesis, limiting-factors, light, CO2, temperature

### Question Q-6.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a "limiting factor" in photosynthesis?
**Answer**: A limiting factor is the factor that is in shortest supply and therefore limits (restricts) the rate of photosynthesis. Even if all other factors are abundant, the rate cannot increase until the limiting factor is also increased. The concept follows the Law of Limiting Factors.
**Explanation**: Shortest supply restricts the rate. A common error is saying a limiting factor "stops" photosynthesis completely.
**Tags**: limiting-factor, definition, photosynthesis

### Question Q-6.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe how increasing light intensity affects the rate of photosynthesis, referring to the shape of the graph.
**Answer**: At low light intensity, the rate of photosynthesis increases proportionally with light (straight line on graph). The rate then levels off to a plateau — at this point, CO₂ concentration or temperature has become the limiting factor. Increasing light beyond this point has no effect on the rate. If CO₂ or temperature were then increased, the rate could rise further until a new limiting factor takes over.
**Explanation**: Linear rise → plateau; another factor limits. A common error is saying the rate keeps increasing indefinitely.
**Tags**: light-intensity, graph, plateau, limiting-factor

### Question Q-6.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe how to investigate the effect of light intensity on photosynthesis using pondweed.
**Answer**: (1) Place pondweed (Elodea) in a beaker of water with sodium hydrogen carbonate (NaHCO₃) as a CO₂ source. (2) Place a lamp at a fixed distance from the beaker. (3) Count the number of oxygen bubbles produced per minute. (4) Repeat at different lamp distances (e.g., 10, 20, 30, 40, 50 cm). (5) The closer the lamp (higher light intensity), the more bubbles per minute. Rate ∝ bubble count. Control temperature with a water bath to keep it constant.
**Explanation**: Pondweed, lamp distance, bubble count, NaHCO₃. A common error is not controlling temperature (heat from lamp).
**Tags**: photosynthesis, experiment, pondweed, light-intensity, method

### Question Q-6.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how temperature affects the rate of photosynthesis.
**Answer**: Photosynthesis is controlled by enzymes. At low temperatures, enzymes have less kinetic energy and the rate is slow. As temperature rises towards the optimum (25-30°C for most plants), the rate increases. Above the optimum, enzymes involved in photosynthesis begin to denature, and the rate drops sharply. At very high temperatures (>45°C), photosynthesis stops as all relevant enzymes are denatured.
**Explanation**: Enzyme-controlled: low → optimum → denature. A common error is forgetting that photosynthesis involves enzymes.
**Tags**: temperature, enzymes, optimum, denaturation

### Question Q-6.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why adding more CO₂ to a greenhouse increases crop yield up to a point, but not beyond.
**Answer**: CO₂ is a raw material for photosynthesis. Adding CO₂ increases the rate of photosynthesis because more CO₂ molecules are available to react with water. However, once another factor (light intensity or temperature) becomes limiting, further increases in CO₂ have no additional effect. In most greenhouses, the optimal CO₂ concentration is about 0.1-0.2% (atmospheric CO₂ is ~0.04%). Beyond this, another factor must be increased simultaneously.
**Explanation**: CO₂ increases rate → another factor becomes limiting → no further gain. A common error is saying "more CO₂ always helps."
**Tags**: CO2, photosynthesis, greenhouse, limiting-factors

### Question Q-6.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A greenhouse farmer wants to maximise tomato yield. Explain how they could manipulate light, CO₂, and temperature simultaneously, and why increasing only one factor is insufficient.
**Answer**: The farmer should: (1) Provide supplementary artificial lighting to extend the photoperiod and increase light intensity. (2) Add CO₂ from a burner or generator to raise concentration to about 0.1-0.2%. (3) Maintain temperature at 25-30°C using heating/ventilation. All three must be raised together because if only one is increased, another quickly becomes the limiting factor. For example, doubling CO₂ with no extra light has little effect because light limits the rate. Simultaneous optimisation ensures no single factor bottlenecks photosynthesis. However, the farmer must balance cost vs yield — CO₂ addition and heating are expensive.
**Explanation**: Raise all three together; each can bottleneck. Cost-benefit trade-off. A common error is suggesting only one change.
**Tags**: greenhouse, tomato, limiting-factors, simultaneous, optimisation

### Question Q-6.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student investigates light intensity using pondweed but notices that bubbles stop appearing at very high light intensities. Another student claims "photosynthesis has stopped." Evaluate this claim.
**Answer**: The claim is incorrect. At very high light intensities, photosynthesis is still occurring — but oxygen is being produced at a constant maximum rate (plateau reached). The rate does NOT decrease at high light — it simply does not increase further. Bubbles still appear, just not more frequently. If bubbles stop completely, one of these is likely: (1) the lamp is too close and the water has heated up, denaturing the enzymes; (2) CO₂ (from NaHCO₃) has been depleted; or (3) the plant is damaged. True cessation of photosynthesis would require enzyme denaturation (extreme heat), not high light alone. The experiment should control temperature with a heat shield or water bath.
**Explanation**: High light → plateau (not stop). If bubbles stop → overheating or CO₂ depletion. A common error is confusing plateau with stopping.
**Tags**: pondweed, plateau, evaluation, temperature-control

### Question Q-6.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: On a hot, bright summer day, a plant's stomata close to conserve water. Predict the effect on the rate of photosynthesis and explain why. If CO₂ is the limiting factor, what could a farmer do to maintain photosynthesis?
**Answer**: When stomata close, CO₂ cannot diffuse into the leaf. CO₂ becomes the limiting factor — photosynthesis rate drops sharply, even though light intensity is high. This is because CO₂ is a raw material for the Calvin cycle. The plant cannot make glucose without CO₂. A farmer could: (1) in a greenhouse, artificially increase CO₂ concentration so that even when stomata partially close, enough CO₂ enters; (2) use shade cloth to reduce light and temperature, reducing water loss so stomata can stay more open; (3) ensure adequate watering so the plant does not need to close stomata to conserve water.
**Explanation**: Closed stomata → no CO₂ entry → CO₂ limits → rate drops. Solutions: add CO₂, shade, water. A common error is saying light limits on a bright day.
**Tags**: stomata, CO2, water-conservation, trade-off, greenhouse
