### Question Q-16.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is natural selection? Describe the process.
**Answer**: Natural selection is the process by which organisms better adapted to their environment survive and reproduce more successfully. Steps: (1) Variation exists in a population (due to mutation). (2) There is competition for limited resources. (3) Individuals with advantageous traits survive and reproduce (survival of the fittest). (4) Advantageous genes are passed to offspring. (5) Over time, the population changes.
**Explanation**: Variation → competition → survival → reproduction → change. A common error is saying individuals "evolve" (populations evolve).
**Tags**: natural-selection, evolution, variation, adaptation

### Question Q-17.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a producer, consumer, and decomposer? Give an example of each.
**Answer**: Producer — makes its own food by photosynthesis (e.g., green plant, algae). Consumer — feeds on other organisms (e.g., rabbit, human). Primary consumer eats producers; secondary eats primary. Decomposer — breaks down dead matter (e.g., bacteria, fungi). All three are needed for nutrient cycling.
**Explanation**: Producer makes food, consumer eats, decomposer breaks down. A common error is saying decomposers are "consumers."
**Tags**: producer, consumer, decomposer, trophic-level

### Question Q-17.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe the nitrogen cycle.
**Answer**: Nitrogen moves through ecosystems: (1) Nitrogen fixation — bacteria convert atmospheric N₂ to nitrates in soil. (2) Plants absorb nitrates to make proteins. (3) Animals eat plants. (4) Decomposers break down dead matter, releasing ammonia (ammonification). (5) Nitrifying bacteria convert ammonia to nitrates. (6) Denitrifying bacteria convert nitrates back to N₂ gas.
**Explanation**: Fixation, absorption, feeding, decomposition, nitrification, denitrification. A common error is forgetting nitrogen fixation.
**Tags**: nitrogen-cycle, fixation, nitrification, denitrification

### Question Q-17.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is competition in an ecosystem? Give an example.
**Answer**: Competition occurs when organisms vie for the same limited resource. Intraspecific competition: between members of the same species (e.g., two oak trees competing for light). Interspecific competition: between different species (e.g., foxes and owls competing for mice). Competition limits population sizes and can drive natural selection.
**Explanation**: Same resource, intra vs inter-specific. A common error is saying competition is "always physical fighting."
**Tags**: competition, intraspecific, interspecific, resources

### Question Q-18.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How is biotechnology used in sewage treatment?
**Answer**: Sewage treatment uses bacteria to break down organic waste. In the aeration tank, bacteria digest organic matter (proteins, carbohydrates, fats) using oxygen supplied by bubbling air. The bacteria convert waste into CO₂, water, and mineral salts. Solid sludge is separated and the treated water is released. This prevents pollution of waterways.
**Explanation**: Bacteria digest organic waste, aeration, treated water. A common error is saying chemicals treat sewage.
**Tags**: sewage-treatment, bacteria, biotechnology, aeration

### Question Q-18.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How is genetic engineering used to produce herbicide-resistant crops?
**Answer**: A gene for herbicide resistance is identified and cut from a resistant organism using restriction enzymes. It is inserted into a plasmid vector and introduced into crop plant cells. The modified plants grow normally when herbicide is sprayed — the herbicide kills weeds but not the crop. This increases yield and reduces the need for manual weeding.
**Explanation**: Resistance gene → plasmid → crop plants → herbicide kills weeds only. A common error is saying the crop "produces" herbicide.
**Tags**: herbicide-resistant, genetic-engineering, crops, weeds
