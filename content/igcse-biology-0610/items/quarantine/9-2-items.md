# Items: 9.2 — Blood: components and functions

### Question Q-9.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Name the four components of blood and state one function of each.
**Answer**: Red blood cells — carry oxygen (haemoglobin). White blood cells — defend against pathogens (phagocytosis, antibodies). Platelets — blood clotting (prevent blood loss and pathogen entry). Plasma — transports dissolved substances (glucose, CO₂, urea, hormones, ions).
**Explanation**: RBC, WBC, platelets, plasma. A common error is saying plasma carries oxygen.
**Tags**: blood, components, RBC, WBC, platelets, plasma

### Question Q-9.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is plasma and why is it important?
**Answer**: Plasma is the liquid component of blood (about 55% of total blood volume). It is about 90% water with dissolved substances. It transports: dissolved nutrients (glucose, amino acids), waste products (CO₂, urea), hormones, heat, mineral ions, and plasma proteins (fibrinogen for clotting, albumin for osmotic balance). Without plasma as a solvent, blood cells could not flow and dissolved substances could not be transported.
**Explanation**: Liquid transport medium, solvent. A common error is saying plasma carries oxygen (RBCs carry O₂).
**Tags**: plasma, transport, solvent, nutrients, waste

### Question Q-9.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How do white blood cells defend the body against pathogens?
**Answer**: Phagocytes engulf and digest pathogens by phagocytosis — the cell extends cytoplasm around the pathogen, forms a vesicle, and fuses with lysosomes containing digestive enzymes that break the pathogen down. Lymphocytes produce specific antibodies that bind to pathogen antigens, marking them for destruction or clumping them together (agglutination). Lymphocytes also produce antitoxins that neutralise bacterial toxins.
**Explanation**: Phagocytosis + antibodies + antitoxins. A common error is saying all WBCs "eat" pathogens — lymphocytes don't.
**Tags**: white-blood-cells, phagocytosis, antibodies, lymphocytes

### Question Q-9.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What substances are transported in blood plasma?
**Answer**: Plasma transports: (1) Glucose and amino acids from the small intestine to cells. (2) Carbon dioxide from respiring cells to the lungs (mostly as hydrogen carbonate ions). (3) Urea from the liver to the kidneys. (4) Hormones from endocrine glands to target organs. (5) Heat from metabolically active organs (liver, muscles) to cooler areas, distributing body temperature. (6) Mineral ions and vitamins to where they are needed. (7) Plasma proteins (fibrinogen, albumin, globulins).
**Explanation**: Transport medium for all dissolved substances. A common error is saying plasma carries oxygen (O₂ carried by haemoglobin in RBCs).
**Tags**: plasma, transport, glucose, CO2, urea, hormones, heat

### Question Q-9.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is blood clotting and why is it important?
**Answer**: Blood clotting is a cascade of enzyme-controlled reactions: damaged blood vessel → platelets gather at the wound → release clotting factors → convert prothrombin to thrombin → thrombin converts soluble fibrinogen into insoluble fibrin → fibrin forms a mesh that traps red blood cells and platelets → forms a clot (scab). Importance: (1) Prevents excessive blood loss. (2) Prevents pathogens from entering through the wound. (3) Allows wound healing to begin underneath the protective scab.
**Explanation**: Fibrinogen → fibrin cascade, scab formation. A common error is saying red blood cells cause clotting (platelets initiate).
**Tags**: blood-clotting, fibrin, platelets, thrombin, prothrombin

### Question Q-9.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How do platelets contribute to blood clotting?
**Answer**: When a blood vessel is damaged, platelets adhere to the exposed collagen at the wound site. They become activated and release clotting factors (thromboplastin). These factors trigger the conversion of prothrombin (in plasma) to the active enzyme thrombin. Thrombin catalyses the conversion of soluble fibrinogen into insoluble fibrin threads. Fibrin forms a mesh that traps more platelets and red blood cells, forming the clot. Platelets initiate the cascade but do not form the clot themselves.
**Explanation**: Platelets trigger cascade → fibrinogen → fibrin → mesh → clot. A common error is saying platelets "are" the clot.
**Tags**: platelets, blood-clotting, fibrin, cascade

### Question Q-9.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain why a person with haemophilia may bleed excessively from a small cut, referring to the blood clotting process.
**Answer**: Haemophilia is a genetic condition where one of the clotting factors (usually Factor VIII) is missing or defective. The clotting cascade requires all clotting factors to work in sequence. If Factor VIII is absent, the cascade is broken — prothrombin cannot be converted to thrombin, so fibrinogen cannot be converted to fibrin. Without fibrin, the mesh that traps blood cells cannot form, and clotting is severely impaired. A small cut continues to bleed because the normal clot-forming mechanism does not work. Treatment: injections of the missing clotting factor (Factor VIII concentrate).
**Explanation**: Missing Factor VIII → cascade broken → no fibrin → no clot. A common error is saying haemophiliacs "have no platelets."
**Tags**: haemophilia, clotting-cascade, Factor-VIII, genetic-disorder

### Question Q-9.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the two main types of white blood cells (phagocytes and lymphocytes) in terms of their appearance, mode of action, and specificity.
**Answer**: Phagocytes — appearance: irregular shape, lobed nucleus, can change shape (amoeboid movement). Mode of action: non-specific engulfing and digestion (phagocytosis) of any foreign particle or pathogen. Do not distinguish between different pathogens. Lymphocytes — appearance: large round nucleus, small cytoplasm. Mode of action: highly specific — produce antibodies that match only one specific antigen on one specific pathogen. Memory lymphocytes remain after infection, providing long-term immunity (rapid response on re-exposure). Phagocytes provide immediate, broad defence; lymphocytes provide specific, memory-based defence.
**Explanation**: Non-specific (phagocytes) vs specific (lymphocytes). A common error is saying phagocytes "recognise" specific pathogens like lymphocytes do.
**Tags**: phagocytes, lymphocytes, comparison, specific, non-specific, immunity

### Question Q-9.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A blood sample is centrifuged. Describe what would be observed in the centrifuge tube and explain what each layer represents.
**Answer**: After centrifugation, blood separates into three distinct layers (from bottom to top): (1) Bottom layer (≈45%): Red blood cells (erythrocytes) — heaviest, packed densely. Dark red colour. (2) Middle layer (<1%): Buffy coat — white blood cells (leukocytes) and platelets (thrombocytes). Thin, whitish layer. (3) Top layer (≈55%): Plasma — pale yellow, straw-coloured liquid. The ratio of RBC volume to total blood volume is the haematocrit. Normal haematocrit: ~45% (male) or ~40% (female). An abnormally low haematocrit may indicate anaemia; abnormally high may indicate dehydration or polycythaemia. If the plasma is cloudy/white, this may indicate high lipid (fat) content after a fatty meal.
**Explanation**: Centrifugation → RBCs bottom, buffy coat middle, plasma top. A common error is saying the buffy coat is "fat" (it's WBCs + platelets).
**Tags**: centrifugation, blood-layers, haematocrit, RBC, plasma, buffy-coat
