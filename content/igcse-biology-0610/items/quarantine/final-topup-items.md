### Question Q-6.1-09
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does a plant use the glucose produced by photosynthesis?
**Answer**: Glucose is used for: (1) Respiration — releasing energy for cell activities. (2) Making starch — stored in roots, stems, and leaves. (3) Making cellulose — for cell walls. (4) Making proteins — combined with nitrates from soil. (5) Making fats/oils — stored in seeds. (6) Making sucrose — transported to other parts of the plant.
**Explanation**: Respiration, starch, cellulose, proteins, fats, transport. A common error is saying glucose is "only used for energy."
**Tags**: glucose, photosynthesis, starch, cellulose, proteins

### Question Q-9.1-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why do veins have valves but arteries do not?
**Answer**: Veins have valves to prevent backflow of blood because blood pressure in veins is low. Valves ensure blood flows in one direction (towards the heart). Arteries do not need valves because blood pressure from the heart is high enough to keep blood flowing forward. The only exceptions are semilunar valves at the heart exit.
**Explanation**: Low pressure in veins → valves needed. High pressure in arteries → no valves. A common error is saying arteries have valves.
**Tags**: veins, valves, arteries, pressure

### Question Q-11.1-08
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the function of the intercostal muscles during breathing?
**Answer**: External intercostal muscles contract during inhalation — they pull the ribs up and out, increasing thoracic volume. Internal intercostal muscles contract during forced exhalation — they pull the ribs down and in, decreasing thoracic volume. Both sets work antagonistically to control rib movement.
**Explanation**: External = up/out (inhalation), internal = down/in (forced exhalation). A common error is saying intercostals "pump" air.
**Tags**: intercostal-muscles, breathing, ribs, thoracic-volume

### Question Q-14.1-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a synapse and how does it work?
**Answer**: A synapse is the gap between two neurones (or between a neurone and an effector). When an impulse reaches the end of a neurone, it triggers the release of neurotransmitter chemicals. These diffuse across the synaptic gap and bind to receptors on the next cell, triggering a new impulse. This ensures impulses travel in one direction only.
**Explanation**: Gap, neurotransmitter, diffusion, one direction. A common error is saying impulses "jump" across the gap.
**Tags**: synapse, neurotransmitter, gap, impulse

### Question Q-16.1-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the difference between genotype and phenotype?
**Answer**: Genotype is the genetic makeup of an organism — the specific alleles it carries (e.g., Bb, BB, bb). Phenotype is the observable characteristic produced by the genotype (e.g., brown eyes, tall plant). Two organisms with different genotypes may have the same phenotype (e.g., BB and Bb both show brown eyes if B is dominant).
**Explanation**: Genotype = genetic code, phenotype = observable trait. A common error is using them interchangeably.
**Tags**: genotype, phenotype, alleles, characteristics
