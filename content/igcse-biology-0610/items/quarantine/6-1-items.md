# Items: 6.1 — Photosynthesis: process and equation

### Question Q-6.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Write the word equation for photosynthesis.
**Answer**: Carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen (in the presence of light and chlorophyll). Light energy is absorbed by chlorophyll and converted into chemical energy stored in glucose.
**Explanation**: CO₂ + H₂O → glucose + O₂ with light + chlorophyll. A common error is forgetting chlorophyll or saying oxygen is "made from" CO₂.
**Tags**: photosynthesis, equation, chlorophyll

### Question Q-6.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What are the raw materials and products of photosynthesis?
**Answer**: Raw materials: carbon dioxide (from air, enters through stomata) and water (from soil, absorbed by roots). Products: glucose (used for energy/storage/growth) and oxygen (released as a waste product through stomata). Light energy is needed but is not a "raw material."
**Explanation**: CO₂ + H₂O → glucose + O₂. A common error is calling sunlight a raw material.
**Tags**: photosynthesis, raw-materials, products

### Question Q-6.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the role of chlorophyll in photosynthesis?
**Answer**: Chlorophyll absorbs light energy (mainly red and blue wavelengths) and converts it to chemical energy. This energy is used to split water molecules (photolysis) and drive the reactions that produce glucose from carbon dioxide. Chlorophyll is located in chloroplasts in plant cells.
**Explanation**: Absorbs light energy, converts to chemical. A common error is saying chlorophyll "makes" glucose directly.
**Tags**: chlorophyll, light-energy, chloroplasts

### Question Q-6.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe how to test a leaf for the presence of starch and explain why each step is necessary.
**Answer**: (1) Boil the leaf in water for 2 min — kills cells and breaks down cell membranes. (2) Boil in ethanol in a water bath for 5 min — removes chlorophyll (leaf turns white), making it easier to see the colour change. (3) Wash in warm water — softens the leaf. (4) Add iodine solution — blue-black colour indicates starch. A leaf kept in darkness as a control should test negative.
**Explanation**: Kill → remove chlorophyll → iodine → blue-black. A common error is adding iodine directly to a green leaf.
**Tags**: starch-test, leaf, iodine, method

### Question Q-6.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What happens to the glucose produced by photosynthesis?
**Answer**: Glucose is used for: (1) respiration — to release energy. (2) Conversion to starch for storage (in chloroplasts, tubers). (3) Synthesis of cellulose for cell walls. (4) Synthesis of proteins (requires nitrate ions from soil). (5) Synthesis of lipids for storage in seeds. (6) Transported as sucrose in phloem to other plant parts.
**Explanation**: Multiple uses. A common error is saying glucose is "stored as glucose."
**Tags**: glucose, photosynthesis, products, uses

### Question Q-6.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why a variegated leaf is used to show that chlorophyll is needed for photosynthesis.
**Answer**: A variegated leaf has green areas (contain chlorophyll) and white areas (no chlorophyll). After testing for starch, only the green areas turn blue-black with iodine. The white areas test negative for starch, proving that photosynthesis (and therefore starch production) only occurs where chlorophyll is present. The white area serves as an internal control.
**Explanation**: Green = starch +, white = starch -. A common error is saying the white areas "have no water."
**Tags**: variegated-leaf, chlorophyll, starch-test, control

### Question Q-6.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Describe how to design an experiment to show that carbon dioxide is required for photosynthesis.
**Answer**: (1) Place a potted plant in the dark for 48 hours to destarch it. (2) Enclose one leaf in a flask containing sodium hydroxide (absorbs CO₂) and another leaf in a flask with water (control — has CO₂). (3) Place the plant in bright light for 6 hours. (4) Test both leaves for starch. Result: the leaf without CO₂ (NaOH flask) tests negative; the control leaf tests positive. This proves CO₂ is needed for photosynthesis. The plant must be destarched first so any starch found is newly produced.
**Explanation**: Destarch → NaOH absorbs CO₂ → light → test starch. A common error is not destarching the plant first.
**Tags**: photosynthesis, CO₂, experiment, destarch, control

### Question Q-6.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Aquatic plants like Elodea produce oxygen bubbles during photosynthesis. A student observes that bubbles stop appearing when the lamp is moved far away. Explain this observation using the concept of limiting factors, and predict what would happen if CO₂ concentration were increased at the far lamp distance.
**Answer**: When the lamp is far away, light intensity is very low — light becomes the limiting factor. Photosynthesis cannot proceed at a measurable rate because there is insufficient light energy to drive the reactions, so little to no oxygen is produced. Bubble production stops at the compensation point (photosynthesis rate = respiration rate). If CO₂ were increased at the far lamp distance, the rate would NOT increase because light is already limiting — CO₂ is no longer the limiting factor. This demonstrates that factors interact: increasing a non-limiting factor has no effect.
**Explanation**: Light limits → no O₂; increase CO₂ has no effect when light limits. A common error is saying "more CO₂ always increases photosynthesis."
**Tags**: Elodea, limiting-factor, light, CO₂, bubbles

### Question Q-6.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student says, "Plants get their food from the soil." Evaluate this statement using your knowledge of photosynthesis.
**Answer**: The statement is largely incorrect. Plants make their own food (glucose) through photosynthesis using carbon dioxide from the air and water from the soil, with light energy. The soil provides: (1) water, which is used in photosynthesis; (2) mineral ions (nitrates, phosphates, potassium, magnesium) which are needed for making proteins, DNA, chlorophyll, etc. — but these are NOT food. Minerals are raw materials for synthesis, not energy sources. The plant's actual food (energy-containing organic molecules) is glucose, which is synthesised by the plant itself, not taken from the soil. The statement reflects a common misconception that confuses mineral nutrients with food.
**Explanation**: Plants make glucose (food) from CO₂+H₂O. Soil provides minerals, not food. A common error is conflating nutrients with food.
**Tags**: photosynthesis, soil, minerals, glucose, evaluation
