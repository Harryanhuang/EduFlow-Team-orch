# Items: 13.1 — Excretion: definition, organs, and urea formation

### Question Q-13.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is excretion? Why is it necessary?
**Answer**: Excretion is the removal of waste products of metabolism (toxic by-products of chemical reactions in cells) from the body. It is necessary because metabolic wastes — CO₂ (from respiration), urea (from deamination of excess amino acids), and excess water/salts — are toxic if allowed to accumulate. Accumulation disrupts enzyme activity, pH balance, and osmotic balance, eventually causing cell death. The main excretory products, organs, and routes are: CO₂ → lungs (exhaled); urea + excess H₂O + excess ions → kidneys (urine); H₂O + salts + trace urea → skin (sweat).
**Explanation**: Removal of metabolic waste, prevents toxicity. A common error is confusing excretion with egestion (removal of undigested food, not metabolic waste).
**Tags**: excretion, definition, metabolic-waste, toxic, homeostasis

### Question Q-13.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the difference between excretion and egestion?
**Answer**: Excretion — removal of metabolic waste products (CO₂, urea, excess water/salts) produced by chemical reactions inside cells. These substances were once part of the body's metabolism. Egestion — removal of undigested food material (faeces) from the alimentary canal via the anus. Faeces were never inside body cells; they are materials that were never absorbed. Key distinction: excreted substances were inside cells/metabolic pathways; egested substances were never absorbed, never entered cells.
**Explanation**: Metabolic waste (inside cells) vs undigested food (never absorbed). A common error is calling faeces "excreted."
**Tags**: excretion, egestion, difference, faeces, metabolic-waste

### Question Q-13.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Name the main excretory organs in humans and state what each removes.
**Answer**: (1) Lungs — remove CO₂ produced by respiration (exhaled). (2) Kidneys — remove urea (from liver deamination of excess amino acids), excess water, excess mineral ions/salts (urine). (3) Skin — removes water, salts, and small amounts of urea via sweat (also functions in thermoregulation). (4) Liver — deaminates excess amino acids to produce urea (sent to kidneys) and breaks down haemoglobin from old RBCs to bile pigments (excreted in faeces via bile). The liver is NOT directly an excretory organ (it produces urea but the kidneys excrete it).
**Explanation**: Four organs with specific waste products. A common error is saying liver "excretes" directly — it processes/metabolises; kidneys excrete.
**Tags**: excretion, organs, lungs, kidneys, skin, liver, urea

### Question Q-13.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How is urea produced in the body? Explain the role of the liver.
**Answer**: Urea is produced in the liver via deamination. Excess amino acids (from protein digestion when intake exceeds need for protein synthesis) cannot be stored. In the liver hepatocytes, the amino group (–NH₂) is removed from the amino acid (deamination). The amino group is converted to ammonia (NH₃, highly toxic), then rapidly converted to urea (CO(NH₂)₂, less toxic) via the ornithine cycle. The remaining carbon skeleton (keto acid) is used in respiration (energy) or converted to glycogen/fat for storage. Urea enters the bloodstream → transported to kidneys → filtered out in urine. This is a detoxification pathway.
**Explanation**: Deamination in liver: amino acid → NH₃ → urea (ornithine cycle). Keto acid used for energy. A common error is saying urea comes from "digestion" directly.
**Tags**: urea, liver, deamination, amino-acids, ornithine-cycle, detoxification

### Question Q-13.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why is carbon dioxide considered a waste product that must be excreted?
**Answer**: CO₂ is a waste product of aerobic respiration (occurs continuously in all respiring cells). If it accumulates: CO₂ dissolves in blood plasma → forms carbonic acid (H₂CO₃) → dissociates to H⁺ + HCO₃⁻ → lowers blood pH (respiratory acidosis) → enzymes denature at low pH → cellular metabolism disrupted. Normal blood pH is 7.35-7.45. Even small shifts are dangerous. CO₂ is transported in blood to the lungs (as HCO₃⁻ in plasma, bound to haemoglobin as carbamino-haemoglobin, and dissolved) and excreted by exhalation. The breathing rate is primarily controlled by blood CO₂/pH, not O₂ — demonstrating the toxicity priority.
**Explanation**: CO₂ → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ → pH↓ → enzyme disruption. CO₂ drives breathing rate. A common error is saying CO₂ is "not harmful."
**Tags**: CO2, waste, pH, respiratory-acidosis, homeostasis

### Question Q-13.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Compare the roles of the liver and kidneys in excretion.
**Answer**: Liver — metabolic processing: (1) deaminates excess amino acids → urea produced. (2) Breaks down haemoglobin from old RBCs → bilirubin (bile pigment, excreted via bile into faeces). (3) Detoxifies alcohol, drugs, hormones → breakdown products excreted by kidneys. The liver converts toxic substances into forms that can be excreted by other organs. Kidneys — filtration and excretion: (1) Filter urea, excess water, excess ions from blood. (2) Form urine. (3) Regulate water potential of blood (osmoregulation). The liver does the chemical conversion; the kidneys do the physical filtration and removal. Neither organ can function without the other in the excretory system.
**Explanation**: Liver = metabolic processing/detox; Kidney = filtration/excretion. Complementary. A common error is treating them as independent.
**Tags**: liver, kidney, excretion, comparison, detoxification, urea

### Question Q-13.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain the significance of excretion for homeostasis, giving examples of how three different excretory products are linked to homeostatic balance.
**Answer**: Excretion maintains homeostasis — the constant internal environment. Three examples: (1) CO₂ — excretion by lungs maintains blood pH at 7.35-7.45. CO₂ accumulation → ↓pH (acidosis) → enzyme denaturation. Increased breathing rate during exercise removes excess CO₂ → pH restored. (2) Urea — excretion by kidneys maintains nitrogen balance. Excess amino acids cannot be stored → deamination in liver produces urea → kidney filtration removes urea. Without excretion, urea accumulates (uraemia) → toxic to brain, nausea, death. (3) Water — excretion by kidneys (urine volume varies with ADH) + skin (sweat) maintains osmotic balance. Water excess → dilute urine. Water deficit → concentrated urine + thirst + reduced sweating. Each excretory product is directly tied to a homeostatic variable (pH, nitrogen balance, osmotic balance), and excretion is the regulatory pathway that keeps the variable within limits.
**Explanation**: CO₂→pH, Urea→nitrogen, H₂O→osmoregulation. Excretion = homeostatic regulatory pathway. A common error is describing excretion products without linking to the homeostatic variable.
**Tags**: homeostasis, excretion, pH, osmoregulation, nitrogen-balance, urea

### Question Q-13.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A person eats a very high-protein diet for a month. Explain how the excretory system responds to handle the increased protein intake, describing what happens in the liver and kidneys.
**Answer**: High protein intake → more amino acids absorbed from digestion than needed for protein synthesis. Response: (1) Liver: increased deamination rate. More amino acids are broken down → more NH₃ produced → more urea produced via the ornithine cycle. The carbon skeletons (keto acids) are used for respiration (energy) or converted to glycogen/fat — long-term excess can lead to weight gain. (2) Kidneys: increased urea in blood → increased filtration load on glomeruli. More urea in glomerular filtrate → more urea removed in urine. (3) Water: urea is an osmotic diuretic — increased urea in filtrate draws more water into urine (osmosis), increasing urine volume → increased water loss. The person will be thirstier and drink more to compensate. (4) Long-term: chronically high protein intake may strain kidneys (hyperfiltration). The urine will have higher urea concentration and possibly lower pH (sulphur-containing amino acids → sulphate → acidic urine). This demonstrates the liver-kidney excretory axis responding to dietary variation to maintain homeostasis.
**Explanation**: High protein → ↑deamination → ↑urea → ↑filtration → ↑urine volume. Liver-kidney axis. A common error is describing only one organ.
**Tags**: high-protein-diet, liver, kidney, deamination, urea, homeostasis

### Question Q-13.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Distinguish between excretion, egestion, and secretion, giving clear examples of each. Explain why a student who says "the liver excretes bile" is partially incorrect.
**Answer**: Excretion — removal of metabolic waste products produced by cells (e.g., CO₂ exhaled by lungs, urea in urine). The substance was inside cells and is toxic if accumulated. Egestion — removal of undigested, unabsorbed material from the alimentary canal (e.g., faeces). The material never entered cells. Secretion — release of useful substances produced by cells for a specific function (e.g., bile from liver, enzymes from pancreas, hormones from endocrine glands). Secreted substances are functional, not waste. "The liver excretes bile" is partially incorrect because: (1) Bile is a secretion — it has a function (emulsifying fats, neutralising acid). (2) However, bile also contains bilirubin (breakdown product of haemoglobin from old RBCs) — this component IS excretory. (3) The bile is secreted into the duodenum, and bilirubin leaves the body in faeces — so the excretion pathway is liver→bile→gut→faeces, making faeces both egested (undigested food) and excreted (bile pigments). This blurring of categories is a common exam pitfall.
**Explanation**: Excretion (metabolic waste), Egestion (unabsorbed), Secretion (functional). Bile straddles secretion + excretion. A common error is treating the three terms as simple synonyms.
**Tags**: excretion, egestion, secretion, bile, distinction
