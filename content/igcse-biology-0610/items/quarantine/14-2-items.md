# Items: 14.2 — Hormones and endocrine system

### Question Q-14.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a hormone? Name three glands and the hormone each produces.
**Answer**: A hormone is a chemical messenger produced by an endocrine gland, secreted directly into the bloodstream, and carried to target organs where it produces a specific effect. Hormones regulate long-term processes: growth, metabolism, reproduction, and homeostasis. Examples: (1) Pancreas — insulin (lowers blood glucose) + glucagon (raises blood glucose). (2) Adrenal glands — adrenaline (fight-or-flight response). (3) Pituitary gland — ADH (controls water reabsorption in kidneys), growth hormone. (4) Thyroid gland — thyroxine (metabolic rate). (5) Ovaries/testes — oestrogen/testosterone (secondary sexual characteristics).
**Explanation**: Chemical messenger, gland → blood → target, specific effect. A common error is saying hormones travel in nerves.
**Tags**: hormone, definition, gland, endocrine, blood

### Question Q-14.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is negative feedback? Give an example from homeostasis.
**Answer**: Negative feedback is a control mechanism that reverses a deviation from the set point to maintain a steady internal state (homeostasis). When a level changes, the body detects the change and triggers a response to return it to normal. Example — blood glucose: high glucose → pancreas releases insulin → liver converts glucose → glycogen → glucose falls → insulin release stops. Low glucose → pancreas releases glucagon → liver converts glycogen → glucose → glucose rises → glucagon release stops. The response is always opposite to the stimulus — hence "negative."
**Explanation**: Detects deviation → triggers opposing response → returns to set point. A common error is saying negative feedback "amplifies" the change (that's positive feedback).
**Tags**: negative-feedback, homeostasis, set-point, insulin

### Question Q-14.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe the role of insulin and glucagon in controlling blood glucose concentration using negative feedback.
**Answer**: This is a dual-hormone negative feedback system. High blood glucose (after eating): β-cells in pancreatic islets of Langerhans detect high glucose → secrete insulin into blood → insulin binds to liver cell receptors → stimulates: (a) increased glucose uptake, (b) enzyme activation for glycogenesis (glucose → glycogen), (c) increased glucose → fat conversion. Blood glucose decreases back to set point (~90 mg/100 cm³). Low blood glucose (between meals, exercise): α-cells in islets detect low glucose → secrete glucagon → glucagon binds to liver receptors → stimulates glycogenolysis (glycogen → glucose) + gluconeogenesis (amino acids → glucose). Blood glucose increases back to set point. Both hormones stop when the set point is reached — this is negative feedback. Insulin and glucagon are antagonistic hormones.
**Explanation**: Insulin (β-cells) ↓glucose; Glucagon (α-cells) ↑glucose. Dual antagonistic negative feedback. A common error is reversing which hormone does what.
**Tags**: insulin, glucagon, blood-glucose, pancreas, negative-feedback, glycogenesis, glycogenolysis

### Question Q-14.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Compare nervous and hormonal control systems in terms of speed, duration, pathway, and type of signal.
**Answer**: | Feature | Nervous | Hormonal |
|---------|----------|----------|
| Signal | Electrical impulse + chemical (neurotransmitter) | Chemical only (hormone) |
| Pathway | Specific neurone pathway (wired) | Bloodstream (broadcast) |
| Speed | Very fast (milliseconds) | Slower (seconds to hours) |
| Duration | Short-lived (impulse stops quickly) | Long-lasting (minutes to days) |
| Target | Very specific (one effector) | Can be widespread (multiple organs) |
| Example | Withdrawal reflex | Blood glucose regulation, growth |

Both are communication systems. The nervous system is for rapid, precise responses. The hormonal system is for sustained, body-wide regulation. They often interact — the hypothalamus links both.
**Explanation**: Fast/electrical/wired vs slow/chemical/broadcast. A common error is saying hormones are "faster" than nerves.
**Tags**: nervous, hormonal, comparison, speed, duration, signal

### Question Q-14.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe the role of adrenaline in the fight-or-flight response. Which gland produces it?
**Answer**: Adrenaline is produced by the adrenal glands (on top of each kidney). Released during stress, fear, or excitement following nerve impulses from the hypothalamus via sympathetic nerves. Effects: (1) Heart rate and stroke volume increase → more blood pumped. (2) Breathing rate and depth increase → more O₂ intake, more CO₂ removal. (3) Pupils dilate → more light enters eye. (4) Liver converts glycogen → glucose (glycogenolysis) → more energy substrate in blood. (5) Blood diverted from skin and digestive system to skeletal muscles (vasoconstriction in gut/skin, vasodilation in muscles). (6) Hair erector muscles contract → hairs stand on end ("goosebumps"). (7) Mental alertness increases. All prepare the body for rapid physical action — fight the threat or run from it.
**Explanation**: Adrenal → heart↑, breathing↑, glucose↑, blood to muscles. A common error is saying adrenaline "gives energy" directly (it mobilises glucose stores).
**Tags**: adrenaline, fight-or-flight, adrenal-gland, glycogenolysis

### Question Q-14.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is Type 1 diabetes, what causes it, and how is it treated?
**Answer**: Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition where the immune system destroys the insulin-producing β-cells in the pancreatic islets of Langerhans. Result: little or no insulin produced. Blood glucose rises dangerously after eating because glucose cannot enter cells efficiently and the liver does not convert glucose to glycogen. Symptoms: excessive thirst (polydipsia), frequent urination (polyuria — glucose in urine draws water by osmosis), tiredness (cells starved of glucose), weight loss (fat and protein broken down for energy). Treatment: (1) Regular insulin injections (subcutaneous, cannot be taken as a tablet — insulin is a protein and would be digested). (2) Blood glucose monitoring before meals. (3) Carbohydrate counting — matching insulin dose to carbohydrate intake. (4) Healthy diet and exercise. Type 1 is NOT caused by diet or lifestyle and cannot be prevented. It is a lifelong condition, not curable.
**Explanation**: Autoimmune destruction of β-cells → no insulin → hyperglycaemia → insulin injections. A common error is saying Type 1 is caused by eating too much sugar.
**Tags**: diabetes, Type-1, insulin, β-cells, autoimmune, hyperglycaemia

### Question Q-14.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes in terms of cause, insulin status, typical age of onset, and treatment.
**Answer**: | Feature | Type 1 | Type 2 |
|---------|--------|--------|
| Cause | Autoimmune destruction of β-cells | Insulin resistance + β-cell exhaustion |
| Insulin | Little or none produced | May be normal, low, or high (but cells don't respond) |
| Onset age | Usually childhood/young adult | Usually middle-aged/older (increasingly in young due to obesity) |
| Body type | Usually lean | Often overweight/obese |
| % of diabetes | ~10% | ~90% |
| Treatment | Insulin injections needed | Diet, exercise, oral drugs (metformin), sometimes insulin later |
| Preventable | No (autoimmune) | Yes (healthy weight, exercise, diet) |

Key distinction: Type 1 = insulin deficiency (lack of production); Type 2 = insulin resistance (cells don't respond to insulin). Type 1 patients become dangerously hyperglycaemic without insulin; Type 2 patients often produce enough insulin for years but their cells become less sensitive.
**Explanation**: Type 1 = no insulin production (autoimmune); Type 2 = insulin resistance (often lifestyle-linked). A common error is treating them as interchangeable.
**Tags**: diabetes, Type-1, Type-2, insulin-resistance, comparison

### Question Q-14.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain how the pancreas acts as both an endocrine and exocrine organ, and why this dual function is important.
**Answer**: Exocrine function (~99% of pancreatic tissue): pancreatic acinar cells secrete digestive enzymes (amylase, protease/trypsinogen, lipase) and sodium hydrogen carbonate into a duct system → pancreatic duct → duodenum. This aids digestion. Endocrine function (~1% of tissue): islets of Langerhans scattered among exocrine tissue. α-cells secrete glucagon (raises blood glucose). β-cells secrete insulin (lowers blood glucose). Both hormones enter the blood directly (no duct). The dual function is important because: the pancreas coordinates digestion AND glucose homeostasis — after a meal, digestive enzymes break down food (exocrine) while insulin regulates the resulting blood glucose rise (endocrine). A failure in either function has serious consequences: exocrine failure → malnutrition (can't digest); endocrine failure → diabetes (can't regulate glucose). This organ illustrates that "gland" is not binary — some organs serve both functions.
**Explanation**: Exocrine (digestive enzymes via duct) + Endocrine (insulin/glucagon via blood). A common error is treating the pancreas as only an endocrine organ.
**Tags**: pancreas, exocrine, endocrine, islets-of-Langerhans, dual-function

### Question Q-14.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A person eats a large sugary meal. Describe the sequence of hormonal responses over the next 3 hours, explaining how blood glucose is maintained within a narrow range.
**Answer**: Timeline: (0 min, meal eaten) — carbohydrates digested, glucose absorbed into blood from small intestine, blood glucose rises. (15-60 min): pancreatic β-cells detect rising glucose → secrete insulin into blood. Insulin binds to receptors on: liver cells (activate glucokinase → glucose enters, glycogenesis → glycogen stored), muscle cells (GLUT4 transporters inserted → glucose uptake), adipose tissue (glucose → fat). Blood glucose peaks ~30-60 min after meal, then begins falling. (60-90 min): blood glucose returns toward set point → insulin secretion decreases proportionally. (90-180 min): blood glucose drifts toward lower end of normal range. Pancreatic α-cells detect this → secrete glucagon → liver performs glycogenolysis (glycogen → glucose) + gluconeogenesis (amino acids → glucose). Blood glucose stabilises at ~90 mg/100 cm³. This negative feedback system ensures blood glucose never rises too high (damages blood vessels, osmotic problems) nor falls too low (brain requires constant glucose, cannot use fat). The timing of insulin release and the sensitivity of α/β cells determine glucose tolerance.
**Explanation**: 3-hour timeline: insulin↑ (0-60 min) → glucose↓ → insulin↓ → glucagon↑ (90-180 min) → glucose stabilised. A common error is describing only insulin and forgetting the glucagon phase.
**Tags**: blood-glucose, insulin, glucagon, negative-feedback, postprandial, glycogen
