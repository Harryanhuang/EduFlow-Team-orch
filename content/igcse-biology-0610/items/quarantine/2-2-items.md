# Items: 2.2 — Specialised cells and their functions

### Question Q-2.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a specialised cell? Give two examples.
**Answer**: A specialised cell has a specific structure adapted for a particular function. Examples: red blood cells (carrying oxygen — biconcave shape, no nucleus), root hair cells (absorbing water — long projection for large surface area).
**Explanation**: Structure adapted for function. A common error is describing a general cell instead of a specialised one.
**Tags**: specialised-cells, adaptation, function

### Question Q-2.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a nerve cell (neurone) and how is it adapted for its function?
**Answer**: Nerve cells transmit electrical impulses. Adaptations: long axon for transmitting signals over distances; branched dendrites to connect to other neurones; myelin sheath (in some neurones) to speed up impulse transmission.
**Explanation**: Axon, dendrites, myelin. A common error is confusing a nerve cell with a muscle cell.
**Tags**: nerve-cell, neurone, impulse, axon

### Question Q-2.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how a red blood cell is adapted for its function.
**Answer**: Red blood cells carry oxygen. Adaptations: biconcave disc shape increases surface area for oxygen absorption; no nucleus leaves more space for haemoglobin (oxygen-carrying protein); thin cell membrane allows oxygen to diffuse through easily; small size allows them to fit through narrow capillaries.
**Explanation**: Shape, no nucleus, haemoglobin, thin membrane. A common error is saying RBCs have a nucleus.
**Tags**: red-blood-cell, adaptation, haemoglobin, oxygen

### Question Q-2.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How is a root hair cell adapted for absorbing water and mineral ions?
**Answer**: Root hair cells have a long, thin projection (root hair) that greatly increases the surface area for absorption. They have a large permanent vacuole that helps draw water in by osmosis. They contain many mitochondria to provide energy for active transport of mineral ions.
**Explanation**: Large surface area, vacuole, mitochondria. A common error is saying they absorb by diffusion (mineral ions use active transport).
**Tags**: root-hair-cell, absorption, surface-area, osmosis

### Question Q-2.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How is a sperm cell adapted for its function?
**Answer**: Sperm cells fertilise egg cells. Adaptations: tail (flagellum) for swimming towards the egg; many mitochondria in the middle section to provide energy for movement; acrosome (enzyme-filled cap) in the head to break through the egg's outer layers; haploid nucleus with half the normal chromosome number.
**Explanation**: Tail, mitochondria, acrosome, haploid. A common error is saying sperm has a diploid nucleus.
**Tags**: sperm-cell, adaptation, fertilisation

### Question Q-2.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How is a palisade mesophyll cell adapted for photosynthesis?
**Answer**: Palisade mesophyll cells are packed with chloroplasts (containing chlorophyll) to absorb maximum light. They are column-shaped and closely packed near the upper leaf surface where light intensity is highest. They have a large permanent vacuole to push chloroplasts towards the cell edge for better light capture.
**Explanation**: Chloroplasts, column shape, position. A common error is saying palisade cells have "no vacuole."
**Tags**: palisade-cell, photosynthesis, chloroplasts, adaptation

### Question Q-2.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Ciliated cells line the trachea. Explain how their structure relates to their function, and predict what would happen if cilia stopped beating.
**Answer**: Ciliated cells have hair-like projections (cilia) on their surface that beat rhythmically to sweep mucus (containing trapped dust and pathogens) up towards the throat, where it is swallowed. If cilia stopped beating, mucus would accumulate in the airways, blocking airflow (breathing difficulty) and trapping pathogens against the airway lining (increased infection risk). This happens in smokers where tar paralyses cilia.
**Explanation**: Cilia beat → move mucus; cilia stop → accumulation + infection. A common error is saying mucus "dissolves."
**Tags**: ciliated-cell, trachea, mucus, prediction

### Question Q-2.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare and contrast the adaptions of xylem and phloem cells, explaining how each is suited to its transport role.
**Answer**: Xylem: dead cells with no cytoplasm or organelles — forms a hollow tube; cell walls strengthened with lignin (waterproof, prevents collapse); no end walls — continuous column for water transport. Phloem: living cells with cytoplasm; sieve plates (perforated end walls) allow sugars to pass; companion cells alongside provide energy (ATP) for active transport of sugars into and out of phloem. Xylem transports water passively; phloem transports sugars actively requiring energy.
**Explanation**: Xylem: dead/hollow/lignin. Phloem: living/sieve-plates/companion. A common error is saying both are dead or both use active transport.
**Tags**: xylem, phloem, transport, comparison

### Question Q-2.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student draws a diagram of a specialised cell and labels it "has no nucleus, therefore it is dead." Evaluate whether this statement is always correct, using two examples of specialised cells.
**Answer**: The statement is partially correct but oversimplified. Red blood cells lose their nucleus during development and are indeed "dead" in the sense they cannot repair themselves or divide — but they still carry out their function (oxygen transport) for ~120 days. Xylem vessels are dead cells with no contents (no nucleus, no cytoplasm) — they form empty tubes for water transport. However, sieve tube cells (phloem) are living but also have no nucleus — they are supported by companion cells. So "no nucleus = dead" is true for RBCs and xylem, but the lack of nucleus alone does not determine if a cell is alive.
**Explanation**: RBCs + xylem: no nucleus, dead. Phloem: no nucleus, alive + companion. A common error is assuming all enucleate cells are dead.
**Tags**: nucleus, dead-cell, RBC, xylem, phloem, evaluation
