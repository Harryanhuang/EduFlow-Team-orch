### Question Q-4.1-08
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the role of magnesium ions in plant nutrition?
**Answer**: Magnesium ions are needed for the synthesis of chlorophyll. Without magnesium, plants cannot make chlorophyll and their leaves turn yellow (chlorosis) between the veins. Magnesium is absorbed from the soil by root hair cells through active transport. It is transported in the xylem to leaves.
**Explanation**: Magnesium → chlorophyll → no Mg = yellow leaves. A common error is saying magnesium is needed for photosynthesis directly (it's needed for chlorophyll, which is needed for photosynthesis).
**Tags**: magnesium, chlorophyll, plant-nutrition, chlorosis

### Question Q-5.1-08
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does bile aid in the digestion of fats?
**Answer**: Bile is alkaline and neutralises acidic chyme from the stomach, providing the optimum pH for lipase to work in the small intestine. Bile also emulsifies fats — it breaks large fat droplets into many small droplets, increasing the surface area for lipase to act on. Bile is made in the liver, stored in the gall bladder.
**Explanation**: Neutralises acid + emulsifies fats. A common error is saying bile "digests" fat (it doesn't contain enzymes).
**Tags**: bile, emulsification, lipase, fat-digestion

### Question Q-8.1-08
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What happens to the body when adrenaline is released?
**Answer**: Adrenaline increases heart rate, increases breathing rate and depth, dilates pupils, diverts blood to muscles (away from skin and gut), triggers glycogen breakdown to glucose (for energy), and increases alertness. These changes prepare the body for "fight or flight" — responding quickly to danger.
**Explanation**: HR up, breathing up, blood to muscles, glycogen → glucose. A common error is saying adrenaline "gives energy" (it triggers release of stored energy).
**Tags**: adrenaline, fight-or-flight, hormone, response

### Question Q-11.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the difference between a reflex arc and a voluntary response?
**Answer**: A reflex arc is fast, automatic, and involuntary — it does not involve the conscious part of the brain (impulse goes spinal cord → relay neurone → motor neurone). A voluntary response involves the brain making a conscious decision. Reflexes protect the body from harm (e.g., withdrawing hand from hot surface) before the brain registers pain.
**Explanation**: Fast, automatic, no conscious brain. A common error is saying reflexes "don't involve the brain at all" (some involve the brainstem).
**Tags**: reflex, voluntary, spinal-cord, automatic

### Question Q-13.1-08
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does the body maintain a constant body temperature when it is hot?
**Answer**: When hot: (1) Sweating — sweat glands secrete sweat, which evaporates and cools the skin. (2) Vasodilation — blood vessels near the skin widen, increasing blood flow to the surface, losing heat by radiation. (3) Hairs lie flat (erector muscles relax). These mechanisms are controlled by the thermoregulatory centre in the hypothalamus.
**Explanation**: Sweating, vasodilation, hairs flat. A common error is saying sweat "cools" directly (it's evaporation that cools).
**Tags**: thermoregulation, sweating, vasodilation, homeostasis

### Question Q-15.1-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What are the advantages of asexual reproduction in plants?
**Answer**: Asexual reproduction is faster because it does not require pollination or seed formation. It produces genetically identical offspring (clones), so successful traits are preserved. Only one parent is needed. It allows rapid colonisation of an area. However, it provides no genetic variation, making the population vulnerable to disease.
**Explanation**: Faster, one parent, clones, no variation. A common error is saying asexual reproduction is "better" than sexual (it depends on environment).
**Tags**: asexual-reproduction, plants, clones, advantages

### Question Q-17.2-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is natural selection and how does it lead to evolution?
**Answer**: Natural selection: (1) There is variation in a population (due to mutation). (2) Some individuals have traits better suited to the environment. (3) These individuals are more likely to survive and reproduce. (4) They pass the advantageous alleles to offspring. (5) Over many generations, the frequency of the advantageous allele increases — this is evolution.
**Explanation**: Variation → selection → survival → reproduction → allele frequency change. A common error is saying individuals "evolve" (populations evolve, not individuals).
**Tags**: natural-selection, evolution, variation, alleles
