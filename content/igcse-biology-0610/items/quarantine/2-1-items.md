# Items: 2.1 — Cell structure: plant and animal cells

### Question Q-2.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Name three structures found in both plant and animal cells.
**Answer**: Nucleus (contains genetic material), cytoplasm (where chemical reactions occur), cell membrane (controls what enters and leaves the cell), mitochondria (site of respiration), and ribosomes (protein synthesis). Any three accepted.
**Explanation**: Shared structures. A common error is including "cell wall" as common to both.
**Tags**: cell-structures, nucleus, cytoplasm, membrane

### Question Q-2.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Name two structures found in plant cells but not in animal cells.
**Answer**: Cell wall (made of cellulose, provides support and shape) and chloroplasts (contain chlorophyll for photosynthesis). Plant cells also have a large permanent vacuole containing cell sap.
**Explanation**: Cell wall + chloroplasts. A common error is saying "mitochondria" (those are in both).
**Tags**: plant-cell, cell-wall, chloroplasts, vacuole

### Question Q-2.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the function of the nucleus in a cell?
**Answer**: The nucleus controls cell activities and contains genetic material (DNA) in the form of chromosomes. It controls what proteins the cell makes and therefore determines cell structure and function.
**Explanation**: Control centre, DNA, chromosomes. A common error is saying the nucleus "makes energy."
**Tags**: nucleus, DNA, chromosomes, function

### Question Q-2.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why do muscle cells contain many mitochondria?
**Answer**: Mitochondria are the site of aerobic respiration, which releases energy. Muscle cells need a large amount of energy for contraction and movement, so they contain many mitochondria to meet this energy demand.
**Explanation**: Energy for contraction. A common error is saying mitochondria "store energy."
**Tags**: mitochondria, muscle, respiration, energy

### Question Q-2.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain the function of the cell membrane and how it controls what enters and leaves the cell.
**Answer**: The cell membrane is partially permeable (selectively permeable) — it allows small molecules like water and oxygen to pass through freely, but controls the movement of larger molecules like glucose and ions. It has protein channels and carrier proteins that selectively transport specific substances in and out of the cell.
**Explanation**: Selectively permeable + protein channels. A common error is saying the membrane is "fully permeable" or "lets everything through."
**Tags**: cell-membrane, selectively-permeable, transport

### Question Q-2.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the function of ribosomes in a cell?
**Answer**: Ribosomes are the site of protein synthesis. They translate the genetic code from DNA (via mRNA) into proteins. Cells that produce large amounts of protein (e.g., pancreas cells making enzymes) have many ribosomes.
**Explanation**: Protein synthesis. A common error is saying ribosomes "make energy."
**Tags**: ribosomes, protein-synthesis, function

### Question Q-2.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A cell is observed under a microscope. It has a cell wall, chloroplasts, and a large vacuole. Identify the type of cell and explain which structures confirm your answer.
**Answer**: This is a plant cell. The presence of a cell wall (not found in animal cells), chloroplasts (for photosynthesis, not in animal cells), and a large permanent vacuole (characteristic of plant cells) confirms this identification.
**Explanation**: Three distinguishing features. A common error is only mentioning one feature.
**Tags**: plant-cell, identification, microscope

### Question Q-2.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain why a bacterial cell is different from both plant and animal cells, even though it shares some features with both. Refer to at least three differences.
**Answer**: Bacterial cells are prokaryotic: (1) They have no nucleus — their DNA is in a single circular chromosome floating in the cytoplasm (not in a membrane-bound nucleus). (2) They are much smaller (1-5 μm) than plant/animal cells (10-100 μm). (3) They have no membrane-bound organelles — no mitochondria, no chloroplasts, no Golgi apparatus. Their cell wall is made of peptidoglycan (not cellulose like plants). They do share a cell membrane, cytoplasm, and ribosomes with plant/animal cells, but their ribosomes are smaller.
**Explanation**: Prokaryotic vs eukaryotic. A common error is saying bacteria have "no DNA" — they do, just not in a nucleus.
**Tags**: bacteria, prokaryotic, eukaryotic, differences

### Question Q-2.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student claims that all cells with a cell wall are plant cells. Evaluate this statement using your knowledge of cell structure.
**Answer**: The statement is incorrect. While plant cells do have a cell wall (made of cellulose), other organisms also have cell walls: (1) Fungal cells have cell walls made of chitin. (2) Bacterial cells have cell walls made of peptidoglycan. (3) Some protists also have cell walls. The key distinction is the material: cellulose (plants) vs chitin (fungi) vs peptidoglycan (bacteria). Additionally, animal cells have NO cell wall at all.
**Explanation**: Cell walls in multiple kingdoms. A common error is thinking only plants have cell walls.
**Tags**: cell-wall, cellulose, chitin, peptidoglycan, evaluation
