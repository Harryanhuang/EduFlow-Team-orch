# Items: 13.2 — Kidney structure and function, dialysis

### Question Q-13.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Describe the gross structure of the kidney including the main blood vessels.
**Answer**: The kidney has: (1) renal artery — branches from aorta, carries blood with urea + excess water/ions TO the kidney at high pressure for filtration. (2) renal vein — carries filtered blood (less urea, balanced water/ions) FROM the kidney back to vena cava. (3) ureter — tube carrying urine from the kidney to the bladder. (4) capsule — outer protective layer. (5) cortex — outer region, contains glomeruli and Bowman's capsules. (6) medulla — inner region, contains loops of Henle and collecting ducts. (7) pelvis — central cavity where urine collects before entering the ureter.
**Explanation**: Renal artery (in), renal vein (out), ureter (urine out), cortex, medulla, pelvis. A common error is confusing renal artery and renal vein direction/contents.
**Tags**: kidney, structure, renal-artery, renal-vein, ureter, cortex, medulla

### Question Q-13.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the function of the nephron in the kidney?
**Answer**: The nephron is the functional unit of the kidney (~1 million per kidney). It performs: (1) Ultrafiltration — blood is filtered under high pressure in the glomerulus/Bowman's capsule, producing glomerular filtrate containing water, glucose, ions, and urea (but not proteins or blood cells). (2) Selective reabsorption — all glucose, most water, and needed ions are reabsorbed back into the blood in the proximal convoluted tubule. (3) Urine concentration — urea, excess water, and excess ions remain in the filtrate, become concentrated in the collecting duct, and form urine. Each step occurs in a specific nephron region.
**Explanation**: Nephron = filter + reabsorb + concentrate. Ultrafiltration + selective reabsorption + urine formation. A common error is saying the nephron "makes urea."
**Tags**: nephron, kidney, ultrafiltration, reabsorption, glomerulus

### Question Q-13.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe the process of ultrafiltration in the glomerulus and Bowman's capsule.
**Answer**: Blood enters the glomerulus (a knot of capillaries) via the afferent arteriole at high pressure. The efferent arteriole is narrower than the afferent → creates high hydrostatic pressure in glomerular capillaries. This pressure forces water, glucose, amino acids, urea, and ions through the capillary wall, through the basement membrane, and through the podocytes (filtration slits) into the Bowman's capsule. Large molecules (proteins, blood cells) are too large to pass through and remain in the blood. The resulting fluid is the glomerular filtrate — ~125 cm³/min in two kidneys (180 dm³/day, but >99% reabsorbed). Ultrafiltration is passive — no energy required.
**Explanation**: High pressure from afferent > efferent diameter → forces small molecules through filter. Proteins/cells retained. A common error is saying proteins pass into filtrate.
**Tags**: ultrafiltration, glomerulus, Bowman's-capsule, hydrostatic-pressure, filtrate

### Question Q-13.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain what happens during selective reabsorption in the proximal convoluted tubule.
**Answer**: In the proximal convoluted tubule (PCT): (1) All glucose is reabsorbed from the filtrate back into the blood by active transport and facilitated diffusion (requires ATP). (2) Most amino acids, vitamins, and hormones are reabsorbed by active transport. (3) ~80% of water is reabsorbed by osmosis (follows the solutes). (4) Most Na⁺, Cl⁻, and other useful ions are reabsorbed by active transport. The PCT cells have: microvilli (large surface area), many mitochondria (ATP for active transport), and numerous carrier proteins. After the PCT, the filtrate still contains urea, some water, and some ions. Glucose should NOT be present in urine — if it is, this indicates diabetes mellitus (blood glucose exceeds kidney threshold).
**Explanation**: All glucose reabsorbed, most water/ions reabsorbed. PCT cells adapted with microvilli + mitochondria. A common error is saying urea is reabsorbed.
**Tags**: selective-reabsorption, PCT, glucose, active-transport, microvilli

### Question Q-13.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does the composition of urine differ from blood plasma? Explain why each difference exists.
**Answer**: | Substance | Plasma | Urine | Reason |
|-----------|--------|-------|--------|
| Urea | Low (~0.03%) | High (~2%) | Urea is concentrated — water reabsorbed, urea not |
| Glucose | ~0.1% | 0% | All glucose actively reabsorbed in PCT (normal health) |
| Protein | ~7% | 0% | Too large to pass glomerular filter |
| Blood cells | Present | 0% | Too large to pass filter |
| Water | ~92% | ~95% | Variable — ADH controls final water reabsorption in collecting duct |
| Na⁺, Cl⁻ | ~0.7% | ~0.4% | Most ions reabsorbed; excess excreted |

Urine is essentially plasma minus proteins, blood cells, glucose, and most water, with urea concentrated ~70×. If glucose or protein appear in urine, this is a diagnostic indicator (diabetes, kidney damage).
**Explanation**: Comparative table with reasons. A common error is saying urine normally contains glucose "a little bit."
**Tags**: urine, blood-plasma, composition, glucose, urea, comparison

### Question Q-13.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is dialysis, when is it needed, and how does it work?
**Answer**: Dialysis is an artificial blood filtration process needed when both kidneys fail (end-stage renal failure — GFR <15 mL/min). How it works: (1) Blood is drawn from a vein and passed through a dialyser containing thousands of hollow fibres of partially permeable membrane. (2) The fibres are bathed in dialysis fluid containing: NO urea (creates steep gradient → urea diffuses out of blood), normal ion concentrations (excess ions diffuse out), same glucose concentration as blood (glucose not lost). (3) The membrane allows small molecules (urea, ions, water) to pass; blood cells and proteins are retained. (4) Cleaned blood is returned to the body. (5) Heparin is added to prevent clotting. Sessions: typically 3-4 hours, 3 times per week. Dialysis manages but does NOT cure kidney failure — it is a life-sustaining treatment, not a cure.
**Explanation**: Artificial kidney — partially permeable membrane + dialysis fluid with correct concentration gradients. A common error is saying dialysis "cures" kidney failure.
**Tags**: dialysis, kidney-failure, partially-permeable-membrane, dialysis-fluid, urea

### Question Q-13.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare kidney transplant and dialysis as treatments for kidney failure, evaluating the advantages and disadvantages of each.
**Answer**: Kidney transplant: ADV — no regular dialysis sessions (freedom), fewer dietary restrictions, better quality of life, lower long-term cost. DIS — requires lifelong immunosuppressant drugs (risk of infections + cancer), risk of organ rejection, severe shortage of donor organs (waiting list), major surgery risk. Dialysis: ADV — no immunosuppressants, no surgery, immediately available. DIS — demanding schedule (12+ hours/week tethered to machine), strict diet (low protein, low salt, restricted fluids), time-consuming, fatigue, long-term vascular access complications, does not fully replace kidney function (anaemia, bone disease still occur). Overall: transplant offers better quality of life and is cost-effective long-term, but is limited by donor availability. Dialysis is a bridge-to-transplant or long-term management when transplant is not possible.
**Explanation**: Balanced pros/cons for both options. A common error is saying transplant "requires no drugs" or dialysis "is just as good as a kidney."
**Tags**: kidney-transplant, dialysis, comparison, immunosuppressant, quality-of-life

### Question Q-13.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain how the nephron's structure is adapted for its function, referring to specific regions and their adaptations.
**Answer**: (1) Glomerulus — highly coiled capillary knot with afferent arteriole wider than efferent → maintains high hydrostatic pressure for ultrafiltration. Fenestrated capillary walls have pores → allow small molecules to pass. (2) Bowman's capsule — surrounds glomerulus; podocytes have filtration slits; basement membrane acts as molecular filter. (3) PCT — cells have microvilli (brush border) → large surface area for reabsorption; many mitochondria → ATP for active transport of glucose/ions; tight junction → prevents back-leak. (4) Loop of Henle — long hairpin structure in medulla; descending limb permeable to water (osmosis out); ascending limb impermeable to water, actively transports Na⁺ out → creates medullary concentration gradient for water reabsorption in collecting duct. (5) Collecting duct — passes through salty medulla; water permeability controlled by ADH → fine-tunes final urine concentration. Each region's structure directly enables its specific step in filtration→reabsorption→concentration.
**Explanation**: Five regions with specific adaptations. A common error is listing nephron parts without explaining functional adaptations.
**Tags**: nephron, glomerulus, PCT, loop-of-Henle, collecting-duct, structure-function

### Question Q-13.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain how ADH (antidiuretic hormone) controls the water content of blood, using the concept of negative feedback.
**Answer**: Negative feedback loop: (1) Receptor: osmoreceptors in the hypothalamus detect blood water potential. (2) If water potential is too low (dehydrated — blood too salty): osmoreceptors signal the posterior pituitary gland to release MORE ADH into the blood. ADH travels to the kidney collecting ducts → increases their permeability to water (inserts aquaporin channels) → MORE water is reabsorbed from filtrate back into the blood → small volume of CONCENTRATED urine produced. (3) If water potential is too high (overhydrated): osmoreceptors signal pituitary to release LESS ADH → collecting ducts become less permeable → LESS water reabsorbed → large volume of DILUTE urine produced. (4) As water potential returns to normal (set point), ADH secretion returns to baseline. This is negative feedback — ADH reduces the deviation from the set point. Other homeostatic inputs: sweating (↑water loss → more ADH), alcohol (inhibits ADH → more urine → dehydration).
**Explanation**: Hypothalamus→pituitary→ADH→collecting duct permeability→water reabsorption→negative feedback. A common error is saying ADH "adds water" to blood.
**Tags**: ADH, osmoregulation, negative-feedback, hypothalamus, collecting-duct, aquaporin
