# Items: 10.2 — Defence mechanisms and immunity

### Question Q-10.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Describe the body's first line of defence against pathogens.
**Answer**: Physical barriers: intact skin (keratinised epidermis blocks pathogen entry), mucous membranes (sticky mucus traps pathogens in respiratory/digestive tracts). Chemical barriers: stomach acid (HCl at pH 1-2 kills most ingested pathogens), lysozyme enzyme in tears and saliva (breaks down bacterial cell walls). Mechanical barriers: cilia in airways (sweep trapped mucus + pathogens upward to throat to be swallowed).
**Explanation**: Skin, mucus, acid, cilia, lysozyme — all non-specific pre-infection barriers. A common error is calling these "immune system" (they are non-specific barriers, immunity is specific).
**Tags**: first-line-defence, skin, mucus, stomach-acid, lysozyme, cilia

### Question Q-10.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the difference between active and passive immunity?
**Answer**: Active immunity: the body produces its own antibodies after exposure to an antigen (through infection or vaccination). It takes days to develop but provides long-lasting protection because memory lymphocytes are produced. Passive immunity: antibodies are acquired ready-made from another source (mother to baby via placenta/breast milk, or antibody injection). It provides immediate protection but is short-lived (weeks-months) because no memory cells are produced.
**Explanation**: Body makes own antibodies + memory (long) vs receives antibodies (short). A common error is saying passive immunity involves memory cells.
**Tags**: active-immunity, passive-immunity, antibodies, memory-cells

### Question Q-10.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How do vaccines provide immunity without causing disease?
**Answer**: Vaccines contain dead or weakened (attenuated) pathogens, or pathogen fragments (antigens). These are unable to cause disease but are recognised as foreign by the immune system. Lymphocytes produce specific antibodies against these antigens, and memory lymphocytes are produced. On subsequent exposure to the real pathogen, memory lymphocytes rapidly divide and produce large amounts of the correct antibody, destroying the pathogen before it can cause illness. The person is immune without ever having been ill.
**Explanation**: Dead/weakened pathogen → antibody + memory cell response → rapid defence on re-exposure. A common error is saying vaccines "contain antibodies."
**Tags**: vaccine, immunity, memory-cells, antibodies, lymphocytes

### Question Q-10.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is herd immunity and why is it important?
**Answer**: Herd immunity occurs when a high percentage of the population is vaccinated, making it difficult for the pathogen to spread. Even unvaccinated individuals (newborns, immunocompromised, those who cannot be vaccinated for medical reasons) are protected because the chain of transmission is broken. The threshold varies by disease — for highly contagious measles it is ~95%, for polio ~80%. Without herd immunity, outbreaks can spread rapidly through unvaccinated populations.
**Explanation**: High vaccination coverage → transmission chain broken → protects unvaccinated. A common error is saying "everyone must be vaccinated" — herd immunity covers those who can't be.
**Tags**: herd-immunity, vaccination, transmission, population

### Question Q-10.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why antibiotics do not work against viral infections.
**Answer**: Antibiotics target specific bacterial structures or metabolic processes: (1) bacterial cell wall synthesis (e.g., penicillin blocks peptidoglycan cross-linking), (2) bacterial protein synthesis (target 70S ribosomes, different from human 80S), (3) bacterial DNA replication enzymes. Viruses have none of these structures — they are non-cellular and use the host cell's machinery to replicate. Antibiotics cannot target viruses without damaging the host's own cells. Using antibiotics for viral infections contributes to antibiotic resistance without any benefit. Antiviral drugs (e.g., acyclovir, oseltamivir) target virus-specific enzymes/processes.
**Explanation**: Antibiotics target bacterial-specific structures, viruses lack them. A common error is saying antibiotics "kill viruses too."
**Tags**: antibiotics, viruses, bacteria, mechanism, resistance

### Question Q-10.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why do organ transplant patients need immunosuppressant drugs, and what is the risk?
**Answer**: The immune system recognises the transplanted organ as "foreign" because cells carry different surface antigens (HLA proteins) from the donor. Without immunosuppressants, T-lymphocytes would attack the transplant, causing organ rejection. Immunosuppressants reduce this immune response, enabling the organ to survive. Risk: reduced immune function makes the patient more vulnerable to opportunistic infections (e.g., fungal pneumonia, CMV) because the immune system cannot respond effectively to real pathogens. Lifelong monitoring is required.
**Explanation**: Foreign antigens → immune attack → immunosuppressant needed; trade-off: infection risk. A common error is saying immunosuppressants "strengthen" the immune system.
**Tags**: transplant, immunosuppressant, rejection, antigens, infection-risk

### Question Q-10.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the body's second and third lines of defence against pathogens, explaining how phagocytes and lymphocytes work together.
**Answer**: Second line (non-specific): phagocytes (neutrophils, macrophages) — engulf any foreign particle by phagocytosis. They are attracted to infection sites by chemical signals (chemotaxis). No memory — same response to every pathogen. Third line (specific): lymphocytes — B-lymphocytes produce specific antibodies that match only one antigen; T-lymphocytes destroy infected host cells directly. Memory lymphocytes persist for years/decades. How they work together: (1) Phagocytes first arrive at infection site, engulf some pathogens. (2) Phagocytes present pathogen antigens on their surface (antigen presentation) to activate T-lymphocytes. (3) Activated T-lymphocytes stimulate B-lymphocytes to produce specific antibodies. (4) Antibodies bind to pathogens, marking them for phagocytosis (opsonisation). Phagocytes then engulf antibody-tagged pathogens more efficiently. The non-specific and specific systems are not separate — they are a coordinated defence cascade.
**Explanation**: Second line (non-specific phagocytes) + third line (specific lymphocytes) = coordinated cascade. A common error is treating them as independent systems.
**Tags**: phagocytes, lymphocytes, specific, non-specific, coordination, antigen-presentation

### Question Q-10.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain the immunological basis of why a person who has recovered from measles is unlikely to get measles again, but can still catch influenza.
**Answer**: Measles immunity: after infection, B-lymphocytes produce specific anti-measles antibodies. Memory B-lymphocytes and memory T-lymphocytes specific to measles virus remain in the body for life. On re-exposure, memory cells recognise the measles virus antigens and mount a rapid, strong response before the virus can establish infection. The person is immune for life. Influenza: the influenza virus mutates rapidly (antigenic drift — point mutations in haemagglutinin and neuraminidase surface proteins). The variant strain has slightly different antigens that the existing memory lymphocytes do not recognise. The person can be re-infected with the new strain. Additionally, antigenic shift (segment re-assortment between different influenza strains) can create entirely new strains (e.g., H1N1 → H5N1) against which no one has pre-existing immunity. This is why influenza vaccines must be updated annually.
**Explanation**: Memory cell specificity + viral mutation rate explains lifelong vs transient immunity. A common error is saying "the immune system forgets."
**Tags**: measles, influenza, memory-cells, antigenic-drift, mutation, immunity

### Question Q-10.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A child has a severely weakened immune system. Evaluate whether it is safe to give the child the MMR (measles, mumps, rubella) vaccine, which is a live attenuated vaccine. Consider both the risk to the child and the public health implications.
**Answer**: For an individual immunocompromised child: live attenuated vaccines contain weakened but still replicating pathogens. In a child with a severely weakened immune system, even attenuated viruses may replicate uncontrollably, causing vaccine-associated disease. Administering the MMR to an immunocompromised child is contraindicated by the WHO and national immunisation guidelines. This child should NOT receive live vaccines. Public health implications: (1) The child relies on herd immunity for protection — a high vaccination rate in the surrounding population (>95% for measles) prevents virus circulation. (2) All close contacts (family, classmates) should be fully vaccinated to protect the child (cocooning strategy). (3) Inactivated vaccines (e.g., inactivated polio vaccine) may be safe alternatives depending on the specific immune deficiency. (4) The child's medical team should assess which vaccines are safe on a case-by-case basis. This scenario illustrates why high vaccination coverage matters — it protects those who cannot be vaccinated.
**Explanation**: Live vaccine + immunocompromised → contraindicated; herd immunity protects. A common error is saying "give a smaller dose."
**Tags**: immunocompromised, live-vaccine, MMR, herd-immunity, contraindication
