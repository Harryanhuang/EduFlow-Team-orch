# Items: 8.1 — Transport tissues: xylem and phloem

### Question Q-8.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the difference between xylem and phloem?
**Answer**: Xylem transports water and mineral ions from roots to leaves (upwards only). Phloem transports sucrose and amino acids (assimilates) from sources (e.g., leaves) to sinks (e.g., roots, growing regions) in both directions. Xylem vessels are dead, hollow tubes; phloem consists of living sieve tube cells.
**Explanation**: Xylem = water up; Phloem = food both ways. A common error is saying phloem only goes down.
**Tags**: xylem, phloem, transport, translocation

### Question Q-8.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the function of root hair cells in water absorption?
**Answer**: Root hair cells are specialised epidermal cells with long extensions that increase the surface area for water and mineral ion absorption. They have thin cell walls for short diffusion distance and many mitochondria to provide energy for active transport of mineral ions. Water enters by osmosis from the soil.
**Explanation**: Large surface area, thin wall, mitochondria. A common error is saying root hairs "pump" water.
**Tags**: root-hair-cell, water-absorption, osmosis, surface-area

### Question Q-8.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How are xylem vessels adapted for their function?
**Answer**: Xylem vessels are long, hollow tubes with no cross-walls or cell contents (dead cells). The walls are thickened with lignin for strength and to prevent collapse. Water moves freely through the hollow lumen. Pits in the walls allow water to move sideways between vessels.
**Explanation**: Dead, hollow, lignin, pits. A common error is saying xylem cells are "alive."
**Tags**: xylem, adaptation, lignin, water-transport

### Question Q-8.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How are phloem sieve tubes adapted for translocation?
**Answer**: Phloem sieve tubes are made of living cells with sieve plates (perforated end walls) that allow sugars to flow through. They have few organelles to allow easy flow of substances. Companion cells provide energy (ATP from mitochondria) for active loading of sucrose into the sieve tubes.
**Explanation**: Sieve plates, living, companion cells provide energy. A common error is saying phloem cells are dead (xylem is dead).
**Tags**: phloem, sieve-tubes, translocation, companion-cells

### Question Q-8.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is translocation?
**Answer**: Translocation is the movement of sucrose and amino acids through the phloem from sources (where they are made or stored, e.g., leaves) to sinks (where they are used, e.g., growing regions, roots, fruits). Unlike xylem transport, translocation requires energy and can occur in both directions.
**Explanation**: Phloem transport, source to sink, energy needed. A common error is confusing translocation with transpiration.
**Tags**: translocation, phloem, sucrose, source, sink

### Question Q-8.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe how water moves through a plant from root to leaf.
**Answer**: Water enters root hair cells by osmosis. It moves across the root cortex by osmosis and the symplast/apoplast pathways. It enters xylem vessels in the root. Water moves up the xylem by the transpiration stream (cohesion and tension). It reaches the leaves where it is used in photosynthesis or lost by transpiration.
**Explanation**: Osmosis → root → xylem → transpiration stream → leaf. A common error is saying water is "pumped" up.
**Tags**: water-transport, root, xylem, transpiration-stream

### Question Q-8.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the structures of xylem and phloem and explain how each tissue's structure relates to its function.
**Answer**: Xylem: dead hollow cells arranged end-to-end forming continuous tubes. Lignin deposited in walls in spiral, ring, or reticulate patterns — provides mechanical strength for support and prevents vessel collapse under tension during transpiration. No end walls — allows unimpeded water column. Narrow bore — maintains cohesion and tension. Phloem: living sieve tube elements with perforated sieve plates — allows sugar solution to flow through. Companion cells attached to each sieve tube element — contain many mitochondria to produce ATP for active loading of sucrose into sieve tubes. Plasmodesmata connect companion cells to sieve elements for communication. The structural differences reflect the physical demands: xylem withstands tension (passive), phloem actively loads and transports (requires energy).
**Explanation**: Structure-function pairing for both tissues. A common error is describing the structures without linking each to its specific functional purpose.
**Tags**: xylem, phloem, structure-function, comparison

### Question Q-8.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain what would happen to a plant if its xylem vessels were blocked and how this differs from the effect of blocking phloem sieve tubes.
**Answer**: Xylem blockage: water and mineral ion transport from roots to leaves stops. Leaves wilt because cells lose turgor pressure (water cannot reach mesophyll cells). Photosynthesis stops as stomata close to conserve water, depriving the plant of CO₂. The plant eventually dies from desiccation. Phloem blockage: sucrose and amino acid transport from source to sink stops. Growing regions (root tips, shoot tips) are starved of sugars for respiration and amino acids for protein synthesis — growth halts. Stored starch in sink organs (e.g., developing fruits) is not replenished. Mature leaves may accumulate excess sugars. The plant survives longer than with xylem blockage because water can still reach leaves, but growth and reproduction are severely impaired.
**Explanation**: Xylem→wilting→death vs Phloem→growth halt. A common error is not distinguishing the downstream consequences.
**Tags**: xylem-blockage, phloem-blockage, consequences, comparison

### Question Q-8.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain how the structure of the plant transport system supports the cohesion-tension theory for water movement in xylem and the pressure-flow hypothesis for translocation in phloem.
**Answer**: Cohesion-tension in xylem: dead hollow vessels with no end walls form continuous narrow columns — ideal for strong cohesion between water molecules (H-bonds) that creates an unbroken chain from root to leaf. Lignified walls prevent collapse under the large negative pressure (tension) generated by transpiration at leaf surfaces. The narrow bore maintains the water column and prevents cavitation. Pressure-flow in phloem: living sieve tubes with perforated sieve plates allow sap to flow freely. Companion cells actively load sucrose (using ATP) into sieve tubes at the source, lowering water potential. Water enters from xylem by osmosis, increasing hydrostatic pressure at source. At the sink, sucrose is actively unloaded, water leaves by osmosis, reducing pressure. The pressure difference drives bulk flow of phloem sap from source to sink.
**Explanation**: Cohesion-tension→xylem structure; Pressure-flow→phloem structure. A common error is mixing up which theory applies to which tissue.
**Tags**: cohesion-tension, pressure-flow, xylem, phloem, transport-mechanisms
