### Question Q-10.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe how Salmonella bacteria cause disease.
**Answer**: Salmonella causes food poisoning. It is transmitted through contaminated food (especially raw eggs, poultry) and water. The bacteria multiply in the intestines and produce toxins that damage the gut lining. Symptoms: vomiting, diarrhoea, abdominal cramps, fever. Prevention: thorough cooking, proper food storage, hand washing.
**Explanation**: Food poisoning, contaminated food, toxins, symptoms. A common error is saying Salmonella is a virus.
**Tags**: Salmonella, food-poisoning, bacteria, symptoms

### Question Q-10.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the difference between a vaccination and receiving antibodies (passive immunisation)?
**Answer**: Vaccination (active): dead/weakened pathogen stimulates the body to produce its own antibodies and memory cells. Long-lasting protection. Passive immunisation: pre-made antibodies are injected. Immediate protection but short-lived (no memory cells produced). Used for emergency treatment after exposure.
**Explanation**: Body makes own vs receives antibodies. A common error is saying vaccinations "contain antibodies."
**Tags**: vaccination, passive-immunisation, antibodies, memory

### Question Q-11.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why is the concentration gradient important for gas exchange in the lungs?
**Answer**: A steep concentration gradient ensures rapid diffusion of oxygen from alveoli into blood and CO₂ from blood into alveoli. The gradient is maintained by: continuous blood flow (carrying away oxygen, bringing CO₂) and continuous ventilation (bringing fresh air, removing stale air). If the gradient is reduced, gas exchange slows.
**Explanation**: Steep gradient = fast diffusion, maintained by blood flow + ventilation. A common error is saying gas exchange "doesn't need a gradient."
**Tags**: concentration-gradient, gas-exchange, lungs, diffusion

### Question Q-11.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What happens to the breathing rate and depth during recovery after exercise?
**Answer**: Breathing rate and depth remain elevated after exercise stops. This is because the body must: remove excess CO₂ built up during exercise, repay the oxygen debt (replenish oxygen stores), and break down lactic acid in the liver. Breathing gradually returns to resting rate as these processes complete.
**Explanation**: Elevated to remove CO₂, repay oxygen debt, break down lactic acid. A common error is saying breathing "returns to normal immediately."
**Tags**: recovery, breathing, oxygen-debt, lactic-acid
