# Items: 15.3 — Human reproduction: gametes, fertilisation and development

### Question Q-15.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Name the male and female gametes in humans and state one difference between them.
**Answer**: Male gamete: sperm cell (spermatozoan). Female gamete: egg cell (ovum / oocyte). Key differences: sperm is small and motile (has a tail/flagellum); egg is large and non-motile (has a cytoplasm full of yolk nutrients); sperm is produced continuously from puberty; egg is released once per menstrual cycle.
**Explanation**: Sperm = small, motile, produced continuously. Egg = large, non-motile, stored nutrients. A common error is saying the egg is motile.
**Tags**: gametes, sperm, egg, ovum, differences

### Question Q-15.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Describe two adaptations of sperm for swimming to the egg, and two adaptations of the egg for receiving the sperm.
**Answer**: Sperm adaptations: (1) Tail/flagellum — whips to propel the sperm through the female reproductive tract. (2) Many mitochondria in the middle piece — provide energy for swimming. (3) Acrosome at the tip — contains enzymes to digest through the egg's jelly coat. Egg adaptations: (1) Jelly coat (zona pellucida) — contains receptors that recognise the species-specific sperm and only allows one sperm to enter. (2) Large cytoplasm containing yolk — provides nutrients for the early embryo before implantation.
**Explanation**: Sperm = motile (tail, mitochondria, acrosome enzymes). Egg = non-motile (coat, yolk nutrients). A common error is saying the egg has a tail.
**Tags**: sperm, egg, adaptations, acrosome, zona-pellucida, yolk

### Question Q-15.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe the process of fertilisation in humans, from copulation to the formation of the zygote.
**Answer**: During copulation, semen containing millions of sperm is deposited in the vagina. Sperm swim through the cervix and uterus into the oviduct. At the oviduct, many sperm surround the egg. The acrosome of each sperm releases enzymes to digest the jelly coat (zona pellucida) of the egg. One sperm penetrates through to the egg cell membrane. The sperm nucleus enters the egg and fuses with the egg nucleus to form a diploid zygote (46 chromosomes). The egg membrane immediately changes to prevent any other sperm from entering (block to polyspermy). The zygote then divides by mitosis (cleavage) as it travels down the oviduct towards the uterus.
**Explanation**: Sperm travel → acrosome reaction → one sperm enters → nuclear fusion → zygote. A common error is saying fertilisation occurs in the uterus.
**Tags**: fertilisation, zygote, acrosome, oviduct, polyspermy

### Question Q-15.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe what happens from the zygote to implantation in the uterus wall.
**Answer**: After fertilisation in the oviduct: (1) The zygote divides by mitosis (cleavage) — first into 2 cells, then 4, then 8 — forming a ball of cells called a morula. (2) The morula continues dividing and becomes a hollow ball of cells called a blastocyst (about 5 days after fertilisation). (3) The blastocyst floats in the uterus for 1–2 days. (4) The blastocyst then implants (embeds) into the thickened endometrium (uterus lining) that was prepared by progesterone. Implantation marks the beginning of pregnancy.
**Explanation**: Zygote → mitosis → morula → blastocyst → implantation in endometrium. A common error is saying implantation happens immediately after fertilisation.
**Tags**: implantation, zygote, morula, blastocyst, endometrium, mitosis

### Question Q-15.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Compare the structure and function of the placenta with the amniotic sac during pregnancy.
**Answer**: Placenta: (1) Exchange organ — oxygen, nutrients, antibodies diffuse from maternal to foetal blood; CO₂, urea, waste products diffuse from foetal to maternal blood. (2) Hormone production — secretes progesterone and oestrogen to maintain pregnancy. (3) Partial barrier — blocks some pathogens and harmful substances but allows many through (e.g., alcohol, nicotine). Maternal and foetal blood do NOT mix; exchange occurs across the placental membrane. Amniotic sac: (1) A membrane filled with amniotic fluid that surrounds and cushions the developing foetus. (2) Protects the foetus from physical shock, maintains a constant temperature, and allows freedom of movement for muscle development. (3) Acts as a barrier against infection.
**Explanation**: Placenta = exchange + hormones; Amniotic sac = cushioning + temperature + movement. A common error is confusing their functions.
**Tags**: placenta, amniotic-sac, amniotic-fluid, pregnancy, exchange, protection

### Question Q-15.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain the role of hormones in the birth process.
**Answer**: Birth (parturition) is controlled by hormones: (1) During late pregnancy, the hormone oxytocin is released from the pituitary gland. Oxytocin stimulates the muscles in the uterus wall to contract rhythmically. (2) These contractions push the baby downwards, stretching the cervix. (3) Stretching of the cervix sends nerve signals back to the brain, triggering MORE oxytocin release — a positive feedback loop. (4) The contractions become stronger and more frequent, dilating the cervix fully and pushing the baby through the vagina. (5) After birth, oxytocin also stimulates the detachment and expulsion of the placenta (afterbirth). Progesterone levels fall as the placenta is expelled, removing the hormone that maintained pregnancy.
**Explanation**: Oxytocin → uterine contractions → cervical stretch → more oxytocin (positive feedback) → birth. A common error is saying oxytocin causes the placenta to detach.
**Tags**: birth, oxytocin, hormones, contractions, positive-feedback, parturition

### Question Q-15.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain how meiosis produces genetically different gametes, and why this is important for sexual reproduction.
**Answer**: Meiosis produces four haploid cells (n = 23 chromosomes), each genetically different: (1) Crossing over in Prophase I — non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes exchange segments of DNA at chiasmata, creating new allele combinations. (2) Independent assortment in Metaphase I — each pair of homologous chromosomes lines up randomly at the equator; the orientation of each pair is independent of others, so maternal and paternal chromosomes can be distributed to daughter cells in many different combinations. (3) Each haploid gamete receives a unique mixture of the parent's alleles. Importance: each fertilisation event combines two genetically different gametes, producing offspring that are genetically different from each other and from their parents. This variation is the raw material for natural selection — in a changing environment, some individuals may have advantageous combinations that help them survive and reproduce.
**Explanation**: Crossing over + independent assortment → genetically different haploid gametes → variation. A common error is saying meiosis produces identical cells.
**Tags**: meiosis, gametes, genetic-variation, crossing-over, independent-assortment, haploid

### Question Q-15.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A woman discovers she is pregnant. Describe the sequence of development from fertilisation to the end of the first trimester (12 weeks), including the roles of the placenta and hormones.
**Answer**: Weeks 1–2: Fertilisation in oviduct → zygote → morula → blastocyst → implantation in endometrium. The placenta begins to form from chorionic villi. hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin) is secreted by the developing placenta — this maintains the corpus luteum, which continues secreting progesterone to prevent menstruation and maintain the endometrium. Weeks 3–8 (embryonic period): Major organs begin to form — neural tube (brain and spinal cord), heart, circulatory system. The embryo is most vulnerable to teratogens (alcohol, rubella, nicotine) during this period. The placenta develops fully and takes over progesterone/oestrogen production from the corpus luteum by week 10. Weeks 9–12 (foetal period): All major organs are formed; the foetus grows rapidly. Limbs, fingers, toes develop. By week 12: the foetus is about 5 cm long, can move, and the heartbeat is detectable. The amniotic sac continues to protect and cushion the foetus throughout.
**Explanation**: Fertilisation → implantation → organogenesis (weeks 3–8) → placental takeover → foetal growth. hCG, progesterone, oestrogen roles. A common error is saying the placenta forms immediately at fertilisation.
**Tags**: pregnancy, development, first-trimester, hCG, placenta, organogenesis, foetus

### Question Q-15.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate two natural methods and two artificial methods of contraception, considering how each prevents fertilisation or implantation.
**Answer**: Natural methods: (1) Abstinence (periodic abstinence / fertility awareness): tracking the menstrual cycle to avoid intercourse during ovulation (when the egg is fertile). Prevents fertilisation by keeping sperm and egg apart. Advantage: no side effects; Disadvantage: high failure rate because ovulation timing is hard to predict. (2) Withdrawal: the male withdraws before ejaculation. Prevents sperm from entering the vagina. Advantage: no equipment needed; Disadvantage: pre-ejaculate fluid may contain sperm; high failure rate. Artificial methods: (3) Condom (barrier): a latex sheath placed over the penis (or female condom in the vagina) that physically blocks sperm from entering the cervix. Advantage: also reduces STI transmission; Disadvantage: can break or slip. (4) Contraceptive pill (hormonal): contains oestrogen and progesterone (or progesterone only) that inhibit FSH release, preventing follicle development and ovulation. No egg is released, so fertilisation cannot occur. Advantage: very effective if taken correctly; Disadvantage: side effects (mood changes, blood clots), requires daily compliance, does not protect against STIs.
**Explanation**: Natural = keeping gametes apart; Artificial = barrier or hormonal suppression of ovulation. A common error is confusing how each method works.
**Tags**: contraception, fertility-awareness, withdrawal, condom, contraceptive-pill, fertilisation
