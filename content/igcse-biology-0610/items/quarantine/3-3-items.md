# Items: 3.3 — Active transport

### Question Q-3.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is active transport?
**Answer**: Active transport is the movement of particles against a concentration gradient (from low to high concentration), using energy from respiration. It requires carrier proteins in the cell membrane and energy in the form of ATP.
**Explanation**: Against gradient, needs energy. A common error is saying active transport "does not need energy."
**Tags**: active-transport, definition, energy, ATP

### Question Q-3.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: How does active transport differ from diffusion and osmosis?
**Answer**: Active transport moves particles against the concentration gradient (low to high) and requires energy. Diffusion and osmosis move particles down the gradient (high to low) and do NOT require energy. Osmosis specifically involves water through a partially permeable membrane.
**Explanation**: Against vs down gradient, energy required vs not required. A common error is saying all three are "the same."
**Tags**: active-transport, diffusion, osmosis, comparison

### Question Q-3.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Give two examples of active transport in living organisms.
**Answer**: (1) Root hair cells absorbing mineral ions from soil — ion concentration is higher in the root than in the dilute soil solution, so active transport moves ions against the gradient. (2) Absorption of glucose from the small intestine into blood — when glucose concentration in the blood is already higher than in the intestine lumen.
**Explanation**: Mineral ions and glucose, both against gradient. A common error is using O₂/CO₂ exchange (that's diffusion).
**Tags**: active-transport, examples, root-hair, glucose

### Question Q-3.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why do root hair cells contain many mitochondria?
**Answer**: Root hair cells use active transport to absorb mineral ions from the soil against a concentration gradient. Active transport requires energy from respiration. Mitochondria are the site of aerobic respiration, providing the ATP needed for carrier proteins to pump ions across the membrane.
**Explanation**: Active transport needs energy, mitochondria provide ATP. A common error is saying mitochondria are "for water absorption" (that's osmosis, which does not need energy).
**Tags**: mitochondria, root-hair-cell, active-transport, respiration

### Question Q-3.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Where does absorption of glucose by active transport occur in the human body, and why is it necessary?
**Answer**: Glucose is absorbed by active transport in the small intestine (villi) when glucose concentration in the blood is already higher than in the intestine lumen. Active transport ensures ALL glucose is absorbed even when the concentration gradient would otherwise prevent further diffusion. Without active transport, some glucose would remain unabsorbed and be lost in faeces.
**Explanation**: Small intestine villi, complete absorption. A common error is saying glucose is only absorbed by diffusion.
**Tags**: active-transport, small-intestine, glucose, absorption

### Question Q-3.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how carrier proteins are involved in active transport.
**Answer**: Carrier proteins in the cell membrane bind to specific particles on the side of lower concentration. Using energy from ATP, the carrier protein changes shape and pushes the particle through to the side of higher concentration. The carrier then returns to its original shape. Each type of particle has its own specific carrier protein.
**Explanation**: Specific binding, ATP → shape change, pumps particle. A common error is saying carrier proteins are "channels" (channels are for facilitated diffusion, not active transport).
**Tags**: carrier-protein, ATP, membrane, specificity

### Question Q-3.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain why a plant placed in a solution containing a respiratory poison cannot absorb mineral ions from the soil. Would water absorption by osmosis also be affected? Justify your answer.
**Answer**: The respiratory poison stops respiration in root cells, so no ATP is produced. Active transport of mineral ions requires ATP, so mineral absorption stops. Water absorption by osmosis is NOT directly affected because osmosis is a passive process that does not require energy — water moves down its water potential gradient without ATP. However, long-term water absorption may decrease because the plant needs mineral ions to maintain the osmotic gradient that drives water uptake.
**Explanation**: No respiration → no ATP → no active transport. Osmosis = passive, not directly affected. A common error is saying osmosis also stops.
**Tags**: active-transport, respiratory-poison, mineral-ions, osmosis

### Question Q-3.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the uptake of water and nitrate ions by a root hair cell from the soil. Explain why each process uses a different transport mechanism.
**Answer**: Water enters by osmosis: water potential in the soil is higher than in the root hair cell vacuole, so water moves passively down the gradient — no energy required. Nitrate ions enter by active transport: nitrate concentration inside the root hair cell is already higher than in the soil, so ions must be pumped against the gradient using ATP. If nitrate ions were only absorbed by diffusion, they would diffuse OUT of the root, not in. The different concentration gradients determine the mechanism.
**Explanation**: Water: osmosis (with gradient); nitrate: active transport (against gradient). A common error is saying both use active transport.
**Tags**: root-hair-cell, water, nitrate, comparison, transport-mechanism

### Question Q-3.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A scientist discovers a new drug that blocks carrier proteins in cell membranes. Predict the effects of this drug on: (a) the absorption of glucose from the small intestine, (b) the absorption of oxygen in the lungs, (c) the uptake of mineral ions by plant roots. Justify each prediction.
**Answer**: (a) Glucose absorption would STOP — absorption occurs by active transport which requires carrier proteins, and co-transport mechanisms also need carriers. (b) Oxygen absorption would CONTINUE — oxygen diffuses directly through the phospholipid bilayer down its concentration gradient; it does not need carrier proteins. (c) Mineral ion uptake would STOP — ions are absorbed by active transport requiring carrier proteins. The root cells would eventually become mineral-deficient.
**Explanation**: Carrier proteins needed for active transport, not for simple diffusion. A common error is saying "all absorption stops."
**Tags**: carrier-protein, drug, prediction, diffusion, active-transport
