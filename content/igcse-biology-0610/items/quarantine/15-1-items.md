# Items: 15.1 — Asexual and sexual reproduction

### Question Q-15.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the difference between asexual and sexual reproduction?
**Answer**: Asexual reproduction involves one parent and produces genetically identical offspring (clones). No fusion of gametes occurs. Sexual reproduction involves two parents, fusion of male and female gametes, and produces genetically different offspring. Asexual reproduction involves mitosis; sexual reproduction involves meiosis.
**Explanation**: One parent/identical vs two parents/different. A common error is saying asexual reproduction involves "no cell division."
**Tags**: asexual, sexual, reproduction, gametes, clones, mitosis, meiosis

### Question Q-15.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Name two examples of asexual reproduction in organisms and explain how each works.
**Answer**: (1) Bacteria — binary fission: the circular DNA replicates and the cell divides into two identical daughter cells. (2) Strawberry plants — runners: horizontal stems grow along the ground and produce new plants at nodes. (3) Potato — tubers: underground swollen stems with buds (eyes) that grow into new plants. (4) Yeast — budding: a small outgrowth develops and detaches to form a new cell.
**Explanation**: Bacteria, runners, tubers, budding are all valid. A common error is saying seed production is asexual.
**Tags**: asexual-reproduction, examples, bacteria, runners, budding, binary-fission

### Question Q-15.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Give three advantages of sexual reproduction over asexual reproduction.
**Answer**: (1) Genetic variation in offspring — allows adaptation to changing environments. (2) Disease resistance — if a disease strikes, some individuals may have resistance due to variation. (3) Evolution — variation provides the raw material for natural selection. Asexual reproduction produces identical offspring, all vulnerable to the same threats.
**Explanation**: Variation, adaptation, disease resistance are the key advantages. A common error is saying sexual reproduction is "faster."
**Tags**: sexual-reproduction, advantages, variation, adaptation, disease-resistance

### Question Q-15.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Give two advantages of asexual reproduction.
**Answer**: (1) Rapid population increase — only one parent is needed, so no time is spent finding a mate. (2) All offspring inherit favourable traits from the parent (if the parent is well-adapted to a stable environment, all offspring are too). (3) Faster than sexual reproduction in stable environments where conditions do not change.
**Explanation**: Fast, no mate needed, favourable traits inherited. A common error is saying asexual reproduction produces "better" offspring.
**Tags**: asexual-reproduction, advantages, speed, clones, stable-environment

### Question Q-15.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe how bacteria reproduce asexually by binary fission.
**Answer**: The circular DNA molecule replicates (copies itself). The cell elongates and the two DNA copies move to opposite ends. The cell membrane pinches inwards and a new cell wall forms, dividing the cell into two identical daughter cells. Under ideal conditions, some bacteria can divide every 20 minutes.
**Explanation**: DNA replicates, cell divides, two identical daughter cells. A common error is saying bacteria reproduce by mitosis (mitosis is for eukaryotic cells).
**Tags**: bacteria, binary-fission, asexual-reproduction, DNA, cell-division

### Question Q-15.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why genetic variation is important for the survival of a species.
**Answer**: Genetic variation means that not all individuals in a population are identical. If the environment changes (e.g., new disease, climate change, new predator), some individuals may have traits that help them survive. These individuals reproduce and pass on their advantageous alleles. Without variation, all individuals would be equally vulnerable, and the species could become extinct.
**Explanation**: Variation allows some individuals to survive environmental change. A common error is saying variation is "always good" without explaining the survival mechanism.
**Tags**: genetic-variation, survival, natural-selection, adaptation, species

### Question Q-15.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A farmer grows a crop plant that reproduces asexually. A new disease attacks the crop and all plants die. Explain why this happened and how sexual reproduction could have prevented it.
**Answer**: Asexual reproduction produces genetically identical offspring (clones), so all plants have the same genes and the same susceptibility to the disease. If one plant is vulnerable, all are vulnerable. Sexual reproduction produces genetically different offspring through meiosis and random fertilisation. Some offspring might inherit alleles that give resistance to the disease. These resistant individuals would survive and reproduce, allowing the species to continue.
**Explanation**: Clones = identical susceptibility; sexual = variation = some resistant. A common error is saying sexual reproduction "prevents disease" rather than allowing some individuals to survive.
**Tags**: asexual, sexual, clones, disease, resistance, variation, meiosis

### Question Q-15.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the roles of mitosis and meiosis in asexual and sexual reproduction. Include the number and genetic makeup of cells produced.
**Answer**: Mitosis: one cell division producing two genetically identical diploid daughter cells (same chromosome number as parent). Used in asexual reproduction, growth, and repair. Meiosis: two cell divisions producing four genetically different haploid gametes (half the chromosome number of parent). Used in sexual reproduction to produce sperm and eggs. At fertilisation, two haploid gametes fuse to restore the diploid number, combining genetic material from both parents.
**Explanation**: Mitosis = 2 identical diploid; Meiosis = 4 different haploid. A common error is confusing the products of mitosis and meiosis.
**Tags**: mitosis, meiosis, diploid, haploid, gametes, asexual, sexual

### Question Q-15.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A species of fungus can reproduce both asexually and sexually. Describe the conditions under which each method might be favoured and explain the biological advantage of each in those conditions.
**Answer**: Asexual reproduction (spores by mitosis) is favoured in stable, favourable environments where the parent is well-adapted. Advantage: rapid colonisation, no need for a mate, all offspring preserve successful traits. Sexual reproduction is favoured in changing or stressful environments (e.g., new disease, temperature shift, nutrient shortage). Advantage: genetic recombination and variation through meiosis creates new allele combinations; some offspring may be better adapted to the new conditions, increasing the chance of species survival.
**Explanation**: Stable = asexual (fast, clones); Changing = sexual (variation, adaptation). A common error is saying one method is always better than the other.
**Tags**: fungus, spores, asexual, sexual, environmental-conditions, adaptation, meiosis, mitosis
