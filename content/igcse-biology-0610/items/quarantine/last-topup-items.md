### Question Q-3.1-09
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does the concentration gradient affect diffusion in the small intestine?
**Answer**: Digested food molecules are at high concentration in the small intestine lumen and low concentration in the blood. This steep gradient causes rapid diffusion into blood capillaries in the villi. Blood flow constantly removes absorbed molecules, maintaining the gradient. The villi's thin walls provide a short diffusion distance.
**Explanation**: High in intestine, low in blood, maintained by blood flow. A common error is saying diffusion goes "against" the gradient.
**Tags**: diffusion, small-intestine, concentration-gradient, absorption

### Question Q-5.2-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why do enzymes have an optimum temperature rather than a single temperature?
**Answer**: The "optimum" is a range (not a single point) because enzyme activity gradually increases with temperature (more kinetic energy) up to a peak, then gradually decreases as denaturation begins. The exact optimum varies between enzymes — human enzymes ~37°C, thermophilic bacteria enzymes ~70°C.
**Explanation**: Range, gradual increase/decrease, varies by enzyme. A common error is saying optimum is one exact temperature.
**Tags**: enzyme, optimum-temperature, range, denaturation

### Question Q-7.1-09
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why do athletes need more carbohydrates than sedentary people?
**Answer**: Athletes use more energy during training and competition. Carbohydrates are the body's main energy source, broken down to glucose for respiration. Athletes store glucose as glycogen in muscles and liver. During exercise, glycogen is broken down to provide quick energy. A high-carbohydrate diet ensures adequate glycogen stores.
**Explanation**: More energy → more carbs → more glycogen. A common error is saying athletes need more protein "for energy."
**Tags**: athletes, carbohydrates, energy, glycogen

### Question Q-10.1-08
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does the skin act as a barrier against pathogens?
**Answer**: Intact skin provides a physical barrier — pathogens cannot pass through the tough, keratinised outer layer (epidermis). Sebaceous glands secrete sebum (slightly acidic), which inhibits bacterial growth. If skin is broken, blood clotting seals the wound and white blood cells attack any pathogens that enter.
**Explanation**: Physical barrier, sebum, clotting. A common error is saying skin "kills" all pathogens.
**Tags**: skin, barrier, pathogens, sebum

### Question Q-12.1-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the difference between aerobic and anaerobic respiration in terms of energy yield?
**Answer**: Aerobic respiration releases much more energy per glucose molecule (~38 ATP) because glucose is completely broken down to CO₂ and H₂O. Anaerobic respiration releases much less energy (~2 ATP) because glucose is only partially broken down (to lactic acid or ethanol). This is why aerobic activity can be sustained longer than anaerobic activity.
**Explanation**: 38 ATP vs 2 ATP, complete vs incomplete breakdown. A common error is saying anaerobic respiration releases "no energy."
**Tags**: aerobic, anaerobic, energy, ATP
