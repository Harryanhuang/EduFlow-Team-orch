# Items: 8.2 — Transpiration and transpiration stream

### Question Q-8.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is transpiration?
**Answer**: Transpiration is the loss of water vapour from the aerial parts of a plant, mainly through the stomata of leaves. Water evaporates from the surfaces of mesophyll cells, then diffuses out through open stomata into the surrounding air. This creates a pull that draws water up the xylem.
**Explanation**: Water vapour loss, stomata, evaporation. A common error is confusing transpiration with translocation.
**Tags**: transpiration, definition, stomata, water-vapour

### Question Q-8.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the transpiration stream?
**Answer**: The transpiration stream is the continuous flow of water from the roots, through the xylem vessels, up the stem to the leaves. It is driven by transpiration at the leaves (evaporation creates suction). Water molecules are cohesive (stick together via H-bonds) so the pull from the top draws the entire water column upwards. It delivers water for photosynthesis and dissolved mineral ions to all plant cells.
**Explanation**: Continuous water column from root to leaf, driven by evaporation. A common error is saying it's "pumped" from the roots.
**Tags**: transpiration-stream, cohesion, water-column, xylem

### Question Q-8.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How do temperature, humidity, wind speed, and light intensity affect the rate of transpiration?
**Answer**: Temperature ↑ → transpiration ↑ (more kinetic energy, faster evaporation). Humidity ↑ → transpiration ↓ (smaller water vapour concentration gradient between leaf and air). Wind speed ↑ → transpiration ↑ (water vapour removed from leaf surface, maintaining steep gradient). Light intensity ↑ → transpiration ↑ (stomata open wider for photosynthesis, increasing water vapour exit pathway).
**Explanation**: Four factors with direction and mechanism. A common error is saying humidity increases transpiration.
**Tags**: transpiration, factors, temperature, humidity, wind, light

### Question Q-8.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How do guard cells control the opening and closing of stomata?
**Answer**: In bright light, guard cells actively transport K⁺ ions in, lowering water potential. Water enters by osmosis and guard cells become turgid. Their unevenly thickened walls cause them to bend outward, opening the stoma. At night or in water shortage, K⁺ ions are pumped out, water follows by osmosis, guard cells become flaccid and the stoma closes. This regulates CO₂ entry for photosynthesis and water loss by transpiration.
**Explanation**: Turgid = open, flaccid = close, K⁺ ion pump mechanism. A common error is saying stomata open at night.
**Tags**: guard-cells, stomata, turgid, osmosis, potassium-ions

### Question Q-8.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe how a potometer is used to measure the rate of transpiration.
**Answer**: A potometer measures water uptake by a plant shoot (≈ water loss by transpiration). Steps: cut a leafy shoot underwater (prevents air entering xylem), fit to potometer underwater, introduce air bubble into capillary tube, record bubble position, measure distance moved in set time. Rate = distance/time (mm/min). Repeat under different conditions (light, wind, humidity) to compare. Note: water used in photosynthesis is very small and ignored.
**Explanation**: Water uptake ≈ transpiration, bubble movement, rate. A common error is saying the potometer measures transpiration directly.
**Tags**: potometer, transpiration, experiment, method

### Question Q-8.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why do plants wilt when they lose more water by transpiration than they absorb?
**Answer**: When water loss exceeds absorption, cells lose water by osmosis and become flaccid. Turgor pressure drops, so cells can no longer support the plant structure. Leaves and stems droop (wilt). This is a temporary protection — wilting reduces leaf surface area exposed to sun, reducing further transpiration. If the water deficit continues, cells may die and the plant cannot recover.
**Explanation**: Water loss → flaccid cells → no turgor → wilting. A common error is saying wilting is caused by "too much sun."
**Tags**: wilting, turgor, transpiration, flaccid

### Question Q-8.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Why do some plants have adaptations to reduce transpiration, and what are the specific structural adaptations found in xerophytes?
**Answer**: Plants in dry environments (xerophytes) need to conserve water because water is scarce and transpiration rates are high (high temperature, low humidity, high wind). Adaptations: (1) small leaves or spines — reduce surface area for evaporation. (2) Thick waxy cuticle — impermeable barrier, water cannot pass through. (3) Sunken stomata in pits — trap humid air, reducing the water vapour concentration gradient. (4) Rolled leaves — create enclosed humid space, reduce air movement over stomata. (5) Hairy leaves — trap a layer of moist air near the leaf surface. (6) Stomata on lower epidermis only — shielded from direct sunlight. Some xerophytes also store water in succulent tissues (e.g., cacti).
**Explanation**: Xerophyte adaptations, reduce water loss, structural detail. A common error is saying these plants "don't transpire" — they do, just at a reduced rate.
**Tags**: xerophyte, transpiration, adaptation, water-conservation, stomata

### Question Q-8.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain the trade-off between photosynthesis and transpiration in terms of stomatal behaviour, and how a plant balances conflicting demands in hot, dry conditions.
**Answer**: Stomata must open for CO₂ entry (photosynthesis) but opening also allows water vapour to escape (transpiration). In hot, dry conditions, the plant faces a conflict: open stomata → photosynthesis proceeds but risk of lethal dehydration; close stomata → conserve water but no CO₂ for photosynthesis. Balance strategies: (1) open stomata only during cooler parts of day (early morning). (2) Close stomata during midday heat. (3) Use stored CO₂ from night respiration for morning photosynthesis. (4) Deciduous plants drop leaves in dry season to eliminate transpiration entirely. The plant prioritises survival over growth — it can survive brief periods without photosynthesis, but cannot survive lethal dehydration.
**Explanation**: CO₂ entry vs water loss trade-off, survival prioritisation. A common error is explaining the trade-off without describing the plant's balancing mechanism.
**Tags**: photosynthesis, transpiration, trade-off, stomatal-behaviour, xerophyte

### Question Q-8.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Design an experiment to investigate the effect of one environmental factor on the rate of transpiration, using a potometer. State your hypothesis, variables, method, expected results, and sources of error.
**Answer**: Hypothesis: Increasing wind speed (using a fan) will increase the rate of transpiration. Independent variable: wind speed (no fan / low / high). Dependent variable: rate of water uptake (mm/min). Controlled variables: same plant species/size, same temperature, same light intensity, same humidity. Method: set up potometer with leafy shoot; introduce air bubble; measure distance moved in 5 minutes with no fan; reset bubble and repeat with low fan speed; repeat with high fan speed; three trials each. Expected results: highest rate at high fan speed (steepest concentration gradient). Sources of error: (1) air leaks in apparatus, (2) temperature rise from fan motor, (3) cutting stem may introduce air embolism, (4) bubble may stick to tube wall. Calculate mean rate and plot bar chart.
**Explanation**: Full experimental design with hypothesis, variables, controls, error analysis. A common error is forgetting to list controlled variables or sources of error.
**Tags**: experiment-design, potometer, transpiration, wind-speed, variables
