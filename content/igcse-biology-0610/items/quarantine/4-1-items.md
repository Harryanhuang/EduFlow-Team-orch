# Items: 4.1 — Carbohydrates, proteins, lipids

### Question Q-4.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What are the elements found in carbohydrates, proteins, and lipids?
**Answer**: Carbohydrates: carbon, hydrogen, oxygen (CHO). Proteins: carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen (CHON), sometimes sulfur. Lipids: carbon, hydrogen, oxygen (CHO) — but with a much lower proportion of oxygen than carbohydrates.
**Explanation**: CHO for carbs/lipids, CHON for proteins. A common error is forgetting nitrogen in proteins.
**Tags**: elements, carbohydrates, proteins, lipids

### Question Q-4.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Give two functions of water in living organisms.
**Answer**: (1) Water is the universal solvent for chemical reactions — all metabolic reactions occur in aqueous solution. (2) Water is a transport medium — blood plasma transports dissolved substances; xylem sap transports minerals. (3) Temperature regulation — high specific heat capacity; evaporation (sweating) cools the body. (4) Reactant in photosynthesis.
**Explanation**: Solvent, transport, temperature regulation. A common error is calling water a "food" or "nutrient."
**Tags**: water, function, solvent, transport

### Question Q-4.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What are the building blocks (monomers) of starch, proteins, and fats?
**Answer**: Starch is made of glucose molecules joined together in chains. Proteins are made of amino acids joined by peptide bonds. Fats (lipids) are made of one glycerol molecule and three fatty acid molecules joined by ester bonds.
**Explanation**: Glucose, amino acids, glycerol + 3 fatty acids. A common error is saying proteins are made of glucose.
**Tags**: monomers, starch, proteins, fats, glucose, amino-acids

### Question Q-4.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Give two functions of proteins in living organisms, with examples.
**Answer**: (1) Enzymes — biological catalysts that speed up chemical reactions (e.g., amylase in saliva breaking down starch). (2) Structural proteins — keratin in hair and nails, collagen in skin and bones. (3) Transport — haemoglobin in red blood cells carries oxygen. (4) Hormones — insulin regulates blood glucose. (5) Antibodies — defend against pathogens.
**Explanation**: Diverse functions. A common error is saying proteins are only for "building muscle."
**Tags**: proteins, functions, enzymes, structure, haemoglobin

### Question Q-4.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why starch and glycogen are both suitable as storage molecules.
**Answer**: Both are polysaccharides made of glucose units, making them compact and insoluble (does not affect osmotic balance of cells). Starch is stored in plants (in chloroplasts and storage organs like tubers). Glycogen is stored in animals (liver and muscles). When energy is needed, enzymes break them down to release glucose for respiration. Their large size means they do not diffuse out of cells.
**Explanation**: Compact, insoluble, glucose release. A common error is confusing where starch and glycogen are found.
**Tags**: starch, glycogen, storage, polysaccharide

### Question Q-4.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the difference between saturated and unsaturated fats? Give an example of each.
**Answer**: Saturated fats have no carbon-carbon double bonds in their fatty acid chains — all carbon atoms are saturated with hydrogen atoms. They are usually solid at room temperature (e.g., butter, lard). Unsaturated fats have one or more carbon-carbon double bonds in their fatty acid chains. They are usually liquid at room temperature (e.g., olive oil, sunflower oil).
**Explanation**: Double bonds absent vs present. A common error is saying "all unsaturated fats are healthy."
**Tags**: saturated, unsaturated, fats, double-bonds, fatty-acids

### Question Q-4.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student tests an unknown food sample and finds it contains carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, and sulfur. When the sample is heated with Biuret reagent (NaOH + CuSO₄), it turns purple. Identify the type of biological molecule and explain how the test result supports your answer.
**Answer**: The sample contains protein. Evidence: (1) The presence of nitrogen and sulfur indicates protein — carbohydrates and lipids contain only C, H, O; only proteins contain N (and sometimes S). (2) The Biuret test turns purple in the presence of peptide bonds (the bonds between amino acids in a protein). The Biuret test is specific for proteins — it does not react with carbohydrates or lipids.
**Explanation**: N+S → protein; Biuret positive = peptide bonds. A common error is saying the Biuret test "detects amino acids" — it detects peptide bonds.
**Tags**: protein, Biuret-test, identification, peptide-bonds

### Question Q-4.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: An athlete's diet must include sufficient protein. Explain why, referring to two specific functions of proteins that are important during training.
**Answer**: (1) Structural repair and growth: intense training causes micro-tears in muscle fibres. Proteins provide amino acids for muscle protein synthesis to repair and build larger muscles (hypertrophy). (2) Enzyme production: training increases metabolic rate; enzymes (which are proteins) catalyse all cellular reactions including respiration — more enzymes are needed to sustain the higher metabolic demand. Without sufficient protein, the athlete would lose muscle mass and recover slowly.
**Explanation**: Muscle repair + enzyme production. A common error is saying "protein is used for energy" — carbohydrates/fats are primary energy sources.
**Tags**: protein, athlete, muscle-repair, enzymes, training

### Question Q-4.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: In a famine, a person loses fat reserves first, then muscle protein. Explain this sequential loss in terms of the functions and energy content of lipids and proteins.
**Answer**: Lipids are the body's main long-term energy store — they contain more energy per gram than carbohydrates or proteins and are specifically designed for energy storage. The body mobilises fat reserves first because (1) their primary function IS energy storage, so using them for energy does not compromise other critical functions. Muscle proteins are only broken down when fat reserves are depleted because (2) proteins have essential functions (enzymes, structure, transport, immunity) — breaking down muscle compromises these functions, leading to weakness, poor immunity, and eventually organ failure. Protein catabolism is a last-resort energy source.
**Explanation**: Lipids = energy store, sacrificial. Proteins = functional, critical. A common error is saying protein is "easier to break down."
**Tags**: lipids, proteins, famine, energy-storage, catabolism
