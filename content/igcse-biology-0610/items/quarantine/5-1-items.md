# Items: 5.1 — Enzyme action and specificity

### Question Q-5.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is an enzyme?
**Answer**: An enzyme is a protein that acts as a biological catalyst. It speeds up chemical reactions in living organisms without being used up. Enzymes are specific — each enzyme catalyses only one type of reaction or a small group of related reactions.
**Explanation**: Protein catalyst, not used up, specific. A common error is saying enzymes are "carbohydrates" or "used up."
**Tags**: enzyme, definition, catalyst, protein

### Question Q-5.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What happens to an enzyme after it has catalysed a reaction?
**Answer**: The enzyme is unchanged and can catalyse the same reaction again. The substrate binds to the active site, the reaction occurs, products are released, and the enzyme is free to bind another substrate molecule. Enzymes are not used up in reactions — they are reusable.
**Explanation**: Unchanged, reusable. A common error is saying enzymes are "consumed" like reactants.
**Tags**: enzyme, unchanged, reusable, catalyst

### Question Q-5.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain the "lock and key" model of enzyme action.
**Answer**: The enzyme has a specifically shaped active site (the lock). Only the correct substrate molecule (the key) fits precisely into the active site. When the substrate binds, an enzyme-substrate complex forms, and the reaction occurs. The products are released and the enzyme is unchanged, ready to catalyse another reaction.
**Explanation**: Specific fit, active site, unchanged. A common error is saying the enzyme changes shape permanently.
**Tags**: lock-and-key, active-site, substrate, specificity

### Question Q-5.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why are enzymes specific to particular substrates?
**Answer**: Each enzyme has a unique three-dimensional shape of its active site, determined by its amino acid sequence and folding. Only a substrate with a complementary shape can fit into the active site, like a key fitting a lock. If the substrate shape does not match the active site shape, the enzyme cannot catalyse the reaction.
**Explanation**: Active site shape determines specificity. A common error is saying enzymes "choose" their substrate.
**Tags**: enzyme, specificity, active-site, shape

### Question Q-5.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how the "lock and key" model explains enzyme specificity at the molecular level.
**Answer**: The enzyme's active site has a precise 3D shape formed by specific amino acids. Only a substrate molecule with a complementary 3D shape can enter the active site and form temporary bonds (hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds) with the amino acids lining the active site. Misfitting substrates cannot form these bonds, so the enzyme-substrate complex cannot form and no reaction occurs. This molecular complementarity is the basis of enzyme specificity.
**Explanation**: 3D shape complementarity, temporary bonds. A common error is saying all substrates can partially fit.
**Tags**: enzyme, specificity, active-site, complementarity

### Question Q-5.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Amylase breaks down starch into maltose. Explain why amylase cannot break down proteins.
**Answer**: The active site of amylase has a specific shape that only fits starch molecules. Proteins have a completely different shape and chemical structure (amino acids, not glucose units). A protein cannot fit into amylase's active site, so no enzyme-substrate complex can form. Different enzymes have differently shaped active sites for their specific substrates.
**Explanation**: Wrong shape = can't bind. A common error is saying "amylase chooses starch" without explaining the shape mismatch.
**Tags**: amylase, specificity, active-site, shape

### Question Q-5.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student adds amylase to a starch solution. After 10 minutes, no starch is detected. The student then adds more starch and the reaction starts again. Explain both observations.
**Answer**: First observation: amylase catalysed the breakdown of all starch into maltose. Iodine test would be negative (no starch left). Second observation: adding more starch restarts the reaction because the amylase molecules are unchanged — they were not used up in the first reaction. The active sites are still available to bind new starch molecules. This demonstrates that enzymes are reusable catalysts.
**Explanation**: Enzymes reusable, substrate depleted→reaction stops, new substrate→reaction restarts. A common error is saying "more enzyme is made."
**Tags**: amylase, starch, reusable, enzyme

### Question Q-5.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Pepsin is a digestive enzyme in the stomach that works at pH 2. If pepsin enters the small intestine (pH 8), it stops working. Using the "lock and key" model, explain what happens to pepsin at pH 8 and why this is important for the body.
**Answer**: Pepsin's active site shape is maintained by ionic and hydrogen bonds that are stable at pH 2. At pH 8, the change in H⁺ concentration disrupts these bonds, causing the enzyme's tertiary structure to unfold — the active site changes shape (denaturation). The substrate (protein) can no longer fit. This is important because (1) the small intestine has its own digestive enzymes (e.g., trypsin) that need to work, and (2) pepsin would damage the intestinal lining if it remained active. The pH change acts as a natural off-switch.
**Explanation**: pH change → denature → enzymatic protection. A common error is saying pepsin "dies."
**Tags**: pepsin, pH, denaturation, lock-and-key, protection

### Question Q-5.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Some drugs work by inhibiting enzymes. Design an explanation for how an inhibitor with a shape similar to the substrate could reduce the rate of an enzyme-controlled reaction, using the "lock and key" model.
**Answer**: A competitive inhibitor has a shape similar enough to the substrate to fit into the enzyme's active site. When the inhibitor binds, it blocks the active site — the real substrate cannot enter. The inhibitor does not react (no product is formed), so the active site is occupied uselessly. This reduces the reaction rate because fewer active sites are available for the real substrate. The effect depends on the relative concentrations of inhibitor and substrate — if enough substrate is present, it can outcompete the inhibitor. This is "competitive inhibition."
**Explanation**: Similar shape → binds active site → blocks substrate → less product. A common error is saying the inhibitor "destroys" the enzyme.
**Tags**: inhibitor, competitive-inhibition, active-site, substrate
