# Items: 12.1 — Aerobic respiration: equation and energy

### Question Q-12.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Write the word equation and balanced symbol equation for aerobic respiration.
**Answer**: Word: glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water (+ energy released). Symbol: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O (+ energy). The energy released is used to phosphorylate ADP + Pᵢ → ATP. Aerobic respiration releases approximately 38 ATP per glucose molecule.
**Explanation**: Glucose + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + energy. A common error is writing light energy as the output (that's photosynthesis, not respiration).
**Tags**: aerobic-respiration, equation, balanced, energy, ATP

### Question Q-12.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Where does aerobic respiration take place in cells? Explain which cells have the most mitochondria.
**Answer**: Aerobic respiration takes place mainly in the mitochondria. The first stage (glycolysis) occurs in the cytoplasm; subsequent stages (Krebs cycle, electron transport chain) occur on and within the inner mitochondrial membrane (cristae). Cells with high energy demands have more mitochondria: muscle cells (contraction), sperm cells (swimming), companion cells in phloem (active loading of sucrose), neurones (ion pumping for nerve impulses). This is a structure-function relationship.
**Explanation**: Mitochondria (cristae for ETC), cytoplasm (glycolysis). High energy demand cells = many mitochondria. A common error is saying respiration only occurs in the cytoplasm.
**Tags**: mitochondria, cristae, glycolysis, ATP, structure-function

### Question Q-12.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the energy released in respiration used for? List six uses.
**Answer**: The energy from respiration is used to synthesise ATP, which powers: (1) Muscle contraction — ATP binds to myosin for the power stroke. (2) Protein synthesis — peptide bond formation on ribosomes. (3) Active transport — carrier proteins pump ions against the concentration gradient (e.g., Na⁺/K⁺ pump in neurones). (4) Cell division — mitosis/meiosis chromosome movement and cytokinesis. (5) Growth and repair — synthesis of new cell components. (6) Maintaining body temperature — some energy is always lost as heat (mammals use this for thermoregulation). All living cells, including plant cells, respire continuously.
**Explanation**: Six uses linked to ATP-consuming processes. A common error is saying energy is "stored" — ATP is used immediately.
**Tags**: respiration, ATP, energy-uses, muscle-contraction, active-transport

### Question Q-12.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why plants must respire even though they photosynthesise.
**Answer**: Photosynthesis makes glucose using light energy (anabolic — building up). Respiration releases energy from glucose (catabolic — breaking down). Plants respire continuously (24 hours/day) in ALL living cells because every cell needs energy for active transport, protein synthesis, growth, and cell division. Photosynthesis occurs only in cells with chloroplasts and only during daylight. At night, plants rely entirely on respiration using stored starch. The gases exchanged during respiration (O₂ in, CO₂ out) are the reverse of photosynthesis and can be measured at night when photosynthesis stops.
**Explanation**: Photosynthesis makes food; respiration uses food for energy. All cells respire all the time. A common error is saying plants "only photosynthesise" or "don't respire."
**Tags**: plants, photosynthesis, respiration, energy, night

### Question Q-12.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe an experiment to show that living organisms release energy (heat) during respiration. Include the control.
**Answer**: Apparatus: two vacuum flasks, each with a thermometer sealed through a bung. Flask A: living germinating peas (respiring). Flask B: boiled/dead peas (control, not respiring). Both flasks are sterilised to eliminate microbial respiration. Record temperature in both flasks at start and every 24 hours for 3 days. Result: Flask A temperature rises (e.g., +2-3°C); Flask B temperature stays constant. Conclusion: the temperature rise in Flask A is due to heat released by respiring cells. The vacuum flask insulates preventing heat loss. The dead pea control shows the heat is biological, not environmental. Optional: disinfect both sets first to eliminate bacteria as a variable.
**Explanation**: Vacuum flasks + thermometers, germinating vs dead peas. Temperature rise only in living. A common error is omitting the dead pea control.
**Tags**: respiration, experiment, heat, germinating-seeds, control

### Question Q-12.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe an experiment to show that living organisms produce CO₂ during respiration. Include the control.
**Answer**: Apparatus: two sealed containers with organisms. Container A: living organisms (e.g., woodlice, germinating seeds). Container B: dead organisms or no organisms (control). Each container connected by a delivery tube to a test tube of limewater (calcium hydroxide solution). Air is drawn through each apparatus slowly (via a vacuum line or aspirator). Soda lime or potassium hydroxide (KOH) is placed at the air inlet to remove CO₂ from incoming air. Result: limewater in Container A turns milky/cloudy (white precipitate of CaCO₃). Limewater in Container B remains clear. Conclusion: CO₂ is produced by respiring organisms. The KOH inlet ensures CO₂ in the output comes from respiration, not from ambient air.
**Explanation**: CO₂ + Ca(OH)₂ → CaCO₃↓ + H₂O (milky). KOH removes ambient CO₂. Dead control. A common error is not removing CO₂ from inlet air.
**Tags**: respiration, CO2, limewater, experiment, control, KOH

### Question Q-12.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare aerobic and anaerobic respiration in terms of: site in cell, O₂ requirement, ATP yield per glucose, products, and organisms that use each.
**Answer**: | Factor | Aerobic | Anaerobic |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| O₂ required | Yes | No |
| Site | Cytoplasm + mitochondria | Cytoplasm only |
| ATP per glucose | ~38 | ~2 (mammals), ~2 (yeast) |
| Glucose breakdown | Complete → CO₂ + H₂O | Incomplete → lactic acid (animals) or ethanol + CO₂ (yeast) |
| Organisms | Most eukaryotes | Muscle cells during intense exercise; yeast (fermentation); some bacteria |
| When used | All the time (baseline) | During O₂ shortage (intense exercise, waterlogged soil) |
| Duration sustainable | Indefinite (with fuel) | Minutes (lactic acid toxic) |

Aerobic respiration is far more efficient (38 vs 2 ATP). Anaerobic respiration is a short-term emergency backup — it allows glycolysis to continue producing 2 ATP when the electron transport chain is starved of O₂. The lactate/ethanol are waste products that must be removed/oxidised later.
**Explanation**: Full comparison table with quantitative ATP, site, and conditions. A common error is saying anaerobic produces "no energy" or "is the same as aerobic."
**Tags**: aerobic, anaerobic, ATP, comparison, lactic-acid, ethanol, fermentation

### Question Q-12.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain why increasing temperature affects the rate of respiration up to a point, and what happens above the optimum. Refer to enzyme activity.
**Answer**: Respiration is controlled by enzymes (e.g., dehydrogenase in Krebs cycle, ATP synthase on cristae). As temperature rises towards the optimum (~37°C for human enzymes, 25-30°C for many plants): molecules have more kinetic energy → more frequent enzyme-substrate collisions → respiration rate increases. At the optimum, enzymes are working at maximum rate. Above the optimum: hydrogen bonds in the enzyme tertiary structure begin to break → active site shape changes (denatures) → substrate no longer fits → enzyme activity drops sharply → respiration rate falls. At extreme temperatures (>60°C), almost all respiratory enzymes are denatured and respiration stops entirely, leading to cell death. Different organisms have different optimum temperatures based on their environment (thermophilic bacteria can respire at >80°C).
**Explanation**: Enzyme-controlled reaction: T↑ → faster (optimum) → denature (sharp drop). A common error is describing temperature effects without enzyme mechanism.
**Tags**: temperature, enzymes, denaturation, optimum, respiration-rate

### Question Q-12.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student claims that germinating peas respire faster than adult pea plants. Design an experiment to test this claim, stating your hypothesis, variables, method, expected results, and how you would calculate the rate of respiration.
**Answer**: Hypothesis: germinating peas have a higher respiration rate than adult pea plants per gram of tissue. Independent variable: developmental stage (germinating vs adult). Dependent variable: respiration rate (CO₂ produced per minute per gram). Controlled variables: temperature (water bath at 25°C), mass of tissue (e.g., 5 g each), pea variety, time. Method: Place 5 g germinating peas in a respirometer (sealed flask with delivery tube to graduated capillary containing coloured liquid). Place 5 g adult pea leaf discs in an identical respirometer. Add KOH to both to absorb CO₂ (measuring O₂ consumption). Record movement of liquid drop every minute for 10 minutes. Rate of respiration = volume of O₂ consumed per minute per gram (mm³/min/g). Expected results: germinating peas consume O₂ faster — they are rapidly growing, synthesising new cells, and have a high metabolic rate. Adult plants have more structural, non-respiring tissue (fibres). Calculate: set respirometers in a water bath for 5 min to equilibrate, then record distance moved per minute, convert to volume using capillary cross-sectional area. Plot O₂ consumed vs time; gradient = respiration rate.
**Explanation**: Respirometer design with hypothesis, variables, method, rate calculation. A common error is not measuring per gram of tissue.
**Tags**: experiment-design, respirometer, germinating-peas, respiration-rate, O2-consumption
