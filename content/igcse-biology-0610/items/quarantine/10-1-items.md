# Items: 10.1 — Pathogens and disease transmission

### Question Q-10.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a pathogen? Name four types of pathogen and give an example of a disease each causes.
**Answer**: A pathogen is a disease-causing organism. Types: (1) Bacteria — e.g., Salmonella (food poisoning), Mycobacterium tuberculosis (TB). (2) Viruses — e.g., HIV (AIDS), influenza virus (flu). (3) Fungi — e.g., Trichophyton (athlete's foot), Diplocarpon rosae (rose black spot). (4) Protoctista — e.g., Plasmodium (malaria).
**Explanation**: Disease-causing organism, four types with named diseases. A common error is lumping bacteria and viruses as "germs."
**Tags**: pathogen, bacteria, virus, fungi, protoctista

### Question Q-10.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the difference between a communicable and a non-communicable disease?
**Answer**: A communicable (infectious) disease can be transmitted from person to person or from organism to organism (e.g., influenza, TB, malaria, cholera). A non-communicable disease cannot be spread between people; it arises from genetic, lifestyle, or environmental factors (e.g., cancer, heart disease, diabetes, scurvy).
**Explanation**: Transmissible vs not transmissible. A common error is saying non-communicable means "not serious."
**Tags**: communicable, non-communicable, disease, transmission

### Question Q-10.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How is malaria transmitted and how can it be prevented?
**Answer**: Malaria is caused by Plasmodium (a protoctist) transmitted by the bite of an infected female Anopheles mosquito. The mosquito injects Plasmodium into the bloodstream when feeding. Prevention: (1) Insecticide-treated bed nets while sleeping. (2) Mosquito repellent on skin. (3) Eliminating standing water (mosquito breeding sites). (4) Indoor residual spraying with insecticide. (5) Antimalarial prophylactic drugs for travellers. (6) Window screens to exclude mosquitoes.
**Explanation**: Mosquito vector, Plasmodium, multiple prevention layers. A common error is saying malaria is caused by a virus.
**Tags**: malaria, Plasmodium, mosquito, vector, prevention

### Question Q-10.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe how HIV is transmitted and why it cannot be transmitted by casual contact.
**Answer**: HIV is transmitted through direct exchange of body fluids: (1) unprotected sexual contact, (2) sharing contaminated needles, (3) from mother to baby during pregnancy, birth, or breastfeeding, (4) contaminated blood transfusions. HIV cannot survive long outside the body — it cannot be transmitted by casual contact (touching, sharing food, hugging, kissing, toilet seats) because saliva, tears, sweat, and urine contain negligible virus, and intact skin blocks entry.
**Explanation**: Body fluid exchange only; casual contact ≠ transmission. A common error is including sharing utensils.
**Tags**: HIV, AIDS, transmission, body-fluids

### Question Q-10.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How can the spread of infectious diseases be controlled?
**Answer**: Control methods: (1) Hygiene — washing hands, sterilising medical equipment. (2) Vaccination — immunising populations to achieve herd immunity. (3) Isolation/quarantine — separating infected individuals. (4) Vector control — destroying mosquitoes, rats. (5) Safe water supply and sewage systems — prevents waterborne disease. (6) Antibiotics — for bacterial infections only (not viruses). (7) Safe food handling — thorough cooking, refrigeration, hand washing. (8) Public health education.
**Explanation**: Multiple control layers. A common error is saying antibiotics work on viruses.
**Tags**: disease-control, hygiene, vaccination, vector-control

### Question Q-10.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe how Salmonella bacteria cause disease and how infection can be prevented.
**Answer**: Salmonella causes food poisoning (salmonellosis). Transmission: contaminated food (especially raw/undercooked eggs, poultry, unpasteurised milk) or contaminated water. Mechanism: bacteria multiply in the intestines and secrete toxins that damage the gut lining, causing inflammation. Symptoms: vomiting, diarrhoea, abdominal cramps, fever (onset 12-72 hours after ingestion). Prevention: thorough cooking (>70°C kills bacteria), proper refrigeration, separate raw and cooked food, hand washing before food preparation, avoiding raw eggs.
**Explanation**: Bacteria multiply in gut → toxins → symptoms. Prevention: cook, chill, separate, clean. A common error is saying Salmonella is a virus.
**Tags**: Salmonella, food-poisoning, bacteria, toxins, prevention

### Question Q-10.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the transmission, causative pathogen type, and prevention of cholera and malaria. Why are different prevention strategies needed?
**Answer**: Cholera — pathogen: bacterium (Vibrio cholerae). Transmission: faecal-oral route via contaminated water and food. Toxin causes severe diarrhoea and dehydration. Prevention: clean water supply, proper sewage disposal, hand washing, oral rehydration therapy. Malaria — pathogen: protoctist (Plasmodium). Transmission: female Anopheles mosquito vector injecting Plasmodium into blood. Prevention: bed nets, insecticide, mosquito repellent, eliminating standing water, antimalarial drugs. Different strategies are needed because: cholera is waterborne (direct faecal-oral) — sanitation and clean water are primary; malaria is vector-borne (mosquito intermediary) — eliminating the vector and blocking bites are primary. Antibiotics help for cholera (bacterial) but not for malaria (protoctist — needs specific antimalarial drugs).
**Explanation**: Waterborne (cholera) vs vector-borne (malaria), different pathogen types → different strategies. A common error is suggesting same prevention for both.
**Tags**: cholera, malaria, comparison, transmission, prevention, pathogen-type

### Question Q-10.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain how the human body's physical and chemical barriers (skin, mucus, stomach acid) prevent pathogen entry, and why a break in any barrier increases infection risk.
**Answer**: Physical barriers: (1) Skin — tough, keratinised outer layer (epidermis) is impermeable to pathogens. Sebaceous glands secrete sebum (slightly acidic, pH ~5.5) which inhibits bacterial growth. (2) Mucus — sticky secretion in airways traps pathogens and dust; cilia sweep mucus upward to throat where it is swallowed. Chemical barriers: (3) Stomach acid — HCl at pH 1-2 kills most ingested pathogens. (4) Lysozyme in tears and saliva — enzyme that breaks down bacterial cell walls. A break in any barrier increases infection risk because: broken skin → direct entry portal for pathogens into blood; reduced mucus (e.g., from smoking) → pathogens reach lung tissue; reduced stomach acid (e.g., from antacids) → ingested pathogens survive to reach intestines; dry eyes → reduced lysozyme washing.
**Explanation**: Multi-layered physical + chemical barriers, barrier breaks → infection portals. A common error is listing barriers without explaining the infection-risk consequence of each breach.
**Tags**: barriers, skin, mucus, stomach-acid, lysozyme, infection

### Question Q-10.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A traveller returns from a tropical region with fever, chills, and diarrhoea. Two doctors disagree: one suspects malaria, the other suspects cholera. Discuss which pathogen type, mode of transmission, and symptoms support each diagnosis, and explain why accurate diagnosis matters for treatment.
**Answer**: Malaria — pathogen: Plasmodium (protoctist). Transmission: mosquito bite (not person-to-person). Symptoms: cyclical fever, chills, sweating (rupture of infected RBCs). Diarrhoea is less common. No person-to-person transmission risk. Treatment: antimalarial drugs (e.g., chloroquine, artemisinin). Cholera — pathogen: Vibrio cholerae (bacterium). Transmission: contaminated water/food (faecal-oral). Main symptom: profuse watery diarrhoea ("rice-water stool"), rapid dehydration. Person-to-person transmission possible via contaminated hands/surfaces. Treatment: oral rehydration therapy (ORT) + antibiotics. Accurate diagnosis matters because: (1) antimalarials are useless against cholera; antibiotics are useless against malaria (protoctist ≠ bacterium). (2) Cholera requires immediate rehydration to prevent death from dehydration within hours. (3) Malaria requires prompt treatment to prevent severe complications (cerebral malaria). (4) Public health response differs: cholera → sanitation/water; malaria → vector control. Laboratory blood smear (malaria) + stool culture (cholera) would confirm.
**Explanation**: Differential diagnosis — pathogen type, symptoms, treatment implications all differ. A common error is treating both as "tropical fever" without recognising the different management urgency.
**Tags**: differential-diagnosis, malaria, cholera, treatment, transmission
