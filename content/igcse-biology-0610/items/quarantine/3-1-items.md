# Items: 3.1 — Diffusion

### Question Q-3.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is diffusion?
**Answer**: Diffusion is the net movement of particles from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration, down a concentration gradient. This happens as a result of the random movement of particles.
**Explanation**: High to low concentration, down gradient. A common error is saying particles "move to where they are needed."
**Tags**: diffusion, definition, concentration-gradient

### Question Q-3.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: State two factors that affect the rate of diffusion.
**Answer**: (1) Temperature — higher temperature gives particles more kinetic energy, so they move faster and diffuse more quickly. (2) Concentration gradient — a steeper gradient results in faster diffusion. (3) Surface area — a larger surface area allows more diffusion per unit time. (4) Distance — the shorter the distance, the faster the diffusion.
**Explanation**: Temperature, gradient, surface area, distance. A common error is saying particles "change size."
**Tags**: diffusion, factors, rate

### Question Q-3.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Give two examples of diffusion in living organisms.
**Answer**: (1) Oxygen diffuses from alveoli into blood capillaries in the lungs (oxygen concentration is higher in alveoli). (2) Carbon dioxide diffuses from blood into alveoli. (3) Glucose diffuses from the small intestine into blood. (4) Oxygen diffuses from blood into cells for respiration.
**Explanation**: Gas exchange or nutrient absorption. A common error is using active transport examples.
**Tags**: diffusion, examples, gas-exchange

### Question Q-3.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does temperature affect the rate of diffusion? Explain your answer.
**Answer**: Higher temperature increases the rate of diffusion. Particles have more kinetic energy at higher temperatures, so they move faster and diffuse more quickly. The increase in particle speed means more particles cross the membrane or spread through the liquid per unit time.
**Explanation**: More kinetic energy = faster movement. A common error is saying temperature "widens" the concentration gradient.
**Tags**: diffusion, temperature, kinetic-energy, rate

### Question Q-3.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why do small organisms rely on diffusion for gas exchange, but large organisms need specialised exchange surfaces?
**Answer**: Small organisms have a large surface area to volume ratio, so diffusion alone is sufficient for gas exchange. Large organisms have a small surface area to volume ratio — diffusion over large distances is too slow, so they need specialised surfaces (lungs, gills) with large surface areas for efficient gas exchange.
**Explanation**: Surface area to volume ratio. A common error is saying diffusion "stops" at a certain size.
**Tags**: diffusion, surface-area-to-volume, gas-exchange, size

### Question Q-3.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does the concentration gradient affect the rate of diffusion?
**Answer**: A steeper concentration gradient (greater difference between the two regions) results in a faster rate of diffusion. As the concentration equalises, the gradient becomes less steep and the rate of diffusion decreases until equilibrium is reached, where particles continue to move but there is no net movement.
**Explanation**: Steeper gradient = faster rate. A common error is saying diffusion stops at equilibrium (it continues but with no net change).
**Tags**: concentration-gradient, rate, diffusion, equilibrium

### Question Q-3.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: In an experiment, potassium permanganate crystals are placed in water. Describe what happens and explain why, in terms of diffusion.
**Answer**: The purple colour spreads from the crystals throughout the water until the solution is evenly coloured. Potassium permanganate particles diffuse from the region of high concentration (near the crystals) to regions of lower concentration (the surrounding water) due to random particle movement. Eventually, dynamic equilibrium is reached — particles continue to move in all directions but there is no net change in concentration anywhere in the solution.
**Explanation**: Spread of colour, high to low, random movement, equilibrium. A common error is saying the colour "dissolves" (it also diffuses).
**Tags**: diffusion, experiment, potassium-permanganate, equilibrium

### Question Q-3.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A patient with emphysema has damaged alveolar walls, reducing the surface area of their lungs. Explain how this affects the diffusion of oxygen and carbon dioxide, and predict the symptoms the patient would experience.
**Answer**: Reduced alveolar surface area directly reduces the area available for gas exchange by diffusion. Since rate of diffusion is proportional to surface area (Fick's law: rate ∝ surface area × concentration gradient ÷ thickness), less oxygen diffuses into the blood and less CO₂ diffuses out. Predicted symptoms: shortness of breath (hypoxia — low blood oxygen), especially during exercise when oxygen demand increases; cyanosis (blue tinge to skin); fatigue; rapid breathing to compensate. The patient cannot get enough oxygen to meet metabolic demands.
**Explanation**: Fick's law applied, reduced diffusion → hypoxia → breathlessness. A common error is saying "diffusion becomes faster" with less surface area.
**Tags**: emphysema, alveoli, surface-area, diffusion, prediction

### Question Q-3.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student places a cube of agar jelly containing universal indicator (pink at neutral pH) into hydrochloric acid. After 10 minutes, the outer layer of the cube is colourless but the centre remains pink. Use your knowledge of diffusion to explain these observations and predict what would happen if the cube were cut into smaller pieces.
**Answer**: The acid diffuses into the agar from the outside. The outer layer changes colour as the acid neutralises the indicator (pink → colourless). The centre remains pink because the acid has NOT yet diffused all the way in — diffusion over a longer distance takes more time. If the cube were cut into smaller pieces, the diffusion distance would be shorter and the surface area to volume ratio would be larger, so all pieces would turn colourless much faster. This demonstrates why living organisms need large surface area to volume ratios for efficient exchange by diffusion.
**Explanation**: Diffusion distance + SA:V ratio. A common error is saying the centre "doesn't let acid in" — it does, just hasn't reached yet.
**Tags**: diffusion, agar, surface-area-to-volume, experiment, prediction
