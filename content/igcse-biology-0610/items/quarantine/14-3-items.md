# Items: 14.3 — Plant tropisms: phototropism and geotropism

### Question Q-14.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is phototropism? Explain how it benefits a plant.
**Answer**: Phototropism is a directional growth response to light. Shoots grow towards light (positive phototropism) — this ensures leaves are positioned to receive maximum light for photosynthesis. Roots grow away from light (negative phototropism) — this helps them grow deeper into the soil where water and minerals are found. The response is controlled by the plant hormone auxin.
**Explanation**: Shoots towards light (+ve), roots away from light (-ve). A common error is saying roots grow towards light.
**Tags**: phototropism, light, shoots, roots, auxin

### Question Q-14.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is geotropism (gravitropism)? How does it differ from phototropism?
**Answer**: Geotropism/gravitropism is a directional growth response to gravity. Roots grow towards gravity (positive geotropism) — this anchors the plant and enables water/mineral absorption. Shoots grow away from gravity (negative geotropism) — this brings leaves towards light. Unlike phototropism (response to light), geotropism operates even in complete darkness. The two tropisms complement each other — together they orient the shoot upward and the root downward, regardless of how the seed lands.
**Explanation**: Roots with gravity (+ve), shoots against (-ve). Operates in darkness, unlike phototropism. A common error is saying shoots grow with gravity.
**Tags**: geotropism, gravitropism, gravity, roots, shoots

### Question Q-14.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What role does auxin play in phototropism? Explain the mechanism.
**Answer**: Auxin (indoleacetic acid, IAA) is a plant growth hormone produced in the shoot tip. In unilateral (one-sided) light: (1) Auxin is redistributed from the lit side to the shaded side of the shoot tip. (2) Auxin moves down the shaded side via active transport + diffusion. (3) Auxin stimulates cell elongation by loosening cell walls (activating expansin proteins → cell wall loosens → turgor-driven expansion). (4) Cells on the shaded side elongate MORE than cells on the lit side. (5) This differential growth causes the shoot to bend TOWARD the light. The tip detects the light direction (sensing organ) but the bending occurs lower down (response zone). If the tip is removed or covered, no bending occurs — proving the tip is the site of auxin production.
**Explanation**: Auxin redistributes to shaded side → more elongation → bend toward light. Tip detects; bending zone responds. A common error is saying auxin is "destroyed" by light (it is redistributed, not broken down).
**Tags**: auxin, IAA, phototropism, cell-elongation, shaded-side

### Question Q-14.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What role does auxin play in geotropism?
**Answer**: In a horizontal root or shoot, auxin accumulates on the lower side (in response to gravity — starch statoliths in root cap cells settle and redirect auxin). In SHOOTS: higher auxin concentration on the lower side stimulates cell elongation → lower side grows faster → shoot bends UPWARD (negative geotropism). In ROOTS: higher auxin concentration on the lower side INHIBITS cell elongation (roots are more sensitive to auxin) → upper side grows faster → root bends DOWNWARD (positive geotropism). The SAME hormone (auxin) produces OPPOSITE responses in shoots and roots due to different auxin sensitivity thresholds.
**Explanation**: Auxin to lower side → stimulates shoot elongation (up) but inhibits root elongation (down). Sensitivity difference. A common error is saying auxin has the same effect in shoots and roots.
**Tags**: auxin, geotropism, statoliths, differential-sensitivity, root, shoot

### Question Q-14.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe an experiment to investigate phototropism in plant shoots. Include a control.
**Answer**: Apparatus: three sets of young oat (or cress) seedlings. Set A: illuminated from all sides equally (overhead light) — control. Set B: illuminated from one side only (unilateral light — lamp placed to one side, other sides covered). Set C: shoot tips covered with aluminium foil caps, unilateral light. All kept at same temperature, watered equally, for 48 hours. Results: A — shoots grow straight up. B — shoots bend toward the light source. C — shoots with covered tips grow straight up (no bending). Conclusion: the shoot TIP detects light direction; covering the tip prevents auxin redistribution → no differential growth → no bending. The response zone is below the tip.
**Explanation**: Three conditions: all-round light (straight), unilateral (bend), tip covered (no bend — tip detects). A common error is omitting the tip-covered control.
**Tags**: phototropism, experiment, auxin, unilateral-light, control

### Question Q-14.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe an experiment to demonstrate geotropism in roots and shoots.
**Answer**: Apparatus: a clinostat (rotating disc) — one set of germinating bean seeds pinned to a vertical disc rotated slowly (1 revolution per hour) so gravity acts equally from all directions. Control: identical seeds on a stationary disc. Both kept in darkness (eliminates phototropism), watered, 2-3 days. Results: Clinostat — roots and shoots grow straight out (no net gravity direction). Control — roots grow downward (positive geotropism), shoots grow upward (negative geotropism). Conclusion: when gravity direction is cancelled by rotation, directional growth disappears. The gravitational stimulus (detected by statoliths in root cap and shoot endodermis) is required for the directional growth response.
**Explanation**: Clinostat eliminates gravity → straight growth. Control → directional growth. A common error is forgetting to control for light.
**Tags**: clinostat, geotropism, experiment, statoliths, control

### Question Q-14.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare how auxin controls phototropism and geotropism, explaining why the same hormone produces bending toward light in shoots and toward gravity in roots.
**Answer**: Both tropisms involve auxin redistribution followed by differential growth. Phototropism (shoot): light → auxin moves from lit to shaded side → more elongation on shaded side → bend toward light. Geotropism (shoot): gravity → auxin accumulates on lower side → more elongation → upward bend. Geotropism (root): gravity → auxin accumulates on lower side → INHIBITS elongation → upper side grows faster → downward bend. The key is DIFFERENTIAL SENSITIVITY: shoots are stimulated by low auxin concentration (~10⁻⁶ M); roots are more sensitive — even low auxin inhibits root growth, so the higher concentration on the lower side retards root cell elongation. Same auxin, opposite effect, different tissue sensitivity. This is an elegant economy: one hormone orchestrates multiple directional responses by exploiting tissue-specific dose-response curves.
**Explanation**: Shoot = auxin stimulates elongation; Root = auxin inhibits elongation (more sensitive). Same auxin, different dose-response. A common error is saying different hormones control shoot vs root tropisms.
**Tags**: auxin, phototropism, geotropism, differential-sensitivity, comparison

### Question Q-14.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain the experimental evidence that the shoot tip is the site of auxin production and light detection in phototropism.
**Answer**: Key experiments (Boysen-Jensen, Went, Darwin): (1) Darwin's experiment (1880): oat coleoptile tip covered with opaque cap → no bending toward light. Tip removed → no bending. Tip covered with transparent cap → bending normal. Conclusion: tip detects light, and some signal travels from tip to bending zone. (2) Boysen-Jensen (1913): tip cut off, gelatin block inserted between tip and stump → bending normal (signal passes through gelatin). Mica sheet inserted → no bending (signal cannot pass through impermeable barrier). If mica on lit side → still bends (signal travels down shaded side). Conclusion: chemical signal travels down the shaded side. (3) Went (1928): cut off tips, placed on agar blocks → auxin diffused into agar. Agar block placed asymmetrically on decapitated shoot → shoot bends away from agar side even in darkness. Conclusion: auxin is the chemical signal; asymmetric auxin causes bending without light. Collectively: tip produces auxin, light redistributes auxin to shaded side, auxin travels down shaded side, causing differential growth.
**Explanation**: Darwin (tip detects) → Boysen-Jensen (chemical signal, shaded path) → Went (isolated auxin). A common error is attributing all findings to one experiment.
**Tags**: Darwin, Boysen-Jensen, Went, auxin, experimental-evidence, coleoptile

### Question Q-14.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A potted plant is placed on its side in a dark room. After 5 days, describe and explain what you would observe in the shoot and root, referring to the role of auxin.
**Answer**: Observations after 5 days: shoot has bent and grown upward (away from gravity — negative geotropism). Root has bent and grown downward (toward gravity — positive geotropism). Mechanism: (1) Gravity sensed by statoliths (dense starch grains) in root cap columella cells and shoot endodermis. (2) Auxin is actively transported to the lower side of both organs. (3) In the SHOOT: higher auxin on lower side stimulates cell elongation → lower side grows faster → shoot curves UPWARD. (4) In the ROOT: higher auxin on lower side INHIBITS cell elongation (roots are more sensitive to auxin) → upper side grows faster → root curves DOWNWARD. (5) Once vertical, auxin becomes symmetrically distributed and differential growth stops. Growth continues straight. This experiment shows that plants can orient themselves using gravity alone, without any light cue, through auxin-mediated differential growth. The dark room control ensures phototropism is not confounding the result.
**Explanation**: Dark room → no phototropism. Shoot up (auxin stimulates), root down (auxin inhibits). Statoliths→auxin redistribution. A common error is forgetting to explain WHY root and shoot respond oppositely.
**Tags**: gravitropism, auxin, statoliths, differential-sensitivity, horizontal-plant
