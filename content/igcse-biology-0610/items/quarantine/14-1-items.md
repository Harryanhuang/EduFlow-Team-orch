# Items: 14.1 — Nervous system: neurones and reflex arcs

### Question Q-14.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Name the three types of neurone and state their function.
**Answer**: Sensory neurone — carries impulses from receptor cells (e.g., in skin, eye) to the CNS (brain/spinal cord). Relay neurone (interneurone) — located entirely within the CNS; connects sensory neurones to motor neurones. Motor neurone — carries impulses from the CNS to effector organs (muscles for contraction or glands for secretion). The reflex arc follows: stimulus → receptor → sensory → relay (CNS) → motor → effector → response.
**Explanation**: Sensory → CNS, relay → connects within CNS, motor → effectors. A common error is reversing sensory and motor direction.
**Tags**: neurones, sensory, relay, motor, CNS

### Question Q-14.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the difference between the central nervous system (CNS) and the peripheral nervous system (PNS)?
**Answer**: CNS = brain + spinal cord. It receives sensory information, processes and integrates it, and coordinates responses (including conscious thought and involuntary reflexes). PNS = all nerves outside the CNS (cranial nerves from brain, spinal nerves from spinal cord). It carries sensory impulses FROM receptors TO the CNS (sensory division), and motor impulses FROM the CNS TO effectors (motor division). The PNS is the "wiring"; the CNS is the "processor."
**Explanation**: CNS = processor (brain + cord); PNS = wiring (nerves throughout body). A common error is saying the PNS "processes" information.
**Tags**: CNS, PNS, brain, spinal-cord, nerves

### Question Q-14.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe the pathway of a simple reflex arc, using the withdrawal reflex as an example.
**Answer**: Stimulus: touching a hot object. (1) Receptor: thermoreceptors and pain receptors in skin detect heat/pain → generate nerve impulse. (2) Sensory neurone: carries impulse from finger skin receptor INTO the spinal cord via the dorsal root. (3) Relay neurone: in the spinal cord grey matter, synapse between sensory and motor neurones. (4) Motor neurone: carries impulse OUT of the spinal cord via the ventral root to the effector. (5) Effector: biceps muscle contracts → hand pulls away from hot object. Response occurs BEFORE the brain registers pain. The brain receives the information after the action — this explains why you feel pain a moment AFTER pulling your hand away.
**Explanation**: Full reflex arc traced with anatomical accuracy. A common error is including the brain as part of the reflex pathway.
**Tags**: reflex-arc, withdrawal-reflex, spinal-cord, sensory, motor

### Question Q-14.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why are reflex actions important? Give two examples beyond the withdrawal reflex.
**Answer**: Reflex actions are rapid, automatic, and involuntary — they bypass conscious brain processing for speed. Importance: (1) Protection from injury — withdrawal reflex removes body from danger before tissue damage occurs. (2) Maintaining posture and balance — stretch reflex keeps muscles at correct length for standing; knee-jerk reflex maintains upright posture. (3) Homeostatic reflexes — pupil reflex adjusts pupil size to control light entering eye (too bright → constrict; dim → dilate); swallowing reflex prevents choking. (4) Survival reflexes in newborns — sucking reflex, grasping reflex enable feeding and attachment. All involve the same basic arc (receptor→sensory→CNS→motor→effector) but serve different biological needs.
**Explanation**: Speed + automation = protection + homeostasis. A common error is saying reflexes "don't involve the nervous system."
**Tags**: reflex, importance, protection, homeostasis, pupil-reflex, stretch-reflex

### Question Q-14.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe the structure of a motor neurone and explain how each part relates to function.
**Answer**: (1) Cell body — contains nucleus and most organelles; located at one end (in CNS). (2) Dendrites — short, highly branched extensions from cell body; receive impulses from relay neurones across synapses → large surface area for multiple connections. (3) Axon — very long (up to 1 m in humans) single extension from cell body; carries impulse AWAY from cell body toward effector. (4) Myelin sheath — layers of Schwann cell membrane wrapped around axon; insulates axon → prevents ion leakage → saltatory conduction (impulse jumps between nodes of Ranvier) → speed increases from ~1 m/s to ~100 m/s. (5) Nodes of Ranvier — gaps between myelin segments where ion channels are concentrated → action potential regenerated. (6) Axon terminals (synaptic knobs) — branched endings containing mitochondria + vesicles of neurotransmitter → transmit signal to effector across synapse.
**Explanation**: Six structural features, each mapped to function. A common error is confusing motor neurone (cell body at end) with sensory neurone (cell body mid-axon).
**Tags**: motor-neurone, structure, axon, myelin, nodes-of-Ranvier, saltatory-conduction

### Question Q-14.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a synapse and how does it ensure one-directional impulse transmission?
**Answer**: A synapse is the junction between two neurones (or neurone + effector). Structure: presynaptic membrane (end of first neurone) → synaptic cleft (~20 nm gap) → postsynaptic membrane (next neurone/effector). Mechanism: (1) Impulse arrives at presynaptic terminal → voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels open → Ca²⁺ enters. (2) Ca²⁺ causes synaptic vesicles containing neurotransmitter (e.g., acetylcholine) to fuse with membrane and release transmitter into cleft by exocytosis. (3) Neurotransmitter diffuses across cleft → binds to specific receptors on postsynaptic membrane. (4) Ligand-gated Na⁺ channels open → Na⁺ enters → postsynaptic membrane depolarises → new impulse generated. One-direction only because: (a) vesicles + neurotransmitter are only in the presynaptic terminal, (b) receptors are only on the postsynaptic membrane. Impulse cannot travel backwards — this ensures orderly signal flow.
**Explanation**: Ca²⁺ → vesicle fusion → neurotransmitter → diffusion → receptor → Na⁺ → impulse. One-direction because vesicles/receptors are asymmetrically distributed. A common error is saying impulses "jump" across.
**Tags**: synapse, neurotransmitter, acetylcholine, synaptic-cleft, one-direction

### Question Q-14.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the structure and function of sensory and motor neurones, explaining how each is adapted for its specific role.
**Answer**: | Feature | Sensory | Motor |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Cell body position | Mid-axon (in dorsal root ganglion) | At one end (in CNS) |
| Dendrites | Connect to receptor | Connect to relay neurones in CNS |
| Axon length | Short from receptor to cell body; long from cell body to CNS | Very long from CNS to effector (up to 1 m) |
| Myelin | Both myelinated (fast conduction) | Both myelinated |
| Direction | Receptor → CNS | CNS → Effector |
| Axon terminal | Synapses with relay neurone | Synapses with effector (muscle/gland) |

Sensory: cell body in dorsal root ganglion protects it; short axon to receptor means receptor signal reaches CNS fast. Motor: long axon reaches distant muscles; many terminal branches innervate many muscle fibres simultaneously. Both are designed for rapid, reliable impulse conduction — speed is critical for reflexes.
**Explanation**: Comparison table with functional rationale. A common error is drawing both with identical structure.
**Tags**: sensory-neurone, motor-neurone, comparison, structure-function

### Question Q-14.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain the significance of the myelin sheath in impulse transmission. What happens when myelin is damaged, as in multiple sclerosis?
**Answer**: Myelin sheath function: (1) Electrical insulation — prevents ion leakage across the axonal membrane between nodes. (2) Saltatory conduction — action potentials occur ONLY at nodes of Ranvier (where voltage-gated Na⁺ channels are concentrated). The impulse appears to "jump" from node to node → conduction velocity increases from ~1 m/s (unmyelinated) to up to 100 m/s (myelinated). (3) Energy efficiency — fewer Na⁺/K⁺ pump cycles needed per unit length (ions only move at nodes). Multiple sclerosis (MS): autoimmune disease where the immune system attacks myelin → myelin replaced by scar tissue (sclerosis). Consequences: (1) Impulse conduction slows or is blocked entirely because current leaks between nodes. (2) Axons may eventually degenerate. (3) Symptoms depend on which nerves are affected: muscle weakness, numbness, vision problems (optic nerve), coordination loss, fatigue. There is currently no cure — treatments suppress the immune attack and manage symptoms. This demonstrates that myelin is not just "insulation" — it is essential for normal nervous system function.
**Explanation**: Myelin → saltatory conduction → speed + efficiency. MS → demyelination → conduction failure. A common error is describing myelin without explaining saltatory conduction.
**Tags**: myelin, saltatory-conduction, nodes-of-Ranvier, multiple-sclerosis, demyelination

### Question Q-14.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A person steps on a sharp pin barefoot. Describe the full nervous system response from stimulus to final outcome, distinguishing the reflex action and the conscious sensation.
**Answer**: Reflex arc (immediate, involuntary, spinal): (1) Stimulus — pin pierces skin. (2) Receptor — pain receptors (nociceptors) in sole of foot generate impulse. (3) Sensory neurone — impulse travels via sciatic nerve → enters spinal cord through dorsal root → synapses with relay neurone in grey matter. (4) Relay neurone — synapses with motor neurone in the same spinal segment. (5) Motor neurone — impulse exits via ventral root → travels to leg muscles (hamstring contracts to flex knee, quadriceps relaxes) → foot lifts off pin. This reflex takes ~0.1 seconds. Conscious sensation (delayed, involves brain): (6) Simultaneously, branches of the sensory neurone carry impulse UP the spinal cord white matter (ascending tracts) → thalamus (sensory relay centre) → somatosensory cortex (parietal lobe). (7) Brain registers "sharp pain in left foot" — this awareness occurs ~0.5 seconds AFTER the foot is already lifted. (8) Conscious response (voluntary, slower): motor cortex sends impulse down corticospinal tract → spinal cord → motor neurones → leg muscles → balance adjustment (shift weight to other foot), look down, say "ouch," remove pin. The reflex is spinal and unconscious; the pain perception and voluntary response are cerebral and conscious. This dual pathway illustrates the separation of speed and awareness in the nervous system.
**Explanation**: Spinal reflex (~0.1s) vs cortical pain awareness (~0.5s) + voluntary response. A common error is treating reflex and sensation as simultaneous.
**Tags**: reflex, pain, spinal-cord, somatosensory-cortex, dual-pathway
