# Items: 12.2 — Anaerobic respiration in humans and yeast

### Question Q-12.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Write the word equation for anaerobic respiration in humans.
**Answer**: glucose → lactic acid (+ small amount of energy, ~2 ATP). No oxygen is used. No carbon dioxide is produced in human anaerobic respiration. The lactic acid causes muscle fatigue and must be removed later.
**Explanation**: Glucose → lactic acid + energy. No O₂, no CO₂. A common error is including CO₂ in the human anaerobic equation.
**Tags**: anaerobic-respiration, lactic-acid, humans, equation

### Question Q-12.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Write the word equation for anaerobic respiration in yeast (fermentation).
**Answer**: glucose → ethanol + carbon dioxide (+ small amount of energy, ~2 ATP). This process is called alcoholic fermentation. No oxygen is used. CO₂ is produced (unlike human anaerobic respiration). Used commercially in brewing (ethanol) and baking (CO₂ makes dough rise).
**Explanation**: Glucose → ethanol + CO₂ + energy. Yeast produces CO₂; humans do not. A common error is writing lactic acid for yeast.
**Tags**: fermentation, yeast, ethanol, CO2, anaerobic

### Question Q-12.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is "oxygen debt" and how is it repaid?
**Answer**: Oxygen debt is the extra oxygen needed after intense exercise to reverse the effects of anaerobic respiration. It is repaid by continuing to breathe deeply and rapidly after exercise stops. The extra O₂ is used to: (1) oxidise lactic acid back to pyruvate → CO₂ + H₂O in the liver (aerobic pathway). (2) Convert some lactic acid to glucose (gluconeogenesis) in the liver, which returns to muscles via blood. (3) Replenish myoglobin O₂ stores in muscles. (4) Restore ATP and phosphocreatine levels. The total extra O₂ consumed during recovery = the oxygen debt.
**Explanation**: O₂ debt = post-exercise O₂ for lactic acid oxidation/gluconeogenesis + O₂ store replenishment. A common error is saying O₂ debt is "O₂ needed during exercise."
**Tags**: oxygen-debt, lactic-acid, liver, gluconeogenesis, recovery

### Question Q-12.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why anaerobic respiration cannot be sustained for long periods in humans.
**Answer**: Three reasons: (1) Low ATP yield — only ~2 ATP per glucose vs ~38 ATP aerobically (5% efficiency). Cells cannot meet energy demands on anaerobic glucose alone — they would deplete glycogen stores too fast. (2) Lactic acid accumulation — lowers muscle cell pH from ~7.2 to ~6.5, inhibiting enzyme activity (including glycolytic enzymes) → muscle fatigue, pain, cramp. (3) Lactic acid is toxic at high concentrations and must be removed via blood to the liver. The liver can process lactate at a limited rate (~0.3 g/min). If production exceeds removal, pH drops dangerously. Anaerobic respiration is an emergency backup, not a primary strategy.
**Explanation**: Low ATP + lactic acid inhibition + limited clearance rate. A common error is saying anaerobic "produces no energy."
**Tags**: anaerobic-respiration, lactic-acid, fatigue, ATP, pH

### Question Q-12.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe an experiment to demonstrate anaerobic respiration in yeast. Include the method, expected results, and control.
**Answer**: Apparatus: conical flask with glucose solution + yeast suspension, sealed with a delivery tube leading to a test tube of limewater. A layer of oil on top of the yeast-glucose mixture prevents O₂ entry (maintaining anaerobic conditions). Control: identical setup with boiled (dead) yeast. Leave both at 25-30°C for 1 hour. Results: test tube with live yeast — limewater turns milky (CO₂ produced). Control — limewater stays clear. The yeast suspension smells of ethanol. Conclusion: respiring yeast produce CO₂ and ethanol anaerobically. Variation: collect gas in inverted measuring cylinder over water; test gas with limewater to confirm CO₂.
**Explanation**: Oil layer excludes O₂; limewater proves CO₂; dead yeast control. A common error is forgetting the oil layer.
**Tags**: yeast, fermentation, experiment, limewater, anaerobic

### Question Q-12.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Compare anaerobic respiration in humans and yeast, explaining why the products differ.
**Answer**: | Factor | Humans (animals) | Yeast (fungus) |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|
| Equation | glucose → lactic acid | glucose → ethanol + CO₂ |
| CO₂ produced | No | Yes |
| End product fate | Lactic acid → liver → oxidised to CO₂+H₂O or converted to glucose | Ethanol accumulates (toxic at >~15%); CO₂ escapes |
| Reversibility | Reversible — lactate can be recycled | Irreversible — ethanol cannot be converted back to glucose |

Why the difference: humans have lactate dehydrogenase which reduces pyruvate → lactate in one step. Yeast has pyruvate decarboxylase + alcohol dehydrogenase → pyruvate → acetaldehyde → ethanol, releasing CO₂ in the first step. The enzymes set the pathway — both are adaptations that regenerate NAD⁺ to keep glycolysis running when O₂ is unavailable.
**Explanation**: Enzymes determine pathway: LDH in animals → lactate; decarboxylase+alcohol DH in yeast → ethanol+CO₂. A common error is saying the products are "arbitrary."
**Tags**: comparison, humans, yeast, lactate, ethanol, NAD-regeneration

### Question Q-12.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain the role of NAD⁺ regeneration in anaerobic respiration. Why is this essential for cell survival during oxygen shortage?
**Answer**: Glycolysis (glucose → 2 pyruvate) requires NAD⁺ as an electron acceptor at the glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase step. In aerobic respiration, NADH is re-oxidised to NAD⁺ at the electron transport chain (ETC), passing electrons to O₂. When O₂ is absent, the ETC stops → NADH accumulates → NAD⁺ is depleted → glycolysis halts → ATP production stops entirely → cell dies within minutes. Anaerobic respiration solves this: in humans, lactate dehydrogenase transfers electrons from NADH to pyruvate → lactate, regenerating NAD⁺. In yeast, alcohol dehydrogenase transfers electrons from NADH to acetaldehyde → ethanol, regenerating NAD⁺. The key purpose is NOT to produce lactate/ethanol — it is to regenerate NAD⁺ so glycolysis can continue producing 2 ATP per glucose, keeping the cell alive. The waste products (lactate/ethanol) are the price paid.
**Explanation**: NAD⁺ regeneration is the purpose; lactate/ethanol are waste. Without it, glycolysis stops → no ATP → cell death. A common error is saying the purpose of anaerobic respiration is "to produce lactic acid."
**Tags**: NAD, glycolysis, regeneration, electron-transport-chain, anaerobic

### Question Q-12.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain the economic importance of anaerobic respiration in yeast, giving specific examples of how different fermentation conditions produce different products.
**Answer**: (1) Bread-making: yeast + flour (starch → maltose → glucose) → ethanol + CO₂. CO₂ bubbles become trapped in gluten matrix → dough rises. Ethanol evaporates during baking. Temperature: ~27°C for rising. (2) Beer brewing: yeast + malted barley (amylase converts starch → maltose → glucose) → ethanol + CO₂. Hops added for flavour. Anaerobic conditions in sealed vat. Product: ~3-8% ethanol. (3) Wine-making: yeast + grape juice (natural glucose + fructose) → ethanol + CO₂. Anaerobic in sealed fermentation tank. Product: ~9-15% ethanol. Fermentation stops when ethanol concentration reaches ~15% (yeast enzymes denatured). (4) Biofuel (bioethanol): yeast + sugarcane/corn starch → ethanol. Distilled to >95% purity for fuel. (5) Soy sauce/yoghurt: involve different microorganisms (Lactobacillus, Aspergillus) with different fermentation pathways. The same principle — anaerobic respiration by microorganisms — underpins all these, with the specific substrate, temperature, yeast strain, and duration determining the final product.
**Explanation**: Five economic applications with process conditions. A common error is listing products without explaining how conditions affect the outcome.
**Tags**: fermentation, yeast, economic-importance, bread, beer, wine, biofuel

### Question Q-12.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A baker notices bread dough rises faster at 35°C than at 20°C, but at 50°C no rising occurs at all. Explain these observations in terms of enzyme activity and fermentation.
**Answer**: 35°C: closer to yeast enzyme optimum temperature (30-37°C). Enzymes involved in fermentation (amylase to break starch → maltose, maltase → glucose, and glycolytic enzymes) have higher kinetic energy → more frequent enzyme-substrate collisions → faster fermentation rate → more CO₂ produced per minute → dough rises faster. Q₁₀ ≈ 2 = rate roughly doubles. 50°C: above the denaturation temperature of yeast enzymes. Hydrogen bonds in enzyme tertiary structures break → active sites permanently change shape → substrates no longer bind → enzymes inactive → fermentation stops entirely → zero CO₂ production → no rising. Yeast cells are killed at this temperature (thermal death point ~45-50°C). The observation demonstrates that yeast respiration is enzyme-controlled. Practical application: bakers use warm water (~27-35°C) for dough rising and never use hot water (>45°C) which would kill the yeast.
**Explanation**: 35°C = closer to optimum, faster; 50°C = denaturation, zero activity. A common error is describing the temperature effect without linking to fermentation enzymes specifically.
**Tags**: temperature, yeast, fermentation, enzyme-denaturation, Q10, dough-rising
