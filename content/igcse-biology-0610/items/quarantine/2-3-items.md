# Items: 2.3 — Levels of organisation: cells, tissues, organs, systems

### Question Q-2.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Arrange the following in order of increasing complexity: organ, cell, organ system, tissue.
**Answer**: Cell → tissue → organ → organ system. Cells are the basic unit. A tissue is a group of similar cells working together. An organ is made of different tissues working together. An organ system is a group of organs working together.
**Explanation**: Cell-tissue-organ-system. A common error is reversing tissue and organ.
**Tags**: organisation, cell, tissue, organ, system

### Question Q-2.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a tissue? Give two examples.
**Answer**: A tissue is a group of similar cells that work together to perform a specific function. Examples: muscle tissue (contracts to produce movement), epithelial tissue (covers surfaces), nervous tissue (transmits impulses), xylem tissue (transports water in plants).
**Explanation**: Group of similar cells. A common error is calling any group of cells a tissue (they must be similar and work together).
**Tags**: tissue, definition, examples

### Question Q-2.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Name three organs in the human digestive system and state the function of each.
**Answer**: Stomach — churns food and mixes with acid and enzymes to begin protein digestion. Small intestine — completes digestion and absorbs nutrients into the blood. Liver — produces bile to emulsify fats. Also: pancreas produces digestive enzymes; large intestine absorbs water.
**Explanation**: Organ + function. A common error is confusing stomach digestion with absorption (absorption is in the small intestine).
**Tags**: digestive-system, stomach, small-intestine, liver

### Question Q-2.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how the leaf is an organ made of different tissues working together.
**Answer**: A leaf contains: epidermal tissue (protects and controls gas exchange via stomata), palisade mesophyll tissue (contains chloroplasts for photosynthesis), spongy mesophyll tissue (gas exchange), xylem tissue (transports water into the leaf), and phloem tissue (transports sugars away). These different tissues work together for photosynthesis.
**Explanation**: Multiple tissues in one organ. A common error is saying a leaf is a "tissue" not an "organ."
**Tags**: leaf, organ, tissues, photosynthesis

### Question Q-2.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how the circulatory system and respiratory system work together as organ systems.
**Answer**: The respiratory system brings oxygen into the lungs (alveoli) and removes carbon dioxide. The circulatory system transports oxygen from the lungs to all body cells (via red blood cells) and carries carbon dioxide from cells back to the lungs. The two systems work together — gas exchange in the lungs connects them: oxygen diffuses from alveoli into blood capillaries; CO₂ diffuses from blood into alveoli.
**Explanation**: Two systems linked at lungs. A common error is saying one system "does everything."
**Tags**: circulatory-system, respiratory-system, cooperation, gas-exchange

### Question Q-2.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A kidney is an organ in the excretory system. Explain why the kidney is classified as an organ, not a tissue or organ system.
**Answer**: The kidney is made of different tissues working together: epithelial tissue (lining the nephron tubules for filtration and reabsorption), muscular tissue (in blood vessel walls), connective tissue (holding the kidney structure together), and nervous tissue (regulating blood flow). Because it contains multiple tissue types working together to perform a specific function (filtering blood and producing urine), it is an organ, not a single tissue. It is part of the excretory system, not a system by itself.
**Explanation**: Multiple tissues → single function = organ. A common error is calling kidney a "system" or confusing nephron (structure within) with kidney.
**Tags**: kidney, organ, tissue, classification

### Question Q-2.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student claims that "stomata are tissues because they are made of similar cells." Evaluate this claim using knowledge of plant organisation.
**Answer**: Stomata are NOT tissues. Each stoma consists of TWO guard cells (a pair of specialised epidermal cells that open and close the pore). Two cells do not constitute a tissue — a tissue requires a group of similar cells. The stomatal pore itself is an opening, not a cell. The correct classification: guard cells are specialised cells of the epidermal tissue; the leaf (containing guard cells, epidermis, palisade, spongy mesophyll, xylem, and phloem tissues) is the organ. The student has confused a structure (stoma) with a tissue.
**Explanation**: Guard cells = cells, stomatal pore = opening, leaf = organ. A common error is calling stomata "tissues."
**Tags**: stomata, guard-cells, tissue, evaluation

### Question Q-2.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the level-of-organisation hierarchy in plants and animals. Is it always true that "organisms are made of organ systems"? Justify your answer with examples.
**Answer**: Animals: cell → tissue → organ → organ system → organism (e.g., muscle cell → muscle tissue → heart → circulatory system → human). Plants: cell → tissue → organ → organism (e.g., palisade cell → palisade tissue → leaf → plant). Plants do NOT have organ systems in the same sense as animals — their organs (roots, stems, leaves, flowers) work together to support life, but most plants are not organised into distinct closed systems equivalent to the circulatory/nervous/digestive systems of animals. Some simple multicellular organisms (e.g., sponges) lack organs altogether, having only tissues. Therefore, "all organisms are made of organ systems" is false.
**Explanation**: Animals: organ systems; plants: organs only; simple organisms: tissues only. A common error is generalising the animal hierarchy to all organisms.
**Tags**: plant, animal, organ-system, comparison, evaluation

### Question Q-2.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: The stomach contains muscular tissue, epithelial tissue, glandular tissue, and connective tissue. Explain the function of each tissue and how their combined action allows the stomach to carry out its role in digestion.
**Answer**: Muscular tissue: contracts (peristalsis) to churn food and mix it with gastric juices. Epithelial tissue: lines the stomach and protects it from acid corrosion. Glandular tissue: secretes hydrochloric acid (kills bacteria, provides acidic pH) and pepsin enzyme (begins protein digestion). Connective tissue: holds the stomach together and provides structure. Their combined action: muscles churn food while glands secrete enzymes and acid onto it; epithelial tissue protects the organ from self-digestion; connective tissue maintains structure. The coordination of all four tissues allows the stomach to mechanically and chemically digest food simultaneously.
**Explanation**: Four tissues, coordinated function. A common error is listing the tissues without explaining how they work together.
**Tags**: stomach, tissue, digestion, coordination
