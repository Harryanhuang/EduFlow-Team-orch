# Items: 11.1 — Gas exchange: lungs and alveoli

### Question Q-11.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the difference between breathing and respiration?
**Answer**: Breathing (ventilation) is the mechanical process of moving air in and out of the lungs (inhalation and exhalation). It is a physical process involving muscles (diaphragm, intercostals). Respiration is the chemical process occurring inside all living cells that releases energy from glucose (using oxygen in aerobic respiration). Breathing supplies oxygen for respiration and removes the CO₂ produced by respiration.
**Explanation**: Mechanical vs chemical — breathing is the pump, respiration is the furnace. A common error is using the terms interchangeably.
**Tags**: breathing, respiration, ventilation, difference

### Question Q-11.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Describe the composition of inhaled air compared to exhaled air.
**Answer**: Inhaled air: ~21% oxygen, ~0.04% carbon dioxide, variable water vapour, ~78% nitrogen. Exhaled air: ~16% oxygen, ~4% carbon dioxide, saturated with water vapour, ~78% nitrogen. Oxygen decreases by ~5% (used in aerobic respiration). Carbon dioxide increases ~100× (produced by respiration). Water vapour increases (evaporation from moist lung surfaces). Nitrogen is unchanged (it is metabolically inert).
**Explanation**: O₂ down 5%, CO₂ up 100×, H₂O vapour up, N₂ unchanged. A common error is saying exhaled air has "no oxygen."
**Tags**: inhaled-air, exhaled-air, composition, oxygen, CO2, nitrogen

### Question Q-11.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How are alveoli adapted for efficient gas exchange?
**Answer**: Alveoli adaptations: (1) Enormous total surface area (~70 m² in adult humans, millions of alveoli) for maximum diffusion. (2) Walls only one cell thick (squamous epithelium) — very short diffusion distance. (3) Rich capillary network surrounding each alveolus — maintains a steep concentration gradient by carrying away O₂ and bringing CO₂. (4) Moist inner surface — O₂ and CO₂ dissolve in the thin film of water before diffusing, speeding up transfer. (5) Good ventilation — breathing continuously refreshes air in alveoli, maintaining high O₂ and low CO₂.
**Explanation**: Five adaptations — all optimise Fick's Law: rate ∝ (surface area × concentration gradient) / diffusion distance. A common error is saying alveoli have "thick walls for protection."
**Tags**: alveoli, gas-exchange, adaptation, surface-area, Fick's-law

### Question Q-11.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the role of the diaphragm and intercostal muscles in inhalation?
**Answer**: During inhalation (inspiration): (1) Diaphragm contracts and flattens (moves downwards). (2) External intercostal muscles contract — ribs are pulled upwards and outwards. (3) Thoracic volume increases. (4) Pressure inside the thorax decreases below atmospheric pressure (Boyle's Law: volume ↑ → pressure ↓). (5) Air flows from higher pressure outside to lower pressure inside, inflating the lungs. This is an active process requiring energy.
**Explanation**: Diaphragm↓ + ribs↑out → volume↑ → pressure↓ → air in. A common error is saying the diaphragm "relaxes" during inhalation.
**Tags**: inhalation, diaphragm, intercostal-muscles, thoracic-volume, pressure

### Question Q-11.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why is the concentration gradient important for gas exchange in the lungs?
**Answer**: According to Fick's Law, rate of diffusion ∝ concentration gradient. A steep O₂ gradient (high in alveoli, low in blood) drives rapid oxygen diffusion into the blood. A steep CO₂ gradient (high in blood, low in alveoli) drives rapid CO₂ diffusion out of the blood. The gradient is maintained by: (1) continuous ventilation — breathing brings fresh O₂-rich air and removes CO₂-rich air. (2) Continuous blood flow — pulmonary circulation carries away oxygenated blood and brings deoxygenated blood. If either stops, the gradient disappears and gas exchange halts.
**Explanation**: Steep gradient = fast diffusion, maintained by dual flow. A common error is saying gas exchange "doesn't need a gradient."
**Tags**: concentration-gradient, gas-exchange, Fick's-law, ventilation, blood-flow

### Question Q-11.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What adaptations does the trachea have for its function?
**Answer**: The trachea (windpipe): (1) C-shaped rings of cartilage — keep the airway permanently open (patent), preventing collapse during inhalation when pressure is negative. The open part faces the oesophagus, allowing food to pass behind. (2) Ciliated epithelium lining — cilia beat rhythmically upwards, sweeping a layer of mucus towards the throat. (3) Goblet cells — secrete sticky mucus that traps dust, pathogens, and pollutants. The mucus-cilia "escalator" is a critical non-specific defence mechanism.
**Explanation**: Cartilage for patency, cilia + mucus for cleaning. A common error is saying cartilage forms complete rings.
**Tags**: trachea, cartilage, cilia, goblet-cells, mucus

### Question Q-11.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Why is tobacco smoke harmful to the gas exchange system? Include specific effects on cilia, alveoli, and gas transport.
**Answer**: Tobacco smoke contains over 4000 chemicals. Harmful effects: (1) Tar — deposits on airway lining, paralyses and destroys cilia → mucus accumulates → smoker's cough ("chronic bronchitis"). Tar also damages alveolar walls → walls break down and alveoli merge → reduced surface area → emphysema (severe breathlessness). (2) Carcinogens (e.g., benzopyrene) — cause mutations in epithelial cells → lung cancer. (3) Carbon monoxide — binds to haemoglobin 250× more strongly than O₂, forming carboxyhaemoglobin → reduces O₂-carrying capacity → tissues receive less oxygen → fatigue, poor exercise tolerance. (4) Nicotine — addictive, constricts blood vessels. (5) Irritants — stimulate excess mucus production. The combined effect is progressive, irreversible destruction of gas exchange structure.
**Explanation**: Tar → cilia death + emphysema; carcinogens → cancer; CO → O₂ transport reduced. A common error is saying smoking "only causes cancer."
**Tags**: tobacco-smoke, cilia, tar, emphysema, carcinogens, carbon-monoxide

### Question Q-11.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare inhalation and exhalation in terms of muscle action, pressure changes, and whether each is an active or passive process.
**Answer**: Inhalation (inspiration) — active process requiring energy: External intercostals CONTRACT → ribs up and out. Diaphragm CONTRACTS → flattens down. Thoracic volume INCREASES → pressure DECREASES below atmospheric → air flows IN. Exhalation (expiration) — at rest, passive process (no energy): External intercostals RELAX → ribs down and in. Diaphragm RELAXES → domes up. Thoracic volume DECREASES → pressure INCREASES above atmospheric → air flows OUT. Elastic recoil of stretched lung tissue contributes. Forced exhalation (e.g., during exercise, coughing) — active: Internal intercostals CONTRACT → ribs pulled down and in. Abdominal muscles CONTRACT → push diaphragm up further. Both accelerate thoracic volume decrease.
**Explanation**: Inhale=active (contraction), exhale at rest=passive (relaxation + elastic recoil), forced exhale=active. A common error is saying all exhalation is active.
**Tags**: inhalation, exhalation, diaphragm, intercostal-muscles, pressure, active, passive

### Question Q-11.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain how Fick's Law relates the structure of the alveoli to the rate of gas exchange, and predict what happens to gas exchange efficiency if a person develops emphysema.
**Answer**: Fick's Law: Rate of diffusion ∝ (surface area × concentration gradient) / diffusion distance. Alveolar structure optimises each variable: (1) Surface area — millions of alveoli provide ~70 m². (2) Concentration gradient — rich capillary network + continuous ventilation maintain steep gradients. (3) Diffusion distance — one-cell-thick alveolar + capillary walls (combined ~0.5-1 μm). In emphysema: alveolar walls are progressively destroyed by tar/protease imbalance → alveoli merge into fewer, larger sacs → surface area decreases drastically (can drop to <30 m²). Concentration gradient also decreases because fewer capillaries remain. Both changes reduce the rate of gas exchange. The person becomes breathless even at rest (hypoxia) and cannot meet O₂ demand during exercise. The damage is irreversible — only supplemental O₂ and bronchodilators manage symptoms.
**Explanation**: Fick's Law applied — emphysema reduces surface area + gradient → rate drops. A common error is describing emphysema without linking to Fick's Law variables.
**Tags**: Fick's-law, emphysema, surface-area, gas-exchange, alveoli, diffusion
