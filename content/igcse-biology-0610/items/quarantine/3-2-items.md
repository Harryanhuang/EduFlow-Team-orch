# Items: 3.2 — Osmosis

### Question Q-3.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is osmosis?
**Answer**: Osmosis is the net movement of water molecules from a region of higher water potential (more dilute solution) to a region of lower water potential (more concentrated solution), through a partially permeable membrane.
**Explanation**: Water, high to low water potential, partially permeable membrane. A common error is saying osmosis moves solute particles.
**Tags**: osmosis, definition, water-potential, membrane

### Question Q-3.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: State two differences between osmosis and diffusion.
**Answer**: (1) Osmosis only involves water molecules; diffusion involves any particles. (2) Osmosis requires a partially permeable membrane; diffusion does not need a membrane. (3) Osmosis is defined in terms of water potential; diffusion is defined in terms of concentration gradient.
**Explanation**: Water only vs any particles; membrane required vs not required. A common error is saying they are the same thing.
**Tags**: osmosis, diffusion, comparison

### Question Q-3.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe what happens to a plant cell placed in pure water.
**Answer**: Water enters the cell by osmosis (water potential is higher outside). The cell becomes turgid — the vacuole swells and pushes the cytoplasm against the cell wall. The cell wall prevents the cell from bursting. Turgor pressure supports the plant.
**Explanation**: Turgid, cell wall prevents bursting. A common error is saying the cell bursts.
**Tags**: osmosis, plant-cell, turgid, water-potential

### Question Q-3.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe what happens to an animal cell placed in pure water.
**Answer**: Water enters the cell by osmosis. The cell swells and eventually bursts (lyses) because it has no cell wall to resist the pressure. Pure water has a higher water potential than the cell cytoplasm.
**Explanation**: Swells and bursts, no cell wall. A common error is saying it becomes "turgid" (turgid applies only to plant cells).
**Tags**: osmosis, animal-cell, lysis, red-blood-cell

### Question Q-3.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What happens to a plant cell placed in a concentrated sugar solution?
**Answer**: Water leaves the cell by osmosis (water potential is higher inside the cell). The vacuole shrinks, the cytoplasm pulls away from the cell wall, and the cell becomes flaccid (plasmolysed). The plant wilts.
**Explanation**: Plasmolysis, water leaves. A common error is saying water enters.
**Tags**: osmosis, plant-cell, plasmolysis, flaccid

### Question Q-3.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why is osmosis important for plants?
**Answer**: Osmosis allows plants to absorb water from the soil through root hair cells. It also maintains turgor pressure, which supports non-woody plants and keeps leaves spread out for maximum light absorption. Without osmosis, plants would wilt.
**Explanation**: Water absorption + turgor support. A common error is saying osmosis "makes food."
**Tags**: osmosis, plants, water-absorption, turgor

### Question Q-3.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student places potato cylinders in solutions of different concentrations. The cylinder in 0.0 mol/dm³ (pure water) increased in mass by 12%. The cylinder in 0.4 mol/dm³ decreased in mass by 8%. Explain both results in terms of osmosis.
**Answer**: In pure water: water potential is much higher outside the potato cells, so water enters by osmosis, increasing mass. In 0.4 mol/dm³: the sugar solution has a lower water potential than the potato cell sap, so water leaves the potato cells by osmosis, decreasing mass. The solution is hypertonic relative to the cells. If the mass had not changed, the solution would be isotonic.
**Explanation**: Water potential differences drive water movement. A common error is saying "sugar enters" the potato.
**Tags**: osmosis, potato, experiment, water-potential, isotonic

### Question Q-3.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A patient is given an intravenous drip of pure water instead of saline solution. Explain what would happen to the patient's red blood cells and why this is dangerous.
**Answer**: The pure water has a much higher water potential than the red blood cell cytoplasm. Water would enter the red blood cells rapidly by osmosis, causing them to swell and burst (haemolysis). This would reduce the number of functional red blood cells in the blood, causing severe anaemia and reducing oxygen transport to tissues. This is why IV drips use isotonic saline (0.9% NaCl), which has the same water potential as blood plasma.
**Explanation**: Haemolysis, pure water → bursting, clinical application. A common error is saying the cells shrink in pure water.
**Tags**: osmosis, red-blood-cell, haemolysis, intravenous, clinical

### Question Q-3.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A marine fish is placed in a freshwater tank. Explain why the fish dies, referring to osmosis in the fish's gill cells. Is the freshwater hypotonic or hypertonic to the fish's body fluids?
**Answer**: Freshwater is hypotonic (higher water potential) relative to the fish's body fluids. Water enters the fish's gill cells and body by osmosis through the gill membranes. The fish's cells swell and burst (lyse), causing organ failure and death. Marine fish are adapted to excrete excess salt and conserve water in saltwater environments. They lack the adaptations freshwater fish have (e.g., producing large volumes of dilute urine) to remove the excess water that enters by osmosis.
**Explanation**: Hypotonic freshwater vs marine fish, water enters, cells lyse. A common error is saying "salt enters" causing death.
**Tags**: osmosis, fish, freshwater, marine, adaptation
