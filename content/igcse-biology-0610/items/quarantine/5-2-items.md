# Items: 5.2 — Factors affecting enzyme activity

### Question Q-5.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is meant by the "optimum temperature" for an enzyme?
**Answer**: The optimum temperature is the temperature at which the enzyme works at its fastest rate. For most human enzymes, the optimum is about 37°C (body temperature). Below the optimum, the rate is slower due to less kinetic energy. Above the optimum, the enzyme denatures and the rate drops sharply.
**Explanation**: Fastest rate, body temperature. A common error is saying the optimum is "any high temperature."
**Tags**: enzyme, optimum, temperature, denaturation

### Question Q-5.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What happens to an enzyme if the temperature is raised too high above its optimum?
**Answer**: The enzyme denatures. The high temperature breaks the hydrogen and ionic bonds that maintain the enzyme's specific three-dimensional shape. The active site changes shape permanently, so the substrate can no longer fit. The enzyme loses its catalytic ability and cannot be restored — denaturation is usually irreversible.
**Explanation**: Shape changes, active site destroyed, irreversible. A common error is saying denaturation is "reversible on cooling."
**Tags**: denaturation, temperature, irreversible, active-site

### Question Q-5.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why the rate of an enzyme-controlled reaction increases as temperature rises from 10°C to 40°C.
**Answer**: Higher temperature gives the enzyme and substrate molecules more kinetic energy. They move faster, collide more frequently, and have more energy to overcome the activation energy barrier. More successful collisions per unit time = more enzyme-substrate complexes formed = faster reaction rate. The rate approximately doubles for every 10°C rise (Q₁₀ = ~2).
**Explanation**: More kinetic energy, more collisions, more ES complexes. A common error is saying "the enzyme works harder."
**Tags**: enzyme, temperature, kinetic-energy, reaction-rate

### Question Q-5.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why the rate of an enzyme-controlled reaction decreases sharply above 50°C.
**Answer**: Above 50°C, the kinetic energy is so high that it breaks the hydrogen and ionic bonds holding the enzyme's tertiary structure together. The enzyme's active site changes shape — it denatures. The substrate can no longer fit into the active site. Fewer functional enzyme molecules mean fewer enzyme-substrate complexes and a much lower reaction rate. At very high temperatures, all enzymes denature and the rate drops to zero.
**Explanation**: Bonds break → denaturation → no active site. A common error is saying the enzyme "dies" — denaturation is physical not biological death.
**Tags**: enzyme, denaturation, temperature, bonds

### Question Q-5.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why increasing substrate concentration increases the rate up to a plateau.
**Answer**: At low substrate concentration, many enzyme active sites are empty and available to accept substrate. More substrate → more enzyme-substrate complexes → faster rate. At high concentration, all active sites are occupied at any given moment — the enzyme is saturated. Adding more substrate cannot increase the rate because there are no free active sites. The rate plateaus: enzyme concentration becomes the limiting factor.
**Explanation**: Empty sites → all filled → saturation. A common error is saying the enzyme "runs out."
**Tags**: substrate-concentration, saturation, active-site, limiting-factor

### Question Q-5.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does pH affect enzyme activity? Why do different enzymes have different optimum pH values?
**Answer**: pH affects the charges on the amino acids that make up the enzyme's active site. Changes in H⁺ concentration can disrupt ionic and hydrogen bonds, altering the active site shape. Each enzyme has an optimum pH where the active site shape is optimal — this depends on the particular amino acids in the active site and the enzyme's environment. Pepsin (stomach) has optimum pH 2; trypsin (small intestine) has optimum pH 8. Most intracellular enzymes have optimum pH near 7.
**Explanation**: pH changes charges on amino acids → alters active site shape. A common error is saying all enzymes work at pH 7.
**Tags**: pH, enzyme, optimum, denaturation, amino-acids

### Question Q-5.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Describe how to investigate the effect of temperature on enzyme activity using amylase and starch, and explain why each control variable is important.
**Answer**: Method: (1) Set up water baths at 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 60°C. (2) Place amylase + starch in a test tube at each temperature for 2 min equilibration. (3) Mix, start timer. (4) Every 30s, remove a drop and test with iodine on a spot tile. (5) Record time until iodine stays orange (no starch). (6) Rate = 1/time. Controls: same enzyme concentration (volume + dilution), same starch concentration + volume, same pH (buffer). Why each: changing any of these would change the rate independent of temperature, making the results unreliable.
**Explanation**: Water baths, iodine test, timed sampling, controlled variables. A common error is not equilibrating the solutions to temperature before mixing.
**Tags**: amylase, starch, temperature, method, variables

### Question Q-5.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: An enzyme in Arctic fish works optimally at 5°C. Explain how its active site structure differs from the same enzyme in tropical fish, and predict what would happen if the Arctic fish enzyme were heated to 37°C.
**Answer**: The Arctic enzyme has a tertiary structure with fewer hydrogen and ionic bonds holding its 3D shape together — this allows it to remain flexible (functional) at low temperatures where molecules have less kinetic energy. The tropical enzyme has MORE bonds, making it more rigid and requiring higher temperatures to achieve the optimal flexibility. If the Arctic enzyme were heated to 37°C, it would denature — the excess kinetic energy would break its weaker bonds, destroying the active site shape. The tropical enzyme would still function at 37°C. This is an example of adaptation to environmental temperature.
**Explanation**: Cold-adapted = fewer bonds, flexible at low T, fragile at high T. A common error is saying enzymes are structurally identical across species.
**Tags**: enzyme, adaptation, Arctic, temperature, denaturation

### Question Q-5.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student says, "At 60°C, the amylase reaction stops because all the substrate has been used up." The student is wrong — the reaction stopped because the enzyme denatured. Design an experiment to test which explanation is correct, using only the original materials.
**Answer**: Test the student's claim: after the reaction stopped at 60°C, cool the test tube to 40°C (optimum). Then add fresh amylase. If the original amylase was denatured (not "substrate used up"), adding fresh enzyme will restart the reaction (iodine → orange). If the student was right (substrate used up), adding enzyme would have no effect — but adding fresh starch would restart it. A second test: add fresh starch instead. If the reaction does NOT restart, the original enzyme was denatured and all the starch is still present (iodine test would be blue-black). The combination confirms denaturation — starch still present but enzyme inactive.
**Explanation**: Cool + add enzyme → restarts = enzyme was denatured. Cool + add starch → no reaction = confirms. A common error in method design is only testing one possibility.
**Tags**: enzyme, denaturation, experiment-design, hypothesis-testing
