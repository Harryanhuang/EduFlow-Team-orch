# Items: 7.1 — Diet and balanced nutrition

### Question Q-7.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a balanced diet?
**Answer**: A balanced diet contains all the essential nutrients in the correct proportions to maintain health. The seven components are: carbohydrates (energy), proteins (growth and repair), lipids (energy store, insulation), vitamins (various functions), minerals (various functions), dietary fibre (aids digestion), and water (solvent, transport).
**Explanation**: All nutrients in correct proportions. A common error is forgetting fibre or water as dietary components.
**Tags**: balanced-diet, nutrients

### Question Q-7.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the main function of carbohydrates in the diet?
**Answer**: Carbohydrates are the main source of energy for the body. They are broken down into glucose during digestion. Glucose is used in cellular respiration to release energy for all cell activities. Excess glucose is stored as glycogen in the liver and muscles, or converted to fat.
**Explanation**: Main energy source. A common error is saying carbohydrates are "for growth" (that's protein).
**Tags**: carbohydrates, energy, glucose

### Question Q-7.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain the effects of scurvy and rickets, and state which nutrient deficiency causes each.
**Answer**: Scurvy: caused by vitamin C deficiency. Symptoms include bleeding gums, slow wound healing, weakened blood vessel walls (easy bruising). Collagen synthesis requires vitamin C. Rickets: caused by vitamin D deficiency. Symptoms include soft, weak bones that bend (bowed legs) because calcium absorption is impaired without vitamin D. Both are deficiency diseases preventable by a balanced diet.
**Explanation**: Vit C → scurvy; Vit D → rickets. A common error is confusing which vitamin causes which disease.
**Tags**: scurvy, rickets, vitamin-C, vitamin-D, deficiency

### Question Q-7.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What are the effects of iron deficiency and calcium deficiency?
**Answer**: Iron deficiency causes anaemia — insufficient haemoglobin in red blood cells, leading to tiredness, lack of energy, breathlessness, and pale skin. Iron is a key component of haemoglobin. Calcium deficiency causes weak bones and teeth, and can lead to rickets in children and osteoporosis (brittle bones) in adults.
**Explanation**: Iron → anaemia (tiredness); Calcium → weak bones. A common error is confusing the symptoms.
**Tags**: iron, calcium, anaemia, deficiency

### Question Q-7.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why fibre is important even though it is not digested.
**Answer**: Fibre (cellulose/roughage) adds bulk to food in the alimentary canal. This stimulates peristalsis — the rhythmic muscular contractions that move food through the digestive system. Fibre helps prevent constipation, reduces the risk of bowel diseases, and promotes a feeling of fullness (satiety), which helps prevent overeating. It is not absorbed but passes through and is egested.
**Explanation**: Bulk → stimulates peristalsis, prevents constipation. A common error is saying fibre is "absorbed" or "digested."
**Tags**: fibre, peristalsis, digestion, constipation

### Question Q-7.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why do pregnant women need more iron, calcium, and protein in their diet?
**Answer**: Iron: needed to produce extra haemoglobin for increased blood volume and to supply oxygen to the developing foetus. Calcium: needed for foetal bone and tooth development. Protein: needed for growth of foetal tissues and the placenta. Deficiency in any of these can harm both mother and baby — e.g., iron-deficiency anaemia increases the risk of premature birth.
**Explanation**: Iron→haemoglobin for foetus; Calcium→foetal bones; Protein→tissue growth. A common error is saying "more energy" without specifying nutrients.
**Tags**: pregnancy, iron, calcium, protein, nutrition

### Question Q-7.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A person switches to a vegan diet and stops eating all animal products. Explain which nutrients they may become deficient in and what foods they could eat to prevent each deficiency.
**Answer**: Potential deficiencies: (1) Iron — plant iron (non-haem) is less easily absorbed than animal iron (haem). Solution: eat dark leafy greens (spinach), legumes (lentils), and fortified cereals with vitamin C (enhances iron absorption). (2) Vitamin B₁₂ — only found naturally in animal products. Solution: eat fortified foods (B₁₂-fortified plant milk, cereal, nutritional yeast) or take supplements. (3) Calcium — dairy is the main source for many people. Solution: eat fortified plant milk, tofu set with calcium, leafy greens. (4) Protein — plant proteins may be incomplete (lacking certain essential amino acids). Solution: combine complementary proteins (rice + beans, hummus + pitta). With careful planning, a vegan diet can meet all nutritional needs.
**Explanation**: Iron/B₁₂/Calcium/Protein strategies. A common error is saying "all vegans will be deficient" — it's manageable with knowledge.
**Tags**: vegan, deficiency, iron, vitamin-B12, calcium, protein

### Question Q-7.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A child in a developing country has a swollen belly, thinning hair, and is underweight. The child eats mainly rice. Identify the likely deficiency condition and explain the cause and treatment.
**Answer**: The condition is kwashiorkor — severe protein deficiency. The child gets enough calories from rice (carbohydrates) but insufficient protein. Protein is needed to make blood plasma proteins (albumin), which maintain osmotic balance. Without enough albumin, fluid leaks from blood into tissues, causing the swollen belly (oedema). Hair thins because keratin (a protein) cannot be made. Treatment: gradually introduce protein-rich foods (eggs, milk, legumes, fish) and ensure a balanced diet. Complications (infections) should be treated simultaneously.
**Explanation**: Kwashiorkor = protein deficiency despite adequate calories → oedema, hair loss. A common error is confusing kwashiorkor with marasmus (total calorie + protein deficiency).
**Tags**: kwashiorkor, protein-deficiency, oedema, malnutrition

### Question Q-7.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the energy requirements and dietary needs of a sedentary office worker and a professional cyclist during the Tour de France, explaining the biological reasons for the differences.
**Answer**: Cyclist needs: much higher total energy intake (8,000-10,000 kcal/day vs ~2,500 for office worker) because cycling 200 km/day burns enormous energy. Carbohydrates: very high — to maintain glycogen stores for prolonged aerobic respiration in muscles. Protein: higher — for muscle repair after daily micro-damage from intense exercise. Water: vastly higher — to replace water lost through sweating (can be >10 L/day) and prevent dehydration which impairs performance. Electrolytes (Na⁺, K⁺): higher — lost in sweat and needed for nerve impulses and muscle contraction. Office worker: lower energy needs, balanced diet sufficient. The difference reflects metabolic rate, muscle activity, and thermoregulatory demands.
**Explanation**: Energy/CHO/protein/water/electrolytes all dramatically higher for elite athlete. A common error is only mentioning "more food" without specifying nutrients.
**Tags**: energy-requirements, athlete, comparison, nutrition
