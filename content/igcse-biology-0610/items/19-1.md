**Question**: What is habitat destruction?
**Answer**: Habitat destruction is the process of damaging or completely destroying natural habitats so that the organisms living there cannot survive. Examples include deforestation, draining wetlands, building on natural land, and pollution that makes habitats uninhabitable.

**Question**: Explain why deforestation in the Amazon rainforest threatens species diversity.
**Answer**: The Amazon rainforest has the highest biodiversity of any terrestrial ecosystem. Deforestation destroys habitats directly, causing species extinction. Endemic species (found nowhere else) are particularly at risk. The forest also regulates climate and water cycles — its loss affects ecosystems far beyond the cleared areas.

**Question**: What is habitat fragmentation?
**Answer**: Habitat fragmentation occurs when large, continuous habitats are broken up into smaller, isolated patches. This can result from road building, agriculture, or urbanisation. Isolated populations cannot interbreed, reducing genetic variation. Small populations are more vulnerable to extinction from disease or environmental change.

**Question**: How does pollution contribute to biodiversity loss?
**Answer**: Pollution (air, water, soil) makes habitats toxic or unsuitable for organisms. Examples: acid rain damages forests and lakes; pesticides accumulate in food chains and harm top predators (biological magnification); oil spills kill marine organisms; plastic waste entangles and is ingested by animals. Pollution can cause local extinctions and reduce species diversity.

**Answer**: Methods: 1. Habitat protection (national parks) — effective but requires enforcement; may not protect migratory species. 2. Captive breeding — useful for critically endangered species but expensive; reintroduction success varies. 3. Legal protection (CITES, anti-poaching laws) — essential but enforcement varies. 4. Sustainable development — balances human needs with conservation but requires international cooperation. 5. Gene banks and seed banks — preserves genetic diversity cheaply but does not protect ecosystems. No single method is sufficient; integrated approaches are needed.

**Question**: Explain why species in small, isolated populations are at greater risk of extinction.
**Answer**: Small populations have reduced genetic variation, making them less adaptable to environmental changes. They are more vulnerable to inbreeding depression (reduced fitness from mating between relatives). Random events (disease, natural disaster) are more likely to affect the entire population. Small populations may not be viable long-term even without additional threats.

**Question**: Discuss the conflict between development and conservation.
**Answer**: Development provides jobs, housing, and economic growth, which can improve human welfare. However, development often destroys natural habitats and accelerates biodiversity loss. Solutions include: sustainable development (meeting current needs without compromising future generations), ecological impact assessments before development, creating wildlife corridors between fragmented habitats, and offset schemes where development in one area is compensated by conservation in another.

**Question**: What is a keystone species?
**Answer**: A keystone species is one whose removal has a disproportionate effect on the ecosystem, causing major changes in species composition and ecosystem function. Examples: sea otters (control sea urchin numbers, protecting kelp forests); bees (major pollinators of many plants); top predators (control prey populations). Their loss can trigger trophic cascades.
