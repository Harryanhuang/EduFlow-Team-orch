**Tags**: natural selection | evolution | adaptation | speciation
**Difficulty**: S
**Question**: What is biodiversity?
**Answer**: Biodiversity is the variety of all living organisms in an ecosystem — the number of different species (species richness), the number of individuals of each species (species evenness), and the variety of habitats and genetic variation within species. High biodiversity means a healthy, resilient ecosystem.

**Difficulty**: S
**Question**: Explain how deforestation leads to loss of biodiversity.
**Answer**: Deforestation destroys habitats directly, killing organisms and forcing others to leave. Species that depend on the forest cannot survive elsewhere, leading to extinction. It also fragments habitats, isolating populations and reducing genetic variation. Deforestation disrupts food chains and nutrient cycles. The loss of plant species reduces oxygen production and carbon dioxide absorption, affecting global climate.

**Question**: What is the difference between conservation and preservation?
**Answer**: Conservation: managed use and protection of natural resources and biodiversity. Allows sustainable human use (e.g., selective logging, national parks). Preservation: maintaining ecosystems in their natural state without human interference. Conservation is more practical as it acknowledges human needs while protecting biodiversity.

**Answer**: Captive breeding programmes: endangered animals are bred in zoos or special centres to increase population numbers. Individuals are carefully managed to maintain genetic diversity. **Difficulty**: C
**Question**: Evaluate the effectiveness of national parks in conserving biodiversity.
**Answer**: National parks protect large areas of natural habitat from development, preserving ecosystems and species. They provide safe zones for endangered species to recover. However, effectiveness is limited by: illegal poaching (e.g., rhinos for horns), insufficient funding for rangers, habitat fragmentation at park boundaries, and climate change altering habitats inside parks. Success varies — some species recover well; others remain threatened because a single park cannot protect large-ranging animals or migratory species.

## Item 7
**Tags**: natural selection | evolution | adaptation | speciation
**Difficulty**: C
**Question**: Explain how habitat fragmentation reduces biodiversity.
**Answer**: Habitat fragmentation divides large continuous habitats into smaller, isolated patches. Species requiring large territories cannot survive in small fragments. Populations become isolated, reducing gene flow and increasing inbreeding, which reduces genetic variation and adaptability. Edge effects change conditions (microclimate, predator numbers) at fragment boundaries. The total habitat area is reduced, supporting fewer individuals and species. Fragments may be too small to sustain species requiring specific habitats (e.g., large predators).

**Question**: Discuss the ethical arguments for and against conservation of biodiversity.
**Answer**: Arguments for: All species have intrinsic value — they have a right to exist regardless of usefulness to humans. Biodiversity provides ecosystem services (pollination, water purification, climate regulation) worth billions. Many medicinal products come from wild species. Future generations should inherit a rich planet. Arguments against: Human needs (food, housing, jobs) sometimes outweigh conservation. Developing countries may need to exploit resources for economic development. Some species may seem harmful (parasites, disease organisms). Conservation costs money that could be spent on human welfare.

**Question**: What is the greenhouse effect?
**Answer**: The greenhouse effect is the warming of Earth's surface caused by gases in the atmosphere (carbon dioxide, methane, water vapour) that absorb and re-emit infrared radiation. Sunlight passes through the atmosphere and warms the Earth's surface. The Earth radiates heat (infrared). Greenhouse gases absorb some of this heat and re-emit it in all directions, including back to Earth, keeping the planet warm enough for life. Without it, Earth would be about -18°C instead of +15°C.
