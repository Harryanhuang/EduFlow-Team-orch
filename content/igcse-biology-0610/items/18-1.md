## Item 1
**Tags**: DNA | RNA | protein synthesis | transcription
**Difficulty**: S
**Question**: What is fermentation?
**Answer**: Fermentation is anaerobic respiration — the breakdown of glucose without oxygen. In yeast, this produces ethanol and carbon dioxide (alcoholic fermentation). In muscle cells, this produces lactic acid. Fermentation releases a small amount of energy from glucose.

**Tags**: DNA | RNA | protein synthesis | transcription
**Difficulty**: S
**Question**: Explain the conditions needed for effective fermentation in yeast.
**Answer**: Yeast needs: warmth (optimal 30-35°C — enzymes work faster at higher temperatures up to this point), anaerobic conditions (no oxygen for anaerobic respiration), water, and a sugar source (glucose, sucrose, or other fermentable sugars). Extreme temperatures or the presence of oxygen will stop or kill fermentation.

## Item 4
**Tags**: DNA | RNA | protein synthesis | transcription
**Difficulty**: S
**Question**: Describe how bread making uses fermentation.
**Answer**: Yeast is added to a dough mixture of flour, water, and sometimes sugar. The yeast carries out anaerobic respiration (fermentation), producing carbon dioxide gas (which makes the dough rise/expand) and ethanol (which evaporates during baking). The dough is left in a warm place for fermentation to occur. Kneading distributes the gas bubbles evenly.

**Tags**: DNA | RNA | protein synthesis | transcription
**Difficulty**: S
**Question**: What is the difference between batch culture and continuous culture for microorganism production?
**Answer**: Batch culture: all nutrients added at the start, fermentation runs until nutrients are exhausted, then product is harvested. Simple but inefficient. Continuous culture: nutrients are constantly added and products continuously removed, maintaining optimum conditions for maximum yield. More efficient but requires careful control.

**Difficulty**: C
**Question**: Evaluate the advantages and disadvantages of using microorganisms to produce human insulin.
**Answer**: Advantages: Pure product, no risk of infection from contaminated human insulin, unlimited supply, cheaper in the long term, consistent quality. Disadvantages: Requires sterile fermenters and equipment (expensive), high energy costs for cooling and aeration, contamination risk can ruin entire batch, public concern about genetically modified organisms, initial development costs high.

**Question**: Explain the conditions needed for industrial-scale fermenter operation.
**Answer**: 1. Sterile conditions to prevent contamination. 2. Controlled temperature (optimal for enzyme activity of the microorganism). 3. Oxygen supply (for aerobic respiration, unless anaerobic fermentation is required). 4. pH control (microorganisms have optimal pH ranges). 5. Nutrient supply — carbohydrate, nitrogen, vitamins, minerals. 6. Agitation — to mix nutrients, oxygen, and distribute heat evenly.

## Item 8
**Tags**: DNA | RNA | protein synthesis | transcription
**Difficulty**: C
**Question**: Why is it important to maintain aseptic conditions in fermenters?
**Answer**: Aseptic conditions prevent contamination by unwanted microorganisms. Contaminants compete for nutrients, reduce product yield, and may produce toxic substances. They can ruin an entire batch, wasting time, nutrients, and energy. Sterilisation of equipment and air filters, plus sterile nutrient media, maintains purity.

**Question**: What is a fermenter (bioreactor)?
**Answer**: A fermenter is a large container used to grow microorganisms under controlled conditions for industrial production of substances like antibiotics, enzymes, or biofuels. Fermenters allow precise control of temperature, pH, oxygen, and nutrient supply to maximise yield.
