**Question**: What is genetic engineering?
**Answer**: Genetic engineering is the process of modifying an organism's DNA by adding, removing, or altering specific genes. This allows scientists to give organisms new traits or modify existing ones. Examples include inserting the human insulin gene into bacteria to produce insulin, or making crops herbicide-resistant.

**Tags**: genetic engineering | DNA | gene | recombinant
**Difficulty**: S
**Question**: Describe the main steps in producing a genetically modified organism (GMO) such as insulin-producing bacteria.
**Answer**: 1. Isolate the desired gene (e.g., human insulin gene) using restriction enzymes. 2. Insert the gene into a vector (plasmid) using ligase enzyme. 3. Transfer the vector into bacterial cells (transformation). 4. Identify transformed bacteria using marker genes (e.g., antibiotic resistance). 5. Clone the transformed bacteria (large-scale culture). 6. Harvest and purify the product (insulin).

**Question**: What is a plasmid and why is it useful in genetic engineering?
**Answer**: A plasmid is a small, circular, extra-chromosomal DNA molecule found in bacteria. Plasmids can replicate independently of the main bacterial chromosome. In genetic engineering, plasmids are used as vectors to carry new genes into bacterial cells. They are easy to isolate, can be cut with restriction enzymes, and can accept inserted genes, making them ideal for transferring genetic material.

## Item 5
**Tags**: genetic engineering | DNA | gene | recombinant
**Difficulty**: S
**Question**: What are restriction enzymes and how are they used in genetic engineering?
**Answer**: Restriction enzymes are enzymes that cut DNA at specific base sequences (recognition sites). Different restriction enzymes cut at different sequences. They are used to cut open plasmids and to cut out the desired gene from donor DNA. This creates complementary sticky ends that can be joined together using DNA ligase.

## Item 6
**Tags**: genetic engineering | DNA | gene | recombinant
**Difficulty**: C
**Question**: Evaluate the advantages and disadvantages of genetic engineering in agriculture.
**Answer**: Advantages: GM crops can be herbicide-resistant (easier weed control), pest-resistant (reduced pesticide use), and drought-tolerant (improved yield). Can increase food production and reduce environmental impact. Can improve nutritional content (e.g., Golden Rice with vitamin A). Disadvantages: potential transfer of genes to wild relatives (gene flow), possible effects on non-target organisms, development of resistant pests, ethical concerns about playing with nature, and uncertainty about long-term health effects. Requires careful regulation.

## Item 7
**Tags**: genetic engineering | DNA | gene | recombinant
**Difficulty**: C
**Question**: Explain the role of DNA ligase in genetic engineering.
**Answer**: DNA ligase is an enzyme that joins DNA fragments together by forming phosphodiester bonds. After restriction enzymes cut both the plasmid vector and the donor DNA, DNA ligase seals the new gene into the plasmid by joining the sugar-phosphate backbone of the DNA strands. This creates a recombinant plasmid that can be taken up by bacterial cells.

## Item 8
**Tags**: genetic engineering | DNA | gene | recombinant
**Difficulty**: C
**Question**: What is CRISPR-Cas9 and how does it differ from traditional genetic engineering?
**Answer**: CRISPR-Cas9 is a gene-editing tool that uses a guide RNA to direct the Cas9 enzyme to a specific DNA sequence, where it cuts both strands. The cell's repair mechanisms then introduce mutations or insert new DNA. Traditional genetic engineering typically adds entire genes using vectors. CRISPR allows precise editing of existing genes without adding foreign DNA, making it faster, cheaper, and more precise. It can knock out, modify, or replace specific genes within an organism's own genome.

**Question**: What is meant by a transgenic organism?
**Answer**: A transgenic organism is one that contains DNA from another species inserted into its genome. The inserted DNA is called a transgene. Examples include bacteria producing human insulin (transgenic bacteria) and crops with bacterial genes for pest resistance (transgenic crops).
