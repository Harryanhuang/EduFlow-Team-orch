## Item 1
**Tags**: mitosis | meiosis | cell division | chromosome
**Difficulty**: S
**Question**: What is the difference between a chromosome and a chromatid?
**Answer**: A chromosome is a single molecule of DNA. Before cell division, each chromosome replicates — each chromosome then consists of two identical sister chromatids joined at the centromere. After replication, each chromatid is considered a complete chromosome when they separate during anaphase.

**Question**: State two differences between mitosis and meiosis.
**Answer**: 1. Mitosis produces two genetically identical daughter cells; meiosis produces four genetically different gametes. 2. Mitosis maintains chromosome number; meiosis halves it. 3. Mitosis has one division; meiosis has two divisions. 4. Mitosis occurs in body cells; meiosis produces gametes.
**Explanation**: Recall that chromosomes are thread-like structures made of DNA that carry genetic information.

## Item 3
**Tags**: mitosis | meiosis | cell division | chromosome
**Difficulty**: S
**Question**: Describe what happens during anaphase of mitosis.
**Answer**: During anaphase, the sister chromatids separate and are pulled to opposite poles of the cell by spindle fibres. Each pole receives one complete set of chromosomes. The cell elongates as the poles move apart. Once separated, each chromatid is considered a full chromosome.

## Item 4
**Tags**: mitosis | meiosis | cell division | chromosome
**Difficulty**: S
**Question**: What is the role of the spindle in cell division?
**Answer**: The spindle is made of protein fibres that form during early cell division. Spindle fibres attach to the centromeres of chromosomes, pulling sister chromatids apart during anaphase. They also position the chromosomes at the cell equator during metaphase. Spindle formation is essential for equal chromosome distribution to daughter cells.
**Explanation**: Standard difficulty: requires applying the core definition to a specific biological context.

## Item 5
**Tags**: mitosis | meiosis | cell division | chromosome
**Difficulty**: S
**Question**: Explain why mitosis produces genetically identical cells.
**Answer**: During interphase, the DNA replicates exactly, so each chromosome consists of two identical sister chromatids. During mitosis, the sister chromatids separate and each is pulled to an opposite pole. Each daughter cell receives exactly the same set of chromosomes with the same alleles. No crossing over occurs in mitosis.
**Explanation**: Standard difficulty: requires applying the core definition to a specific biological context.

## Item 6
**Tags**: mitosis | meiosis | cell division | chromosome
**Difficulty**: C
**Question**: Compare the products of mitosis and meiosis.
**Answer**: Mitosis: one division produces 2 daughter cells, each diploid (2n), genetically identical to each other and the parent. Meiosis: two divisions produce 4 gametes, each haploid (n), genetically different from each other and the parent due to crossing over and independent assortment. Mitosis is for growth and repair; meiosis is for sexual reproduction.
**Explanation**: Challenge level: requires connecting multiple genetic concepts to explain the biological mechanism.

## Item 7
**Tags**: mitosis | meiosis | cell division | chromosome
**Difficulty**: C
**Question**: Why is it important that gametes are haploid?
**Answer**: Gametes must be haploid so that when two gametes fuse during fertilisation, the zygote has the correct diploid number. If gametes were diploid, the chromosome number would double with each generation. This would cause serious developmental problems and the species could not maintain its characteristic chromosome number.
**Explanation**: Challenge level: requires connecting multiple genetic concepts to explain the biological mechanism.

## Item 8
**Tags**: mitosis | meiosis | cell division | chromosome
**Difficulty**: C
**Question**: Describe and explain the differences between stem cells and differentiated cells.
**Answer**: Stem cells are unspecialised cells that can divide to produce more stem cells or differentiate into specific cell types. Differentiated cells are specialised with a specific structure and function (e.g., nerve cells, muscle cells). Stem cells can become any cell type (totipotent) or specific cell types (pluripotent). Differentiation occurs when genes are switched on or off — not all genes are active in every cell.
**Explanation**: Challenge level: requires connecting multiple genetic concepts to explain the biological mechanism.

## Item 9
**Tags**: mitosis | meiosis | cell division | chromosome
**Difficulty**: F
**Question**: What is the role of the nucleus in cell division?
**Answer**: The nucleus contains the DNA that carries genetic information. Before cell division, DNA replicates so each chromosome becomes two identical chromatids. The nucleus controls cell activities including cell division. After mitosis, nuclei reform in the daughter cells to control their activities.
**Explanation**: Recall that chromosomes are thread-like structures made of DNA that carry genetic information.
