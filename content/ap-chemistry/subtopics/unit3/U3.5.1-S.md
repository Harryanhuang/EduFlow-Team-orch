A solution has an absorbance of $0.60$ measured in a cuvette with a path length of $1.0 \text{ cm}$. The molar absorptivity of the solute is $1.5 \times 10^{3} \text{ L·mol}^{-1}\text{·cm}^{-1}$. What is the concentration of the solution?

## Options
A) $9.0 \times 10^{2} \text{ M}$
B) $4.0 \times 10^{-4} \text{ M}$
C) $2.5 \times 10^{-3} \text{ M}$
D) $4.0 \times 10^{-3} \text{ M}$

## Answer
B

## Explanation
Rearrange the Beer-Lambert law to solve for concentration: $c = A/(\varepsilon b) = 0.60 / (1.5\times10^{3} \times 1.0) = 0.60 / 1500 = 4.0\times10^{-4}\text{ M}$. Absorbance is dimensionless, and the units of $\varepsilon$ (L·mol⁻¹·cm⁻¹) and $b$ (cm) cancel to leave mol/L. A common error is multiplying the three quantities instead of dividing.
