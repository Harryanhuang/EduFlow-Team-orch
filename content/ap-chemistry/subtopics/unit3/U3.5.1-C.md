A standard solution of concentration $2.0\times10^{-4}\text{ M}$ gives an absorbance of $0.40$ in a $1.0\text{ cm}$ cuvette. An unknown sample is diluted by a factor of 5 (1 part sample to 4 parts water) and the diluted sample then gives an absorbance of $0.30$ in the same cuvette. What is the concentration of the original undiluted unknown?

## Options
A) $1.5\times10^{-4} \text{ M}$
B) $3.0\times10^{-5} \text{ M}$
C) $7.5\times10^{-4} \text{ M}$
D) $3.0\times10^{-4} \text{ M}$

## Answer
C

## Explanation
First find the molar absorptivity from the standard: $\varepsilon = A/(bc) = 0.40/(1.0 \times 2.0\times10^{-4}) = 2.0\times10^{3}\text{ L·mol}^{-1}\text{·cm}^{-1}$. The diluted unknown's concentration is $c = A/(\varepsilon b) = 0.30/(2.0\times10^{3} \times 1.0) = 1.5\times10^{-4}\text{ M}$. Since the sample was diluted by a factor of 5, multiply back: original concentration $= 1.5\times10^{-4} \times 5 = 7.5\times10^{-4}\text{ M}$. Forgetting the dilution factor gives the trap answer A.
