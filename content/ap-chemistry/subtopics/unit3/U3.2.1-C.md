Which of the following best explains why $\text{MgO}$ (mp ≈ 2852 °C) has a much higher melting point than $\text{NaCl}$ (mp ≈ 801 °C), even though both are ionic solids?

## Options
A) MgO forms a covalent network solid, whereas NaCl is truly ionic.
B) The $\text{Mg}^{2+}$ and $\text{O}^{2-}$ ions have higher charges, producing a greater lattice energy than $\text{Na}^+$/$\text{Cl}^-$.
C) MgO molecules form hydrogen bonds in the solid state.
D) The ionic radius of $\text{Mg}^{2+}$ is larger than that of $\text{Na}^+$, which increases the lattice spacing and bond strength.

## Answer
B

## Explanation
Lattice energy is proportional to the product of ionic charges divided by the sum of ionic radii ($U \propto Q_+ Q_- / r$). For MgO, the charges are $2^+$ and $2^-$, giving a charge product of 4, compared to $1^+ \times 1^- = 1$ for NaCl. This four-fold greater electrostatic attraction requires far more energy to overcome, dramatically raising the melting point. Option D is incorrect in its reasoning: $\text{Mg}^{2+}$ is actually smaller than $\text{Na}^+$, which further increases (not decreases) lattice energy.
