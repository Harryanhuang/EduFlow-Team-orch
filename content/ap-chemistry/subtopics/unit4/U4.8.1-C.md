A 50.0 mL sample of $0.100\ \mathrm{M}$ $\mathrm{H_2SO_4}$ is mixed with 50.0 mL of $0.100\ \mathrm{M}$ $\mathrm{NaOH}$. After reaction, what is the concentration of the ion in excess? ($\mathrm{H_2SO_4}$ is diprotic and treated as a strong acid.)

## Options
A. $\mathrm{[H^+]} = 0.0500\ \mathrm{M}$
B. $\mathrm{[OH^-]} = 0.0500\ \mathrm{M}$
C. $\mathrm{[H^+]} = 0.100\ \mathrm{M}$
D. $\mathrm{[H^+]} = 0.0250\ \mathrm{M}$

## Answer
A

## Explanation
Step 1: Moles of $\mathrm{H^+}$. Each $\mathrm{H_2SO_4}$ supplies 2 $\mathrm{H^+}$:

$$n_{\mathrm{H^+}} = 0.100\ \mathrm{M} \times 0.0500\ \mathrm{L} \times 2 = 0.0100\ \mathrm{mol}$$

Step 2: Moles of $\mathrm{OH^-}$:

$$n_{\mathrm{OH^-}} = 0.100\ \mathrm{M} \times 0.0500\ \mathrm{L} = 0.00500\ \mathrm{mol}$$

Step 3: Neutralization consumes $\mathrm{OH^-}$ first. Excess $\mathrm{H^+}$:

$$0.0100 - 0.00500 = 0.00500\ \mathrm{mol\ H^+}$$

Step 4: Divide by the total volume (50.0 + 50.0 = 100.0 mL = 0.100 L):

$$\mathrm{[H^+]} = \frac{0.00500\ \mathrm{mol}}{0.100\ \mathrm{L}} = 0.0500\ \mathrm{M}$$

The solution is acidic because the diprotic acid supplies twice the $\mathrm{H^+}$.

Option B wrongly assumes base is in excess.
Option C forgets to divide by the total combined volume.
Option D omits the factor of 2 from the diprotic acid.
