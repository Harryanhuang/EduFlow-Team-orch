Consider the reaction $\mathrm{N_2(g) + 3H_2(g) \rightarrow 2NH_3(g)}$. A reaction vessel contains 4 molecules of $\mathrm{N_2}$ and 9 molecules of $\mathrm{H_2}$. After the reaction proceeds as far as possible, how many $\mathrm{NH_3}$ molecules form and which reactant is in excess?

## Options
A. 8 $\mathrm{NH_3}$ form; $\mathrm{H_2}$ is in excess
B. 6 $\mathrm{NH_3}$ form; $\mathrm{N_2}$ is in excess
C. 6 $\mathrm{NH_3}$ form; $\mathrm{H_2}$ is in excess
D. 9 $\mathrm{NH_3}$ form; $\mathrm{N_2}$ is in excess

## Answer
B

## Explanation
Determine the product each reactant could make:

From $\mathrm{N_2}$: $4\ \mathrm{N_2} \times \dfrac{2\ \mathrm{NH_3}}{1\ \mathrm{N_2}} = 8\ \mathrm{NH_3}$

From $\mathrm{H_2}$: $9\ \mathrm{H_2} \times \dfrac{2\ \mathrm{NH_3}}{3\ \mathrm{H_2}} = 6\ \mathrm{NH_3}$

The smaller value (6) governs, so $\mathrm{H_2}$ is the limiting reactant and 6 $\mathrm{NH_3}$ molecules form.

Check the excess: forming 6 $\mathrm{NH_3}$ consumes $6 \times \frac{1\ \mathrm{N_2}}{2\ \mathrm{NH_3}} = 3\ \mathrm{N_2}$. Starting with 4 $\mathrm{N_2}$, one $\mathrm{N_2}$ molecule remains, so $\mathrm{N_2}$ is in excess.

Option A wrongly treats $\mathrm{N_2}$ as limiting.
Option C correctly counts product but misidentifies the excess reactant.
Option D is not stoichiometrically possible.
