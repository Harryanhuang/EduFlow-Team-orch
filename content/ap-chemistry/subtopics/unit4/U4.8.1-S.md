How many moles of $\mathrm{HCl}$ are required to completely neutralize 0.0500 mol of $\mathrm{Ca(OH)_2}$? The reaction is $\mathrm{2HCl + Ca(OH)_2 \rightarrow CaCl_2 + 2H_2O}$.

## Options
A. 0.0250 mol
B. 0.0500 mol
C. 0.100 mol
D. 0.200 mol

## Answer
C

## Explanation
Each formula unit of $\mathrm{Ca(OH)_2}$ provides 2 moles of $\mathrm{OH^-}$, so 0.0500 mol $\mathrm{Ca(OH)_2}$ supplies:

$$0.0500\ \mathrm{mol} \times 2 = 0.100\ \mathrm{mol\ OH^-}$$

Complete neutralization requires equal moles of $\mathrm{H^+}$. Since each $\mathrm{HCl}$ supplies 1 mole of $\mathrm{H^+}$:

$$n_{\mathrm{HCl}} = 0.100\ \mathrm{mol}$$

This matches the 2:1 mole ratio in the balanced equation:

$$0.0500\ \mathrm{mol\ Ca(OH)_2} \times \frac{2\ \mathrm{mol\ HCl}}{1\ \mathrm{mol\ Ca(OH)_2}} = 0.100\ \mathrm{mol\ HCl}$$

Option A and B ignore that $\mathrm{Ca(OH)_2}$ provides two hydroxide ions.
Option D doubles the requirement.
