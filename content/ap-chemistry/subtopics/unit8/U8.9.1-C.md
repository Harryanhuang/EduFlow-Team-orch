A chemist wants to prepare a buffer at $\text{pH} = 4.44$ using acetic acid ($\text{p}K_a = 4.74$). The solution already contains $0.20 \text{ mol}$ of acetic acid. How many moles of sodium acetate ($\text{CH}_3\text{COONa}$) should be added (assume a fixed total volume)?

## Options
A) $0.40 \text{ mol}$
B) $0.20 \text{ mol}$
C) $0.10 \text{ mol}$
D) $0.050 \text{ mol}$

## Answer
C

## Explanation
Rearrange Henderson-Hasselbalch: $\dfrac{[\text{A}^-]}{[\text{HA}]} = 10^{(\text{pH}-\text{p}K_a)} = 10^{(4.44-4.74)} = 10^{-0.30} = 0.50$. Since volume is common to both, this equals the mole ratio: $\dfrac{n_{\text{A}^-}}{n_{\text{HA}}} = 0.50$. With $n_{\text{HA}} = 0.20$ mol, $n_{\text{A}^-} = 0.50 \times 0.20 = 0.10$ mol of sodium acetate. Because the target pH is below $\text{p}K_a$, the buffer must contain less conjugate base than acid, confirming the ratio is less than 1.
