A galvanic cell is constructed from the following standard reduction half-reactions:

$$\mathrm{Cu^{2+}} + 2e^- \rightarrow \mathrm{Cu} \qquad E° = +0.34 \ \mathrm{V}$$
$$\mathrm{Zn^{2+}} + 2e^- \rightarrow \mathrm{Zn} \qquad E° = -0.76 \ \mathrm{V}$$

What is the standard cell potential $E°_{cell}$ for the spontaneous cell?

## Options
A. $+1.10 \ \mathrm{V}$
B. $-1.10 \ \mathrm{V}$
C. $-0.42 \ \mathrm{V}$
D. $+0.42 \ \mathrm{V}$

## Answer
A

## Explanation
For a spontaneous galvanic cell, the half-reaction with the **more positive** reduction potential serves as the cathode (reduction), and the other is reversed to act as the anode (oxidation).

- Cathode (more positive): $\mathrm{Cu^{2+}} + 2e^- \rightarrow \mathrm{Cu}$, $E° = +0.34 \ \mathrm{V}$
- Anode (oxidized): $\mathrm{Zn} \rightarrow \mathrm{Zn^{2+}} + 2e^-$, with reduction $E° = -0.76 \ \mathrm{V}$

Apply the formula using **reduction potentials**:

$$E°_{cell} = E°_{cathode} - E°_{anode} = (+0.34) - (-0.76) = +1.10 \ \mathrm{V}$$

The positive $E°_{cell}$ confirms a spontaneous galvanic cell. Option A is correct.

Option B reverses the assignment of cathode and anode. Option C and D result from sign errors when subtracting the negative anode potential.
