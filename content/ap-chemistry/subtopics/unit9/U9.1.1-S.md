A chemical reaction has a system entropy change of $\Delta S_{sys} = -125 \ \mathrm{J/(mol \cdot K)}$. At the temperature of interest, the entropy change of the surroundings is $\Delta S_{surr} = +210 \ \mathrm{J/(mol \cdot K)}$. Based on the second law of thermodynamics, is this process spontaneous?

## Options
A. Yes, because $\Delta S_{univ} = +85 \ \mathrm{J/(mol \cdot K)} > 0$
B. No, because $\Delta S_{sys} < 0$
C. No, because $\Delta S_{univ} = -85 \ \mathrm{J/(mol \cdot K)} < 0$
D. Yes, because $\Delta S_{surr} > 0$ regardless of the system

## Answer
A

## Explanation
The second law of thermodynamics states that a process is spontaneous only when the total entropy of the universe increases:

$$\Delta S_{univ} = \Delta S_{sys} + \Delta S_{surr}$$

$$\Delta S_{univ} = (-125) + (+210) = +85 \ \mathrm{J/(mol \cdot K)}$$

Since $\Delta S_{univ} > 0$, the process **is** spontaneous.

Option B is wrong because spontaneity is judged by $\Delta S_{univ}$, not $\Delta S_{sys}$ alone. Even though the system becomes more ordered, the surroundings gain enough entropy to make the overall process favorable.
Option C uses the wrong sign in the sum.
Option D reaches the right conclusion but for an incomplete reason; both terms must be considered.
