The thermal decomposition of calcium carbonate is given by:

$$\mathrm{CaCO_3}(s) \rightarrow \mathrm{CaO}(s) + \mathrm{CO_2}(g)$$

with $\Delta H = +178.3 \ \mathrm{kJ/mol}$ and $\Delta S = +160.5 \ \mathrm{J/(mol \cdot K)}$. Above what approximate temperature does this reaction become thermodynamically favorable?

## Options
A. 1111 K
B. 838 K
C. 1.11 K
D. The reaction is favorable at all temperatures.

## Answer
A

## Explanation
Both $\Delta H > 0$ and $\Delta S > 0$, so the $-T\Delta S$ term becomes increasingly negative as temperature rises. The reaction is favorable ($\Delta G < 0$) only at high temperature. The threshold occurs where $\Delta G = 0$:

$$\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S = 0 \implies T = \frac{\Delta H}{\Delta S}$$

Convert $\Delta S$ to kJ for consistency:

$$\Delta S = 160.5 \ \mathrm{J/(mol \cdot K)} = 0.1605 \ \mathrm{kJ/(mol \cdot K)}$$

$$T = \frac{178.3 \ \mathrm{kJ/mol}}{0.1605 \ \mathrm{kJ/(mol \cdot K)}} = 1111 \ \mathrm{K}$$

Because both terms are positive, the reaction is non-spontaneous below 1111 K and **favorable above** 1111 K. Option A is correct.

Option B uses an incorrect entropy value. Option C fails to convert $\Delta S$ from J to kJ (off by 1000). Option D is wrong because at low temperatures the positive $\Delta H$ dominates, making $\Delta G > 0$.
