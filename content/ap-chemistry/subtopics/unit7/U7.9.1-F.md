Solid $\mathrm{AgCl}$ is in equilibrium with its saturated solution:

$$\mathrm{AgCl(s) \rightleftharpoons Ag^+(aq) + Cl^-(aq)}$$

How does the molar solubility of $\mathrm{AgCl}$ change if some solid $\mathrm{NaCl}$ (a soluble source of $\mathrm{Cl^-}$) is dissolved in the solution?

## Options
A. The solubility of $\mathrm{AgCl}$ decreases.
B. The solubility of $\mathrm{AgCl}$ increases.
C. The solubility of $\mathrm{AgCl}$ is unchanged because $K_{sp}$ is constant.
D. The $\mathrm{AgCl}$ becomes more soluble because $\mathrm{Na^+}$ reacts with $\mathrm{Cl^-}$.

## Answer
A

## Explanation
$\mathrm{NaCl}$ provides the common ion $\mathrm{Cl^-}$, which is a product of the dissolution equilibrium. By Le Chatelier's principle, increasing the concentration of $\mathrm{Cl^-}$ shifts the equilibrium to the **left**, toward solid $\mathrm{AgCl}$. This precipitates some silver chloride and **decreases** its molar solubility — the common-ion effect.

Although $K_{sp}$ itself stays constant, the increased $[\mathrm{Cl^-}]$ forces $[\mathrm{Ag^+}]$ to be smaller, meaning less $\mathrm{AgCl}$ dissolves.

Option B is the opposite of the common-ion effect.
Option C confuses a constant $K_{sp}$ with constant solubility; the equilibrium position shifts even though $K_{sp}$ is fixed.
Option D describes a reaction that does not occur ($\mathrm{Na^+}$ is a spectator ion).
