The solubility product of $\mathrm{AgCl}$ is $K_{sp} = 1.8 \times 10^{-10}$. What is the molar solubility of $\mathrm{AgCl}$ in a $0.10\ \mathrm{M}$ $\mathrm{NaCl}$ solution?

$$\mathrm{AgCl(s) \rightleftharpoons Ag^+(aq) + Cl^-(aq)}$$

## Options
A. $1.3 \times 10^{-5}$ M
B. $1.8 \times 10^{-9}$ M
C. $1.8 \times 10^{-11}$ M
D. $1.8 \times 10^{-10}$ M

## Answer
B

## Explanation
Let $s$ = molar solubility of $\mathrm{AgCl}$ in the $\mathrm{NaCl}$ solution. Then:

$$[\mathrm{Ag^+}] = s, \qquad [\mathrm{Cl^-}] = 0.10 + s$$

Because the common-ion effect makes $s$ very small, approximate $0.10 + s \approx 0.10$. Substitute into the solubility product:

$$K_{sp} = [\mathrm{Ag^+}][\mathrm{Cl^-}] = s(0.10) = 1.8 \times 10^{-10}$$

Solve for $s$:

$$s = \frac{1.8 \times 10^{-10}}{0.10} = 1.8 \times 10^{-9} \text{ M}$$

For comparison, the solubility in pure water is $\sqrt{1.8 \times 10^{-10}} = 1.3 \times 10^{-5}$ M (Option A). The common ion has reduced solubility by about four orders of magnitude.

Option C divides by an incorrect factor.
Option D is the value of $K_{sp}$ itself.
