The solubility product of $\mathrm{PbCl_2}$ is $K_{sp} = 1.6 \times 10^{-5}$. What is the molar solubility of $\mathrm{PbCl_2}$ in a $0.20\ \mathrm{M}$ $\mathrm{NaCl}$ solution?

$$\mathrm{PbCl_2(s) \rightleftharpoons Pb^{2+}(aq) + 2\,Cl^-(aq)}$$

## Options
A. $4.0 \times 10^{-4}$ M
B. $8.0 \times 10^{-5}$ M
C. $1.6 \times 10^{-2}$ M
D. $2.0 \times 10^{-3}$ M

## Answer
A

## Explanation
Let $s$ = molar solubility of $\mathrm{PbCl_2}$. The chloride ion is the common ion, supplied mostly by the $\mathrm{NaCl}$:

$$[\mathrm{Pb^{2+}}] = s, \qquad [\mathrm{Cl^-}] = 0.20 + 2s \approx 0.20$$

The approximation is valid because the common-ion effect makes $s$ small. Substitute into the solubility product, applying the exponent of 2 to chloride:

$$K_{sp} = [\mathrm{Pb^{2+}}][\mathrm{Cl^-}]^2 = (s)(0.20)^2 = 1.6 \times 10^{-5}$$

$$s(0.040) = 1.6 \times 10^{-5}$$

$$s = \frac{1.6 \times 10^{-5}}{0.040} = 4.0 \times 10^{-4} \text{ M}$$

Check: $2s = 8.0 \times 10^{-4} \ll 0.20$, so the approximation holds.

Option B forgets to square the $0.20$ term.
Option C is the value used incorrectly without dividing.
Option D corresponds to the pure-water solubility ($4s^3 = K_{sp}$), not the common-ion case.
