Consider the following equilibrium:

$$\mathrm{N_2(g) + 3\,H_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2\,NH_3(g)}$$

If additional $\mathrm{H_2}$ gas is injected into the system at constant temperature and volume, in which direction will the equilibrium shift?

## Options
A. To the right, producing more $\mathrm{NH_3}$.
B. To the left, producing more $\mathrm{N_2}$ and $\mathrm{H_2}$.
C. No shift occurs because $K$ is unchanged.
D. The equilibrium shifts left, then right.

## Answer
A

## Explanation
Le Chatelier's principle states that a system at equilibrium responds to a stress by shifting in the direction that partially counteracts the stress.

Adding $\mathrm{H_2}$ increases the concentration of a reactant. The system relieves this stress by consuming the added $\mathrm{H_2}$, which means the equilibrium shifts to the **right** (toward products), producing more $\mathrm{NH_3}$.

$$Q = \frac{[\mathrm{NH_3}]^2}{[\mathrm{N_2}][\mathrm{H_2}]^3} \;\downarrow\; \text{(denominator increases)} \Rightarrow Q < K \Rightarrow \text{shift right}$$

Option B is the opposite (incorrect) direction.
Option C is wrong because although $K$ is constant, the concentrations are no longer at equilibrium, so a shift must occur to restore $Q = K$.
Option D describes no real process.
