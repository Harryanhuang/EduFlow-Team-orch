For the reaction $\mathrm{N_2(g) + 3H_2(g) \rightarrow 2NH_3(g)}$, which expression correctly represents the rate of the reaction?

## Options
A. $-\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{H_2}]}{\Delta t}$
B. $-\dfrac{1}{3}\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{H_2}]}{\Delta t}$
C. $+\dfrac{1}{3}\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{NH_3}]}{\Delta t}$
D. $+\dfrac{\Delta[\mathrm{N_2}]}{\Delta t}$

## Answer
B

## Explanation
The rate of reaction is defined so that it is the same value no matter which species is monitored. For a general reaction the rate equals the concentration change of a species divided by its stoichiometric coefficient, with a negative sign for reactants (which are consumed):

$$\text{rate} = -\frac{\Delta[\mathrm{N_2}]}{\Delta t} = -\frac{1}{3}\frac{\Delta[\mathrm{H_2}]}{\Delta t} = +\frac{1}{2}\frac{\Delta[\mathrm{NH_3}]}{\Delta t}$$

Option B correctly divides the hydrogen rate by its coefficient (3) and uses the negative sign because $\mathrm{H_2}$ is a reactant.

Option A omits the coefficient of 3.
Option C uses the wrong coefficient for $\mathrm{NH_3}$ (should be $\frac{1}{2}$).
Option D omits the negative sign for the reactant $\mathrm{N_2}$.
