A reaction has the following mechanism, where the first step is slow:

Step 1 (slow): $\mathrm{2NO \rightarrow N_2O_2}$
Step 2 (fast): $\mathrm{N_2O_2 + H_2 \rightarrow N_2O + H_2O}$

What is the predicted rate law for the overall reaction?

## Options
A. rate $= k[\mathrm{NO}][\mathrm{H_2}]$
B. rate $= k[\mathrm{NO}]^2$
C. rate $= k[\mathrm{NO}]^2[\mathrm{H_2}]$
D. rate $= k[\mathrm{N_2O_2}]$

## Answer
B

## Explanation
The rate of the overall reaction is set by the **slow (rate-determining) step**. Because elementary steps occur exactly as written, the rate law for step 1 uses its reactants raised to their stoichiometric coefficients:

$$\text{rate} = k[\mathrm{NO}]^2$$

Since the slow step is first, no fast-equilibrium substitution is needed, and species that appear only in the fast step (like $\mathrm{H_2}$) do not enter the rate law.

Option A and C wrongly include $[\mathrm{H_2}]$, which is consumed only in the fast step.
Option D uses the intermediate $\mathrm{N_2O_2}$, which cannot appear in a valid rate law.
