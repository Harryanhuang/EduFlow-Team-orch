The reaction $\mathrm{2NO(g) + Br_2(g) \rightarrow 2NOBr(g)}$ has the experimentally observed rate law rate $= k[\mathrm{NO}]^2[\mathrm{Br_2}]$. Which proposed mechanism is consistent with this rate law?

## Options
A. Single step: $\mathrm{2NO + Br_2 \rightarrow 2NOBr}$ (slow)
B. Step 1 fast equilibrium: $\mathrm{NO + Br_2 \rightleftharpoons NOBr_2}$; Step 2 slow: $\mathrm{NOBr_2 + NO \rightarrow 2NOBr}$
C. Step 1 slow: $\mathrm{NO + NO \rightarrow N_2O_2}$; Step 2 fast: $\mathrm{N_2O_2 + Br_2 \rightarrow 2NOBr}$
D. Step 1 fast: $\mathrm{Br_2 \rightleftharpoons 2Br}$; Step 2 slow: $\mathrm{NO + Br \rightarrow NOBr}$

## Answer
B

## Explanation
A valid mechanism must reproduce the observed rate law rate $= k[\mathrm{NO}]^2[\mathrm{Br_2}]$.

**Option B:** The slow step gives rate $= k_2[\mathrm{NOBr_2}][\mathrm{NO}]$. The intermediate $\mathrm{NOBr_2}$ must be eliminated using the fast pre-equilibrium of step 1: $K = \frac{[\mathrm{NOBr_2}]}{[\mathrm{NO}][\mathrm{Br_2}]}$, so $[\mathrm{NOBr_2}] = K[\mathrm{NO}][\mathrm{Br_2}]$. Substituting:

$$\text{rate} = k_2 K[\mathrm{NO}][\mathrm{Br_2}][\mathrm{NO}] = k[\mathrm{NO}]^2[\mathrm{Br_2}]$$

This matches the observed rate law exactly.

Option A (a single termolecular step) would give the right rate law, but simultaneous three-body collisions are extremely improbable, so a multi-step mechanism is strongly preferred; B is the better-supported answer.
Option C predicts rate $= k[\mathrm{NO}]^2$ with no $[\mathrm{Br_2}]$ dependence — inconsistent.
Option D predicts a rate law that is first order in $\mathrm{NO}$ and half order in $\mathrm{Br_2}$ — inconsistent.
