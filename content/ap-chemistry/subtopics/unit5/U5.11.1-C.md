An uncatalyzed reaction has a forward activation energy of $75\ \mathrm{kJ/mol}$ and a reverse activation energy of $50\ \mathrm{kJ/mol}$. A catalyst lowers the forward activation energy to $30\ \mathrm{kJ/mol}$. What is the activation energy of the reverse reaction in the catalyzed pathway?

## Options
A. $50\ \mathrm{kJ/mol}$
B. $25\ \mathrm{kJ/mol}$
C. $5\ \mathrm{kJ/mol}$
D. $80\ \mathrm{kJ/mol}$

## Answer
C

## Explanation
A catalyst does not change the enthalpy of reaction, so $\Delta H$ must be the same with or without the catalyst. From the uncatalyzed values:

$$\Delta H = E_{a,\text{fwd}} - E_{a,\text{rev}} = 75 - 50 = +25\ \mathrm{kJ/mol}$$

The catalyzed pathway must give the same $\Delta H = +25\ \mathrm{kJ/mol}$. With the new forward activation energy of $30\ \mathrm{kJ/mol}$:

$$E_{a,\text{rev}} = E_{a,\text{fwd}} - \Delta H = 30 - 25 = 5\ \mathrm{kJ/mol}$$

Equivalently, the catalyst lowered the forward barrier by $75 - 30 = 45\ \mathrm{kJ/mol}$, so the reverse barrier also drops by $45\ \mathrm{kJ/mol}$: $50 - 45 = 5\ \mathrm{kJ/mol}$. Both directions are lowered by the same amount, confirming the catalyst speeds the forward and reverse reactions equally.

Option A leaves the reverse barrier unchanged, ignoring that the catalyst lowers both.
Option B and D come from incorrect arithmetic or changing $\Delta H$.
