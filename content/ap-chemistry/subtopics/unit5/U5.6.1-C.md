Two reactions, X and Y, are run at the same temperature. Reaction X has an activation energy of $40\ \mathrm{kJ/mol}$ and reaction Y has an activation energy of $90\ \mathrm{kJ/mol}$. Assuming similar frequency factors, which statement is correct?

## Options
A. Reaction Y has a larger rate constant because it has a larger activation energy.
B. Reaction X has a larger fraction of collisions with sufficient energy, so it proceeds faster.
C. Both reactions have the same rate because the temperature is the same.
D. Reaction Y proceeds faster because higher-energy barriers release more product.

## Answer
B

## Explanation
The Arrhenius equation, $k = A e^{-E_a/RT}$, shows that the rate constant depends exponentially on the negative ratio of activation energy to $RT$. The term $e^{-E_a/RT}$ represents the fraction of collisions with enough energy to overcome the barrier.

A **lower** activation energy makes $-E_a/RT$ less negative, so $e^{-E_a/RT}$ is larger. Reaction X ($E_a = 40\ \mathrm{kJ/mol}$) therefore has a greater fraction of sufficiently energetic collisions than reaction Y ($E_a = 90\ \mathrm{kJ/mol}$), giving X a larger rate constant and a faster rate.

Option A reverses the relationship; larger $E_a$ means a smaller $k$.
Option C ignores that $E_a$ also controls the rate at a fixed temperature.
Option D incorrectly links barrier height to the amount of product released.
