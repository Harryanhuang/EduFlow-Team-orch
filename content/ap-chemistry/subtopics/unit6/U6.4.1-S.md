When $0.0500 \text{ mol}$ of solid NaOH is dissolved in $100.0 \text{ g}$ of water in a coffee-cup calorimeter, the water temperature rises from $22.0 \text{ °C}$ to $27.3 \text{ °C}$. Assuming the specific heat of the solution is $4.18 \text{ J/(g·°C)}$ and ignoring the calorimeter's heat capacity, what is the molar enthalpy of dissolution?

## Options
A) $-44.3 \text{ kJ/mol}$
B) $-22.2 \text{ kJ/mol}$
C) $+22.2 \text{ kJ/mol}$
D) $+44.3 \text{ kJ/mol}$

## Answer
A

## Explanation
The heat gained by the water is $q_\text{water} = mc\Delta T = (100.0 \text{ g})(4.18 \text{ J/(g·°C)})(27.3 - 22.0) = (100.0)(4.18)(5.3) = 2{,}215 \text{ J}$. Because the dissolution releases this heat, $q_\text{rxn} = -2{,}215 \text{ J} = -2.215 \text{ kJ}$. Dividing by moles: $\Delta H = \dfrac{-2.215 \text{ kJ}}{0.0500 \text{ mol}} = -44.3 \text{ kJ/mol}$. The negative sign confirms an exothermic dissolution, consistent with the rising temperature.
