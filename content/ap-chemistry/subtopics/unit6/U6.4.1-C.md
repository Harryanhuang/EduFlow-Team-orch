A $1.50 \text{ g}$ sample of glucose ($\text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6$, molar mass $180.0 \text{ g/mol}$) is burned in a bomb calorimeter with a total heat capacity of $9.85 \text{ kJ/°C}$. The temperature rises from $24.00 \text{ °C}$ to $26.38 \text{ °C}$. What is the energy released per mole of glucose?

## Options
A) $-15.6 \text{ kJ/mol}$
B) $-1{,}560 \text{ kJ/mol}$
C) $-2{,}810 \text{ kJ/mol}$
D) $-5{,}620 \text{ kJ/mol}$

## Answer
C

## Explanation
The heat absorbed by the calorimeter is $q_\text{cal} = C_\text{cal}\Delta T = (9.85 \text{ kJ/°C})(26.38 - 24.00) = (9.85)(2.38) = 23.44 \text{ kJ}$. This heat came from the combustion, so $q_\text{combustion} = -23.44 \text{ kJ}$ for $1.50 \text{ g}$. Converting the sample to moles: $n = \dfrac{1.50 \text{ g}}{180.0 \text{ g/mol}} = 0.00833 \text{ mol}$. The molar energy is $\dfrac{-23.44 \text{ kJ}}{0.00833 \text{ mol}} = -2{,}810 \text{ kJ/mol}$. The negative sign reflects the exothermic combustion, and using $C_\text{cal}$ (not $mc$ of the sample) is essential.
