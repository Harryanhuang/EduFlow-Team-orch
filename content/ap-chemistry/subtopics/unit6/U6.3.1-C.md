A $120.0 \text{ g}$ block of copper ($c = 0.385 \text{ J/(g·°C)}$) at $95.0 \text{ °C}$ is dropped into $80.0 \text{ g}$ of water ($c = 4.18 \text{ J/(g·°C)}$) at $22.0 \text{ °C}$ in an insulated cup. What is the final equilibrium temperature, to the nearest $0.1 \text{ °C}$?

## Options
A) $24.8 \text{ °C}$
B) $30.9 \text{ °C}$
C) $45.2 \text{ °C}$
D) $58.5 \text{ °C}$

## Answer
B

## Explanation
Set the heat lost by copper equal to the heat gained by water: $m_\text{Cu} c_\text{Cu}(95.0 - T_f) = m_\text{w} c_\text{w}(T_f - 22.0)$. The heat capacities are $m_\text{Cu} c_\text{Cu} = 120.0 \times 0.385 = 46.2 \text{ J/°C}$ and $m_\text{w} c_\text{w} = 80.0 \times 4.18 = 334.4 \text{ J/°C}$. Then $46.2(95.0 - T_f) = 334.4(T_f - 22.0)$, which expands to $4389.0 - 46.2 T_f = 334.4 T_f - 7356.8$. Combining gives $11745.8 = 380.6 T_f$, so $T_f = 30.9 \text{ °C}$. The result lies far below the simple average because water's heat capacity ($334.4 \text{ J/°C}$) is roughly seven times copper's ($46.2 \text{ J/°C}$), so the equilibrium temperature stays close to water's initial temperature.
