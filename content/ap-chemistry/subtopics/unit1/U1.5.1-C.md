Copper exhibits an anomalous ground-state electron configuration. Based on this configuration, what is the correct electron configuration of the $\mathrm{Cu^{+}}$ ion (Z = 29)?

## Options
A. $[\mathrm{Ar}] 3d^{10}$
B. $[\mathrm{Ar}] 4s^2 3d^7$
C. $[\mathrm{Ar}] 4s^1 3d^9$
D. $[\mathrm{Ar}] 3d^9$

## Answer
A

## Explanation
The expected Aufbau configuration of copper would be $[\mathrm{Ar}] 4s^2 3d^9$. However, copper is anomalous: a completely filled $3d^{10}$ subshell provides extra stability, so one $4s$ electron is promoted to $3d$.

Ground-state Cu: $[\mathrm{Ar}] 4s^1 3d^{10}$ (option C is the neutral atom configuration)

To form $\mathrm{Cu^{+}}$, remove 1 electron. The $4s$ electron is removed first:

$$\mathrm{Cu^{+}}: [\mathrm{Ar}] 3d^{10}$$

This produces a particularly stable ion with a completely filled $3d$ subshell, which is why $\mathrm{Cu^{+}}$ is a common copper ion.

Option B uses the incorrect expected configuration and removes wrong electrons.
Option C is the neutral copper atom, not the ion.
Option D would result from removing a $3d$ electron from the incorrect expected configuration.
