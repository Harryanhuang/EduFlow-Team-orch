Across Period 2, first ionization energy generally increases from lithium to neon. However, oxygen has a lower first ionization energy than nitrogen. Which statement best explains this irregularity?

## Options
A. Removing an electron from oxygen breaks up a paired $2p$ electron set, relieving electron-electron repulsion, while nitrogen has a stable half-filled $2p^3$ configuration
B. Oxygen has a larger atomic radius than nitrogen, so its valence electrons are easier to remove
C. Oxygen has a smaller effective nuclear charge than nitrogen
D. Nitrogen has more protons than oxygen, holding its electrons more tightly

## Answer
A

## Explanation
The electron configurations of the relevant atoms are:
- Nitrogen: $1s^2 2s^2 2p^3$ (three $2p$ electrons, each in a separate orbital, half-filled subshell)
- Oxygen: $1s^2 2s^2 2p^4$ (one $2p$ orbital now holds a pair)

Nitrogen's half-filled $2p^3$ configuration is especially stable, with all three $2p$ electrons unpaired and spread across separate orbitals (minimizing repulsion).

In oxygen, the fourth $2p$ electron must pair up in an orbital already containing one electron. This pairing increases electron-electron repulsion. Removing that paired electron relieves this repulsion and returns oxygen to a stable half-filled $2p^3$ cation, so oxygen's first ionization energy is lower than nitrogen's despite oxygen having a higher nuclear charge.

Option B is incorrect: oxygen has a smaller radius than nitrogen (higher Z_eff across the period).
Option C is incorrect: oxygen has a greater effective nuclear charge than nitrogen.
Option D is factually wrong: oxygen (Z = 8) has more protons than nitrogen (Z = 7).
