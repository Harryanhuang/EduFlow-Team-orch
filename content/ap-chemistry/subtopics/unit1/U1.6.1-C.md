The photoelectron spectrum of neon (Z = 10) shows three distinct peaks rather than two, even though all $n = 2$ electrons are in the same principal energy level. The $2s$ peak appears at higher binding energy than the $2p$ peak. Which statement best explains this observation?

## Options
A. The $2s$ electrons experience a greater effective nuclear charge because $2s$ orbitals penetrate closer to the nucleus than $2p$ orbitals
B. The $2s$ electrons are farther from the nucleus than the $2p$ electrons, so they are easier to remove
C. There are more $2s$ electrons than $2p$ electrons, increasing electron-electron repulsion
D. The $2p$ electrons have a higher principal quantum number than the $2s$ electrons

## Answer
A

## Explanation
Although $2s$ and $2p$ electrons share the same principal quantum number ($n = 2$), they have different radial distributions. The $2s$ orbital has greater penetration toward the nucleus: its electron density has a significant probability close to the nucleus, inside the region occupied by the $1s$ electrons.

Because $2s$ electrons penetrate closer to the nucleus, they are shielded less effectively by the core $1s$ electrons and experience a greater effective nuclear charge ($Z_{\text{eff}}$). A higher $Z_{\text{eff}}$ means stronger attraction, so more energy is required to remove a $2s$ electron, placing its PES peak at higher binding energy than the $2p$ peak.

This is why PES resolves subshells (s, p, d) separately rather than just principal levels, providing direct experimental evidence for subshell structure.

Option B is incorrect: $2s$ penetration places significant density closer to the nucleus, not farther.
Option C confuses peak height (electron count) with binding energy and misstates the cause.
Option D is factually wrong: $2s$ and $2p$ have the same $n = 2$.
