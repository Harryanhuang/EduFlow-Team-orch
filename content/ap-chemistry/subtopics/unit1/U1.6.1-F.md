In a photoelectron spectroscopy (PES) spectrum, peaks at higher binding energy correspond to electrons that are:

## Options
A. Closer to the nucleus and more tightly bound
B. Farther from the nucleus and more tightly bound
C. Closer to the nucleus and less tightly bound
D. In the highest occupied energy level

## Answer
A

## Explanation
In photoelectron spectroscopy, the binding energy measured for each peak represents the energy required to remove an electron from a particular subshell.

Electrons closer to the nucleus (lower principal quantum number $n$, e.g., $1s$) experience a stronger attraction from the nuclear charge and are shielded less. Therefore, they require more energy to remove, appearing at higher binding energy.

Electrons farther from the nucleus (higher $n$, valence electrons) are more shielded and experience less effective nuclear charge, so they have lower binding energy.

Thus, peaks at higher binding energy correspond to electrons closer to the nucleus and more tightly bound.

Option B is contradictory: tightly bound electrons are not far from the nucleus.
Option C is contradictory: closer electrons are more tightly bound, not less.
Option D describes the lowest binding energy peak (valence electrons), the opposite.
