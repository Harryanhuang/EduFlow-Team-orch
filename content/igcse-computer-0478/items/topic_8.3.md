**Item 10 [S]**
Question: Explain how streaming movies and music online affects intellectual property rights compared to purchasing physical media.
Answer: When streaming, users do not own a copy of the content; they are licensing access to it. This means they cannot redistribute, resell, or keep the content permanently. Physical media purchases give ownership rights, including the ability to resell or lend the item. Streaming services hold the intellectual property licenses and pay royalties to creators.
Difficulty: S
Topic: 8.3
Explanation: Streaming changed the economics of intellectual property by shifting from ownership to access-based models.
Tags: streaming, copyright, intellectual-property

**Item 13 [C]**
Question: Automated vehicles use AI to make split-second decisions that could affect the safety of pedestrians and passengers. If an accident is unavoidable, should the AI prioritize the safety of the occupant or minimize total harm across all parties? Discuss with reference to ethical frameworks.
Answer: This raises the classic "trolley problem" in applied ethics. A utilitarian approach would minimize total harm across all parties, potentially sacrificing occupants. A rights-based approach might protect the occupant's right to life regardless of consequences. A virtue ethics approach would ask what a responsible driver would do. Society must decide these trade-offs collectively through regulation, not leave them to individual AI developers. Many argue that AI should be designed to prioritize vulnerable road users and minimize harm to all, rather than protecting occupants at all costs.
Difficulty: C
Topic: 8.3
Explanation: Autonomous vehicle ethics involve competing moral theories and require societal consensus, not just technical solutions.
Tags: AI-ethics, autonomous-vehicles, moral-dilemma, philosophy

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: Compare and contrast deontological and consequentialist approaches to evaluating the ethics of automation replacing human workers.
Answer: A deontological approach focuses on duties and rights: employers may have a duty to maximize profit, but workers have rights to fair treatment. It asks whether replacing workers respects their dignity and contractual obligations, regardless of outcomes. A consequentialist approach examines actual outcomes: if automation creates more jobs overall, increases productivity, and improves living standards, it may be justified even if specific workers suffer. Virtue ethics would ask what a virtuous employer would do—balancing business needs with compassion for workers. Pure consequentialism might ignore the moral weight of individual suffering, while pure deontology might ignore beneficial systemic outcomes.
Difficulty: C
Topic: 8.3
Explanation: This demonstrates how different ethical frameworks can lead to different conclusions about the same technological change.
Tags: automation, ethics, philosophy, employment

**Item 18 [C]**
Question: Open-source software licenses like GPL require derivative works to also be open source, while permissive licenses like MIT allow proprietary use. Analyze the ethical arguments on both sides of this debate.
Answer: Arguments for copyleft (GPL): ensures software remains freely available, prevents corporations from profiting from community work without contributing back, preserves user freedoms. Arguments against: restricts commercial use and may limit adoption, prevents hybrid business models, some argue freedom means freedom to choose any license. Permissive license advocates say this maximizes contributions and adoption. The debate reflects deeper values about property, commons, and how to sustain open-source development. Some argue copyleft is ideological purity that harms the movement; others see permissive licensing as exploitation of volunteer labor.
Difficulty: C
Topic: 8.3
Explanation: This debate touches fundamental questions about intellectual property philosophy and sustainable open-source development models.
Tags: open-source, licensing, intellectual-property, ethics
