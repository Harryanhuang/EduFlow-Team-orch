# Topic 8.4 — Environmental Impact of Computing

## Items File

**Item 1 [F]**
Question: What is e-waste and why is it a growing problem?
Answer: E-waste (electronic waste) refers to discarded electrical and electronic equipment. It is a growing problem because the rapid pace of technology upgrade cycles generates large volumes of toxic materials that cannot decompose, and many countries lack proper disposal infrastructure.
Difficulty: F
Topic: 8.4
Explanation: E-waste contains hazardous materials like lead, mercury, and cadmium that can contaminate soil and water if not properly managed.
Tags: e-waste, electronics, pollution

**Item 2 [F]**
Question: Name two materials found in electronic devices that are harmful to the environment if disposed of incorrectly.
Answer: Lead (found in circuit boards and screens) and mercury (found in some displays and switches) are harmful to the environment when electronics are improperly disposed of.
Difficulty: F
Topic: 8.4

**Item 3 [F]**
Question: What is meant by "planned obsolescence" in the context of computing?
Answer: Planned obsolescence is when manufacturers design products with a limited lifespan so that they become outdated or stop working within a known timeframe, encouraging consumers to purchase new devices.
Difficulty: F
Topic: 8.4

Question: How does streaming video content contribute to energy consumption?

**Item 7 [S]**
Question: Explain how cryptocurrency mining consumes significant energy and its environmental implications.
Answer: Cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin use proof-of-work consensus mechanisms that require computers to solve complex mathematical problems, consuming enormous amounts of electricity. Most mining occurs in regions with cheap electricity, often from fossil fuels. This contributes to carbon emissions and can increase electricity prices for local communities. The energy consumption of Bitcoin alone has been compared to that of some small countries.
Difficulty: S
Topic: 8.4
Explanation: The energy intensity of proof-of-work cryptocurrencies is a significant environmental concern that has led to debates about sustainability.
Tags: cryptocurrency, energy, carbon-footprint

**Item 8 [S]**
Question: Data centers require cooling systems to prevent overheating. Discuss how this contributes to their environmental impact and what solutions exist.
Answer: Cooling systems consume substantial energy, accounting for up to 40% of a data center's power usage. This heat contributes to thermal pollution when expelled into waterways. Solutions include using ambient air cooling, locating data centers in cooler climates, liquid cooling, waste heat recovery for district heating, and improving server efficiency to reduce heat generation.
Difficulty: S
Topic: 8.4
Explanation: The cooling infrastructure represents a major inefficiency that innovative design and location choices can address.
Tags: data-centers, cooling, energy-efficiency

**Item 9 [S]**
Question: Compare refurbishing electronics with recycling in terms of environmental benefit.
Answer: Refurbishing extends a device's useful life, avoiding the environmental cost of manufacturing a new product entirely. Manufacturing new electronics consumes significant energy and raw materials. Recycling recovers materials but still requires energy for collection, processing, and manufacturing new products. Refurbishing generally has a lower carbon footprint than recycling because it avoids most manufacturing impacts, provided the refurbished device is actually used rather than discarded.
Difficulty: S
Topic: 8.4
Explanation: The most sustainable device is often the one already owned; refurbishing maximizes the value extracted before recycling becomes necessary.
Tags: refurbishing, recycling, sustainability, e-waste

**Item 10 [S]**
Question: What are "carbon offsets" and how are they used in the IT industry?
Answer: Carbon offsets are certificates representing the reduction or removal of one tonne of CO2 emissions, which can be purchased to compensate for emissions produced elsewhere. IT companies may purchase offsets to claim carbon neutrality. Critics argue offsets can distract from actual emissions reductions and may involve questionable verification. Some offsets fund reforestation or renewable energy projects.
Difficulty: S
Topic: 8.4
Explanation: Carbon offsets are controversial because they allow companies to continue emitting while claiming environmental credit through projects elsewhere.
Tags: carbon-footprint, offsets, climate

**Item 11 [S]**
Question: Describe three strategies that organizations can adopt to reduce the environmental impact of their data storage.
Answer: (1) Data deduplication: eliminating duplicate copies of data reduces storage needs. (2) Storage tiering: moving infrequently accessed data to lower-power storage media. (3) Data lifecycle management: automatically deleting data that is no longer needed. (4) Using more efficient storage technologies like solid-state drives where appropriate. (5) Compressing data to reduce storage volume.
Difficulty: S
Topic: 8.4
Explanation: Storage efficiency directly correlates with energy consumption, so optimizing data management reduces environmental impact.
Tags: data-storage, energy-efficiency, sustainability

**Item 12 [S]**
Question: Explain the concept of "embodied energy" in computing devices and why it matters for environmental impact.
Answer: Embodied energy is the total energy consumed throughout a product's lifecycle, including raw material extraction, manufacturing, transportation, and disposal, excluding the operational energy used during the product's life. For smartphones and laptops, embodied energy can account for 50-80% of total lifetime energy consumption. This means extending device lifespan through repairs and refurbishment significantly reduces overall environmental impact.
Difficulty: S
Topic: 8.4
Explanation: People often focus only on operational energy, but embodied energy often dominates the total environmental impact of electronic devices.
Tags: embodied-energy, lifecycle, sustainability

**Item 13 [C]**
Question: Analyze the environmental trade-offs between cloud computing (centralized shared infrastructure) and local computing (individual devices processing data).
Answer: Cloud computing centralizes resources, allowing more efficient utilization of servers and cooling systems. It enables virtualization to run multiple virtual machines on one physical server, reducing hardware needs. However, it increases network traffic and requires always-on data centers. Local computing distributes energy consumption to end-user devices and works offline but may result in underutilized hardware that is discarded more frequently. The environmental optimal choice depends on utilization rates, data transfer patterns, and data center efficiency. Hybrid approaches that process sensitive data locally while using cloud for scalable workloads often offer the best balance.
Difficulty: C
Topic: 8.4
Explanation: This demonstrates the complexity of evaluating environmental impacts, which depends on usage patterns rather than technology categories alone.
Tags: cloud-computing, local-computing, energy, trade-offs

**Item 14 [C]**
Question: The European Union has introduced the "Right to Repair" directive requiring manufacturers to make devices repairable. Evaluate this policy's potential environmental impact.
Answer: Right to Repair reduces e-waste by extending device lifespans, reducing the embodied energy wasted when functional devices are discarded. It shifts economic value from new sales to repair services, potentially supporting local businesses. However, it may increase short-term manufacturing costs, which could be passed to consumers or reduce company investment in new features. The policy also requires standardized components and software support for longer periods. The net environmental benefit depends on whether consumers actually choose repair over replacement and whether repair shops have lower environmental footprints than manufacturing new devices.
Difficulty: C
Topic: 8.4
Explanation: Policy interventions in technology markets create complex incentive structures with environmental, economic, and social consequences.
Tags: right-to-repair, policy, e-waste, sustainability

**Item 15 [C]**
Question: Discuss the environmental implications of artificial intelligence development, considering both training and inference phases.
Answer: Training large AI models requires massive computational resources over days or weeks, consuming energy equivalent to hundreds of households' annual consumption. This generates significant carbon emissions, particularly when powered by fossil fuels. Once deployed, "inference" (running the model) creates continuous smaller energy costs across millions of users. However, AI can also enable environmental benefits through optimization in logistics, energy grids, and materials science. The net effect depends on whether AI applications genuinely reduce emissions elsewhere. Researchers are developing more efficient model architectures and hardware to reduce AI's environmental footprint.
Difficulty: C
Topic: 8.4
Explanation: AI's environmental impact spans both development (one-time large cost) and deployment (ongoing cumulative cost), requiring lifecycle thinking.
Tags: AI, energy, carbon-footprint, machine-learning

**Item 16 [C]**
Question: Evaluate the environmental ethics of planned obsolescence in the context of sustainable development goals.
Answer: Planned obsolescence conflicts directly with sustainable development goal 12 (responsible consumption and production). It creates artificial demand, wastes resources, generates e-waste containing toxic materials, and externalizes environmental costs to communities near mines and landfills. Defenders argue it creates jobs in manufacturing and retail, makes technology affordable by reducing production costs, and drives innovation. The ethical case against planned obsolescence rests on intergenerational justice—the current generation shifting environmental costs to the future—and environmental justice—impacts concentrated in developing nations where e-waste is often processed. A circular economy approach that designs for longevity, repairability, and recyclability offers an alternative model.
Difficulty: C
Topic: 8.4
Explanation: This requires balancing economic arguments against environmental and social justice concerns within the framework of sustainable development.
Tags: planned-obsolescence, sustainable-development, ethics, circular-economy

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: Analyze how the growth of Internet of Things (IoT) devices affects environmental sustainability, considering both benefits and challenges.
Answer: IoT devices can improve sustainability through smart grids that balance energy supply and demand, precision agriculture that reduces water and pesticide use, and industrial sensors that optimize resource usage. However, billions of low-power devices with short lifespans generate enormous e-waste volumes. The batteries in these devices pose disposal challenges. Individual devices consume little energy, but aggregate consumption across billions of always-on devices can be substantial. Manufacturing billions of specialized chips requires rare earth elements with significant mining impacts. The environmental net benefit depends entirely on whether the efficiency gains from IoT applications exceed the manufacturing and disposal impacts—a calculation that varies by application.
Difficulty: C
Topic: 8.4
Explanation: IoT presents a classic rebound effect problem where efficiency gains may be offset by increased total consumption.
Tags: IoT, sustainability, e-waste, rebound-effect

**Item 18 [C]**
Question: Compare the environmental impact of blockchain technology applications in supply chain tracking versus its use in cryptocurrencies, and evaluate whether the technology can be justified environmentally.
Answer: Supply chain blockchain applications typically involve a small number of validated transactions that can run on energy-efficient proof-of-stake networks, providing traceability benefits that may reduce food waste or detect illegal logging. Cryptocurrency blockchains, particularly Bitcoin, use energy-intensive proof-of-work mechanisms processing millions of speculative transactions. Both use distributed ledgers, but their environmental impacts differ by orders of magnitude. The ethical justification depends on whether the social benefit (financial inclusion for cryptocurrency, transparency for supply chains) outweighs environmental costs. Critics argue few cryptocurrency transactions provide genuine social value, while supply chain applications offer clearer benefits. Some blockchains are transitioning to more efficient consensus mechanisms, potentially reducing future environmental impact.
Difficulty: C
Topic: 8.4
Explanation: This demonstrates that technology assessment requires distinguishing between applications rather than evaluating entire technology categories.
Tags: blockchain, cryptocurrency, supply-chain, energy
