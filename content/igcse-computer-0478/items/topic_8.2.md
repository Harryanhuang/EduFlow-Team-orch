# Topic 8.2 — Data Protection

## Items File

**Item 1 [F]**
Question: What is the purpose of the Data Protection Act?
Answer: The Data Protection Act is legislation designed to protect individuals' personal data from misuse, ensuring that organisations handle data fairly, lawfully, transparently, and securely. It gives individuals rights over their data and sets rules for those who process personal information.
Difficulty: F
Topic: 8.2
Explanation: The Data Protection Act establishes principles that organisations must follow when collecting, storing, using, and disposing of personal data. Compliance is monitored by a supervisory authority.
Tags: data-protection, legislation

**Item 2 [F]**
Question: What does GDPR stand for and in which region does it apply?
Answer: GDPR stands for General Data Protection Regulation. It applies to all organisations processing personal data of individuals within the European Union and European Economic Area, regardless of where the organisation is based.
Difficulty: F
Topic: 8.2
Explanation: GDPR came into force in 2018 and is considered the most comprehensive and influential data protection law globally, influencing similar legislation in other regions.
Tags: gdpr, legislation

**Item 3 [F]**
Question: What are cookies in the context of web browsing?
Answer: Cookies are small text files stored on a user's computer by websites to remember information about the user, such as login status, preferences, and browsing history. They allow websites to track users across sessions.
Difficulty: F
Topic: 8.2
Explanation: Cookies can be used for legitimate purposes (remembering login credentials, shopping cart contents) or for tracking and advertising purposes (third-party cookies that follow users across multiple websites).
Tags: cookies, privacy, web-browsing

**Item 4 [F]**
Question: What is meant by digital footprint?
Answer: A digital footprint is the trail of data left behind by a user's online activities, including websites visited, social media posts, emails sent, files downloaded, and information submitted online.
Difficulty: F
Topic: 8.2
Explanation: Digital footprints can be active (data deliberately shared) or passive (data collected automatically through tracking). Once created, digital footprints can be difficult to remove entirely.
Tags: digital-footprint, privacy

**Item 5 [F]**
Question: What is meant by copyright in the context of digital content?
Answer: Copyright is a legal right that grants the creator of original work exclusive rights to control how their work is used, distributed, reproduced, and displayed. In digital contexts, it protects software, music, videos, images, and written content.
Difficulty: F
Topic: 8.2
Explanation: Copyright is automatically applied when original work is created and fixed in tangible form. No registration is required in most jurisdictions, though registration provides additional legal benefits.
Tags: copyright, intellectual-property

**Item 6 [F]**
Question: What is data minimisation?
Answer: Data minimisation is the principle of collecting only the minimum amount of personal data necessary for a specified purpose. Organisations should not collect more data than needed or retain data longer than necessary.
Difficulty: F
Topic: 8.2
Explanation: Data minimisation is a core principle of GDPR, reducing both privacy risks and the impact of potential data breaches by limiting the amount of personal information stored.
Tags: data-minimisation, gdpr

**Item 7 [S]**
Question: Explain the right to be forgotten (right to erasure) under GDPR.
Answer: Under GDPR, individuals have the right to request the deletion of their personal data when it is no longer necessary for the purpose it was collected, when consent is withdrawn, or when they object to processing. Organisations must erase data without undue delay, though exceptions exist for legal obligations, public health, and exercising legal rights.
Difficulty: S
Topic: 8.2
Explanation: This right enables individuals to have control over their personal data, particularly information shared on social media or in other public contexts. It balances privacy rights against other interests such as freedom of expression and historical research.
Tags: right-to-be-forgotten, gdpr, privacy

**Item 8 [S]**
Question: Describe two ethical issues in computing beyond legal requirements.
Answer: Any two of: bias in AI and machine learning algorithms leading to discrimination; automation causing job displacement; surveillance technology threatening civil liberties; environmental impact of electronic waste and data centre energy consumption; digital divide creating inequality in access to technology; and the ethical implications of autonomous systems making decisions that affect human lives.
Difficulty: S
Topic: 8.2
Explanation: Ethical issues often extend beyond what is legally required, challenging organisations to consider broader societal impacts of their technology decisions.
Tags: ethics, computing

**Item 9 [S]**
Question: What is intellectual property and why is it important in software development?
Answer: Intellectual property (IP) refers to creations of the mind such as inventions, designs, and artistic works that are protected by law through patents, copyrights, trademarks, and trade secrets. In software development, IP protection ensures developers retain control over their code, can monetise their work, and receive recognition for their contributions.
Difficulty: S
Topic: 8.2
Explanation: Software is protected primarily by copyright (covering code) and trade secrets (covering algorithms and methods). Patents in software are more controversial and vary by jurisdiction.
Tags: intellectual-property, software

**Item 10 [S]**
Question: Explain how cookies can raise privacy concerns and what regulations address this.
Answer: Cookies can track users across multiple websites, building detailed profiles of browsing habits without explicit consent. Third-party tracking cookies are particularly invasive as they are placed by advertising networks rather than the websites users visit directly. GDPR and the ePrivacy Directive require websites to obtain informed consent before placing non-essential cookies, and users must be given clear information about what data is collected.
Difficulty: S
Topic: 8.2
Explanation: The introduction of cookie consent banners on websites is a direct result of GDPR enforcement. However, some critics argue that consent pop-ups have become a compliance tick-box exercise rather than genuinely informing users.
Tags: cookies, privacy, gdpr

**Item 11 [S]**
Question: What steps should an organisation take to comply with the Data Protection Act or GDPR?
Answer: Steps include: appointing a data protection officer; documenting all data processing activities; implementing privacy by design (building data protection into systems from the start); ensuring lawful basis for processing; providing clear privacy notices; implementing data subject rights procedures; ensuring data security measures are in place; conducting data protection impact assessments for high-risk processing; and establishing procedures for reporting data breaches within 72 hours.
Difficulty: S
Topic: 8.2
Explanation: Compliance is not a one-time achievement but an ongoing process requiring regular audits, staff training, and updates to procedures as regulations evolve.
Tags: compliance, gdpr, data-protection

**Item 12 [S]**
Question: Describe the concept of a digital footprint and explain why it matters for personal privacy.
Answer: A digital footprint is the accumulated record of all data created through online activities including social media posts, online purchases, search queries, and app usage. It matters for privacy because this data can be aggregated to build comprehensive profiles used for targeted advertising, identity theft, or algorithmic decision-making that affects job prospects, insurance rates, or creditworthiness. Once shared online, content can be difficult to control and may resurface unexpectedly.
Difficulty: S
Topic: 8.2
Explanation: Employers and universities increasingly check social media profiles as part of recruitment and admissions processes, making digital footprints consequential beyond immediate privacy concerns.
Tags: digital-footprint, privacy, social-media

**Item 13 [C]**
Question: Evaluate the tension between data protection regulations and freedom of expression online.
Answer: GDPR and similar regulations can conflict with freedom of expression when individuals request deletion of information that others wish to discuss or preserve. Journalists, researchers, and archivists may legitimately need to process personal data without consent. GDPR includes exemptions for journalism and academic research, but applying these exemptions requires balancing competing rights. Conversely, online harassment victims may seek deletion of personal information that perpetrators use to continue abuse. The right to be forgotten is not absolute and courts must weigh individual privacy against public interest on a case-by-case basis.
Difficulty: C
Topic: 8.2
Explanation: This tension is particularly evident in debates about de-platforming, where content moderation decisions intersect with free speech principles and data protection rules.
Tags: freedom-of-expression, gdpr, privacy

**Item 14 [C]**
Question: Critically analyse the effectiveness of cookie consent mechanisms in protecting user privacy.
Answer: Cookie consent mechanisms implemented since GDPR enforcement have been widely criticised as "consent walls" or "dark patterns" rather than genuine privacy choices. Common issues include: pre-ticked boxes or accepting all as the dominant button; confusing language designed to encourage acceptance; inability to use websites without accepting cookies; and consent being collected by middlemen rather than first-party publishers. Research shows most users accept all cookies due to friction, undermining the purpose of consent. Effective alternatives might include browser-based tracking protection, contextually appropriate consent, or regulatory default-on rather than opt-in approaches.
Difficulty: C
Topic: 8.2
Explanation: The gap between the theoretical ideal of informed consent and actual user behaviour demonstrates that compliance with privacy laws does not automatically achieve privacy protection in practice.
Tags: cookies, consent, privacy, dark-patterns

**Item 15 [C]**
Question: Discuss the ethical implications of biometric data collection and processing by organisations.
Answer: Biometric data (fingerprints, facial recognition, iris scans) presents unique ethical challenges because it is inherently personal, biologically unique, and cannot be changed if compromised. Collection raises questions about informed consent, purpose limitation, and proportionality. Facial recognition technology used in public surveillance has been criticised for enabling mass surveillance, disproportionately affecting marginalised communities, and creating chilling effects on public assembly. Biometric employee monitoring raises concerns about workplace autonomy and dignity. Organisations must consider whether less invasive alternatives could achieve the same purpose and ensure that biometric systems are accurate, transparent, and subject to human oversight.
Difficulty: C
Topic: 8.2
Explanation: GDPR classifies biometric data as special category data requiring explicit consent and heightened protection. However, the technical capability to identify individuals at scale raises questions about whether legal compliance is sufficient for ethical deployment.
Tags: biometrics, ethics, privacy, surveillance

**Item 16 [C]**
Question: Evaluate the challenges of enforcing data protection laws in a global digital economy where data crosses borders freely.
Answer: Data protection enforcement faces significant challenges in a global context: different jurisdictions have conflicting laws; enforcement resources are limited; organisations can relocate data processing to less regulated regions; international legal cooperation is slow; and technical solutions like VPN and distributed systems make it difficult to determine where data is processed. GDPR attempts to address this through extraterritorial applicability, but can only fine organisations with EU presence or serving EU customers. Cross-border cooperation between data protection authorities, adequacy decisions, and binding corporate rules attempt to bridge regulatory gaps, but enforcement against non-compliant foreign organisations remains difficult.
Difficulty: C
Topic: 8.2
Explanation: The Cloud Act in the United States and similar laws in other jurisdictions create additional conflicts where law enforcement can access data stored abroad, complicating compliance with local data protection requirements.
Tags: data-protection, global-enforcement, jurisdiction

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: Analyse the role of ethics in artificial intelligence development and deployment, including issues of bias and algorithmic accountability.
Answer: AI systems can perpetuate and amplify existing biases present in training data, leading to discriminatory outcomes in hiring, lending, criminal justice, and healthcare. Algorithmic accountability requires that organisations understand how their AI systems make decisions and can explain them to affected individuals. Ethical concerns include: lack of transparency in complex models; displacement of human judgement in consequential decisions; power imbalances between those deploying and those affected by AI; and the difficulty of assigning responsibility when AI causes harm. Responsible AI development requires diverse and representative training data, bias auditing, human oversight of automated decisions, transparency about AI use, and mechanisms for affected individuals to seek redress.
Difficulty: C
Topic: 8.2
Explanation: The EU AI Act represents the first comprehensive attempt to regulate AI systems based on risk levels, requiring high-risk AI to meet transparency, accuracy, and human oversight requirements.
Tags: ai-ethics, bias, algorithmic-accountability

**Item 18 [C]**
Question: Evaluate the effectiveness of data protection legislation in protecting children online and discuss additional measures that may be needed.
Answer: GDPR includes special protections for children, typically requiring parental consent for those under 16 (or lower ages depending on national law). The Age Appropriate Design Code and similar legislation require platforms to consider children's best interests by default. However, effectiveness is limited by: age verification challenges (easily bypassed with false information); limited enforcement resources; the global nature of platforms; and the rapid pace of technology change outstripping legislation. Additional measures might include: mandatory age verification at point of account creation (using trusted third parties to avoid mass data collection); platform design restrictions on data collection from children; parental controls with meaningful oversight; education programmes; and technology designed with children's safety as a primary consideration rather than an afterthought.
Difficulty: C
Topic: 8.2
Explanation: The tension between protecting children and preserving their privacy and autonomy is particularly acute. Overly restrictive measures may push children toward less regulated platforms or prevent them from accessing beneficial online services.
Tags: child-protection, data-protection, online-safety
