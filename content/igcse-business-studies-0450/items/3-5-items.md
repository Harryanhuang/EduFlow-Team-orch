# Items: 3.5 — Pricing methods and sales forecasting

### Question Q-3.5-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a pricing method?
**Answer**: A pricing method is a systematic approach a business uses to set the price of its products or services. Common methods include cost-plus pricing, mark-up pricing, penetration pricing, price skimming, competitive pricing, and psychological pricing.
**Explanation**: Pricing methods ensure prices cover costs and align with business strategy. A common error is confusing pricing method with pricing strategy — method is the calculation; strategy is the market positioning.
**Tags**: pricing-method, definition, pricing

### Question Q-3.5-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Explain cost-plus pricing with an example.
**Answer**: Cost-plus pricing sets the selling price by adding a fixed profit amount or percentage (mark-up) to the total cost of producing one unit. For example, if a product costs $10 to produce and the business adds a 30% mark-up, the selling price is $13.
**Explanation**: It is simple and ensures every sale makes a profit. A common error is confusing mark-up percentage with profit margin — a 30% mark-up on $10 gives a $3 profit, but the margin is $3/$13 = 23%.
**Tags**: cost-plus-pricing, mark-up, calculation

### Question Q-3.5-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Distinguish between competitive pricing and predatory pricing.
**Answer**: Competitive pricing means setting prices at or near the level of rivals to avoid price wars — common in oligopoly markets. Predatory pricing means setting prices below cost deliberately to drive competitors out of the market, then raising prices once competition is reduced — this is illegal in many countries.
**Explanation**: Both involve looking at competitor prices, but the intent and legality differ. A common error is treating them as the same — predatory pricing requires prices below cost with anti-competitive intent.
**Tags**: competitive-pricing, predatory-pricing, distinction

### Question Q-3.5-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is sales forecasting? Why is it important?
**Answer**: Sales forecasting is estimating future sales revenue over a given period based on past data, market research, or expert judgment. It is important because it helps businesses plan production, manage cash flow, set budgets, allocate resources, and make staffing decisions.
**Explanation**: Forecasts are never perfect but provide a basis for planning. A common error is treating a forecast as a guarantee rather than an estimate.
**Tags**: sales-forecasting, definition, planning

### Question Q-3.5-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe two methods of sales forecasting.
**Answer**: 1) Trend extrapolation — using past sales data to project future sales by extending the trend line. 2) Market research — surveying customers or testing the market to estimate demand. 3) Salesforce estimates — aggregating predictions from the sales team who interact with customers. 4) Delphi method — consulting a panel of experts to reach a consensus forecast.
**Explanation**: Different methods suit different situations. A common error is relying on a single method — combining approaches improves accuracy.
**Tags**: sales-forecasting, methods, market-research

### Question Q-3.5-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business currently uses cost-plus pricing. Explain two advantages and two disadvantages of this method.
**Answer**: Advantages: 1) Simple to calculate and apply — ensures every sale covers costs and generates profit. 2) Stable prices — customers are not confused by frequent changes. Disadvantages: 1) Ignores demand — if customers are willing to pay more, the business leaves money on the table. 2) Ignores competition — rivals may charge significantly less, making the product uncompetitive. 3) May incentivise inefficiency if costs are not controlled.
**Explanation**: Cost-plus is simple but market-blind. A common error is listing only advantages without acknowledging the method's key weakness — it ignores what customers will actually pay.
**Tags**: cost-plus-pricing, advantage, disadvantage

### Question Q-3.5-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the limitations of sales forecasting for a new business with no sales history.
**Answer**: Without historical data, trend-based forecasting is impossible. New businesses must rely on market research, competitor benchmarks, and expert judgment — all of which carry higher uncertainty. Consumer behaviour may differ from predictions, market conditions can change rapidly, and initial forecasts often overestimate demand. However, forecasting is still necessary for securing finance and planning. New businesses should use scenario planning (optimistic, realistic, pessimistic) rather than a single forecast.
**Explanation**: Forecasts for new businesses are inherently uncertain. A common error is presenting a single forecast without sensitivity analysis.
**Tags**: sales-forecasting, limitations, evaluation

### Question Q-3.5-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A technology company has developed a breakthrough wearable health monitor with no direct competitors. Recommend a pricing method and justify your choice.
**Answer**: Recommend price skimming. Justification: the product is innovative with no direct competition, allowing a high initial price to recover R&D costs from early adopters and health enthusiasts willing to pay a premium. As competitors enter and the market matures, prices can be lowered. This maximises revenue at each stage. The company should also consider a premium pricing strategy to reinforce the product's perceived quality and technological superiority.
**Explanation**: The lack of competitors and the innovative nature make skimming appropriate. A common error is recommending penetration pricing for a unique product — that forfeits early-margin advantages.
**Tags**: pricing-method, price-skimming, evaluation, application

### Question Q-3.5-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A coffee shop chain faces rising coffee bean costs and increasing competition from a new budget chain. Recommend how it should adapt its pricing approach and justify your recommendation.
**Answer**: The chain should avoid a simple cost-plus increase across all products, as this risks losing price-sensitive customers to the budget competitor. Instead: 1) Apply selective price increases on premium drinks where customers are less price-sensitive. 2) Introduce a value range at competitive prices to defend market share. 3) Use bundle pricing (coffee + pastry) to increase perceived value. 4) Strengthen loyalty programmes to reduce switching. Justification: a differentiated pricing approach protects margins on premium items while defending volume against the budget competitor.
**Explanation**: A uniform price rise is risky when a budget competitor enters. A common error is passing all cost increases to customers without considering competitive positioning.
**Tags**: pricing, competition, evaluation, application, cost-changes
