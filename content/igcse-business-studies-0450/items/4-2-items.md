# Items: 4.2 — Inventory management and supply chain

### Question Q-4.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is inventory (stock)? Name three types of inventory a manufacturing business might hold.
**Answer**: Inventory (or stock) refers to the goods and materials a business holds for the purpose of production or resale. Three types: 1) Raw materials — inputs waiting to be used in production. 2) Work in progress — partly finished goods still on the production line. 3) Finished goods — completed products ready for sale to customers.
**Explanation**: Inventory is a current asset on the balance sheet. A common error is thinking inventory only means finished products — raw materials and WIP are equally important.
**Tags**: inventory, stock, types, definition

### Question Q-4.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What are the costs of holding inventory?
**Answer**: Costs include: 1) Storage costs — warehousing, rent, utilities. 2) Insurance and security costs. 3) Obsolescence — products going out of date or becoming outdated. 4) Deterioration or damage while in storage. 5) Opportunity cost — money tied up in stock could be used elsewhere in the business.
**Explanation**: Holding costs make excessive inventory a financial burden. A common error is only counting the physical storage cost and ignoring the opportunity cost of tied-up capital.
**Tags**: inventory, holding-costs, warehousing

### Question Q-4.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a stock control chart? Explain the terms reorder level, lead time, and maximum stock level.
**Answer**: A stock control chart tracks inventory levels over time. Key terms: 1) Reorder level — the stock level at which a new order must be placed to avoid running out. 2) Lead time — the time between placing an order and receiving it. 3) Maximum stock level — the highest amount of inventory the business wants to hold, often determined by storage capacity or cost.
**Explanation**: These levels help businesses know when and how much to order. A common error is confusing reorder level with buffer stock — reorder level triggers an order; buffer stock is the safety minimum.
**Tags**: stock-control-chart, reorder-level, lead-time, inventory

### Question Q-4.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a supply chain? Describe a typical supply chain for a manufactured product.
**Answer**: A supply chain is the sequence of activities and organisations involved in producing and delivering a product from raw material to the end customer. A typical chain: raw material supplier → component manufacturer → finished goods manufacturer → wholesaler → retailer → consumer. Each link adds value and depends on the others for timely delivery and quality.
**Explanation**: Supply chain management coordinates all links to reduce costs and improve service. A common error is thinking the supply chain only includes the manufacturer — it spans from raw materials to end user.
**Tags**: supply-chain, definition, logistics

### Question Q-4.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain the benefits of effective supply chain management.
**Answer**: Benefits include: 1) Lower costs through efficient ordering and reduced inventory. 2) Faster delivery to customers. 3) Better quality from reliable suppliers. 4) Improved communication and coordination between supply chain partners. 5) Reduced risk of stock-outs or production delays. 6) Greater flexibility to respond to changes in demand.
**Explanation**: Good supply chain management is a competitive advantage. A common error is focusing only on cost reduction — speed, quality, and flexibility are equally important.
**Tags**: supply-chain-management, benefits, operations

### Question Q-4.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is stock rotation? Why is it important, particularly for perishable goods?
**Answer**: Stock rotation is the practice of using or selling older stock before newer stock. The most common method is FIFO (first in, first out). For perishable goods like food and medicine, stock rotation prevents items from passing their use-by or expiry dates, which would cause waste, financial loss, and potential health risks. It also ensures customers receive products with the maximum remaining shelf life.
**Explanation**: FIFO is the dominant method for perishables. A common error is confusing FIFO with LIFO — LIFO (last in, first out) is rarely used for physical goods and unsuitable for perishables.
**Tags**: stock-rotation, FIFO, perishable-goods, inventory

### Question Q-4.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the costs and benefits of holding large inventories for a retail business.
**Answer**: Benefits: 1) No stock-outs — customers find what they want, protecting sales and reputation. 2) Ability to take advantage of bulk discounts from suppliers. 3) Buffer against supply chain disruptions or seasonal demand spikes. 4) Display of full product range attracts more customers. Costs: 1) High storage, insurance, and security expenses. 2) Risk of obsolescence, especially for fashion or technology products. 3) Capital tied up in stock cannot be used for investment or marketing. 4) Deterioration, theft, or damage. For most retailers, the costs of excessive inventory outweigh the benefits — lean inventory with reliable restocking is preferable.
**Explanation**: The optimal level balances service and cost. A common error is presenting only one side — both costs and benefits must be weighed for the specific business context.
**Tags**: inventory, evaluation, retail, cost-benefit

### Question Q-4.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A supermarket chain wants to reduce its inventory costs without increasing the risk of running out of stock. Recommend strategies it should adopt.
**Answer**: 1) Invest in electronic point of sale (EPOS) systems linked to automated reordering — real-time data reduces both over-ordering and stock-outs. 2) Work with suppliers on just-in-time deliveries for fast-moving items. 3) Use demand forecasting based on seasonal patterns and promotional calendars. 4) Negotiate shorter lead times with key suppliers. 5) Reduce product range (SKU rationalisation) for slow-moving items. 6) Implement cross-docking — transferring goods directly from inbound to outbound transport with minimal storage. Justification: technology-driven inventory management reduces holding costs while maintaining availability.
**Explanation**: The focus should be on better information, not just less stock. A common error is recommending simple stock cuts without addressing the systems that generate orders.
**Tags**: inventory-management, cost-reduction, evaluation, application, retail

### Question Q-4.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the impact of a global supply chain disruption (such as a pandemic or trade dispute) on a manufacturer that relies on just-in-time inventory management.
**Answer**: A JIT manufacturer is highly vulnerable to supply chain disruption because it holds minimal buffer stock. Any delay — port closures, supplier shutdowns, transport restrictions — can halt the entire production line within hours or days, causing lost sales, idle workers, and damaged customer relationships. However, JIT firms with strong supplier relationships may receive priority during shortages. Response strategies include: diversifying the supplier base across regions, increasing safety stock for critical components, and nearshoring key suppliers. Justification: JIT's efficiency advantage disappears when supply reliability collapses — resilience becomes more important than lean inventory.
**Explanation**: The trade-off between efficiency and resilience is central. A common error is dismissing JIT entirely — it works well in stable conditions but needs contingency planning.
**Tags**: just-in-time, supply-chain-disruption, evaluation, risk
