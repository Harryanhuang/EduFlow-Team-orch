# Items: 6.2 — Economic climate and business decisions

### Question Q-6.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the business cycle (economic cycle)?
**Answer**: The business cycle describes the periodic fluctuations in economic activity (GDP) over time. It has four phases: 1) Boom (peak) — high growth, low unemployment, rising inflation. 2) Recession (downturn) — falling GDP for two or more consecutive quarters, rising unemployment. 3) Slump (trough) — the lowest point, high unemployment, low demand. 4) Recovery — GDP begins to grow again, confidence returns, employment starts to rise.
**Explanation**: The cycle is not perfectly regular or predictable. A common error is thinking recession means a complete economic collapse — it is a temporary contraction within a longer pattern of growth.
**Tags**: business-cycle, definition, phases

### Question Q-6.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is inflation? How is it measured?
**Answer**: Inflation is a sustained increase in the general price level of goods and services over time, reducing the purchasing power of money. It is measured using the Consumer Price Index (CPI), which tracks the cost of a representative basket of goods and services bought by a typical household. The inflation rate is the percentage change in the CPI over a period (usually one year).
**Explanation**: Inflation is about the average price level, not individual prices. A common error is confusing inflation with a single price increase — it must be economy-wide and sustained.
**Tags**: inflation, CPI, definition

### Question Q-6.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does a recession affect business decisions? Give three examples.
**Answer**: During a recession: 1) Demand falls as consumers cut spending — businesses may reduce prices, offer discounts, or focus on essential products. 2) Investment is delayed or cancelled due to uncertainty and lower expected returns. 3) Cost-cutting measures are implemented — reducing staff, cutting marketing budgets, renegotiating supplier contracts. 4) Businesses may diversify into cheaper product lines (inferior goods) that see increased demand during downturns. 5) Cash flow becomes critical — businesses focus on collecting receivables and reducing inventory.
**Explanation**: Recession forces businesses to prioritise survival. A common error is assuming all businesses suffer equally — some (e.g., discount retailers, repair services) may benefit.
**Tags**: recession, business-decisions, impact

### Question Q-6.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does high inflation affect a business's costs, pricing, and competitiveness?
**Answer**: High inflation increases input costs (raw materials, energy, wages), squeezing profit margins if prices cannot be raised proportionately. Businesses may raise prices to protect margins, but this risks losing price-sensitive customers. Frequent price changes create menu costs (the cost of updating prices, catalogues, and systems). If domestic inflation is higher than in competitor countries, exports become less competitive internationally. Workers may demand higher wages to maintain living standards, creating a wage-price spiral.
**Explanation**: Inflation affects both costs and revenues. A common error is thinking businesses can always pass inflation on to customers — price elasticity limits this.
**Tags**: inflation, costs, pricing, competitiveness

### Question Q-6.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is GDP (gross domestic product)? How does GDP growth affect business planning?
**Answer**: GDP is the total value of all goods and services produced in a country in a given period. It measures the size and health of the economy. Rising GDP (economic growth) indicates expanding output and typically higher consumer income and spending — businesses plan for increased demand, investment, and hiring. Falling GDP signals contraction — businesses may delay investment, reduce inventory, and focus on cost control. GDP growth rates help businesses forecast market size and set realistic sales targets.
**Explanation**: GDP is the broadest indicator of economic conditions. A common error is equating GDP with business profits — GDP measures output, not profitability.
**Tags**: GDP, economic-growth, business-planning

### Question Q-6.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the difference between demand-pull and cost-push inflation?
**Answer**: Demand-pull inflation occurs when aggregate demand exceeds aggregate supply — too much money chasing too few goods. It is caused by strong consumer spending, government expenditure, or investment. Cost-push inflation occurs when production costs rise (e.g., higher wages, increased raw material prices, higher taxes), forcing businesses to raise prices to maintain margins. Both result in rising prices, but the causes and policy responses differ. Demand-pull can be addressed by reducing demand (higher interest rates); cost-push requires addressing supply-side constraints.
**Explanation**: The distinction matters for diagnosis and response. A common error is treating all inflation as the same phenomenon — the underlying cause determines the appropriate business and policy response.
**Tags**: inflation, demand-pull, cost-push, distinction

### Question Q-6.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate how a business should adjust its strategy during each phase of the business cycle.
**Answer**: Boom: focus on growth — expand capacity, hire staff, invest in marketing, consider premium product lines as consumer confidence is high. Recession: focus on survival — cut costs, preserve cash, reduce inventory, renegotiate supplier terms, consider discount lines. Slump: focus on efficiency — restructure operations, train remaining staff, maintain relationships with key customers, prepare for recovery. Recovery: focus on positioning — invest ahead of demand, launch new products, hire ahead of competitors, secure financing while rates may still be low. The key is anticipating transitions rather than reacting — businesses that prepare in the slump gain share in recovery.
**Explanation**: Strategy should cycle with the economy. A common error is pursuing the same strategy regardless of economic conditions.
**Tags**: business-cycle, strategy, evaluation

### Question Q-6.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business operates in an economy experiencing stagflation (high inflation combined with high unemployment and stagnant demand). Analyse the challenges this creates and recommend how the business should respond.
**Answer**: Stagflation is particularly difficult because the usual remedies conflict: raising prices to cover inflation further reduces already-weak demand, while cutting prices to stimulate sales erodes margins that are already squeezed by rising costs. Challenges: 1) Costs rise through inflation but revenue cannot grow due to stagnant demand. 2) Unemployment reduces consumer spending power. 3) Uncertainty makes investment planning nearly impossible. Recommendations: 1) Focus on operational efficiency to reduce controllable costs. 2) Maintain prices on essential products while trimming non-essential expenses. 3) Preserve cash and avoid long-term commitments. 4) Consider exporting if the domestic currency is overvalued. 5) Diversify into products with inelastic demand (necessities).
**Explanation**: Stagflation defies normal business logic. A common error is applying standard recession strategies without accounting for the cost inflation component.
**Tags**: stagflation, inflation, unemployment, evaluation, application

### Question Q-6.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the impact of a sudden sharp increase in oil prices on an airline, a logistics company, and a software company. What does this illustrate about economic shocks?
**Answer**: Airline: fuel is one of the largest operating costs (typically 25-35% of total costs). A sharp oil price increase directly and significantly raises costs, squeezing margins or forcing fare increases that reduce demand. Logistics company: fuel costs are significant but a smaller proportion than for airlines. The company can partially offset through route optimisation, fuel-efficient vehicles, and fuel surcharges passed to customers. Software company: minimal direct impact from oil prices. Indirect effects include potential reduction in corporate IT spending if the broader economy slows, and higher commuting costs affecting employee wage demands. This illustrates that economic shocks affect industries unevenly — the same event creates existential risk for one business and negligible impact for another.
**Explanation**: Industry structure determines shock vulnerability. A common error is assessing economic impact at the economy level without considering industry-specific exposure.
**Tags**: oil-prices, economic-shock, industry-comparison, evaluation
