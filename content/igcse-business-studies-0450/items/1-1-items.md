### Question Q-1.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Calculate value added: a furniture maker sells a table for $200. Bought-in materials cost $60 and labour costs $80.
**Answer**: Value added = selling price - cost of bought-in materials = $200 - $60 = $140. Labour costs are not subtracted for value added calculation.
**Explanation**: Value added = price - materials only. A common error is subtracting labour too: $200-$60-$80 = $60.
**Tags**: adding-value, calculation

### Question Q-1.1-04

**Question**: What is the difference between goods and services?
**Answer**: Goods are tangible, physical products that can be seen and touched (e.g., cars, food, furniture). Services are intangible actions performed for customers (e.g., haircuts, banking, education).
**Explanation**: Goods = tangible. Services = intangible. A common error is saying services cannot be sold.

### Question Q-1.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A manufacturer buys raw materials for $50 and sells the finished product for $180. Calculate value added and explain why it is not the same as profit.
**Answer**: Value added = $180 - $50 = $130. Profit = value added - other costs (labour, rent, utilities, etc.). Value added only subtracts bought-in materials, not all costs.
**Explanation**: Value added ≠ profit. Profit subtracts all costs. A common error is treating them as the same.
**Tags**: adding-value, profit, calculation

### Question Q-1.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does branding help a business add value?
**Answer**: A strong brand allows a business to charge higher prices because customers associate the brand with quality, trust, or status. The bought-in costs remain the same, so value added increases.
**Explanation**: Brand → higher price → more value added. A common error is saying branding reduces costs.
**Tags**: branding, adding-value, premium-price

### Question Q-1.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether a luxury brand always adds more value than a budget brand.
**Answer**: Luxury brands may charge higher prices but also have higher bought-in costs (premium materials). Budget brands may have low bought-in costs and decent value added through efficiency. Value added depends on the gap between price and materials, not just the price. A luxury brand with $500 selling price and $400 materials adds only $100, while a budget brand with $50 price and $10 materials adds $40 — proportionally much higher.
**Explanation**: Value added = price - materials. Luxury ≠ automatically more value added. A common error is assuming higher price = more value added without considering bought-in costs.
**Tags**: adding-value, luxury, evaluation

### Question Q-1.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A restaurant buys ingredients for $5 per meal and sells for $20. A fast-food chain buys for $3 and sells for $8. Compare value added and discuss which business might be more profitable.
**Answer**: Restaurant value added = $15 per meal. Fast-food value added = $5 per meal. The restaurant adds more value per unit. However, profitability depends on other costs — the fast-food chain may have much lower labour, rent, and overhead costs per meal due to high volume and standardised processes. The fast-food chain may sell thousands of meals per day, generating more total profit despite lower value added per unit.
**Explanation**: Value added ≠ profitability. Volume and other costs matter. A common error is assuming more value added automatically means more profit.
**Tags**: adding-value, profitability, comparison

### Question Q-1.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: To what extent is adding value the most important factor in the success of a business?
**Answer**: Adding value is essential because it generates the revenue needed to cover costs and earn profit. Without sufficient value added, a business cannot survive long-term. However, other factors are equally important: effective marketing to reach customers, cost control to manage expenses, good customer service to retain buyers, and sound financial management to maintain cash flow. A business with high value added but poor management may still fail. Therefore, adding value is necessary but not sufficient on its own — it must be combined with other business strengths.
**Explanation**: Adding value is one of several critical success factors. A common error is treating it as the only factor or ignoring the role of costs, management, and market conditions.
**Tags**: adding-value, business-success, evaluation
