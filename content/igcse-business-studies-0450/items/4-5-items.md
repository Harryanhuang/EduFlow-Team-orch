# Items: 4.5 — Location decisions and outsourcing

### Question Q-4.5-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is outsourcing? Give an example.
**Answer**: Outsourcing is when a business contracts out a non-core activity or function to an external third-party provider rather than performing it in-house. Examples include a manufacturer outsourcing its cleaning services, an airline outsourcing its IT helpdesk, or a retailer outsourcing its delivery logistics to a courier company.
**Explanation**: Outsourcing allows the business to focus on its core activities. A common error is confusing outsourcing with redundancy — outsourcing transfers work externally; redundancy eliminates the role.
**Tags**: outsourcing, definition, examples

### Question Q-4.5-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is offshoring? How does it differ from outsourcing?
**Answer**: Offshoring is relocating a business process or production to another country, often to take advantage of lower labour costs or favourable regulations. It differs from outsourcing in that offshoring is about location (moving to another country), while outsourcing is about who performs the work (external provider). A business can offshore without outsourcing (moving its own operations abroad), or outsource without offshoring (hiring a local third party).
**Explanation**: The two concepts are independent but often combined. A common error is using offshoring and outsourcing as synonyms — they describe different dimensions.
**Tags**: offshoring, outsourcing, distinction

### Question Q-4.5-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain the advantages and disadvantages of outsourcing a business function.
**Answer**: Advantages: 1) Cost savings — external providers may achieve economies of scale. 2) Access to specialist expertise and technology. 3) Allows the business to focus on core activities. 4) Flexibility — easier to scale up or down without hiring or firing. Disadvantages: 1) Loss of direct control over quality and timing. 2) Dependency on the supplier — if they fail, the business is disrupted. 3) Confidentiality and data security risks. 4) Possible negative public perception, especially if outsourcing leads to domestic job losses.
**Explanation**: Outsourcing is a trade-off between cost and control. A common error is listing only advantages — the loss of control is a significant risk.
**Tags**: outsourcing, advantage, disadvantage

### Question Q-4.5-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain the advantages and disadvantages of offshoring production to a lower-cost country.
**Answer**: Advantages: 1) Significantly lower labour costs. 2) Access to new and growing markets. 3) Potentially fewer regulations or lower taxes. 4) Extended operating hours through time-zone differences (for service offshoring). Disadvantages: 1) Longer supply chains and lead times. 2) Quality control challenges from a distance. 3) Language and cultural barriers. 4) Political and currency risk. 5) Negative publicity about job losses in the home country. 6) Intellectual property risks in countries with weaker legal protection.
**Explanation**: Offshoring savings must be weighed against coordination complexity. A common error is assuming lower wages automatically mean lower total cost — transport and quality issues can offset the saving.
**Tags**: offshoring, advantage, disadvantage

### Question Q-4.5-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is nearshoring? How does it compare to offshoring?
**Answer**: Nearshoring is relocating business processes or production to a nearby country rather than a distant one. For example, a US company moving production to Mexico rather than China. Compared to offshoring, nearshoring offers shorter supply chains, similar time zones, cultural and language proximity, and easier visits for quality control. However, cost savings may be less than with distant low-wage countries. It is a compromise between domestic production and distant offshoring.
**Explanation**: Nearshoring balances cost and control. A common error is treating nearshoring as just a form of offshoring — it is specifically chosen for geographic and cultural closeness.
**Tags**: nearshoring, offshoring, comparison

### Question Q-4.5-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What are the risks of relocating a business to a new location? How can these risks be reduced?
**Answer**: Risks: 1) High one-off relocation costs (moving equipment, terminating leases, setting up new facilities). 2) Loss of experienced workers who are unwilling to relocate. 3) Disruption to operations and customer service during the transition. 4) Uncertainty about the new location — labour market, local regulations, community acceptance. 5) Damage to brand image if the move is seen as abandoning the local community. Mitigation: 1) Detailed planning and phased relocation. 2) Retention bonuses for key staff. 3) Maintaining a temporary presence at the old location during transition. 4) Thorough research and pilot visits to the new location.
**Explanation**: Relocation is disruptive even when well-planned. A common error is underestimating the human cost — losing skilled workers can be more damaging than the financial cost.
**Tags**: relocation, risks, mitigation, location-decisions

### Question Q-4.5-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A UK-based clothing retailer currently manufactures in the UK but faces high labour costs. It is considering moving production to Bangladesh. Evaluate the decision.
**Answer**: Advantages of moving to Bangladesh: significantly lower labour costs, a well-established textile industry, and access to growing Asian supply chains. This would allow lower retail prices or higher margins. Disadvantages: long lead times (weeks by sea) make responding to fashion trends difficult; quality control at a distance is challenging; ethical concerns about labour conditions in garment factories could damage brand reputation; import duties may offset some cost savings. For a fast-fashion retailer needing rapid turnaround, nearshoring to Eastern Europe might balance cost and speed better. For basic, predictable lines, Bangladesh's cost advantage is compelling.
**Explanation**: The evaluation must weigh cost savings against supply chain agility and reputation risk. A common error is focusing only on labour cost without considering lead time and brand image.
**Tags**: offshoring, evaluation, application, clothing, ethical-considerations

### Question Q-4.5-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the make-or-buy decision for a smartphone manufacturer considering whether to produce its own batteries or purchase them from a specialist supplier.
**Answer**: Make (in-house): gives full control over battery quality, design, and supply timing — important if battery life is a key selling point. However, it requires significant capital investment in production facilities and specialist expertise. Buy (outsource): benefits from the supplier's economies of scale, specialist knowledge, and existing R&D in battery technology. The manufacturer avoids capital expenditure and can switch suppliers if better technology emerges. Recommendation: buy from a specialist supplier, as battery technology evolves rapidly and a single manufacturer is unlikely to match a dedicated battery firm's expertise and scale. Exception: if the manufacturer is very large (like Apple), vertical integration may be justified for differentiation.
**Explanation**: The decision hinges on strategic importance versus specialist advantage. A common error is assuming vertical integration is always better for quality control.
**Tags**: make-or-buy, outsourcing, evaluation, application, technology

### Question Q-4.5-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A software company with 200 employees is based in London but struggles with high office rents and staff commute times. It is considering: (a) moving to a cheaper city in the UK, (b) adopting fully remote working, or (c) a hybrid model. Evaluate the options and recommend.
**Answer**: Option (a) — relocation to a cheaper UK city — reduces rent but forces staff to relocate or resign, losing institutional knowledge. Option (b) — fully remote — eliminates office costs entirely and allows hiring from a wider talent pool, but risks reduced collaboration, culture erosion, and isolation for some workers. Option (c) — hybrid (e.g., 2-3 days in a smaller office) — balances cost savings with team interaction and culture maintenance. Recommendation: hybrid. It addresses the cost problem (smaller office = lower rent) while preserving the collaboration and culture that make a software company innovative. Fully remote could work long-term but a sudden full shift risks damaging productivity during the transition.
**Explanation**: The recommendation should balance cost, people, and business model needs. A common error is treating this as purely a financial decision — culture and collaboration matter for knowledge-intensive businesses.
**Tags**: location-decisions, remote-working, evaluation, application, software
