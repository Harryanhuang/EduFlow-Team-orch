# Items: 5.4 — Sources of finance and business accounts

### Question Q-5.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What are sources of finance? Distinguish between internal and external sources.
**Answer**: Sources of finance are the ways a business raises money. Internal sources come from within the business — retained profit (profit kept rather than distributed to owners) and sale of unused assets. External sources come from outside the business — bank loans, share capital, venture capital, overdrafts, trade credit, and government grants. Internal sources are generally cheaper and do not create debt, but may be insufficient for large projects.
**Explanation**: The source chosen affects the business's cost of capital and risk. A common error is treating retained profit as 'free' — it has an opportunity cost because owners forgo dividends.
**Tags**: sources-of-finance, internal, external, definition

### Question Q-5.4-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is retained profit? Why is it an important source of finance for established businesses?
**Answer**: Retained profit is the portion of net profit that is kept in the business rather than distributed to owners as dividends or drawings. It is an important source because: 1) It does not incur interest charges. 2) It does not dilute ownership or control. 3) It is readily available without application processes. 4) It signals financial strength to lenders. However, new businesses have no retained profit, and owners may prefer immediate returns.
**Explanation**: Retained profit is the cheapest long-term source of finance. A common error is confusing retained profit with cash — retained profit is an accounting figure; actual cash may be tied up in assets.
**Tags**: retained-profit, internal-finance, definition

### Question Q-5.4-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a debenture? How does it differ from an ordinary share?
**Answer**: A debenture is a type of long-term loan capital issued by companies, secured against the company's assets. Debenture holders receive a fixed rate of interest regardless of whether the company makes a profit. They differ from ordinary shares in that: 1) Debenture holders are lenders, not owners — they have no voting rights. 2) Interest on debentures is a fixed charge, while dividends on shares vary with profit. 3) Debentures must be repaid at a specified date; share capital does not. 4) In liquidation, debenture holders are paid before shareholders.
**Explanation**: Debentures are debt, not equity. A common error is treating debenture holders as part-owners of the company.
**Tags**: debenture, loan-capital, shares, distinction

### Question Q-5.4-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is venture capital? When is it a suitable source of finance?
**Answer**: Venture capital is finance provided by specialist investors (venture capitalists) in exchange for a share of ownership in the business. Venture capitalists typically invest in new or expanding businesses with high growth potential. They often provide expertise, contacts, and guidance alongside finance. It is suitable for: 1) Startups that cannot access bank loans due to lack of collateral or track record. 2) Businesses with high growth potential seeking expansion capital. 3) Businesses that benefit from the investor's industry knowledge. The trade-off is giving up a portion of ownership and control.
**Explanation**: Venture capital is more than money — it brings expertise. A common error is thinking venture capital is a loan — it is equity investment.
**Tags**: venture-capital, equity-finance, startup

### Question Q-5.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is trade credit? How does it help a business manage its cash flow?
**Answer**: Trade credit is when a supplier allows a business to receive goods or services now and pay for them at a later date (typically 30, 60, or 90 days). It is a short-term source of finance because the business can sell the goods and generate revenue before the payment is due. It helps cash flow by: 1) Deferring cash outflows. 2) Reducing the need for external borrowing. 3) Being readily available without formal application. However, discounts for early payment may be lost, and late payment can damage supplier relationships.
**Explanation**: Trade credit is the most common short-term finance for businesses. A common error is not recognising trade credit as a source of finance — it is effectively an interest-free loan from suppliers.
**Tags**: trade-credit, cash-flow, short-term-finance

### Question Q-5.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Distinguish between capital expenditure and revenue expenditure. Give examples.
**Answer**: Capital expenditure (capex) is spending on non-current assets that provide long-term benefits — lasting more than one year. Examples include buying machinery, buildings, vehicles, or equipment. Revenue expenditure (opex) is spending on the day-to-day running of the business — benefits are consumed within the accounting period. Examples include wages, rent, utilities, raw materials, and repairs. The distinction matters because capital expenditure appears on the balance sheet (as assets), while revenue expenditure appears on the income statement (as expenses).
**Explanation**: The key difference is duration of benefit. A common error is classifying asset improvements as repairs — improvements are capital expenditure; repairs that maintain existing condition are revenue expenditure.
**Tags**: capital-expenditure, revenue-expenditure, accounting

### Question Q-5.4-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A sole trader wants to expand and needs $200,000. Evaluate whether they should seek a bank loan, bring in a partner, or use crowdfunding.
**Answer**: Bank loan: retains full ownership and control, but requires collateral and regular interest repayments — risky if the expansion does not generate revenue quickly. Bring in a partner: provides capital and shared expertise without debt, but ownership and decision-making must be shared — potential for conflict. Crowdfunding: raises money from many small investors, provides marketing exposure, but may not reach the full amount required and involves public disclosure of the business idea. Recommendation depends on the sole trader's risk tolerance and willingness to share control. A bank loan is suitable if the business has collateral and predictable cash flows; a partner is better if expertise and risk-sharing are valued; crowdfunding suits consumer-facing businesses with an engaging story.
**Explanation**: The choice involves trade-offs between control, risk, and cost. A common error is recommending one source without considering the business's specific circumstances.
**Tags**: sources-of-finance, evaluation, sole-trader, expansion

### Question Q-5.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain why a business might choose to raise finance through a combination of sources rather than relying on a single source.
**Answer**: Using multiple sources spreads risk and matches finance to specific needs. Long-term assets (buildings, machinery) should be funded by long-term sources (share capital, retained profit, long-term loans) to avoid maturity mismatch. Short-term working capital needs suit short-term sources (overdraft, trade credit). A mixed approach also avoids over-reliance on one lender or investor, keeps the cost of capital lower (debt is cheaper than equity but carries repayment risk), and maintains flexibility. For example, a business might use retained profit for part of an expansion, a bank loan for equipment, and an overdraft for working capital — each source matched to its purpose.
**Explanation**: Diversifying finance is analogous to diversifying investment — it reduces concentration risk. A common error is seeking one 'best' source when most businesses use several.
**Tags**: sources-of-finance, diversification, strategy, evaluation

### Question Q-5.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A public limited company (PLC) wants to raise $10 million for a major expansion. Compare issuing new shares versus issuing debentures as methods of raising this finance.
**Answer**: Issuing new shares (rights issue or public offering): provides permanent capital with no obligation to pay dividends if profits are weak. No interest burden and no repayment date. However, it dilutes existing shareholders' ownership and control, and the cost of equity is typically higher than debt (investors expect higher returns). Issuing debentures: does not dilute ownership, interest payments are tax-deductible, and debenture holders have no voting rights. However, interest must be paid regardless of profitability, and the company's assets are pledged as security — if the company defaults, assets can be seized. Recommendation: if the expansion is high-risk or the company already has significant debt, shares are safer. If the company has stable cash flows and wants to preserve ownership, debentures are more cost-effective.
**Explanation**: The equity-versus-debt decision is fundamental to corporate finance. A common error is not considering the company's existing debt level and risk profile.
**Tags**: shares, debentures, PLC, evaluation, corporate-finance
