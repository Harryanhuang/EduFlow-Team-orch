# Items: 2.4 — Dismissal, redundancy, and trade unions


### Question Q-2.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define 'dismissal'.
**Answer**: Dismissal is when an employer terminates an employee's contract of employment. It can be with or without notice depending on the circumstances.
**Explanation**: Dismissal is initiated by the employer. A common error is confusing dismissal with resignation — resignation is initiated by the employee.
**Tags**: dismissal, definition

### Question Q-2.4-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is redundancy?
**Answer**: Redundancy occurs when an employee is dismissed because the job they do is no longer needed — for example, the business is closing, downsizing, or the work is being done by machines.
**Explanation**: Redundancy is about the role disappearing, not the person's performance. A common error is treating redundancy as a form of punishment.
**Tags**: redundancy, definition

### Question Q-2.4-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Give three reasons why an employee might be fairly dismissed.
**Answer**: 1) Gross misconduct (e.g., theft, violence). 2) Lack of capability or qualifications for the role. 3) Long-term illness preventing the employee from doing the job. 4) Some other substantial reason (e.g., a genuine business reorganisation).
**Explanation**: Fair dismissal must follow proper procedures. A common error is listing 'personal dislike' as a reason — this is not a fair ground.
**Tags**: fair-dismissal, reasons, employment

### Question Q-2.4-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain the difference between voluntary and compulsory redundancy.
**Answer**: Voluntary redundancy is when employees choose to leave in exchange for a redundancy package. Compulsory redundancy is when the employer selects employees to leave. Voluntary redundancy is generally preferred as it reduces resentment and legal risk.
**Explanation**: Both are legitimate business practices. A common error is thinking voluntary redundancy means the employee was not truly redundant.
**Tags**: voluntary-redundancy, compulsory-redundancy, distinction

### Question Q-2.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe three ways a trade union may achieve its objectives.
**Answer**: 1) Collective bargaining — negotiating with employers on behalf of members. 2) Industrial action — strikes, work-to-rule, overtime bans. 3) Negotiating — reaching agreements without conflict. 4) Consultation — being involved in employer decision-making that affects workers.
**Explanation**: Different methods suit different situations. A common error is listing only industrial action — unions use a range of tactics.
**Tags**: trade-union, methods, collective-bargaining

### Question Q-2.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What rights do employees have against unfair dismissal?
**Answer**: Employees generally have the right to: a written reason for dismissal, a notice period, appeal against the decision, and (after a qualifying period) the right to claim unfair dismissal at an employment tribunal. Employers must follow a fair procedure including investigation and hearing.
**Explanation**: Rights vary by jurisdiction. A common error is claiming all dismissals are automatically unfair — some are lawful if proper procedure is followed.
**Tags**: unfair-dismissal, employee-rights, employment-law

### Question Q-2.4-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the benefits and disadvantages of trade unions for a business.
**Answer**: Benefits: single point of contact for workforce concerns, can improve communication, may help resolve disputes efficiently, and can represent workers in grievances. Disadvantages: industrial action disrupts production, collective bargaining may increase labour costs, unions may resist changes the business needs. The overall impact depends heavily on the relationship between management and the union — a cooperative partnership can benefit both sides, while an adversarial relationship damages productivity and morale. For most businesses, the benefits of constructive union engagement outweigh the disadvantages of having no union representation at all.
**Explanation**: The relationship quality matters more than union existence. A common error is only listing negatives.
**Tags**: trade-union, evaluation, business-impact

### Question Q-2.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the impact of redundancy and dismissal on an employee. To what extent is redundancy less damaging than dismissal?
**Answer**: Redundancy is not personal — the role no longer exists. The employee usually receives a redundancy payment and may find it easier to explain to future employers. Dismissal (especially for misconduct) is personal, can damage reputation, and may not include severance. Both cause financial stress and emotional distress. However, redundancy generally has less stigma and provides financial cushioning through redundancy pay. Dismissal for gross misconduct can make finding new employment significantly harder. To a large extent, redundancy is less damaging because it preserves the employee's professional reputation and provides transitional financial support, though both experiences are stressful and disruptive.
**Explanation**: Both are stressful but for different reasons. A common error is treating them as equivalent experiences.
**Tags**: redundancy, dismissal, comparison, impact, evaluation

### Question Q-2.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A manufacturing firm is replacing manual assembly lines with automated machines, making 50 workers redundant. Evaluate how the business should handle this change and justify your recommendations.
**Answer**: Recommended approach: 1) Give adequate consultation and notice to affected workers — legally required and maintains trust. 2) Offer voluntary redundancy first to reduce compulsory selections and resentment. 3) Provide retraining for other roles within the business where possible (e.g., machine operation, maintenance). 4) Pay above-statutory redundancy packages to maintain goodwill and reduce legal risk. 5) Offer outplacement support (CV writing, job search help) to departing workers. Justification: fair handling reduces legal risk, protects the employer brand for future recruitment, and maintains morale among the remaining workforce who will be watching how colleagues are treated. The short-term cost of generous redundancy terms is outweighed by long-term benefits to reputation and workforce engagement. Failing to handle the change sensitively risks industrial action, tribunal claims, and loss of trust.
**Explanation**: Handling redundancy well protects the business long-term. A common error is recommending only the minimum legal requirements.
**Tags**: redundancy, evaluation, application, change-management
