# Items: 4.4 — Capacity utilisation and productivity

### Question Q-4.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is capacity utilisation?
**Answer**: Capacity utilisation measures how much of a business's maximum possible output is actually being produced. It is expressed as a percentage: (actual output ÷ maximum possible output) × 100. A business operating at 100% capacity is producing at full potential.
**Explanation**: Capacity utilisation shows whether resources are being used efficiently. A common error is confusing capacity utilisation with productivity — utilisation is about volume relative to potential; productivity is about output per unit of input.
**Tags**: capacity-utilisation, definition, calculation

### Question Q-4.4-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is labour productivity? How is it calculated?
**Answer**: Labour productivity measures the output produced per worker (or per hour worked). It is calculated as: total output ÷ number of workers (or total hours worked). For example, if a factory produces 1,000 units per day with 50 workers, labour productivity is 20 units per worker per day.
**Explanation**: Higher productivity means more output from the same input, which lowers average cost. A common error is confusing productivity with production — production is total output; productivity is output per unit of input.
**Tags**: labour-productivity, definition, calculation

### Question Q-4.4-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe three ways a business can increase its capacity utilisation.
**Answer**: 1) Increase demand through marketing, sales promotions, or entering new markets. 2) Reduce capacity by closing underused facilities or selling idle equipment — raising the percentage by lowering the denominator. 3) Offer discounts or special deals to fill unused capacity (e.g., off-peak pricing for hotels). 4) Diversify the product range to use existing capacity for different products. 5) Accept contract work from other businesses to fill spare capacity.
**Explanation**: Both raising output and lowering capacity can improve the percentage. A common error is only considering ways to increase demand — reducing capacity is also a valid strategy.
**Tags**: capacity-utilisation, improvement, strategies

### Question Q-4.4-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe four ways a business can improve labour productivity.
**Answer**: 1) Invest in training to improve workers' skills and efficiency. 2) Introduce better technology or machinery to speed up production. 3) Improve motivation through financial rewards (bonuses, piece-rate pay) or non-financial methods (teamwork, job enrichment). 4) Improve the organisation and management of work — better layout, clearer targets, reduced idle time. 5) Hire more skilled or experienced workers.
**Explanation**: Productivity improvements come from people, technology, and processes. A common error is assuming productivity gains only come from working harder — better methods and tools are often more effective.
**Tags**: labour-productivity, improvement, methods

### Question Q-4.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain the relationship between capacity utilisation and unit costs.
**Answer**: When capacity utilisation increases, fixed costs are spread over a larger number of units, reducing the average cost per unit. This is one source of economies of scale. For example, a factory with $10,000 in monthly fixed costs producing 1,000 units has $10 fixed cost per unit; at 2,000 units it drops to $5 per unit. However, beyond a certain point, pushing utilisation towards 100% can increase costs — overtime pay, machine wear, and bottlenecks may offset the benefit.
**Explanation**: The relationship is not linear at very high utilisation levels. A common error is assuming that higher utilisation always lowers costs without limit.
**Tags**: capacity-utilisation, unit-costs, economies-of-scale

### Question Q-4.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Calculate the capacity utilisation and labour productivity for a factory that has a maximum capacity of 5,000 units per week, currently produces 3,500 units, and employs 70 workers.
**Answer**: Capacity utilisation = (3,500 ÷ 5,000) × 100 = 70%. Labour productivity = 3,500 ÷ 70 = 50 units per worker per week. The factory has 30% unused capacity, meaning fixed costs are spread over fewer units than possible. If demand allows, increasing output would lower the average cost per unit.
**Explanation**: Always show the formula and working. A common error is swapping the numerator and denominator in the capacity utilisation formula — actual output always goes on top.
**Tags**: capacity-utilisation, labour-productivity, calculation

### Question Q-4.4-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether a business should always aim for 100% capacity utilisation.
**Answer**: Not always. Operating at 100% leaves no flexibility for unexpected orders, machine maintenance, or peak seasonal demand. It can cause excessive machine wear, force overtime payments, and increase employee stress and turnover. Most businesses aim for 80-95% utilisation, leaving headroom for maintenance and demand spikes. However, capital-intensive industries (e.g., airlines, hotels) have high fixed costs and need utilisation close to 100% to be profitable. The ideal level depends on the industry, cost structure, and demand variability.
**Explanation**: 100% sounds ideal but creates operational risk. A common error is treating 100% as a universal target without considering maintenance needs and demand fluctuations.
**Tags**: capacity-utilisation, evaluation, operational-efficiency

### Question Q-4.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A car factory operates at 60% capacity utilisation. The management is considering either (a) closing one production line to reduce capacity, or (b) launching an aggressive marketing campaign to increase demand. Recommend and justify which option is better.
**Answer**: Option (a) — closing one line — immediately raises utilisation by reducing capacity, lowering the fixed cost burden. However, it means permanently giving up potential output if demand recovers. Option (b) — increasing demand — is more ambitious but risks failure if the market does not respond, and marketing costs may not be recovered. A phased approach is best: first close the underused line to stabilise costs, then invest in targeted marketing for specific models or markets where the business has a competitive advantage. Justification: reducing capacity is a lower-risk first step; demand growth should be pursued selectively where the business can win.
**Explanation**: Both options change the utilisation percentage through different levers. A common error is treating this as an either-or decision — a phased combination often works best.
**Tags**: capacity-utilisation, evaluation, decision-making, application

### Question Q-4.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the impact of improving capacity utilisation versus improving labour productivity on a manufacturing business's profitability.
**Answer**: Improving capacity utilisation spreads fixed costs over more units, reducing average cost per unit and increasing the margin on each sale. It requires sufficient demand to absorb the extra output. Improving labour productivity reduces the variable cost per unit — each worker produces more, so labour cost per unit falls. This benefits profitability regardless of demand levels. Together, they are powerful: higher utilisation ensures output is sold, and higher productivity ensures each unit costs less to make. However, if demand is weak, improving utilisation may lead to unsold inventory, making productivity improvements the safer first priority.
**Explanation**: Both reduce costs but through different mechanisms. A common error is treating them as the same thing — utilisation addresses fixed cost absorption; productivity addresses labour efficiency.
**Tags**: capacity-utilisation, labour-productivity, profitability, comparison
