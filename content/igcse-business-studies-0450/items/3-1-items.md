# Items: 3.1 — Market research

### Question Q-3.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define 'market research'.
**Answer**: Market research is the process of collecting and analysing information about customers, competitors and the market to help a business make better decisions.
**Explanation**: Market research reduces risk in decision-making. A common error is confusing market research with marketing — research gathers information, marketing acts on it.
**Tags**: market-research, definition

### Question Q-3.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is primary research? Give two examples.
**Answer**: Primary (or field) research is data collected first-hand directly from the market. Examples: questionnaires/surveys, interviews, focus groups, observation, test marketing.
**Explanation**: Primary research is specific and up-to-date but more expensive. A common error is listing 'internet research' as primary — that is usually secondary.
**Tags**: primary-research, definition, examples

### Question Q-3.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Compare primary and secondary research.
**Answer**: Primary research is collected first-hand, specific to the business's needs, and up-to-date, but is expensive and time-consuming. Secondary research uses existing data, is cheaper and faster, but may be outdated or not specific enough. Most businesses use both.
**Explanation**: The choice depends on budget and urgency. A common error is claiming one is always better.
**Tags**: primary-research, secondary-research, comparison

### Question Q-3.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a questionnaire? Give one advantage and one disadvantage.
**Answer**: A questionnaire is a written set of questions given to a sample of people to gather their opinions. Advantage: can collect a lot of data quickly and cheaply, especially online. Disadvantage: response rates can be low, and questions may be misunderstood without clarification.
**Explanation**: Questionnaires are the most common primary research method. A common error is claiming questionnaires always give reliable results — biased questions ruin data quality.
**Tags**: questionnaire, advantage, disadvantage

### Question Q-3.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a sample? Why is it important that a sample is representative?
**Answer**: A sample is a subset of the total population used in research. A representative sample accurately reflects the characteristics of the whole population. If the sample is biased, the results will not be reliable for the whole market.
**Explanation**: Sample quality determines data quality. A common error is thinking larger samples always mean better results — a large but biased sample is still unreliable.
**Tags**: sample, representative, bias

### Question Q-3.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is test marketing? Give one advantage and one disadvantage.
**Answer**: Test marketing is launching a product in a limited area before a full national launch to gauge customer response. Advantage: identifies problems at lower cost before committing to a full launch. Disadvantage: competitors can observe the test and copy or disrupt the launch.
**Explanation**: Test marketing reduces launch risk. A common error is confusing test marketing with a product launch — test marketing is a trial, not the full rollout.
**Tags**: test-marketing, advantage, disadvantage

### Question Q-3.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Analyse the differences between quantitative and qualitative research data and explain when each type is most useful.
**Answer**: Quantitative data is numerical — it can be measured and analysed statistically (e.g., '65% of respondents prefer product A'). Qualitative data is descriptive — it explores opinions, feelings and motivations (e.g., interview transcripts). Quantitative data shows 'what' is happening and is most useful when a business needs measurable evidence to support decisions, such as estimating market size. Qualitative data explains 'why' and is most useful when a business needs to understand customer motivations, such as during product development. Using only one type risks an incomplete picture.
**Explanation**: Both types complement each other. A common error is treating qualitative data as 'less useful' — it provides depth that numbers alone cannot.
**Tags**: quantitative-data, qualitative-data, analysis

### Question Q-3.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate why a business might use a mixed-method approach to market research.
**Answer**: Using both primary and secondary, and both quantitative and qualitative methods, gives a more complete picture. Secondary research provides background cheaply; primary research fills specific gaps. Quantitative data identifies trends; qualitative data explains them. The cost is higher but the risk of bad decisions is reduced.
**Explanation**: Mixed methods reduce blind spots. A common error is listing methods without explaining why combining them is valuable.
**Tags**: market-research, evaluation, mixed-methods

### Question Q-3.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A small bakery wants to launch a new range of gluten-free cakes. Recommend a market research plan and justify your choices.
**Answer**: 1) Secondary: research industry reports on gluten-free food trends and market size — cheap starting point. 2) Primary qualitative: run a focus group with existing customers to understand preferences — deep insights. 3) Primary quantitative: distribute an online questionnaire to a sample of local residents to measure demand — cost-effective numeric data. 4) Test marketing: sell a small batch at the bakery to observe real purchase behaviour. Justification: a small business needs low-cost methods first, then validation through actual sales.
**Explanation**: Recommendations should be proportionate to business size and budget. A common error is recommending expensive methods (e.g., large-scale surveys) for a small business.
**Tags**: market-research, evaluation, application, small-business

### Question Q-3.1-11
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how market research can affect business performance.
**Answer**: Market Research affects business performance by influencing key operational decisions, resource allocation, and strategic planning. Businesses must consider these factors when making day-to-day and long-term decisions. The impact varies depending on the size of the business, the market it operates in, and the competitive environment.
**Explanation**: This tests application and analysis skills. A common error is describing rather than explaining the link between market research and business performance.
**Tags**: market-research, application, explanation

### Question Q-3.1-12
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the importance of market research for a business operating in a competitive market.
**Answer**: Market Research is important because it enables businesses to make informed decisions, respond to market changes, and maintain competitive advantage. In a competitive market, businesses that effectively manage market research are better positioned to increase market share and profitability. However, its importance depends on other factors such as the business's financial resources, the quality of management, and external economic conditions. Some businesses may succeed despite weaknesses in this area if they excel in other aspects such as marketing or innovation. Therefore, while market research is significant, it should be considered alongside other business functions rather than in isolation.
**Explanation**: This tests evaluation skills requiring balanced argument. A common error is giving a one-sided answer without considering limitations or alternative viewpoints.
**Tags**: market-research, evaluation, challenge
