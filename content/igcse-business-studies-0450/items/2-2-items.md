# Items: 2.2 — Motivation and management


### Question Q-2.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is meant by 'motivation' in a business context?
**Answer**: Motivation is the internal and external factors that stimulate desire and energy in people to be continually interested, committed and engaged in their work.
**Explanation**: Motivation drives effort and persistence. A common error is confusing motivation with satisfaction — satisfied employees are not necessarily motivated.
**Tags**: motivation, definition

### Question Q-2.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define 'financial reward'. Give two examples.
**Answer**: A financial reward is a monetary benefit given to employees. Examples: wages/salary, bonus, commission, profit sharing, share options, fringe benefits.
**Explanation**: Financial rewards directly satisfy economic needs. A common error is listing non-financial rewards such as praise or promotion.
**Tags**: financial-reward, definition, examples

### Question Q-2.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe two advantages of using performance-related pay to motivate employees.
**Answer**: 1) It links pay directly to effort, encouraging employees to work harder and be more productive. 2) It helps retain high performers who can earn more, reducing turnover of the best staff.
**Explanation**: Performance-related pay works best when output is measurable. A common error is claiming it suits all jobs — creative or team-based roles may not benefit.
**Tags**: performance-related-pay, advantages, motivation

### Question Q-2.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how job rotation can motivate employees.
**Answer**: Job rotation involves moving employees between different tasks at regular intervals. It reduces boredom and monotony, broadens skills, and gives employees a wider understanding of the business, which can increase engagement.
**Explanation**: Rotation works best for routine or repetitive roles. A common error is confusing job rotation with job enlargement — rotation moves between jobs, enlargement adds more tasks to the same job.
**Tags**: job-rotation, motivation, methods

### Question Q-2.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is job enrichment? How does it differ from job enlargement?
**Answer**: Job enrichment gives employees more control, responsibility and decision-making over their work (vertical expansion). Job enlargement gives employees a greater variety of similar tasks at the same level (horizontal expansion).
**Explanation**: Enrichment adds depth; enlargement adds breadth. A common error is treating them as the same thing.
**Tags**: job-enrichment, job-enlargement, distinction

### Question Q-2.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe Taylor's view of what motivates workers.
**Answer**: Taylor believed workers are primarily motivated by money. He advocated piece-rate pay — paying workers per unit produced — to maximize output. He viewed workers as economically driven and needing close supervision.
**Explanation**: Taylor's theory is useful in manufacturing but ignores social and psychological needs. A common error is attributing hierarchy-of-needs theory to Taylor.
**Tags**: taylor, scientific-management, financial-motivation

### Question Q-2.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare Herzberg's two-factor theory with Maslow's hierarchy of needs. Which theory provides more practical guidance for managers?
**Answer**: Herzberg identifies hygiene factors (pay, conditions, policies — prevent dissatisfaction) and motivators (recognition, responsibility, achievement — create satisfaction). Maslow's hierarchy has five sequential levels from physiological to self-actualisation. Both recognise money alone does not sustain motivation. Herzberg is more workplace-specific and directly distinguishes what prevents dissatisfaction from what creates motivation, making it more actionable for managers designing reward systems. However, Maslow's framework helps managers understand the broader range of employee needs. For practical management, Herzberg offers clearer guidance on job design, while Maslow helps with understanding individual employee circumstances.
**Explanation**: Herzberg's theory is more workplace-specific. A common error is treating hygiene factors as motivators — they only prevent dissatisfaction, they do not create it.
**Tags**: herzberg, maslow, comparison, motivation-theory, evaluation

### Question Q-2.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether financial or non-financial rewards are more effective for motivating employees in a manufacturing business.
**Answer**: Financial rewards (piece-rate, bonuses) may work short-term and for routine tasks common in manufacturing, providing clear incentives for higher output. However, research shows non-financial rewards (recognition, autonomy, job enrichment) sustain motivation longer and reduce absenteeism and turnover. In manufacturing, workers on repetitive tasks may value financial rewards more initially, but over time job rotation, team working and involvement in decision-making can improve engagement more cost-effectively. A combination of both is usually most effective: competitive base pay to meet basic needs, supplemented by non-financial strategies to address higher-level motivation.
**Explanation**: Effectiveness depends on context — employee type, culture, and job nature. A common error is claiming one type is universally superior.
**Tags**: financial-reward, non-financial-reward, evaluation, manufacturing

### Question Q-2.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A small software company has high staff turnover despite paying above-average salaries. Analyse the likely causes and recommend two motivation strategies, justifying your choices.
**Answer**: Likely causes: software developers often seek more than money — they value autonomy, creative freedom, learning opportunities and meaningful work. High pay alone does not address Herzberg's motivators (achievement, recognition, responsibility). Recommended strategies: 1) Job enrichment and empowerment — give developers ownership of projects, allow choice of technologies and involvement in product decisions. This addresses higher-level needs and intrinsic motivation. 2) Professional development — fund training, conference attendance and career progression paths. This addresses self-actualisation needs and signals long-term investment in employees. Justification: tech workers are skilled professionals who respond more to intrinsic motivators; financial rewards are hygiene factors that prevent dissatisfaction but do not create lasting motivation.
**Explanation**: Recommendations must be justified against the workforce type. A common error is recommending only one type of reward without considering the context.
**Tags**: empowerment, motivation, evaluation, application, herzberg
