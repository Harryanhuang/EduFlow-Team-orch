# Items: 2.3 — Recruitment, selection and training of employees


### Question Q-2.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define 'recruitment' in a business context.
**Answer**: Recruitment is the process of finding and attracting suitable people to fill job vacancies in a business.
**Explanation**: Recruitment is the first stage in employment. A common error is confusing recruitment with selection — recruitment attracts candidates, selection chooses among them.
**Tags**: recruitment, definition

### Question Q-2.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a job description?
**Answer**: A job description is a document that outlines the main features and responsibilities of a job, including the tasks to be performed, working conditions, and to whom the employee reports.
**Explanation**: It helps candidates understand what the job involves. A common error is confusing job description with person specification — the former describes the job, the latter describes the ideal person.
**Tags**: job-description, definition

### Question Q-2.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Distinguish between internal and external recruitment. Give one advantage of each.
**Answer**: Internal recruitment fills vacancies with existing employees (e.g., promotion, transfer). Advantage: cheaper, faster, and motivates current staff. External recruitment hires from outside the business. Advantage: brings new skills, ideas and experience into the business.
**Explanation**: Each method suits different situations. A common error is claiming internal recruitment brings 'new ideas' — that is an external recruitment advantage.
**Tags**: internal-recruitment, external-recruitment, distinction

### Question Q-2.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe three methods a business can use to recruit externally.
**Answer**: 1) Advertising in newspapers, magazines or online job boards. 2) Using recruitment agencies or headhunters. 3) Recruiting through social media and professional networks (e.g., LinkedIn). Other methods include job fairs, school or university partnerships.
**Explanation**: Online recruitment is increasingly popular. A common error is listing 'promotion' as an external method — promotion is internal.
**Tags**: external-recruitment, methods

### Question Q-2.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is an interview? Give one advantage and one disadvantage as a selection method.
**Answer**: An interview is a face-to-face (or virtual) meeting where the employer asks questions to assess a candidate's suitability. Advantage: allows direct assessment of communication skills and personality. Disadvantage: can be subjective — the interviewer may be influenced by first impressions rather than actual ability.
**Explanation**: Interviews are the most common selection method. A common error is claiming interviews are always objective.
**Tags**: interview, selection, advantage, disadvantage

### Question Q-2.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why training is important for a business.
**Answer**: Training develops employee skills, knowledge and competence. Benefits: higher productivity and quality, fewer accidents, improved employee motivation and retention, easier adoption of new technology, and a stronger employer brand for recruitment.
**Explanation**: Training is an investment, not just a cost. A common error is listing only one benefit — exam answers should give multiple linked points.
**Tags**: training, importance, benefits

### Question Q-2.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare on-the-job and off-the-job training. Which approach is more suitable for a business introducing new technology?
**Answer**: On-the-job training takes place at the workplace while doing the actual job (e.g., coaching, job rotation). It is cheaper and job-specific but may pass on bad habits. Off-the-job training takes place away from work (e.g., college course, external workshop). It brings fresh ideas and expert trainers but is more expensive and may not be job-specific. For new technology, off-the-job training may be more suitable initially because external specialists can teach the technology properly without the pressure of daily production targets. However, follow-up on-the-job coaching is valuable to embed learning. A blended approach is usually most effective, combining expert instruction with practical workplace application.
**Explanation**: Many businesses use a blend of both. A common error is claiming one is always better — the choice depends on the job and budget.
**Tags**: on-the-job-training, off-the-job-training, comparison, evaluation

### Question Q-2.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the costs and benefits of recruitment for a small business expanding from 10 to 25 employees.
**Answer**: Costs include advertising, agency fees, management time for interviews, onboarding and training new staff. For a small business, these costs are proportionally higher due to limited HR resources and the owner-manager's time being diverted from operations. Benefits include filling skill gaps needed for expansion, increasing capacity to serve more customers, bringing in fresh ideas, and reducing workload on existing staff. The key risk is poor recruitment leading to early leavers and wasted investment. To minimise costs, the business should use cost-effective methods (social media, word-of-mouth), write clear job descriptions and person specifications, and invest in thorough induction training. On balance, the benefits of well-planned recruitment outweigh the costs, as failure to recruit would limit growth.
**Explanation**: Cost-benefit analysis depends on business size and urgency. A common error is listing only costs without linking to benefits.
**Tags**: recruitment, costs, benefits, evaluation, small-business

### Question Q-2.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A retail company has high staff turnover in its stores. Analyse the possible causes and recommend how to improve recruitment and retention, justifying your choices.
**Answer**: Possible causes: unrealistic job expectations, poor induction, lack of training and career progression, low pay relative to competitors, or poor management. Recommendations: 1) Use realistic job previews in recruitment advertising to set accurate expectations and reduce early leavers — this addresses job-fit issues before they arise. 2) Improve induction training so new staff feel supported from day one — research shows most leavers occur in the first 90 days. 3) Invest in ongoing on-the-job training and clear career progression paths to increase motivation — employees who see a future stay longer. 4) Consider internal recruitment for management roles to motivate existing staff. Justification: high turnover often stems from poor job-fit expectations and lack of development, not just pay. Addressing the full employee lifecycle is more effective than focusing on recruitment alone.
**Explanation**: Recommendations should address both recruitment and retention stages. A common error is recommending only recruitment fixes without addressing why people leave.
**Tags**: recruitment, retention, evaluation, application
