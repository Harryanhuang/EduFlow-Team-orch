# Items: 3.3 — The marketing mix: promotion and place

### Question Q-3.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is meant by 'promotion' in the marketing mix?
**Answer**: Promotion refers to the methods a business uses to communicate with and inform customers about its products. The goal is to increase sales by raising awareness, persuading customers, and reinforcing brand image.
**Explanation**: Promotion is about communication, not just advertising. A common error is confusing promotion with the entire marketing function.
**Tags**: promotion, definition, marketing-mix

### Question Q-3.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is meant by 'place' in the marketing mix?
**Answer**: Place refers to how a product is distributed and made available to customers — the channels, locations, and methods used to get the product from the producer to the end consumer.
**Explanation**: Place is about accessibility. A common error is thinking place only means physical location — it includes online channels, retailers, and wholesalers.
**Tags**: place, distribution, definition

### Question Q-3.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Distinguish between above the line and below the line promotion.
**Answer**: Above the line promotion uses paid media where the business has no direct control over content (e.g., TV ads, billboards). Below the line promotion uses methods the business fully controls (e.g., sales promotion, direct mail, social media, loyalty schemes).
**Explanation**: The distinction is about control and cost. A common error is thinking 'above the line' means higher quality.
**Tags**: above-the-line, below-the-line, distinction

### Question Q-3.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a distribution channel? Describe two channels.
**Answer**: A distribution channel is the route a product takes from producer to consumer. Channels include: 1) Producer to Consumer (direct selling, online). 2) Producer to Retailer to Consumer (shorter channel). 3) Producer to Wholesaler to Retailer to Consumer (longest channel).
**Explanation**: Channel choice affects cost and control. A common error is thinking longer channels are always worse — they can reach more customers.
**Tags**: distribution-channel, methods

### Question Q-3.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What are the advantages and disadvantages of selling directly to consumers?
**Answer**: Advantages: full control over price and brand, direct customer feedback, no intermediary margins, faster response to market changes. Disadvantages: higher distribution costs, limited geographic reach, the business must handle all logistics and customer service.
**Explanation**: Direct selling suits businesses with strong online presence. A common error is ignoring the cost burden of handling all distribution.
**Tags**: direct-selling, advantage, disadvantage

### Question Q-3.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is personal selling? When is it most effective?
**Answer**: Personal selling is face-to-face or direct interaction between a salesperson and a customer to persuade a purchase. It is most effective for complex, expensive, or customised products where the customer needs detailed information and the salesperson can address specific concerns.
**Explanation**: Personal selling is expensive but high-impact. A common error is using it for low-value products where advertising would be more cost-effective.
**Tags**: personal-selling, application, promotion

### Question Q-3.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the role of digital marketing compared to traditional advertising for a small business.
**Answer**: Digital marketing (social media, search engines, email) offers precise targeting, measurable results, lower cost per reach, and two-way interaction. Traditional advertising (TV, print, billboards) offers broader reach and greater perceived credibility. Digital is better for small budgets and niche audiences; traditional still dominates for mass-market brand building. The most effective campaigns often combine both.
**Explanation**: Digital is not universally superior. A common error is dismissing traditional media entirely.
**Tags**: digital-marketing, traditional-advertising, evaluation

### Question Q-3.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Analyse the factors a business should consider when choosing its distribution channels.
**Answer**: Factors include: 1) Product type — perishable goods need short channels. 2) Cost — intermediaries add margin but reduce the business's distribution costs. 3) Customer expectations — where do target customers shop? 4) Competition — do rivals use certain channels effectively? 5) Business size — small businesses may lack resources for direct distribution.
**Explanation**: Channel choice is strategic, not just logistical. A common error is choosing channels based only on cost.
**Tags**: distribution-channel, selection-factors, strategy

### Question Q-3.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A new energy drink is launching in a crowded market dominated by Red Bull and Monster. Recommend a promotion and distribution strategy and justify your choices.
**Answer**: Promotion: use digital and social media marketing targeting young, active consumers — influencer partnerships, event sponsorship (sports, music festivals), and below-the-line promotions (free samples at gyms and events). Avoid expensive TV ads initially. Distribution: start with intensive distribution through convenience stores, supermarkets, and online platforms to maximise availability. Justification: in a crowded market, targeted digital promotion and wide availability are more cost-effective than mass advertising.
**Explanation**: Recommendations must address both promotion and distribution. A common error is focusing only on promotion and ignoring how customers will find the product.
**Tags**: promotion, distribution, evaluation, application, competitive-strategy
