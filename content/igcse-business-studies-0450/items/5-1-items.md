# Items: 5.1 — Costs, revenue, and profit

### Question Q-5.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a fixed cost? Give two examples.
**Answer**: A fixed cost is a cost that does not change with the level of output in the short term. It is incurred even if the business produces nothing. Examples include rent, salaries of permanent staff, insurance premiums, and loan repayments.
**Explanation**: Fixed costs are time-related, not output-related. A common error is thinking fixed costs never change — they can change for other reasons (e.g., a rent increase) but not because output changes.
**Tags**: fixed-costs, definition, examples

### Question Q-5.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a variable cost? Give two examples.
**Answer**: A variable cost is a cost that changes directly with the level of output. As production increases, total variable costs increase. Examples include raw materials, direct labour (if paid per unit), packaging, and sales commissions.
**Explanation**: Variable costs are output-related. A common error is confusing total variable cost with variable cost per unit — the total rises with output, but the cost per unit often stays constant.
**Tags**: variable-costs, definition, examples

### Question Q-5.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is contribution? How is it calculated and why is it useful?
**Answer**: Contribution is the amount of revenue remaining after deducting variable costs. It contributes towards covering fixed costs and then generating profit. Per unit: contribution = selling price − variable cost per unit. In total: total contribution = total revenue − total variable costs. It is useful for break-even analysis, pricing decisions, and determining which products are most profitable.
**Explanation**: Contribution comes before profit — fixed costs must still be deducted. A common error is treating contribution as the same as profit.
**Tags**: contribution, calculation, break-even

### Question Q-5.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Distinguish between gross profit and net profit.
**Answer**: Gross profit = revenue − cost of sales (direct production costs such as raw materials and direct labour). It measures how efficiently a business produces its goods. Net profit = gross profit − all other expenses (overheads, administrative costs, interest, tax). It measures overall profitability after all costs. Gross profit is always higher than or equal to net profit.
**Explanation**: The gap between gross and net profit shows how much overhead the business carries. A common error is including overheads in the cost of sales when calculating gross profit.
**Tags**: gross-profit, net-profit, distinction

### Question Q-5.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business has fixed costs of $12,000 per month, variable cost per unit of $5, and sells each unit for $15. Calculate total cost, total revenue, contribution per unit, and profit at a production level of 1,500 units per month.
**Answer**: Total cost = $12,000 + ($5 × 1,500) = $12,000 + $7,500 = $19,500. Total revenue = $15 × 1,500 = $22,500. Contribution per unit = $15 − $5 = $10. Total contribution = $10 × 1,500 = $15,000. Profit = total revenue − total cost = $22,500 − $19,500 = $3,000 (or profit = total contribution − fixed costs = $15,000 − $12,000 = $3,000).
**Explanation**: Both profit formulas should give the same result — use this as a check. A common error is forgetting to multiply variable cost per unit by the number of units.
**Tags**: costs, revenue, profit, contribution, calculation

### Question Q-5.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is profit margin? Calculate the profit margin for a business with revenue of $500,000 and net profit of $75,000.
**Answer**: Profit margin expresses net profit as a percentage of revenue: (net profit ÷ revenue) × 100. For this business: ($75,000 ÷ $500,000) × 100 = 15%. This means the business keeps $0.15 of profit from every $1 of revenue. A higher margin indicates greater profitability and cost efficiency.
**Explanation**: Profit margin allows comparison between businesses of different sizes. A common error is calculating (revenue ÷ profit) or confusing margin with markup.
**Tags**: profit-margin, calculation, percentage

### Question Q-5.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain how a business can improve its profit margin without increasing sales volume.
**Answer**: 1) Reduce variable costs — negotiate cheaper raw materials, improve production efficiency, or switch to lower-cost suppliers. 2) Reduce fixed costs — renegotiate rent, automate administrative tasks, or outsource non-core functions. 3) Increase selling price — if demand is inelastic, a price rise boosts revenue without needing more sales. 4) Change product mix — focus on higher-margin products and discontinue low-margin ones. 5) Reduce waste and defects to lower the cost per unit. Each approach has risks — cost-cutting may affect quality, and price increases may reduce demand.
**Explanation**: Profit margin improvement comes from cost control or pricing power. A common error is suggesting 'sell more' when the question specifically excludes volume increases.
**Tags**: profit, margin, improvement, cost-reduction

### Question Q-5.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business is deciding whether to accept a special order at a price below its normal selling price. Using the concept of contribution, explain when this would be financially worthwhile.
**Answer**: Accepting a lower-priced order is worthwhile if the order price exceeds the variable cost per unit — meaning it generates positive contribution. Since fixed costs are already covered by normal sales, any additional contribution flows directly to profit. For example, if variable cost is $8 per unit and the special order offers $10 per unit, each unit contributes $2 to profit. However, conditions apply: 1) The business must have spare capacity. 2) The order must not cannibalise normal-priced sales. 3) It should not set a price precedent that customers expect regularly.
**Explanation**: Contribution analysis separates short-term decisions from long-term pricing strategy. A common error is rejecting the order because the price is below total cost per unit, ignoring that fixed costs are already covered.
**Tags**: contribution, special-order, decision-making, evaluation

### Question Q-5.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A small café has high fixed costs (rent in a prime location) and relatively low variable costs per customer. Analyse how changes in customer numbers affect its profitability compared to a café with lower rent but higher ingredient costs per item.
**Answer**: The prime-location café has high operating leverage — once fixed costs are covered, each additional customer adds significant profit because variable costs are low. However, it is more vulnerable to drops in customer numbers since fixed costs remain unchanged. The lower-rent café has lower operating leverage — its profit per customer is smaller due to higher variable costs, but it faces less risk during quiet periods because its fixed costs are lower. The prime-location café benefits more from volume growth but carries higher risk during downturns.
**Explanation**: Operating leverage (the ratio of fixed to variable costs) determines profit sensitivity to volume changes. A common error is only comparing absolute costs without considering the fixed-variable mix.
**Tags**: fixed-costs, variable-costs, operating-leverage, profitability, analysis
