# Items: 3.4 — Marketing strategy and the marketing mix

### Question Q-3.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is market segmentation?
**Answer**: Market segmentation is dividing a market into distinct groups of consumers who have similar needs, characteristics, or behaviours, and who might require a separate marketing mix.
**Explanation**: Segmentation helps businesses target more effectively. A common error is confusing segmentation with targeting — segmentation identifies groups; targeting selects which group(s) to serve.
**Tags**: segmentation, definition, marketing

### Question Q-3.4-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a target market?
**Answer**: A target market is the specific group or segment of consumers that a business aims its marketing efforts and products at.
**Explanation**: A business can have more than one target market. A common error is thinking the target market is 'everyone' — effective targeting requires focus.
**Tags**: target-market, definition, segmentation

### Question Q-3.4-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a marketing strategy? How does it differ from a marketing tactic?
**Answer**: A marketing strategy is a long-term plan for achieving a business's marketing objectives, based on understanding customer needs and competitor activity. A marketing tactic is a short-term, specific action taken to implement the strategy (e.g., running a social media ad this week).
**Explanation**: Strategy sets direction; tactics execute it. A common error is using 'strategy' to describe individual promotional activities.
**Tags**: marketing-strategy, tactics, distinction

### Question Q-3.4-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how a business should adapt its marketing mix during the decline stage of the product life cycle.
**Answer**: During decline, a business may: 1) Reduce or stop promotion spending. 2) Lower price to clear remaining stock. 3) Reduce distribution to only the most profitable outlets. 4) Discontinue the product and replace it. Alternatively, use an extension strategy (repackaging, finding new uses) to delay decline.
**Explanation**: The response depends on whether the business plans to exit or extend. A common error is suggesting increased spending on a declining product.
**Tags**: product-life-cycle, decline, adapting-marketing-mix

### Question Q-3.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why is it important for the four elements of the marketing mix (product, price, promotion, place) to be coordinated?
**Answer**: The 4Ps must be consistent with each other and with the business's overall strategy. A premium product needs a premium price, appropriate promotion, and selective distribution. If one element is misaligned — e.g., a high-quality product sold through low-status outlets at a discount price — the overall marketing message becomes confusing and sales suffer.
**Explanation**: Coordination creates a clear market position. A common error is treating each P independently rather than as an integrated mix.
**Tags**: marketing-mix, coordination, integration

### Question Q-3.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is niche marketing? How does it differ from mass marketing?
**Answer**: Niche marketing focuses on a small, well-defined segment of the market with specific needs. Mass marketing targets the whole market with a single marketing mix. Niche marketing allows higher prices and customer loyalty but limits volume; mass marketing achieves economies of scale but faces more competition and lower margins.
**Explanation**: Niche marketing suits smaller businesses or differentiated products. A common error is assuming niche marketing is always more profitable — it depends on segment size and willingness to pay.
**Tags**: niche-marketing, mass-marketing, distinction

### Question Q-3.4-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the usefulness of the product life cycle model as a planning tool for a marketing manager.
**Answer**: The PLC model helps managers anticipate changes in sales and profit, plan appropriate marketing strategies for each stage, and manage a portfolio of products at different stages. However, it has limitations: not all products follow the classic curve; some products experience renewed growth through repositioning; and the model describes rather than prescribes — it does not tell managers how to act. It is a useful framework but should be combined with market data and competitor analysis.
**Explanation**: The PLC is descriptive, not predictive. A common error is assuming every product will follow the standard curve.
**Tags**: product-life-cycle, evaluation, planning

### Question Q-3.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A clothing retailer has identified two segments: teenagers seeking trendy fashion and professionals seeking smart casual wear. Analyse how it should adapt its marketing mix for each segment.
**Answer**: Teenagers: product — trendy, seasonal items with bold designs; price — lower to match limited budgets; promotion — social media, influencer marketing, student discounts; place — online stores and high-street locations near schools. Professionals: product — classic, higher-quality materials; price — mid to premium reflecting quality; promotion — targeted email campaigns, quality-focused advertising; place — premium shopping centres and quality online shopping experience. The two mixes differ across all 4Ps to match distinct needs.
**Explanation**: Each segment needs a tailored mix. A common error is changing only promotion while leaving product and price the same.
**Tags**: segmentation, target-market, marketing-mix, analysis

### Question Q-3.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A small business is launching an organic energy bar. Recommend a market segmentation approach and a matching marketing mix. Justify your choices.
**Answer**: Segmentation: target health-conscious consumers aged 18-35 with disposable income, segmented by psychographics (active lifestyle, environmental awareness) and behaviour (regular gym-goers). Product: organic, sustainably sourced ingredients, attractive eco-friendly packaging, multiple flavours. Price: premium pricing reflecting quality and target segment's willingness to pay. Promotion: social media fitness influencers, sampling at gyms and health food stores, content marketing about nutrition. Place: health food shops, gym cafeterias, online direct-to-consumer. Justification: a premium, niche-focused approach avoids direct competition with mass-market energy drinks and builds a loyal customer base.
**Explanation**: All 4Ps must align with the chosen segment. A common error is recommending mass-market distribution for a niche product.
**Tags**: segmentation, marketing-mix, evaluation, application, niche-marketing
