### Question Q-1.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a business objective?
**Answer**: A business objective is a goal or target that a business aims to achieve. Examples: survive, make a profit, grow, maximise market share.
**Explanation**: Goal or target. A common error is confusing with a mission statement.
**Tags**: business-objective, definition

### Question Q-1.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a mission statement?
**Answer**: A mission statement is a formal summary of the aims and values of a business. It communicates the business's purpose to stakeholders.
**Explanation**: Statement of purpose. A common error is confusing with objectives (which are specific targets).
**Tags**: mission-statement, definition

### Question Q-1.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why might a new business's first objective be survival rather than profit?
**Answer**: New businesses often face uncertainty, low sales, and cash flow problems. Survival ensures the business stays open long enough to build a customer base and become profitable.
**Explanation**: Survival comes before profit for new businesses. A common error is saying profit is always the first objective.
**Tags**: survival, new-business, objective

### Question Q-1.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How might the objectives of different stakeholders conflict?
**Answer**: Owners want profit; employees want higher wages; customers want lower prices; government wants tax revenue. These objectives can conflict — higher wages reduce profits, lower prices reduce revenue.
**Explanation**: Different groups want different things. A common error is saying stakeholders always agree.
**Tags**: stakeholders, conflict, objectives

### Question Q-1.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How can a business's objectives change over time?
**Answer**: A new business may focus on survival, then shift to profit, then growth. Objectives change due to market conditions, competition, economic climate, and business size.
**Explanation**: Objectives evolve with business lifecycle. A common error is saying objectives never change.
**Tags**: business-objectives, change, lifecycle

### Question Q-1.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How do employees' objectives differ from owners' objectives?
**Answer**: Employees want job security, good wages, and working conditions. Owners want profit, growth, and return on investment. These may conflict — higher wages reduce profits.
**Explanation**: Employees vs owners: different priorities. A common error is saying they always align.
**Tags**: stakeholders, employees, owners, conflict

### Question Q-1.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether profit maximisation should be the main objective of all businesses.
**Answer**: Arguments for: profit is essential for survival, funds growth, rewards owners for risk, and attracts investment. Arguments against: short-term profit maximisation may harm long-term success by cutting quality, employee welfare, or environmental standards. Social enterprises and non-profits prioritise social goals over profit. Many successful businesses balance profit with customer satisfaction, employee welfare, and corporate social responsibility. The importance of profit maximisation depends on the type of business and its stage of development.
**Explanation**: Profit is important but not the only objective. A common error is saying profit is always the only goal or ignoring other legitimate objectives.
**Tags**: profit-maximisation, evaluation, objectives

### Question Q-1.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: To what extent should a business consider the interests of all stakeholders equally when making decisions?
**Answer**: Considering all stakeholders can improve reputation, employee motivation, customer loyalty, and community support — all contributing to long-term success. However, it is impractical to satisfy everyone equally as stakeholder interests often conflict (e.g., higher wages vs higher profits). Owners and shareholders typically have the greatest financial stake and may expect their interests to be prioritised. In practice, businesses must balance stakeholder interests based on their power, influence, and the potential impact on the business. Equal consideration is an ideal but rarely achievable in reality.
**Explanation**: Balanced stakeholder management is important but equal treatment is impractical. A common error is saying businesses should always prioritise one group.
**Tags**: stakeholders, evaluation, decision-making

### Question Q-1.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Analyse whether corporate social responsibility (CSR) always benefits a business.
**Answer**: Benefits of CSR include improved brand image, customer loyalty, employee motivation, and positive relationships with the local community and government — all supporting long-term profitability. However, CSR involves costs such as investment in sustainable materials, community programmes, and environmental measures that may reduce short-term profit. Some CSR activities may not directly benefit the business or may be seen as superficial 'greenwashing'. Small businesses may struggle to afford CSR compared to large multinationals. CSR is most beneficial when it aligns with the business's core activities and values rather than being a superficial add-on.
**Explanation**: CSR has both benefits and costs. A common error is one-sided analysis that only lists benefits or ignores the cost implications.
**Tags**: csr, evaluation, business-benefits
