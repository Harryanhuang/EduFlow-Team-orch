# Items: 3.2 — The marketing mix: product and price

### Question Q-3.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is meant by the 'marketing mix'?
**Answer**: The marketing mix is the combination of four elements — product, price, promotion, and place — that a business uses to satisfy customer needs and wants.
**Explanation**: The 4Ps framework is fundamental to marketing strategy. A common error is adding 'people' or 'process' — those belong to the extended 7Ps model, not the core 4Ps.
**Tags**: marketing-mix, 4ps, definition

### Question Q-3.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define 'product' as an element of the marketing mix.
**Answer**: The product is the good or service being offered to customers. It includes features, design, quality, branding, packaging, and after-sales service.
**Explanation**: Product is more than the physical item — it includes the total customer experience. A common error is only describing the physical good.
**Tags**: product, definition, marketing-mix

### Question Q-3.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe three pricing strategies and when each is appropriate.
**Answer**: 1) Cost-plus: adding a markup to cost — simple, ensures profit, suits small businesses. 2) Penetration: setting a low initial price to gain market share — suits new entrants to competitive markets. 3) Price skimming: setting a high initial price then lowering it — suits innovative or unique products. 4) Competitive: matching rivals' prices — suits oligopoly markets.
**Explanation**: Choice depends on product type, competition, and business objectives. A common error is describing only the strategy without when to use it.
**Tags**: pricing-strategies, application

### Question Q-3.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is product differentiation? Why is it important?
**Answer**: Product differentiation is making a product distinct from competitors' offerings through features, design, quality, branding, or service. It helps a business stand out in the market, reduce price competition, and build customer loyalty.
**Explanation**: Differentiation supports premium pricing. A common error is confusing differentiation with diversification — the former makes existing products unique, the latter enters new markets.
**Tags**: product-differentiation, importance, marketing

### Question Q-3.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a product life cycle? Describe its stages.
**Answer**: The product life cycle describes the stages a product goes through: 1) Development — research and testing, no sales. 2) Introduction — launch, low sales, high costs. 3) Growth — rising sales and profits. 4) Maturity — peak sales, competition increases. 5) Decline — falling sales, product is withdrawn or replaced.
**Explanation**: Not all products go through all stages at the same speed. A common error is confusing product life cycle with business life cycle.
**Tags**: product-life-cycle, stages, marketing

### Question Q-3.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain the role of branding in the product element of the marketing mix.
**Answer**: Branding creates a distinct identity for a product through a name, logo, design, or combination. It helps customers recognise and trust the product, supports premium pricing, builds loyalty, and differentiates from competitors.
**Explanation**: Branding is a long-term investment. A common error is confusing branding with advertising — branding is the identity, advertising is the communication.
**Tags**: branding, product, marketing-mix

### Question Q-3.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate price skimming versus penetration pricing for a new technology product.
**Answer**: Price skimming works when the product is innovative, has few competitors, and targets early adopters willing to pay more — it recovers R&D costs quickly. Penetration pricing works when the market is competitive and price-sensitive — it builds volume fast. The choice depends on competition, product uniqueness, and business cash flow needs. Skimming risks attracting competitors; penetration risks establishing a low-price image that is hard to reverse.
**Explanation**: The best choice depends on market conditions. A common error is claiming one strategy is always superior.
**Tags**: price-skimming, penetration-pricing, evaluation

### Question Q-3.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Analyse how a business should adapt its product strategy when a product reaches the maturity stage of the product life cycle.
**Answer**: At maturity, sales peak and competition intensifies. Strategies include: 1) Product improvements or extensions to refresh interest. 2) Finding new market segments or uses. 3) Reducing costs to maintain margins as prices fall. 4) Planning for replacement with new products. 5) Heavy promotion to defend market share.
**Explanation**: Extension strategies can prolong maturity. A common error is suggesting 'reduce price' as the only response — this triggers price wars.
**Tags**: product-life-cycle, maturity, strategy

### Question Q-3.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A new smartphone brand wants to enter a market dominated by Apple and Samsung. Recommend a product and price strategy and justify your choices.
**Answer**: Product: differentiate through a unique feature (e.g., exceptional camera, long battery life, or superior customer service) rather than competing on specifications alone. Price: use penetration pricing to attract customers away from established brands, or target a niche segment with premium pricing if the differentiation is strong. Justification: competing head-on with Apple and Samsung on brand recognition is unrealistic; differentiation and value pricing offer a viable entry path.
**Explanation**: Recommendations must be realistic for the competitive landscape. A common error is suggesting a 'me-too' approach against dominant players.
**Tags**: product, price, evaluation, application, competitive-strategy
