# Items: 4.1 — Production planning and methods

### Question Q-4.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is job production? Give an example.
**Answer**: Job production is making one complete item at a time from start to finish before starting the next one. Each product is made to a customer's specific requirements. Examples include a custom-made wedding dress, a bridge, or a made-to-order kitchen.
**Explanation**: Job production is highly customised but labour-intensive. A common error is confusing job production with a factory that makes many different products — job production means each individual item is completed before the next begins.
**Tags**: job-production, definition, examples

### Question Q-4.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is flow production? Give an example.
**Answer**: Flow production (also called mass production) is the continuous large-scale production of identical products on an assembly line. Products move from one stage to the next without interruption. Examples include car manufacturing, soft drink bottling, and electronic device assembly.
**Explanation**: Flow production achieves the lowest cost per unit through economies of scale. A common error is thinking flow production means 'fast' — it means continuous and standardised, not necessarily quick to set up.
**Tags**: flow-production, mass-production, definition

### Question Q-4.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Compare the advantages of job production and flow production.
**Answer**: Job production advantages: 1) Each item is customised to customer specifications. 2) Workers often have varied, skilled tasks — higher motivation. 3) Flexible — can change design between jobs. Flow production advantages: 1) Lowest cost per unit due to economies of scale. 2) Consistent quality — standardised processes. 3) Can be automated for speed. 4) No work-in-progress delays — continuous output.
**Explanation**: The best choice depends on volume and variety. A common error is listing only one side's advantages in a comparison question.
**Tags**: job-production, flow-production, comparison, advantage

### Question Q-4.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain the disadvantages of batch production.
**Answer**: 1) Work-in-progress inventory builds up between batches. 2) Equipment must be cleaned and re-set between batches, causing downtime. 3) Per-unit cost is higher than flow production because batches are smaller. 4) Storage space is needed for raw materials and finished batches. 5) Workers may repeat the same tasks within a batch, reducing motivation.
**Explanation**: Batch is a compromise between job and flow. A common error is listing only advantages — batch production carries storage costs and setup delays that flow production avoids.
**Tags**: batch-production, disadvantage, operations

### Question Q-4.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What factors should a business consider when choosing a production method?
**Answer**: 1) Volume of output required — high volume favours flow production. 2) Level of customisation needed — high customisation favours job production. 3) Available capital — flow production needs expensive machinery. 4) Skills of the workforce — job production needs skilled labour. 5) Nature of the product — perishable or complex products may suit specific methods. 6) Market demand stability — predictable demand supports flow production.
**Explanation**: No single method is best. A common error is choosing based only on cost without considering product type and market needs.
**Tags**: production-methods, selection-factors, decision-making

### Question Q-4.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is just-in-time (JIT) production? What are its benefits?
**Answer**: Just-in-time production is a method where raw materials and components are delivered exactly when they are needed in the production process, so the business holds minimal or no inventory. Benefits include: 1) Lower storage and warehousing costs. 2) Less capital tied up in stock. 3) Reduced waste from obsolete or damaged inventory. 4) Encourages closer relationships with reliable suppliers.
**Explanation**: JIT requires reliable suppliers and good planning. A common error is confusing JIT with just-in-case — the latter holds buffer stock as insurance against supply disruption.
**Tags**: just-in-time, JIT, inventory, benefits

### Question Q-4.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether a small bakery should use job, batch, or flow production.
**Answer**: A small bakery is best suited to batch production. It can produce batches of different products (bread, cakes, pastries) to meet varied daily demand without the high capital cost of flow production. Job production would be too slow for the volume needed; flow production would require uniform, very high demand and expensive automated equipment that a small bakery cannot justify. Batch allows flexibility in product range and reasonable cost per unit. However, the bakery could offer a small job-production element (custom cakes) as a premium service alongside its main batch output.
**Explanation**: The context matters — scale, product variety, and capital all influence the choice. A common error is recommending flow production without considering the capital and volume requirements.
**Tags**: production-methods, evaluation, application, bakery

### Question Q-4.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Analyse the risks of just-in-time production and evaluate how a business can reduce them.
**Answer**: Risks: 1) A single supplier delay can halt the entire production line. 2) No buffer stock to handle unexpected demand spikes. 3) Heavy dependence on supplier reliability and quality. 4) Vulnerable to external disruptions (weather, strikes, transport issues). Mitigation: 1) Use multiple suppliers for critical materials. 2) Maintain strong communication and shared IT systems with suppliers. 3) Keep a small safety stock of the most critical components. 4) Have contingency transport arrangements. Evaluation: JIT trades inventory cost for supply chain risk — businesses must assess whether their suppliers are reliable enough before adopting JIT, as the cost of a production halt far exceeds the savings from lower stock levels.
**Explanation**: JIT trades inventory cost for supply chain risk. A common error is presenting JIT as purely advantageous without acknowledging its vulnerability to disruption.
**Tags**: just-in-time, risks, mitigation, supply-chain, evaluation

### Question Q-4.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A car manufacturer currently uses flow production. It is considering introducing a custom-build option for customers who want personalised specifications. Recommend how it should adapt its production approach. Justify your answer.
**Answer**: The manufacturer should adopt a hybrid approach. Keep flow production for standard models to maintain low costs and high volume. For custom-build orders, use flexible manufacturing systems (CAM-controlled robots that can switch between specifications on the same line) or set up a separate job-production cell for bespoke orders. This allows the business to capture the premium custom market without disrupting the efficiency of its main flow line. Justification: maintaining the flow line protects margins on the core business, while a custom option captures higher-margin niche demand.
**Explanation**: The solution must protect existing efficiency. A common error is suggesting the entire line switch to batch or job production, which would destroy the economies of scale.
**Tags**: flow-production, customisation, evaluation, application, technology
