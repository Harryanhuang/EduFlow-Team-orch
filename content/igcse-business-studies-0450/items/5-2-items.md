# Items: 5.2 — Break-even analysis

### Question Q-5.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the break-even point?
**Answer**: The break-even point is the level of output (or sales revenue) at which a business's total revenue equals its total costs — meaning it makes neither a profit nor a loss. Below this point, the business makes a loss; above it, the business makes a profit.
**Explanation**: Break-even is a critical planning tool. A common error is thinking break-even means zero revenue — it means zero profit.
**Tags**: break-even, definition, profit-loss

### Question Q-5.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: How is the break-even point calculated using the formula method?
**Answer**: Break-even point (in units) = total fixed costs ÷ contribution per unit, where contribution per unit = selling price − variable cost per unit. For example, if fixed costs are $20,000, selling price is $50, and variable cost is $30: contribution per unit = $20; break-even = $20,000 ÷ $20 = 1,000 units.
**Explanation**: The formula shows how many units must be sold to cover all costs. A common error is dividing fixed costs by selling price instead of contribution.
**Tags**: break-even, calculation, formula

### Question Q-5.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a break-even chart? Label and describe its key components.
**Answer**: A break-even chart plots costs and revenue against output. Key components: 1) Fixed cost line — horizontal, as fixed costs do not change with output. 2) Total cost line — starts at the fixed cost level and rises with output (fixed + variable). 3) Total revenue line — starts at zero and rises with output. 4) Break-even point — where total revenue and total cost lines cross. 5) Margin of safety — the gap between actual/budgeted output and the break-even point. The area above break-even is profit; below is loss.
**Explanation**: The chart visualises the relationship between costs, revenue, and output. A common error is drawing the total cost line starting from zero — it starts from the fixed cost level.
**Tags**: break-even, chart, margin-of-safety

### Question Q-5.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business has fixed costs of $30,000, sells each unit for $25, and has a variable cost of $10 per unit. Calculate the break-even point and the margin of safety if it expects to sell 3,000 units.
**Answer**: Contribution per unit = $25 − $10 = $15. Break-even = $30,000 ÷ $15 = 2,000 units. Margin of safety = expected sales − break-even = 3,000 − 2,000 = 1,000 units (or 33% of expected sales). This means sales could fall by 1,000 units before the business starts making a loss.
**Explanation**: Margin of safety measures the cushion above break-even. A common error is confusing margin of safety with break-even point itself.
**Tags**: break-even, margin-of-safety, calculation

### Question Q-5.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Calculate how many units a business must sell to achieve a target profit of $10,000, given fixed costs of $40,000 and contribution per unit of $8.
**Answer**: Units required = (fixed costs + target profit) ÷ contribution per unit = ($40,000 + $10,000) ÷ $8 = $50,000 ÷ $8 = 6,250 units. At break-even the business would need $40,000 ÷ $8 = 5,000 units. The additional 1,250 units generate the $10,000 target profit ($10,000 ÷ $8 = 1,250).
**Explanation**: Target profit calculations treat the desired profit as an additional fixed cost to be covered. A common error is forgetting to add the target profit to fixed costs.
**Tags**: target-profit, break-even, calculation

### Question Q-5.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What are the limitations of break-even analysis?
**Answer**: 1) It assumes all output is sold — no inventory build-up. 2) It assumes variable cost per unit and selling price are constant at all output levels (no bulk discounts or price changes). 3) Fixed costs may change in steps (semi-fixed), not remain perfectly flat. 4) It is a static snapshot — does not account for changing market conditions over time. 5) For multi-product businesses, it requires a constant sales mix assumption. 6) It is only as accurate as the cost and revenue data used.
**Explanation**: Break-even is a simplification of reality. A common error is treating break-even figures as exact predictions rather than planning estimates.
**Tags**: break-even, limitations, assumptions

### Question Q-5.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business currently breaks even at 4,000 units. It is considering reducing its selling price by 10% to boost sales volume. Explain the likely effect on the break-even point and whether this is a good decision.
**Answer**: Reducing the selling price lowers contribution per unit (assuming variable costs stay the same), which raises the break-even point — more units must be sold to cover the same fixed costs. For the decision to be worthwhile, the increase in sales volume must more than offset the lower contribution per unit. If demand is elastic (responsive to price), the extra volume may increase total profit. If demand is inelastic, the business will sell slightly more but at a lower margin, potentially reducing profit. The decision depends on the price elasticity of demand for the product.
**Explanation**: Price changes affect contribution, which shifts break-even. A common error is assuming lower prices always increase profit through higher volume.
**Tags**: break-even, pricing, elasticity, evaluation

### Question Q-5.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare two production methods: Method A has fixed costs of $50,000 and variable cost of $5 per unit; Method B has fixed costs of $20,000 and variable cost of $12 per unit. Both sell at $20 per unit. At what output level does Method A become more profitable? Calculate break-even for each.
**Answer**: Method A: contribution = $20 − $5 = $15. Break-even = $50,000 ÷ $15 = 3,333 units. Method B: contribution = $20 − $12 = $8. Break-even = $20,000 ÷ $8 = 2,500 units. Method A has a higher break-even but higher contribution per unit. The indifference point (where both methods give the same profit): $50,000 + $5Q = $20,000 + $12Q → $30,000 = $7Q → Q = 4,286 units. Above 4,286 units, Method A is more profitable; below, Method B is better.
**Explanation**: The choice depends on expected output volume. A common error is choosing based only on break-even point without considering contribution per unit at expected volumes.
**Tags**: break-even, cost-comparison, calculation, decision-making

### Question Q-5.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the usefulness of break-even analysis for a startup tech company developing a mobile app with very high development costs but near-zero variable costs per download.
**Answer**: Break-even analysis is conceptually useful — it clearly shows how many downloads are needed to recover the high fixed development costs. With near-zero variable costs, contribution per download is almost equal to the selling price, so the break-even formula is straightforward. However, limitations are significant: 1) Revenue per download may vary (freemium model means most users pay nothing). 2) Fixed costs may increase through ongoing updates and marketing. 3) The model cannot easily account for network effects (value increases with more users). Despite limitations, it provides a minimum target for the startup to plan around.
**Explanation**: Break-even works mathematically but may oversimplify digital business models. A common error is ignoring that freemium revenue is highly variable per user.
**Tags**: break-even, startup, digital-business, evaluation
