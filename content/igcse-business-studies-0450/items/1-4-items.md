### Question Q-1.4-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether government should do more to support entrepreneurs.
**Answer**: Arguments for: entrepreneurs create jobs, drive innovation, and stimulate economic growth — government support (grants, training, tax breaks) can amplify these benefits, especially in deprived areas. Arguments against: resources may be wasted on businesses that fail, intervention may distort market forces, and support could create dependency rather than genuine enterprise. Government support is most effective when it addresses specific barriers (e.g., access to finance) rather than providing blanket subsidies. The level of support should reflect the economic benefits entrepreneurs generate.
**Explanation**: Support has benefits but also risks. A common error is one-sided evaluation.
**Tags**: entrepreneur, government-support, evaluation

### Question Q-1.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Discuss whether the personal qualities of an entrepreneur are more important than market conditions in determining business success.
**Answer**: Personal qualities such as determination, creativity, and leadership help entrepreneurs overcome challenges and adapt to changing circumstances — qualities can compensate for difficult market conditions. However, even the most talented entrepreneur may fail in a declining market with no demand, intense competition, or during a severe recession. Market conditions such as growing demand, low competition, and favourable economic policies can make success easier even for less experienced entrepreneurs. Success usually requires both strong personal qualities and favourable market conditions — neither alone is sufficient.
**Explanation**: Both personal qualities and market conditions matter. A common error is attributing success entirely to one factor.
**Tags**: entrepreneur, qualities, market-conditions, evaluation

### Question Q-1.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether social entrepreneurs face greater challenges than traditional entrepreneurs.
**Answer**: Social entrepreneurs face additional challenges: they must balance their social mission with financial sustainability, may struggle to attract investors who seek financial returns, and often operate in sectors with limited profit potential. They may also face difficulty measuring success when social impact is hard to quantify. However, social entrepreneurs may benefit from government grants, public goodwill, and motivated staff who share the social mission — advantages that traditional entrepreneurs may lack. Traditional entrepreneurs face their own challenges including intense competition and pressure to maximise returns. The challenges differ rather than one being clearly greater.
**Explanation**: Different challenges for different types. A common error is assuming one type always has it harder without balanced comparison.
**Tags**: social-entrepreneur, traditional-entrepreneur, comparison, evaluation
