# Items: 4.3 — Quality management

### Question Q-4.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is quality control? How does it work?
**Answer**: Quality control is the process of inspecting and testing products at various stages of production (or the finished product) to identify and remove defective items before they reach customers. It is a reactive approach — finding faults after they have occurred.
**Explanation**: Quality control catches defects but does not prevent them. A common error is confusing quality control with quality assurance — control inspects; assurance prevents.
**Tags**: quality-control, inspection, definition

### Question Q-4.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is quality assurance? How does it differ from quality control?
**Answer**: Quality assurance is a system of processes and procedures designed to prevent defects from occurring in the first place. It focuses on building quality into every stage of production. Unlike quality control, which inspects finished products, quality assurance sets standards, training, and procedures so that mistakes are less likely to happen.
**Explanation**: Assurance is proactive; control is reactive. A common error is using the terms interchangeably — they serve different purposes in quality management.
**Tags**: quality-assurance, quality-control, distinction

### Question Q-4.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is Total Quality Management (TQM)? Describe its key principles.
**Answer**: TQM is a management approach where every employee is committed to maintaining high standards of quality in all aspects of operations. Key principles: 1) Quality is everyone's responsibility — from the shop floor to senior management. 2) Continuous improvement (kaizen) — always seeking to do things better. 3) Customer focus — quality is defined by the customer. 4) Right first time — aim for zero defects. 5) Teamwork and open communication.
**Explanation**: TQM is a cultural shift, not just a set of procedures. A common error is thinking TQM is only about the production department — it involves the entire organisation.
**Tags**: TQM, total-quality-management, kaizen, principles

### Question Q-4.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What are the costs of poor quality to a business?
**Answer**: Costs include: 1) Internal failure costs — waste, scrap, rework, and re-inspection of defective products found before sale. 2) External failure costs — returns, refunds, warranty claims, lost sales, and damage to reputation from defective products reaching customers. 3) Prevention costs — training, quality planning, and process design to avoid defects. 4) Appraisal costs — inspection and testing to detect defects.
**Explanation**: Poor quality affects both internal operations and external reputation. A common error is only counting the visible costs (returns, refunds) and ignoring reputation damage and lost future sales.
**Tags**: quality, costs, failure, prevention

### Question Q-4.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a quality standard such as ISO 9001? Why do businesses seek certification?
**Answer**: ISO 9001 is an internationally recognised quality management standard that specifies requirements for a quality management system. Businesses seek certification because it: 1) Demonstrates commitment to quality to customers and partners. 2) Can be a requirement to bid for certain contracts. 3) Provides a framework for consistent processes. 4) Improves efficiency by reducing errors and waste. 5) Enhances brand reputation and customer confidence.
**Explanation**: Certification is a marketing and operational tool, not just a badge. A common error is thinking certification guarantees product quality — it certifies the management system, not individual products.
**Tags**: ISO-9001, quality-standard, certification

### Question Q-4.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Compare quality control and quality assurance in terms of their approach, advantages, and disadvantages.
**Answer**: Quality control: inspects finished products to find defects. Advantage — catches faults before customers see them. Disadvantage — reactive, wastes resources on defective items, does not prevent recurrence. Quality assurance: builds quality into processes to prevent defects. Advantage — reduces waste and rework long-term, consistent quality. Disadvantage — requires significant investment in training, systems, and cultural change; benefits take time to materialise.
**Explanation**: Both have a role — assurance prevents, control catches. A common error is presenting one as universally superior — in practice, most businesses use both.
**Tags**: quality-control, quality-assurance, comparison

### Question Q-4.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether a small manufacturer should implement a formal quality assurance system or rely on quality control inspection.
**Answer**: For a small manufacturer, the choice depends on product type and customer expectations. Quality control inspection has lower upfront costs and is easier to implement immediately — suitable if defect rates are low and products are simple. However, quality assurance delivers long-term savings through waste reduction and consistent quality, which is critical if the manufacturer supplies larger businesses that demand certification (e.g., ISO 9001). The initial investment in QA (training, documentation, process redesign) may strain a small business, but the competitive advantage and reduced rework often justify it. A hybrid approach — basic QC now, gradual QA adoption — is often the most practical path.
**Explanation**: Scale and resources matter. A common error is recommending full TQM to a small firm without acknowledging the implementation cost.
**Tags**: quality-assurance, quality-control, evaluation, small-business

### Question Q-4.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate how quality management can give a business a competitive advantage.
**Answer**: Consistent quality builds customer trust and brand loyalty, reducing the likelihood that customers switch to competitors. High quality can justify premium pricing, improving profit margins. It also reduces internal costs through less waste, fewer returns, and lower rework — cost savings that can be passed to customers or retained as profit. Quality certification (e.g., ISO 9001) can be a differentiator when bidding for contracts. Finally, a reputation for quality reduces marketing spend because satisfied customers become advocates through word-of-mouth. However, the investment in quality systems must be proportionate to the business's size and market position — excessive quality spending on a budget product will erode margins without adding customer value.
**Explanation**: Quality affects both revenue (customer loyalty, premium pricing) and costs (waste reduction). A common error is focusing only on one side of this equation.
**Tags**: quality, competitive-advantage, strategy, evaluation

### Question Q-4.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A food processing company has been experiencing high rates of product recall due to contamination. Recommend a quality management approach to address this problem. Justify your recommendation.
**Answer**: The company needs a layered quality management approach. Immediate action: strengthen quality control with more frequent testing and inspection at critical control points (HACCP system). Medium-term: implement quality assurance by standardising hygiene procedures, installing automated monitoring equipment, and achieving food safety certification (e.g., ISO 22000). Long-term: adopt TQM principles — train all employees in hygiene protocols, create a culture where any worker can halt the line if they spot a contamination risk, and establish continuous improvement cycles. Justification: food recalls carry legal and reputational risks that require both immediate inspection (QC) and systemic prevention (QA + TQM).
**Explanation**: Food safety demands both detection and prevention. A common error is recommending only one approach — in high-risk industries, a single layer is insufficient.
**Tags**: quality-management, food-safety, evaluation, application, HACCP

### Question Q-4.3-12

**Question**: Evaluate the importance of quality management for a business operating in a competitive market.
**Answer**: Quality Management is important because it enables businesses to make informed decisions, respond to market changes, and maintain competitive advantage. In a competitive market, businesses that effectively manage quality management are better positioned to increase market share and profitability. However, its importance depends on other factors such as the business's financial resources, the quality of management, and external economic conditions. Some businesses may succeed despite weaknesses in this area if they excel in other aspects such as marketing or innovation. Therefore, while quality management is significant, it should be considered alongside other business functions rather than in isolation.
