**Question**: What is a sole trader?

### Question Q-1.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A sole trader wants to expand and needs $500,000. Evaluate the most suitable business structure for this expansion.
**Answer**: Converting to a private limited company would allow raising capital through share sales to family and friends while retaining control. A PLC could raise the most capital from the stock exchange but requires more regulation and risks loss of control through takeover. Remaining a sole trader avoids complexity but limits capital to personal savings and loans. The best option depends on whether the owner prioritises control (stay private) or maximum capital (go public).
**Explanation**: Limited company for capital raising, but control trade-offs matter. A common error is saying one option is always best without justification.
**Tags**: business-structure, advice, expansion, evaluation

### Question Q-1.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether a family business should remain a private limited company or become a public limited company.
**Answer**: Staying private retains family control, avoids public scrutiny, and has fewer regulatory requirements — important for maintaining the family vision. Going public raises more capital for growth, improves credibility, and allows shares to be traded easily. However, a PLC faces risk of hostile takeover, must publish detailed accounts, and may lose the personal culture of the family firm. The decision depends on whether capital needs outweigh the desire to maintain family control.
**Explanation**: Control vs capital trade-off. A common error is saying one is clearly better without balanced evaluation.
**Tags**: private-vs-public, family-business, evaluation

### Question Q-1.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Discuss whether limited liability is the most important advantage of operating as a limited company compared to a sole trader.
**Answer**: Limited liability protects personal assets, which encourages investment and risk-taking — this is a significant advantage. However, other advantages may be equally important: easier access to finance through share sales, greater credibility with suppliers and customers, continuity of the business if owners change, and the ability to attract professional management. For some businesses, the ability to raise capital may matter more than limited liability. The most important advantage depends on the business's size, growth plans, and risk profile.
**Explanation**: Limited liability is important but not the only advantage. A common error is focusing solely on liability without considering finance, credibility, and continuity.
**Tags**: limited-liability, sole-trader, limited-company, evaluation
