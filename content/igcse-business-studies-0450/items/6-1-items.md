# Items: 6.1 — Government policies and business

### Question Q-6.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is fiscal policy?
**Answer**: Fiscal policy is the use of government spending and taxation to influence the economy. Expansionary fiscal policy (increased spending or lower taxes) aims to boost economic growth and reduce unemployment. Contractionary fiscal policy (reduced spending or higher taxes) aims to reduce inflation and government debt.
**Explanation**: Fiscal policy is set by the government, not the central bank. A common error is confusing fiscal policy with monetary policy — fiscal is taxes and spending; monetary is interest rates and money supply.
**Tags**: fiscal-policy, definition, government

### Question Q-6.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is monetary policy? How does changing interest rates affect businesses?
**Answer**: Monetary policy is the use of interest rates and money supply by a central bank to influence economic activity. When interest rates rise: borrowing becomes more expensive, so businesses may delay investment; consumers spend less because loan and mortgage repayments increase; the exchange rate may strengthen, making exports more expensive. When interest rates fall: borrowing is cheaper, encouraging investment and consumer spending; exports become more competitive.
**Explanation**: Monetary policy affects businesses through borrowing costs and consumer demand. A common error is thinking interest rates are set by the government — they are set by the central bank.
**Tags**: monetary-policy, interest-rates, business

### Question Q-6.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How do changes in taxation affect business decisions?
**Answer**: Taxation affects businesses in several ways: 1) Corporation tax — higher rates reduce retained profit available for reinvestment. 2) Income tax — higher rates may make workers demand higher wages, increasing labour costs. 3) Indirect taxes (e.g., VAT, sales tax) — increase the price of goods, potentially reducing demand. 4) Tax incentives — lower taxes in certain sectors or regions can encourage investment. 5) Changes in tax affect cash flow and the timing of investment decisions. Businesses may relocate to lower-tax jurisdictions or adjust their pricing in response.
**Explanation**: Tax changes affect costs, pricing, investment, and location. A common error is only considering corporation tax — indirect taxes and income tax also have significant business impacts.
**Tags**: taxation, business-decisions, impact

### Question Q-6.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a subsidy? How does it affect businesses and consumers?
**Answer**: A subsidy is a payment made by the government to businesses or producers to lower their costs of production. This encourages increased output and lower prices. For businesses, subsidies reduce costs and can make unprofitable activities viable (e.g., renewable energy). For consumers, subsidies can lead to lower prices and greater availability of goods. However, subsidies cost government money and may keep inefficient businesses alive if not targeted well.
**Explanation**: Subsidies are the opposite of taxes in terms of cost impact. A common error is thinking subsidies benefit only businesses — the main intent is often to benefit consumers or society.
**Tags**: subsidy, government, impact

### Question Q-6.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does government regulation affect business operations? Give two examples of regulations and their impact.
**Answer**: Government regulation sets legal standards that businesses must comply with. Examples: 1) Employment law — minimum wage, working time limits, health and safety requirements increase labour costs but protect workers. 2) Environmental regulation — emissions standards, waste disposal rules increase production costs but reduce pollution. 3) Consumer protection law — requirements for product safety, truthful advertising, and refund rights increase compliance costs but build consumer trust. Regulation can create barriers to entry for small firms but also levels the playing field through consistent standards.
**Explanation**: Regulation creates costs but also benefits. A common error is presenting regulation as purely negative — it creates certainty and protects businesses from unfair competition.
**Tags**: regulation, employment-law, environmental-regulation, impact

### Question Q-6.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how an increase in government spending on infrastructure can benefit businesses.
**Answer**: Infrastructure spending (roads, railways, broadband, ports) benefits businesses by: 1) Reducing transport costs and delivery times. 2) Improving access to wider markets and a larger pool of workers. 3) Enhancing communication through better digital connectivity. 4) Increasing economic activity in the area, boosting demand for local businesses. 5) Making the country or region more attractive to foreign direct investment. The benefits may take years to materialise but provide long-term improvements to business efficiency.
**Explanation**: Infrastructure is a public good that lowers private-sector costs. A common error is thinking government spending only helps through demand stimulation — supply-side benefits are equally important.
**Tags**: government-spending, infrastructure, business-benefits

### Question Q-6.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the impact on a manufacturing business of a government decision to raise the minimum wage by 15%.
**Answer**: A 15% minimum wage increase raises labour costs directly for workers on or near the minimum wage, and indirectly through wage differentials (workers above the minimum may demand proportionate increases). This increases production costs, potentially reducing profit margins or requiring price increases. If prices rise, demand may fall, especially for price-sensitive products. The business may respond by: 1) Automating tasks to reduce labour needs. 2) Reducing hours or headcount. 3) Absorbing the cost through efficiency improvements. However, higher wages can also benefit the business through improved worker motivation, lower staff turnover, and reduced recruitment and training costs. For a labour-intensive manufacturer, the negative impact is greater than for a capital-intensive one.
**Explanation**: The impact varies by business type and labour intensity. A common error is only presenting the cost increase without considering productivity and retention benefits.
**Tags**: minimum-wage, manufacturing, evaluation, impact

### Question Q-6.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A government wants to reduce carbon emissions and is considering either a carbon tax or a cap-and-trade system. Compare the two approaches from a business perspective.
**Answer**: Carbon tax: sets a fixed price per tonne of emissions, giving businesses cost certainty but not guaranteeing a specific emissions reduction. Businesses can plan for the known cost and may pass it to customers. Simple to administer. Cap-and-trade: sets a total emissions limit (cap) and allows businesses to trade permits. This guarantees the emissions target but price volatility makes costs uncertain. Efficient firms can sell excess permits for profit; inefficient firms must buy more. Cap-and-trade rewards early action and innovation more directly, but is more complex. From a business perspective, a carbon tax is easier to budget for; cap-and-trade offers greater opportunity for firms that reduce emissions quickly.
**Explanation**: Both put a price on carbon but through different mechanisms. A common error is treating them as equivalent — the certainty versus flexibility trade-off matters for business planning.
**Tags**: carbon-tax, cap-and-trade, environmental-policy, evaluation

### Question Q-6.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate how a combination of rising interest rates, higher corporation tax, and stricter environmental regulation would affect a medium-sized manufacturing business.
**Answer**: Rising interest rates increase the cost of existing variable-rate loans and make new investment more expensive, likely delaying expansion or equipment upgrades. Higher corporation tax reduces retained profit, limiting funds available for reinvestment and dividend distribution. Stricter environmental regulation requires investment in cleaner technology or processes, adding capital expenditure and ongoing compliance costs. Together, these three policies create a significant cost burden: borrowing is more expensive, profits are more heavily taxed, and regulatory compliance requires additional spending. The business may respond by cutting costs (reducing staff, delaying maintenance), raising prices (risking lost sales), or seeking government support. Long-term, the business may need to invest in energy-efficient technology to reduce future compliance costs.
**Explanation**: Policy combinations can have compounding effects. A common error is analysing each policy in isolation without considering their combined impact on the business's financial position.
**Tags**: interest-rates, taxation, regulation, evaluation, manufacturing
