# Items: 6.3 — Globalisation and international trade

### Question Q-6.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is globalisation?
**Answer**: Globalisation is the process by which economies, businesses, and cultures around the world become increasingly interconnected through trade, investment, technology, and the movement of people. It leads to the integration of markets, production, and financial systems across national borders.
**Explanation**: Globalisation is a long-term trend, not a single event. A common error is equating globalisation with international trade — trade is one component; globalisation also covers investment, technology transfer, and cultural exchange.
**Tags**: globalisation, definition, international-business

### Question Q-6.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What are the main causes of globalisation?
**Answer**: Key causes include: 1) Trade liberalisation — reduction of tariffs and trade barriers through agreements (WTO, free trade areas). 2) Technological advances — container shipping, the internet, and communication technology reduce the cost of trading. 3) Financial deregulation — easier cross-border investment and capital flows. 4) Growth of multinational corporations (MNCs) operating across multiple countries. 5) Converging consumer preferences creating global markets for brands.
**Explanation**: Multiple forces drive globalisation simultaneously. A common error is attributing it to a single cause like technology — policy changes and corporate strategy are equally important.
**Tags**: globalisation, causes, trade

### Question Q-6.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What are the advantages and disadvantages of globalisation for businesses?
**Answer**: Advantages: 1) Access to larger markets and more customers. 2) Lower production costs through offshoring and global supply chains. 3) Economies of scale from producing for a global market. 4) Access to new technology and expertise. 5) Diversification of risk across multiple markets. Disadvantages: 1) Increased competition from foreign firms. 2) Cultural and language barriers in new markets. 3) Political and currency risks in foreign countries. 4) Complexity of managing operations across time zones. 5) Negative public perception regarding labour practices or environmental impact in host countries.
**Explanation**: Globalisation creates both opportunities and threats. A common error is presenting it as universally positive — small domestic firms may struggle against global competitors.
**Tags**: globalisation, advantage, disadvantage

### Question Q-6.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is free trade? What are its benefits?
**Answer**: Free trade is international trade without restrictions such as tariffs, quotas, or subsidies. Its benefits include: 1) Countries specialise in producing goods where they have a comparative advantage, increasing global output. 2) Consumers get lower prices and greater choice. 3) Businesses access cheaper raw materials and components. 4) Competition encourages innovation and efficiency. 5) Developing countries can grow through export-led growth.
**Explanation**: Free trade is based on the theory of comparative advantage. A common error is thinking free trade means no rules — it still requires agreements on standards, intellectual property, and dispute resolution.
**Tags**: free-trade, comparative-advantage, benefits

### Question Q-6.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is protectionism? Describe three methods of protectionism.
**Answer**: Protectionism is government policy to restrict imports and protect domestic industries from foreign competition. Methods: 1) Tariffs — taxes on imported goods, making them more expensive. 2) Quotas — limits on the quantity of a specific good that can be imported. 3) Subsidies — payments to domestic producers to lower their costs and make them more competitive. 4) Administrative barriers — complex regulations, safety standards, or paperwork that makes importing difficult. 5) Embargoes — complete bans on trade with specific countries.
**Explanation**: Protectionism is the opposite of free trade. A common error is thinking tariffs are the only tool — quotas, subsidies, and regulations are equally important.
**Tags**: protectionism, tariffs, quotas, subsidies

### Question Q-6.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What are the advantages and disadvantages of protectionism for a country's economy?
**Answer**: Advantages: 1) Protects infant industries until they are strong enough to compete globally. 2) Protects domestic jobs in industries threatened by cheap imports. 3) Reduces dependency on foreign suppliers for essential goods (national security). 4) Can reduce a trade deficit. Disadvantages: 1) Consumers face higher prices and less choice. 2) Protected industries may become inefficient without competition. 3) Other countries may retaliate with their own trade barriers, reducing exports. 4) Global output is reduced because countries do not fully specialise according to comparative advantage.
**Explanation**: Protectionism has short-term benefits but long-term costs. A common error is assuming protecting one industry helps the whole economy — the costs are borne by consumers and export industries.
**Tags**: protectionism, economy, advantage, disadvantage

### Question Q-6.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the impact of an MNC setting up a factory in a developing country on the host country's economy.
**Answer**: Positive impacts: 1) Job creation and skills transfer through training. 2) Infrastructure development (roads, power) that benefits the wider community. 3) Tax revenue for the government. 4) Technology transfer and knowledge spillovers to local firms. 5) Increased exports improving the balance of payments. Negative impacts: 1) Profits may be repatriated to the home country rather than reinvested locally. 2) Environmental damage if regulations are weak. 3) Local businesses may be unable to compete. 4) Exploitation of low-wage labour with poor working conditions. 5) The MNC may have disproportionate political influence. The net impact depends on the strength of local institutions and the terms negotiated with the MNC.
**Explanation**: MNC investment is not automatically beneficial. A common error is focusing only on job creation without considering profit repatriation and environmental costs.
**Tags**: MNC, developing-country, evaluation, FDI

### Question Q-6.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A small country is debating whether to join a free trade agreement. Evaluate the arguments for and against from the perspective of domestic businesses.
**Answer**: Arguments for: 1) Access to a much larger market without tariffs — export opportunities expand significantly. 2) Cheaper imported raw materials and components reduce production costs. 3) Foreign investment may increase as the country becomes part of a larger trading zone. 4) Exposure to international competition drives domestic firms to become more efficient. Arguments against: 1) Domestic firms that cannot compete with cheaper or higher-quality imports may fail, leading to job losses. 2) Infant industries may never develop because they cannot compete with established foreign rivals. 3) The country loses some sovereignty over trade policy. 4) Economic dependency on larger partners. The outcome depends on whether domestic firms are competitive enough to benefit from expanded market access or vulnerable to import competition.
**Explanation**: Free trade creates winners and losers within each country. A common error is assessing only aggregate national benefits without considering distributional effects on different industries.
**Tags**: free-trade-agreement, evaluation, domestic-business

### Question Q-6.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether a local clothing manufacturer should begin exporting its products or focus exclusively on the domestic market.
**Answer**: Exporting advantages: 1) Access to a much larger customer base, reducing dependency on the domestic economy. 2) Economies of scale from higher production volumes. 3) Risk diversification — a downturn in one market may be offset by growth in another. 4) Enhanced brand reputation from international presence. Exporting challenges: 1) Tariffs and trade barriers in target markets. 2) Need to adapt products to local tastes, regulations, and sizing standards. 3) Higher distribution and logistics costs. 4) Currency fluctuation risk. 5) Need for local market knowledge and potentially local partners. Recommendation: begin with a limited export programme to one or two culturally similar, low-barrier markets while maintaining the domestic base. This limits risk while building export experience. Justification: the clothing industry benefits from brand building across markets, and starting small allows learning before committing significant resources.
**Explanation**: The decision balances growth opportunity against complexity and risk. A common error is either refusing to export at all or entering too many markets simultaneously without adequate preparation.
**Tags**: exporting, international-trade, evaluation, application, manufacturing
