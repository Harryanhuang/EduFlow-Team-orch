# Items: 2.1 — Organisation structure and authority


### Question Q-2.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is meant by 'organisation structure'?
**Answer**: Organisation structure is the framework of authority and responsibility that shows how tasks, power and reporting relationships are arranged within a business. It is usually shown as an organisational chart.
**Explanation**: The structure clarifies who reports to whom. A common error is confusing organisation structure with organisational culture.
**Tags**: organisation-structure, definition

### Question Q-2.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define 'span of control'.
**Answer**: Span of control is the number of subordinates directly reporting to a manager. A wide span means one manager supervises many people; a narrow span means fewer subordinates per manager.
**Explanation**: Span of control affects how closely managers can supervise. A common error is confusing it with chain of command.
**Tags**: span-of-control, definition

### Question Q-2.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Distinguish between a tall and a flat organisational structure.
**Answer**: A tall structure has many levels of hierarchy and a long chain of command, with narrow spans of control. A flat structure has few hierarchy levels, wider spans of control, and shorter chains of command.
**Explanation**: Tall structures allow close supervision but slower communication. Flat structures enable faster decisions but managers have less time per subordinate.
**Tags**: tall-structure, flat-structure, distinction

### Question Q-2.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain what is meant by 'delegation'. Give one advantage and one disadvantage.
**Answer**: Delegation is giving lower-level employees authority and responsibility to carry out tasks. Advantage: saves senior managers' time and motivates junior staff. Disadvantage: tasks may be done poorly if the employee lacks skills, and the manager remains accountable for failures.
**Explanation**: Delegation is not abdication — the manager retains accountability. A common error is thinking delegation removes responsibility from the manager.
**Tags**: delegation, advantage, disadvantage

### Question Q-2.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the difference between line authority and staff authority?
**Answer**: Line authority gives managers the power to direct the work of subordinates and make decisions (e.g., production manager). Staff authority gives specialists the power to advise and support but not to command (e.g., HR advisor, legal counsel).
**Explanation**: Line managers make decisions; staff specialists advise. A common error is assuming staff managers have the same command authority as line managers.
**Tags**: line-authority, staff-authority, distinction

### Question Q-2.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a matrix organisation structure? Give one advantage and one disadvantage.
**Answer**: A matrix structure combines functional and project-based reporting — employees report to both a functional manager and a project manager. Advantage: flexible use of skills across projects. Disadvantage: dual reporting can cause conflict and confusion over priorities.
**Explanation**: Matrix structures suit project-based industries. A common error is thinking employees have only one manager in a matrix.
**Tags**: matrix-structure, advantage, disadvantage

### Question Q-2.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the advantages and disadvantages of a wide span of control.
**Answer**: Advantages: fewer managers reduce costs; subordinates gain more autonomy and develop skills; faster communication with fewer levels. Disadvantages: managers have less time per subordinate, which may reduce supervision quality; risk of overload for managers; subordinates may feel unsupported. Best suited to experienced, self-motivated teams.
**Explanation**: The ideal span depends on task complexity and employee capability. A common error is claiming one span size is always better.
**Tags**: span-of-control, evaluation, management

### Question Q-2.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare centralised and decentralised decision-making. To what extent should a large retail chain decentralise its decisions?
**Answer**: Centralisation keeps decision-making at senior levels — ensures consistency and control but is slow and demotivating. Decentralisation pushes decisions to lower levels — faster, more responsive, and motivating, but risks inconsistency. A large retail chain could decentralise operational decisions (e.g., local promotions, staffing rotas) to store managers who understand local markets, while centralising strategic decisions (e.g., brand strategy, major purchasing) to maintain consistency. Neither approach is inherently superior; most businesses use a mix depending on the decision type.
**Explanation**: Neither approach is inherently superior. A common error is treating centralisation and decentralisation as opposites rather than a continuum.
**Tags**: centralisation, decentralisation, comparison, decision-making, evaluation

### Question Q-2.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A growing tech startup of 200 employees still uses the same flat structure it had with 20 staff. Problems include managers being overloaded and decisions taking too long. Evaluate whether the business should adopt a taller structure or maintain its flat approach, and justify your recommendation.
**Answer**: Adopting a taller structure would introduce additional hierarchy levels with a clear chain of command, delegate operational decisions to department heads, and widen span of control at the top. This reduces manager overload, speeds decisions at the right level, and creates clear reporting lines. However, it may slow some communication and reduce the informal culture that attracted early employees. On balance, the benefits outweigh the drawbacks: at 200 staff, the original flat structure with everyone reporting to founders is unsustainable. The business should introduce middle managers while preserving open communication channels (e.g., regular all-hands meetings) to retain some flat-structure culture.
**Explanation**: Structural changes must match organisational size and complexity. A common error is recommending delayering when the problem is too few levels.
**Tags**: organisation-structure, delegation, evaluation, application
