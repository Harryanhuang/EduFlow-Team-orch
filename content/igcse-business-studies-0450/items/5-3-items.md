# Items: 5.3 — Cash flow and working capital

### Question Q-5.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is cash flow?
**Answer**: Cash flow is the movement of money into and out of a business over a period. Cash inflows include receipts from customers, loans received, and capital introduced. Cash outflows include payments to suppliers, wages, rent, and loan repayments. Net cash flow = total cash inflows − total cash outflows.
**Explanation**: Cash flow tracks actual money, not accounting profit. A common error is thinking a profitable business always has positive cash flow — timing differences can create cash problems even when profitable.
**Tags**: cash-flow, definition, inflows, outflows

### Question Q-5.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a cash flow forecast? Why do businesses prepare one?
**Answer**: A cash flow forecast is a prediction of cash inflows and outflows over a future period, usually month by month. It shows the expected closing bank balance at the end of each period. Businesses prepare one to: 1) Identify when cash shortages may occur so they can arrange finance in advance. 2) Plan for periods of surplus cash (e.g., investments). 3) Satisfy lenders or investors that the business can meet its obligations. 4) Monitor actual performance against the forecast.
**Explanation**: Forecasting prevents surprise cash crises. A common error is confusing a cash flow forecast with a profit and loss account — the forecast tracks cash timing, not accruals.
**Tags**: cash-flow-forecast, planning, prediction

### Question Q-5.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why a profitable business can still run out of cash.
**Answer**: Profit is calculated on an accruals basis (revenue when earned, costs when incurred), while cash flow tracks actual money received and paid. A profitable business can run out of cash if: 1) Customers pay slowly (high receivables) while suppliers must be paid promptly. 2) The business has invested heavily in inventory or equipment. 3) Loan repayments are due. 4) Rapid growth requires upfront cash for materials and wages before customer payments arrive. Profit without cash is not immediately available to pay bills.
**Explanation**: Cash and profit are different measures. A common error is assuming profit = cash — the timing gap between earning and receiving is critical.
**Tags**: cash-flow, profit, liquidity, working-capital

### Question Q-5.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is working capital? How is it calculated?
**Answer**: Working capital is the capital available for the day-to-day running of a business. It is calculated as: working capital = current assets − current liabilities. Current assets include cash, inventory, and trade receivables (money owed by customers). Current liabilities include trade payables (money owed to suppliers), overdrafts, and short-term loans. Positive working capital means the business can meet its short-term obligations.
**Explanation**: Working capital measures liquidity. A common error is including non-current assets (machinery, buildings) in the calculation — only current assets count.
**Tags**: working-capital, current-assets, current-liabilities, calculation

### Question Q-5.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business has current assets of $80,000 (cash $15,000, inventory $35,000, receivables $30,000) and current liabilities of $50,000 (payables $40,000, overdraft $10,000). Calculate its working capital and comment on its liquidity position.
**Answer**: Working capital = $80,000 − $50,000 = $30,000. This is positive, meaning the business has sufficient current assets to cover its short-term liabilities. However, $35,000 of the $80,000 in current assets is inventory, which may not be quickly convertible to cash. Quick liquidity depends on collecting receivables ($30,000) and available cash ($15,000) — only $45,000 against $50,000 of immediate liabilities. The business should monitor receivables collection and consider reducing inventory levels.
**Explanation**: Positive working capital does not guarantee immediate liquidity if assets are tied up in slow-moving inventory. A common error is not considering the composition of current assets.
**Tags**: working-capital, liquidity, calculation, analysis

### Question Q-5.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe three methods a business can use to improve its cash flow.
**Answer**: 1) Speed up receivables collection — offer early-payment discounts, tighten credit terms, or chase overdue invoices more actively. 2) Delay payables — negotiate longer payment terms with suppliers (without damaging relationships). 3) Reduce inventory levels — avoid overstocking, use just-in-time ordering. 4) Arrange short-term finance — overdraft, short-term loan, or invoice factoring to cover temporary shortfalls. 5) Sell unused assets for cash. 6) Sale and leaseback — sell equipment or property and lease it back to release cash.
**Explanation**: Cash flow improvement targets either inflows (speed them up) or outflows (slow them down). A common error is suggesting long-term strategies (e.g., increase sales) for an immediate cash crisis.
**Tags**: cash-flow, improvement, methods

### Question Q-5.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain how invoice factoring works and evaluate it as a cash flow solution for a small business.
**Answer**: Invoice factoring (or factoring) is when a business sells its outstanding receivables (invoices owed by customers) to a third-party factor at a discount. The factor pays the business immediately (typically 70-90% of the invoice value) and then collects payment directly from customers. Advantages: immediate cash without taking on debt; the factor handles credit control. Disadvantages: the business receives less than the full invoice value; customers may perceive the business as financially weak; it can be expensive for small invoice volumes. It is a practical solution for B2B businesses with long payment terms but may be unsuitable if customer relationships are sensitive.
**Explanation**: Factoring converts receivables to cash quickly but at a cost. A common error is confusing factoring with a loan — it is a sale of assets, not borrowing.
**Tags**: invoice-factoring, cash-flow, evaluation

### Question Q-5.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A rapidly growing business has increasing sales but faces a worsening cash position. Analyse the causes and recommend actions.
**Answer**: This is 'overtrading' — growing faster than available finance can support. Causes: 1) More sales mean more inventory and receivables, tying up cash. 2) Fixed capacity means the business must pay for inputs before receiving customer payments. 3) Profit is growing but cash is being consumed by working capital expansion. Recommendations: 1) Arrange additional finance (overdraft for short-term gap, loan for expansion). 2) Tighten credit control — collect receivables faster. 3) Negotiate longer payment terms with suppliers. 4) Slow growth temporarily to match available cash. 5) Consider invoice factoring. Justification: the business is fundamentally viable but needs working capital to fund the growth cycle.
**Explanation**: Overtrading is a common cause of business failure despite growing profits. A common error is recommending cost-cutting when the underlying issue is growth outpacing cash.
**Tags**: overtrading, cash-flow, growth, evaluation, application

### Question Q-5.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether a seasonal business (such as a garden centre with peak sales in spring and summer) should rely on an overdraft or a bank loan to manage its annual cash flow cycle.
**Answer**: An overdraft is the more appropriate choice for a seasonal cash flow cycle. The business can draw on the overdraft during off-peak months when outflows exceed inflows and repay it during peak months. Interest is only charged on the amount used and for the period it is needed, making it cost-effective for predictable, short-term fluctuations. A bank loan would provide a fixed lump sum regardless of when the cash is needed, and interest would be charged on the full amount for the entire term — less efficient for seasonal needs. However, if the business needs capital for expansion (new greenhouse, equipment), a loan would be appropriate for that specific investment, separate from the working capital cycle.
**Explanation**: The finance type should match the need — flexible for cyclical, fixed for investment. A common error is using a long-term loan to fund short-term seasonal gaps.
**Tags**: overdraft, bank-loan, seasonal-business, evaluation, application
