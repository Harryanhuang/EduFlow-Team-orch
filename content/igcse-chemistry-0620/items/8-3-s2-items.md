# Items: 8.3 — Noble gases and uses (supplementary batch)


### Question Q-8.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why noble gases are unreactive. Include reference to their electronic configuration in your answer.
**Answer**: Noble gases have a full outer shell of electrons (helium: 2, others: 8). This is a very stable arrangement. They do not need to lose, gain, or share electrons to achieve stability, so they do not readily form chemical bonds with other elements.
**Explanation**: Students must link the full outer shell to chemical stability. A common error is saying noble gases have "no electrons to share" rather than "a complete outer shell".
**Tags**: noble-gas, electronic-configuration, inert, full-outer-shell

### Question Q-8.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Helium is used to fill weather balloons instead of hydrogen. Explain why, even though hydrogen is lighter than helium.
**Answer**: Hydrogen is highly flammable and forms explosive mixtures with air. Helium is non-flammable and chemically inert, making it much safer despite being slightly heavier than hydrogen. Safety outweighs the small loss of lift.
**Explanation**: Students must identify the safety concern with hydrogen. A common error is saying helium is "the lightest gas" — hydrogen is actually lighter.
**Tags**: helium, weather-balloon, safety, hydrogen

### Question Q-8.3-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Argon is used in filament light bulbs. Explain the role of argon in the bulb and why nitrogen would be less suitable.
**Answer**: Argon provides an inert atmosphere that prevents the hot tungsten filament from reacting with oxygen and burning away. Nitrogen is less suitable because at very high temperatures it can react with tungsten to form tungsten nitride, whereas argon is completely inert.
**Explanation**: Students must explain the protective role of the inert gas. A common error is saying argon "cools" the filament or "makes it brighter".
**Tags**: argon, light-bulb, inert-atmosphere, filament

### Question Q-8.3-08
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Neon is used in advertising signs. Describe how a neon sign produces light and state the colour it produces.
**Answer**: When a high voltage is applied across a tube containing neon gas at low pressure, electrons in the neon atoms become excited to higher energy levels. When they fall back to lower levels, they emit light. Neon produces a characteristic red-orange glow.
**Explanation**: Students need to describe the excitation and emission process. A common error is saying the gas "burns" or "heats up to glow" rather than electron transitions.
**Tags**: neon, discharge, light-emission, excitation

### Question Q-8.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Going down Group VIII (Group 0), the boiling points of the noble gases increase: helium (−269°C) < neon (−246°C) < argon (−186°C) < krypton (−153°C) < xenon (−108°C). (a) Explain this trend in terms of intermolecular forces. (b) Predict whether radon would have a higher or lower boiling point than xenon, and explain your reasoning.
**Answer**: (a) Going down the group, atoms have more electrons and larger electron clouds. This increases the strength of London dispersion forces (van der Waals forces) between atoms. Stronger intermolecular forces require more energy to overcome, so boiling points increase.
(b) Radon would have a higher boiling point than xenon because it is further down the group, has more electrons, and therefore stronger dispersion forces.
**Explanation**: This requires understanding of intermolecular forces — a concept not always explicitly taught for noble gases. A common error is attributing boiling point differences to atomic mass rather than intermolecular forces.
**Tags**: noble-gas, boiling-point, intermolecular-forces, trend, prediction
