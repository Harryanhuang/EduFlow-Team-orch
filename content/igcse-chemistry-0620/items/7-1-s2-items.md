# Items: 7.1 — Properties of acids and bases, pH (additional)


### Question Q-7.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student has two unlabelled solutions: one is 0.1 mol/dm³ hydrochloric acid and the other is 0.1 mol/dm³ ethanoic acid. (a) Predict which has the lower pH and explain why. (b) Both solutions react with magnesium. Predict which reacts faster and explain. (c) If both are titrated with the same NaOH solution, will the volume of NaOH needed be the same or different? Explain.
**Answer**: (a) HCl has the lower pH. It is a strong acid, fully ionised, so [H⁺] = 0.1 mol/dm³. Ethanoic acid is weak, partially ionised, so [H⁺] < 0.1 mol/dm³.
(b) HCl reacts faster. It has a higher [H⁺], so more frequent successful collisions with magnesium.
(c) The volume of NaOH needed will be the SAME. Both acids have the same concentration and are monoprotic. The total number of moles of H⁺ available is the same (the weak acid fully reacts during neutralisation, not just the ionised fraction).
**Explanation**: Part (c) is the most commonly misunderstood — total acid content determines titration volume, not just the ionised fraction.
**Tags**: strong-acid, weak-acid, pH, rate, titration, comparison

### Question Q-7.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain why the reaction between a strong acid and a strong base always has the same ionic equation regardless of which specific acid and base are used. Write this ionic equation. Why is the enthalpy change of neutralisation approximately the same for all strong acid–strong base reactions?
**Answer**: The ionic equation is: H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H2O(l)
Strong acids are fully dissociated into H⁺ and anions. Strong bases are fully dissociated into OH⁻ and cations. The anions and cations are spectator ions.
The enthalpy of neutralisation is approximately −57 kJ/mol for all strong acid–strong base reactions because the same reaction is occurring (H⁺ + OH⁻ → H2O). The spectator ions do not participate in the energy change.
**Explanation**: Students must identify the ionic equation and explain why it is universal. A common error is writing the full molecular equation instead of the ionic equation.
**Tags**: neutralisation, ionic-equation, enthalpy, strong-acid, strong-base

### Question Q-7.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student adds water to 10 cm³ of a strong acid with pH 1. (a) What happens to the pH if the solution is diluted by a factor of 10? (b) What is the new [H⁺]? (c) Can the pH ever reach 7 by adding water alone? Explain. (d) Write the expression for pH in terms of [H⁺].
**Answer**: (a) pH increases by 1 unit (from 1 to 2). Diluting by 10 reduces [H⁺] by a factor of 10.
(b) Original [H⁺] = 10⁻¹ = 0.1 mol/dm³. New [H⁺] = 0.1/10 = 0.01 = 10⁻² mol/dm³.
(c) No — adding water only dilutes the acid. The [H⁺] approaches the [H⁺] of pure water (10⁻⁷ mol/dm³) but never reaches it. The solution remains acidic, just more dilute.
(d) pH = −log₁₀[H⁺]
**Explanation**: Students must understand the logarithmic nature of the pH scale. A common error is saying pH goes to 7 when you "add enough water."
**Tags**: pH, dilution, concentration, logarithmic, calculation
