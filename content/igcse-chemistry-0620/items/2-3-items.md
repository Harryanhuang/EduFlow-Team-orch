# Items: 2.3 — Bonding: ionic, covalent, metallic

### Question Q-2.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is ionic bonding?
**Answer**: Ionic bonding is the electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions, formed when one or more electrons are transferred from a metal atom to a non-metal atom.
**Explanation**: Transfer → ions → electrostatic attraction. A common error is saying electrons are "shared" in ionic bonds.
**Tags**: ionic-bonding, definition, electron-transfer

### Question Q-2.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Use a dot-and-cross diagram to show the formation of sodium chloride from sodium and chlorine atoms.
**Answer**: Sodium atom (2,8,1) loses one electron to become Na⁺ (2,8). Chlorine atom (2,8,7) gains one electron to become Cl⁻ (2,8,8). The Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions are held together by electrostatic attraction.
**Explanation**: Metal loses, non-metal gains. A common error is showing both atoms gaining electrons.
**Tags**: dot-and-cross, NaCl, ionic-bonding

### Question Q-2.3-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a covalent bond?
**Answer**: A covalent bond is a shared pair of electrons between two non-metal atoms. Each atom contributes one electron to the shared pair.
**Explanation**: Sharing between non-metals. A common error is saying covalent bonds involve ions.
**Tags**: covalent-bond, definition, sharing

### Question Q-2.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Draw a dot-and-cross diagram for a molecule of water (H₂O).
**Answer**: Oxygen atom (6 valence electrons) shares one electron with each of two hydrogen atoms (1 valence electron each). The oxygen has two lone pairs remaining. Each hydrogen achieves a full shell of 2 electrons; oxygen achieves 8.
**Explanation**: O shares with 2 H atoms. A common error is showing O with only 6 electrons in the bonded molecule.
**Tags**: water, dot-and-cross, covalent-bonding

### Question Q-2.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why does carbon dioxide (CO₂) have double bonds?
**Answer**: Carbon has 4 valence electrons and each oxygen has 6. Carbon shares two electrons with each oxygen, and each oxygen shares two electrons back, forming two double bonds (C=O). This gives carbon and each oxygen a full outer shell of 8 electrons.
**Explanation**: Double bonds = 2 shared pairs. A common error is showing single bonds only.
**Tags**: CO2, double-bond, covalent-bonding

### Question Q-2.3-06
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Describe metallic bonding.
**Answer**: Metallic bonding is the electrostatic attraction between a lattice of positive metal ions and a "sea" of delocalised electrons that move freely throughout the structure.
**Explanation**: Positive ions + delocalised electrons. A common error is saying electrons are "fixed" in metals.
**Tags**: metallic-bonding, delocalised-electrons, definition

### Question Q-2.3-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why metals can conduct electricity but ionic compounds only conduct when molten or dissolved.
**Answer**: Metals have delocalised electrons that are free to move throughout the solid structure at all times. Ionic compounds have ions held in fixed positions in the solid but free to move when molten or dissolved, allowing charge to flow only in those states.
**Explanation**: Delocalised electrons vs. mobile ions. A common error is saying ionic compounds "never" conduct.
**Tags**: conductivity, metals, ionic-compounds, explanation
