# Items: 9.3 — Corrosion rusting (supplementary)


### Question Q-9.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A student places an iron nail in each of three test tubes: (A) dry air with anhydrous calcium chloride, (B) boiled water covered with a layer of oil, (C) water exposed to air. Predict what happens in each tube after one week and explain what this shows about the conditions needed for rusting.
**Answer**: A: No rust — water is absent (calcium chloride absorbs moisture).
B: No rust — oxygen is absent (boiling removes dissolved oxygen, oil prevents air from re-dissolving).
C: Rust forms — both oxygen and water are present.
This shows that rusting requires BOTH oxygen AND water. Neither alone is sufficient.
**Explanation**: Students must predict all three outcomes and draw the correct conclusion. A common error is saying rusting needs "air" without specifying oxygen, or thinking water alone causes rusting.
**Tags**: rusting, experiment, oxygen, water, conditions

### Question Q-9.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student coats an iron nail with zinc (galvanising) and scratches it. A second nail is painted and scratched. After exposure to water and air, the galvanised nail shows no rust but the painted nail rusts at the scratch. Explain this difference in terms of sacrificial protection and barrier methods, including why zinc protects iron but paint does not once damaged.
**Answer**: Galvanising provides sacrificial protection: zinc is more reactive than iron in the reactivity series, so when the coating is scratched and both metals are exposed to water/oxygen, zinc corrodes preferentially (loses electrons first). The zinc acts as a sacrificial anode, protecting the iron even at the scratch. Painting is only a barrier method — once scratched, the barrier is broken and the exposed iron has no protection, so it rusts immediately.
**Explanation**: Students must understand the key difference: barrier methods fail when damaged, but sacrificial protection continues to work because of the reactivity difference. A common error is saying "zinc is a better barrier" — the protection is electrochemical, not physical.
**Tags**: rust-prevention, galvanising, sacrificial-protection, barrier-method, electron-transfer

### Question Q-9.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain in terms of electron transfer why zinc protects iron from rusting when the two metals are in contact. Include the relevant half-equations in your answer.
**Answer**: Zinc is more reactive than iron (higher in the reactivity series). When zinc and iron are in contact in the presence of water and oxygen, zinc loses electrons more readily: Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻. These electrons are transferred to the iron, preventing iron from losing electrons (Fe → Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻). The iron is cathodically protected because the zinc acts as the anode (sacrificial electrode).
**Explanation**: Students must explain the electrochemical basis of sacrificial protection. A common error is saying zinc "blocks" rusting rather than "gives electrons to iron".
**Tags**: sacrificial-protection, electron-transfer, half-equation, zinc, cathodic-protection

### Question Q-9.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: The cost of rusting to the global economy is estimated to be hundreds of billions of dollars per year. (a) Write the chemical formula for rust. (b) Explain why rusting is classified as an oxidation reaction. (c) A bridge made of steel is located near the sea. Suggest the most effective rust prevention method and justify your choice.
**Answer**: (a) Hydrated iron(III) oxide: Fe2O3·xH2O
(b) Rusting is oxidation because iron atoms lose electrons (Fe → Fe³⁺ + 3e⁻) and combine with oxygen. Iron is oxidised by oxygen in the presence of water.
(c) Near the sea, salt accelerates rusting. The most effective method would be a combination: paint coating (barrier) + sacrificial protection (e.g. zinc or magnesium blocks). The paint provides the primary barrier; if scratched, the sacrificial anode still protects. Salt water increases conductivity, making electrochemical rusting faster.
**Explanation**: Students must connect the chemistry to real-world economics. A common error is writing just Fe2O3 without the water of crystallisation.
**Tags**: rust, oxidation, sacrificial-protection, salt, economic-impact
