# Items: 5.1 — Exothermic and endothermic reactions (additional)


### Question Q-5.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Draw and describe the features of a reaction profile for an exothermic reaction. Label the activation energy and the enthalpy change (ΔH).
**Answer**: The reaction profile shows energy on the y-axis and reaction progress on the x-axis. Reactants start at a higher energy level than products (energy released). The "hump" represents the activation energy (minimum energy for successful collisions). ΔH is the energy difference between reactants and products (negative for exothermic).
**Explanation**: Students must draw and label all features. A common error is drawing products higher than reactants for an exothermic reaction.
**Tags**: reaction-profile, activation-energy, enthalpy, diagram, exothermic

### Question Q-5.1-06
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: In the reaction N2(g) + 3H2(g) ⇌ 2NH3(g), ΔH = −92 kJ/mol. (a) Is the forward reaction exothermic or endothermic? (b) What is the ΔH for the reverse reaction? (c) If 0.50 mol of N2 reacts completely with excess H2, how much energy is released? (d) Sketch the reaction profile showing both forward and reverse activation energies.
**Answer**: (a) Exothermic (ΔH is negative).
(b) +92 kJ/mol (the reverse reaction has the same magnitude but opposite sign).
(c) 0.50 mol N2 releases 0.50 × 92 = 46 kJ.
(d) Profile: reactants (N2 + 3H2) at higher energy than products (2NH3). Ea(forward) shown from reactants to peak. Ea(reverse) shown from products to peak — larger than Ea(forward).
**Explanation**: Students must apply the concept of reversible reactions and energy. A common error in (c) is using the stoichiometric ratio wrongly.
**Tags**: haber-process, reversible-reaction, enthalpy, calculation, reaction-profile

### Question Q-5.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student dissolves ammonium nitrate in water and observes the temperature drops from 22°C to 15°C. (a) Is this process exothermic or endothermic? (b) Explain the observation in terms of energy changes. (c) Suggest one practical use of this type of process.
**Answer**: (a) Endothermic (temperature decreases — energy absorbed from surroundings).
(b) The dissolution of ammonium nitrate requires more energy to break the ionic lattice apart than is released when the ions are hydrated. The net energy is absorbed from the water, lowering its temperature.
(c) Instant cold packs for sports injuries — the endothermic reaction absorbs heat from the affected area, reducing swelling and pain.
**Explanation**: Students must link observation to the energy process. A common error is saying the salt "is cold" rather than "absorbs heat from the water".
**Tags**: endothermic, dissolution, ammonium-nitrate, cold-pack, application

### Question Q-5.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: The complete combustion of 1.00 g of ethanol (C2H5OH) releases 29.7 kJ of energy. (a) Write the balanced equation for the complete combustion of ethanol. (b) Calculate the molar enthalpy of combustion of ethanol. (c) In an experiment, a student measured only 22.1 kJ per gram. Suggest two reasons for this discrepancy.
**Answer**: (a) C2H5OH(l) + 3O2(g) → 2CO2(g) + 3H2O(l)
(b) Mr(C2H5OH) = 46. Moles in 1.00 g = 1.00/46 = 0.0217 mol.
Molar enthalpy = 29.7/0.0217 = −1367 kJ/mol (negative because exothermic).
(c) Heat loss to surroundings (beaker, air); incomplete combustion; evaporation of ethanol; heat absorbed by the thermometer.
**Explanation**: Students must calculate molar enthalpy from mass data. A common error is not converting to per-mole or forgetting the negative sign.
**Tags**: combustion, enthalpy, calculation, heat-loss, experimental-error

### Question Q-5.1-09
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe an experiment to measure the energy released when burning a fuel such as ethanol. Include a labelled diagram and explain how to calculate the energy released.
**Answer**: Set up: spirit burner with ethanol, copper calorimeter with known mass of water, thermometer, draught shield.
Procedure: weigh burner, record initial water temperature, burn fuel to heat water, record final temperature, reweigh burner.
Calculation: Q = mcΔT (m = mass of water, c = 4.18 J/g°C, ΔT = temperature change).
Energy per gram = Q / mass of fuel burned.
**Explanation**: Students must describe the full experimental setup. A common error is not including the draught shield or not mentioning weighing before and after.
**Tags**: combustion, calorimetry, experiment, calculation, energy
