# Items: 7.2 — Salt preparation methods

### Question Q-7.2-01
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Which method of salt preparation would you use for sodium chloride? Explain why.
**Answer**: Titration (acid + soluble alkali). Both sodium hydroxide and hydrochloric acid are soluble, so titration is needed to find the exact endpoint. After titration, the solution is evaporated and crystallised.
**Explanation**: Soluble base + soluble acid → titration. A common error is using excess base method (that's for insoluble bases).
**Tags**: salt-preparation, NaCl, titration

### Question Q-7.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Which method of salt preparation would you use for copper(II) sulfate? Explain why.
**Answer**: Excess base method (acid + insoluble base). Copper(II) oxide is insoluble in water, so it is added in excess to ensure all the acid is neutralised. The excess is filtered off.
**Explanation**: Insoluble base → excess method. A common error is using titration.
**Tags**: salt-preparation, copper-sulfate, excess-base

### Question Q-7.2-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Which method of salt preparation would you use for lead(II) sulfate? Explain why.
**Answer**: Precipitation. Both lead(II) nitrate and sodium sulfate are soluble, but lead(II) sulfate is insoluble. When the two solutions are mixed, lead(II) sulfate precipitates out and can be filtered, washed, and dried.
**Explanation**: Insoluble salt → precipitation. A common error is using acid + base method.
**Tags**: salt-preparation, lead-sulfate, precipitation

### Question Q-7.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How would you prepare a pure, dry sample of silver chloride?
**Answer**: Mix aqueous silver nitrate with aqueous sodium chloride. Silver chloride precipitates as a white solid. Filter the precipitate, wash with distilled water (to remove soluble impurities), and dry in an oven or between filter paper.
**Explanation**: Precipitation from two soluble salts. A common error is trying to react silver metal with HCl.
**Tags**: silver-chloride, precipitation, method

### Question Q-7.2-05
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student adds excess zinc carbonate to dilute sulfuric acid to make zinc sulfate crystals. Explain why this method may give an impure product, and describe a better preparation route.
**Answer**: Zinc carbonate reacts with sulfuric acid to form zinc sulfate, water and carbon dioxide, but the insoluble excess solid must be filtered carefully; if excess carbonate or side products remain, the crystals may be impure. A better route is to add excess zinc oxide to warm dilute sulfuric acid, filter off the unreacted zinc oxide, then concentrate the filtrate and crystallise zinc sulfate.
**Explanation**: The key is choosing an insoluble base method and removing excess solid before crystallisation. Common errors are evaporating to dryness or forgetting filtration.
**Tags**: salt-preparation, zinc-sulfate, excess-base, crystallisation
