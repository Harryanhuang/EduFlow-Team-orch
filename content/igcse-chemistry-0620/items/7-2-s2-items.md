# Items: 7.2 — Salt preparation (supplementary batch)


### Question Q-7.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe how you would prepare a pure, dry sample of copper(II) sulfate crystals starting from dilute sulfuric acid and an insoluble base.
**Answer**: 1. Warm dilute sulfuric acid in a beaker.
2. Add excess copper(II) oxide (insoluble base) and stir until no more dissolves.
3. Filter to remove excess copper(II) oxide.
4. Heat the filtrate to the point of crystallisation (or evaporate some water).
5. Allow to cool so crystals form.
6. Filter and dry the crystals between filter paper.
**Explanation**: Students must include all steps: excess base, filtration, crystallisation, drying. Common errors: saying "evaporate to dryness" (gives powder, not crystals) or forgetting to remove excess base.
**Tags**: salt-preparation, copper-sulfate, crystallisation, insoluble-base

### Question Q-7.2-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why potassium sulfate can be prepared by titration of potassium hydroxide with sulfuric acid, but copper(II) sulfate cannot. Describe the titration method for potassium sulfate.
**Answer**: Potassium hydroxide is a soluble base (alkali), so it can be titrated with acid using an indicator. Copper(II) oxide is insoluble, so titration is not possible.
Titration method: 1. Add measured volume of KOH to a flask with indicator. 2. Add H2SO4 from burette until colour changes. 3. Repeat without indicator. 4. Evaporate solution to crystallise potassium sulfate.
**Explanation**: Students must link solubility of the base to the choice of method. A common error is suggesting titration with an insoluble base.
**Tags**: salt-preparation, titration, soluble-base, potassium-sulfate

### Question Q-7.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student wants to prepare lead(II) chloride. (a) Explain why reacting lead(II) oxide with hydrochloric acid may not give a pure product. (b) Suggest an alternative method using precipitation. Include a balanced ionic equation.
**Answer**: (a) Lead(II) chloride is insoluble in cold water, so it would coat the lead(II) oxide and prevent further reaction. Also, lead(II) oxide may not fully react.
(b) Mix aqueous solutions of lead(II) nitrate and sodium chloride. Lead(II) chloride precipitates.
Ionic equation: Pb²⁺(aq) + 2Cl⁻(aq) → PbCl2(s)
Filter, wash with distilled water, and dry the precipitate.
**Explanation**: Students must recognise that PbCl2's low solubility prevents the acid-base method and requires precipitation. Common error: not recognising PbCl2 as an exception to chloride solubility.
**Tags**: salt-preparation, precipitation, lead-chloride, solubility, ionic-equation

### Question Q-7.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Ammonium sulfate is a fertiliser made by reacting ammonia with sulfuric acid. (a) Write the balanced equation. (b) Why is this method suitable for making ammonium sulfate on an industrial scale? (c) How would you test that a white solid is indeed ammonium sulfate?
**Answer**: (a) 2NH3(g) + H2SO4(aq) → (NH4)2SO4(aq)
(b) Both reactants are soluble, the reaction goes to completion, and ammonium sulfate crystallises readily from solution. It's a simple neutralisation that can be scaled.
(c) Test for ammonium: add NaOH and warm — ammonia gas is produced (turns damp red litmus blue). Test for sulfate: add acidified BaCl2 — white precipitate of BaSO4 forms.
**Explanation**: Students need to connect the industrial context with the chemistry. Common errors: wrong equation (forgetting the 2:1 ratio) or not testing both ions.
**Tags**: ammonium-sulfate, fertiliser, neutralisation, ion-test
