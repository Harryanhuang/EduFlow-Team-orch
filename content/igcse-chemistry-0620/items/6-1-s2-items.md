# Items: 6.1 — Rate collision theory (supplementary)


### Question Q-6.1-08
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why increasing the concentration of a reactant in solution increases the rate of reaction. Your answer should refer to collision theory.
**Answer**: Increasing concentration means more reactant particles per unit volume. This increases the frequency of collisions between reacting particles per second. More collisions per second means more successful collisions (those with energy ≥ activation energy), so the rate of reaction increases.
**Explanation**: Students must link concentration → particle density → collision frequency → rate. A common error is saying particles "move faster" (concentration doesn't affect speed, only temperature does).
**Tags**: collision-theory, concentration, rate, frequency

### Question Q-6.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student investigates the reaction between magnesium ribbon and dilute hydrochloric acid by measuring the volume of hydrogen gas produced every 10 seconds. (a) Sketch and label a graph of volume against time for the reaction at 25°C. (b) On the same axes, sketch the curve if the experiment is repeated at 40°C. (c) Explain the difference between the two curves using collision theory.
**Answer**: (a) The graph starts steep and curves to a horizontal plateau (all reactant used up).
(b) The 40°C curve starts steeper (faster initial rate) but reaches the same final volume.
(c) At 40°C, particles have more kinetic energy. More particles have energy ≥ activation energy, and collisions are more frequent. Both factors increase the rate. The final volume is the same because the amount of reactant hasn't changed.
**Explanation**: Students must connect the graph shape to the chemical reality. Common errors: drawing the 40°C curve reaching a higher final volume, or saying "more energy" without referencing activation energy.
**Tags**: rate-graph, temperature, collision-theory, activation-energy, experiment
