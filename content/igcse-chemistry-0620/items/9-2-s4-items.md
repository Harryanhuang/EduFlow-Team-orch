# Items: 9.2 — Reactivity series extraction (additional batch)


### Question Q-9.2-13
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe the blast furnace extraction of iron from haematite (Fe2O3). Include the role of coke and limestone, and write the key equations.
**Answer**: Raw materials: haematite (Fe2O3), coke (C), limestone (CaCO3), hot air.
Coke burns: C + O2 → CO2 (exothermic, provides heat)
CO2 + C → 2CO (produces reducing agent)
Reduction: Fe2O3 + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO2
Limestone: CaCO3 → CaO + CO2; CaO + SiO2 → CaSiO3 (slag, removes sand impurity)
Molten iron collects at the bottom and is tapped off.
**Explanation**: Students must include all three key equations. A common error is saying carbon directly reduces iron oxide (it's actually CO that does most of the reducing).
**Tags**: blast-furnace, iron-extraction, haematite, coke, limestone, reduction

### Question Q-9.2-14
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Recycling metals is important economically and environmentally. (a) Compare the energy required to produce aluminium from its ore versus recycling aluminium. Give approximate values. (b) State three benefits of recycling metals. (c) Why is recycling steel easier than recycling aluminium alloys?
**Answer**: (a) Extracting aluminium by electrolysis requires about 15 kWh/kg. Recycling aluminium requires only about 0.7 kWh/kg — about 95% energy saving.
(b) Any three: saves energy; conserves finite ore resources; reduces landfill waste; reduces mining damage; reduces CO2 emissions.
(c) Steel is magnetic and can be easily separated from waste using magnets. Steel also has a simpler composition. Aluminium alloys contain various metals that are harder to separate, though melting down aluminium scrap is still straightforward.
**Explanation**: Students must provide data for the energy comparison. A common error is saying recycling "uses no energy" — it still requires melting.
**Tags**: metal-recycling, energy, environment, aluminium, steel

### Question Q-9.2-15
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A metal oxide MO is reduced by hydrogen gas to produce the metal M and water. 7.95 g of MO produces 6.35 g of M. (Ar: O = 16) (a) Calculate the relative atomic mass of M. (b) Identify the metal M. (c) Write the balanced equation for the reaction.
**Answer**: (a) Mass of oxygen in MO = 7.95 − 6.35 = 1.60 g.
Moles of O = 1.60/16 = 0.10 mol.
Since MO has 1:1 ratio, moles of M = 0.10 mol.
Ar of M = 6.35/0.10 = 63.5. This is copper (Cu).
(b) Copper.
(c) CuO(s) + H2(g) → Cu(s) + H2O(g)
**Explanation**: This requires multi-step calculation. A common error is dividing 7.95 by 0.10 (giving 79.5) instead of using just the metal mass.
**Tags**: reduction, calculation, metal-identification, hydrogen-reduction

### Question Q-9.2-16
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Titanium is extracted from titanium(IV) chloride by reduction with magnesium. (a) Write the balanced equation. (b) Why can't titanium be extracted by reduction with carbon like iron? (c) Suggest why this makes titanium more expensive than iron.
**Answer**: (a) TiCl4(l) + 2Mg(s) → Ti(s) + 2MgCl2(s)
(b) Titanium forms titanium carbide with carbon, not pure titanium. Also, Ti is above carbon in reactivity for this compound, so carbon is not a strong enough reducing agent.
(c) The process requires high temperature, an inert atmosphere (argon) to prevent oxidation of Ti and Mg, and expensive magnesium metal. These factors make it a batch process (not continuous like the blast furnace), increasing cost.
**Explanation**: Students must explain why carbon reduction doesn't work for Ti. A common error is saying "Ti is too reactive" without explaining the carbide issue.
**Tags**: titanium, extraction, magnesium-reduction, cost, batch-process
