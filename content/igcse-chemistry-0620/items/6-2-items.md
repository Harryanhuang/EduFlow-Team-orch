# Items: 6.2 — Reversible reactions, equilibrium


### Question Q-6.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is meant by a reversible reaction? Give one example.
**Answer**: A reversible reaction can proceed in both the forward and backward directions. It is represented by the symbol ⇌. Example: heating hydrated copper(II) sulfate: CuSO4·5H2O(s) ⇌ CuSO4(s) + 5H2O(g). Heating drives off water (forward); adding water reverses it.
**Explanation**: Students must identify the double direction. A common error is confusing reversible reactions with reactions that can be "undone" by a different chemical process.
**Tags**: reversible-reaction, definition, example, symbol

### Question Q-6.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is dynamic equilibrium? State two conditions that must be met for it to occur.
**Answer**: Dynamic equilibrium occurs in a closed system when the forward and backward reactions happen at the same rate. The concentrations of reactants and products remain constant (but not necessarily equal). Conditions: the system must be closed (nothing added or removed) and the reaction must be reversible.
**Explanation**: Students must identify "same rate" and "constant concentrations." A common error is saying "equal concentrations" — they are constant, not equal.
**Tags**: dynamic-equilibrium, definition, closed-system, conditions

### Question Q-6.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: For the reaction N2(g) + 3H2(g) ⇌ 2NH3(g) ΔH = −92 kJ/mol, predict the effect of: (a) increasing temperature, (b) increasing pressure, (c) adding a catalyst.
**Answer**: (a) Increasing temperature shifts equilibrium to the left (endothermic direction) — less ammonia produced.
(b) Increasing pressure shifts equilibrium to the right (fewer moles of gas: 4 → 2) — more ammonia.
(c) A catalyst has no effect on the position of equilibrium — it speeds up both forward and backward reactions equally. It only helps equilibrium to be reached faster.
**Explanation**: Students must apply Le Chatelier's principle. A common error is saying a catalyst increases yield.
**Tags**: le-chatelier, temperature, pressure, catalyst, haber-process

### Question Q-6.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why the Haber process operates at 450°C and 200 atm rather than at room temperature or very high pressure.
**Answer**: Room temperature would give a higher yield (exothermic forward reaction) but the rate would be too slow. 450°C is a compromise — reasonable yield with acceptable rate. Very high pressure would increase yield but is expensive and dangerous. 200 atm is a practical and economic compromise. An iron catalyst is used to increase the rate.
**Explanation**: Students must balance yield, rate, and economic factors. A common error is saying "high temperature gives high yield" for an exothermic reaction.
**Tags**: haber-process, compromise, yield, rate, economic-factors
