# Items: 10.1 — The atmosphere, air and water (additional batch)


### Question Q-10.1-10
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student is given a colourless liquid and asked to determine whether it is pure water. (a) Describe TWO chemical tests the student could perform and state the expected results if the liquid is water. (b) Explain why a boiling point measurement would provide stronger evidence than the chemical tests alone. (c) The boiling point of the liquid is measured as 102°C. Is this pure water? Explain what this result indicates.
**Answer**: (a) Chemical test 1: Add a few drops to white anhydrous copper(II) sulfate — it turns blue (CuSO4·5H2O forms). Chemical test 2: Dip blue anhydrous cobalt(II) chloride paper — it turns pink (CoCl2·6H2O forms). (b) The chemical tests only detect the PRESENCE of water, not its purity. Any liquid containing water (e.g. salt solution) would give a positive result. Boiling point is specific: pure water boils at exactly 100°C at standard pressure. (c) 102°C is not pure water. The elevated boiling point indicates dissolved substances are present (boiling point elevation). This is an impure water sample.
**Explanation**: Students must distinguish between testing for presence of water and testing for purity. A common error is thinking positive chemical tests prove purity.
**Tags**: water-test, copper-sulfate, cobalt-chloride, purity, boiling-point, analysis

### Question Q-10.1-11
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how oxides of nitrogen and sulfur dioxide contribute to acid rain. Include the sources of these pollutants and the effects of acid rain on the environment.
**Answer**: Sources: oxides of nitrogen from vehicle engines (high temperature causes N2 + O2 → NOx); sulfur dioxide from burning fossil fuels containing sulfur impurities.
In the atmosphere: SO2 + H2O → H2SO3 (sulfurous acid); further oxidation gives H2SO4. NOx reacts with water to form nitric acid.
Effects: acidifies lakes and rivers (kills fish and aquatic life); damages trees; corrodes limestone buildings and metal structures; leaches toxic metals from soil.
**Explanation**: Students must link the pollutant to the acid formed and the environmental damage. A common error is saying CO2 causes acid rain (it causes ocean acidification, not acid rain).
**Tags**: acid-rain, sulfur-dioxide, nitrogen-oxides, pollution, environmental-impact

### Question Q-10.1-12
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Water treatment involves several stages before it is safe to drink. (a) Describe the stages of water treatment and explain the purpose of each. (b) Why is chlorination essential even after filtration? (c) A town's water supply comes from a river downstream of a sewage works. What additional treatment might be needed?
**Answer**: (a) Stages:
1. Screening — removes large debris (sticks, leaves).
2. Sedimentation — fine particles settle in a tank.
3. Filtration through sand/gravel beds — removes remaining solids and some microorganisms.
4. Chlorination — kills remaining bacteria and microorganisms.
(b) Filtration removes solids but not dissolved bacteria and viruses. Chlorination kills these pathogens and also provides residual protection in the pipes.
(c) Additional treatment may include: activated carbon filtration (removes organic pollutants), UV treatment (kills additional pathogens), or advanced oxidation to remove pharmaceutical residues.
**Explanation**: Students must describe all stages. A common error is thinking filtration alone makes water safe to drink.
**Tags**: water-treatment, filtration, chlorination, purification, public-health
