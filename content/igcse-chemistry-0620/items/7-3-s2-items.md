# Items: 7.3 — Ion and gas identification (supplementary batch)


### Question Q-7.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A student is given an unknown white solid. Describe a series of tests that would identify whether it contains carbonate, sulfate, or chloride ions.
**Answer**: Carbonate test: Add dilute acid — effervescence indicates CO2 (turns limewater milky).
Sulfate test: Add acidified BaCl2 solution — white precipitate of BaSO4 confirms sulfate.
Chloride test: Add acidified AgNO3 solution — white precipitate of AgCl confirms chloride.
Note: The acid is added first in the sulfate and chloride tests to remove carbonate interference (which would also give a white precipitate with Ba²⁺).
**Explanation**: Students must describe all three tests and understand the order (carbonate test first, and acidify before sulfate/chloride tests). A common error is not acidifying the BaCl2 or AgNO3.
**Tags**: ion-test, carbonate, sulfate, chloride, identification

### Question Q-7.3-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe the flame test procedure and the characteristic flame colours produced by lithium, sodium, potassium, calcium and copper(II) compounds.
**Answer**: Procedure: Dip a clean platinum or nichrome wire into concentrated HCl, then into the solid sample. Hold in a blue Bunsen flame.
Colours: Li⁺ = red/crimson; Na⁺ = yellow; K⁺ = lilac; Ca²⁺ = brick-red; Cu²⁺ = blue-green.
**Explanation**: Students must know the colours. Common errors: confusing lithium (crimson) with calcium (brick-red), or not cleaning the wire between tests.
**Tags**: flame-test, metal-ion, colour, identification

### Question Q-7.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A solution contains either iron(II) or iron(III) ions. (a) Describe how aqueous sodium hydroxide can distinguish between them, including the observations. (b) Write the ionic equations for both reactions. (c) What happens to a precipitate of iron(II) hydroxide if left standing in air?
**Answer**: (a) With NaOH: Fe²⁺ gives a green precipitate of Fe(OH)2. Fe³⁺ gives a brown/rust-coloured precipitate of Fe(OH)3.
(b) Fe²⁺(aq) + 2OH⁻(aq) → Fe(OH)2(s); Fe³⁺(aq) + 3OH⁻(aq) → Fe(OH)3(s)
(c) The green Fe(OH)2 slowly turns brown as it is oxidised by air to Fe(OH)3.
**Explanation**: Students must link the colour to the oxidation state. A common error is confusing the two precipitate colours or not writing balanced ionic equations.
**Tags**: iron, precipitation, sodium-hydroxide, oxidation-state, ionic-equation

### Question Q-7.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A colourless gas is produced when zinc reacts with dilute sulfuric acid. (a) Name and give a test for this gas. (b) When the same acid reacts with a carbonate, a different gas is produced. Name this gas and give the test. (c) How could you distinguish between these two gases using only a lit splint and limewater?
**Answer**: (a) Hydrogen. Test: holds a lighted splint to the gas — it burns with a "pop" sound.
(b) Carbon dioxide. Test: bubbles the gas through limewater — it turns milky/cloudy.
(c) A lit splint: hydrogen will pop; CO2 will extinguish the flame. Limewater: CO2 turns it milky; hydrogen has no effect.
**Explanation**: Students must know both gas tests and how to distinguish them. Common errors: confusing the hydrogen "pop" test with oxygen's "relights a glowing splint" test.
**Tags**: gas-test, hydrogen, carbon-dioxide, identification
