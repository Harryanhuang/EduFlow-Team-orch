# Items: 2.5 — Giant covalent structures (diamond, graphite, silica)

### Question Q-2.4-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why does diamond have a very high melting point?
**Answer**: Diamond has a giant covalent structure where each carbon atom forms four strong covalent bonds in a rigid 3D tetrahedral lattice. Breaking this structure requires breaking many strong covalent bonds, needing a large amount of energy.
**Explanation**: Many strong covalent bonds to break. A common error is saying "strong intermolecular forces."
**Tags**: diamond, melting-point, covalent-network

### Question Q-2.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Why can graphite be used as a lubricant?
**Answer**: Graphite consists of layers of carbon atoms held by weak forces between the layers. The layers can easily slide over each other, making graphite slippery and useful as a lubricant.
**Explanation**: Sliding layers. A common error is saying the layers are "smooth."
**Tags**: graphite, lubricant, layers

### Question Q-2.4-09
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Both diamond and graphite are forms of carbon. What is the term for different forms of the same element?
**Answer**: Allotropes. Diamond and graphite are allotropes of carbon — different structural forms of the same element in the same physical state.
**Explanation**: Same element, different structure. A common error is saying "isotopes."
**Tags**: allotropes, carbon, diamond, graphite, definition

### Question Q-2.4-10
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why silica (SiO₂) is used to make glass.
**Answer**: Silica has a giant covalent structure that forms a rigid 3D network. When melted and cooled rapidly, it forms an amorphous (non-crystalline) solid — glass. Its hardness and transparency make it useful.
**Explanation**: Giant covalent → can be melted → amorphous solid. A common error is saying silica is "a simple molecule."
**Tags**: silica, glass, covalent-network
