# Items: 6.4 — Catalysts and enzyme action

### Question Q-6.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a catalyst? Give one example of a catalyst used in an industrial process.
**Answer**: A catalyst is a substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without being used up. It is not consumed and can be reused. Example: iron in the Haber process (making ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen).
**Explanation**: Increases rate, not consumed. A common error is saying a catalyst "makes the reaction happen" (it only speeds up reactions that would happen anyway).
**Tags**: catalyst, definition, Haber-process, iron

### Question Q-6.4-02
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how enzymes act as biological catalysts.
**Answer**: Enzymes are protein molecules that act as catalysts in living organisms. They have a specific active site shape that substrate molecules fit into (lock and key model). The enzyme-substrate complex lowers the activation energy for the reaction. After the reaction, the enzyme is unchanged and can catalyse another reaction.
**Explanation**: Lock and key, active site, unchanged. A common error is saying enzymes are "used up."
**Tags**: enzyme, biological-catalyst, lock-and-key, active-site

### Question Q-6.4-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why do enzymes stop working at high temperatures?
**Answer**: At high temperatures, the enzyme molecule vibrates more and the bonds holding its shape break. The active site changes shape (denaturation), so the substrate no longer fits. The reaction rate drops to zero. This is irreversible.
**Explanation**: Denaturation = active site shape lost. A common error is saying the enzyme "dies" (enzymes are not alive).
**Tags**: enzyme, denaturation, temperature, rate

### Question Q-6.4-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why enzymes are specific to particular reactions.
**Answer**: Each enzyme has a uniquely shaped active site that only fits one specific substrate (lock and key model). Only the correct substrate can bind to the active site and react. This means each enzyme catalyses only one type of reaction.
**Explanation**: Active site shape matches specific substrate. A common error is saying enzymes "choose" which reaction to catalyse.
**Tags**: enzyme, specificity, lock-and-key

### Question Q-6.4-05
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student investigates the effect of pH on enzyme activity. Describe the expected results and explain why pH affects enzyme activity.
**Answer**: The graph of rate vs pH shows a peak at the optimum pH. At pH values away from the optimum, the rate decreases. Extreme pH denatures the enzyme by breaking bonds in the active site, changing its shape so the substrate no longer fits.
**Explanation**: Optimum pH peak, denaturation at extremes. A common error is saying the enzyme is "destroyed" at wrong pH.
**Tags**: enzyme, pH, optimum, denaturation, experiment
