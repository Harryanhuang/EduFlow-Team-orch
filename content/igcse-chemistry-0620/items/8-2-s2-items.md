### Question Q-8.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Compare the reactions of lithium and potassium with water. Include observations and explain the difference in terms of reactivity.
**Answer**: Potassium reacts much more vigorously than lithium with water — it melts into a ball, moves rapidly across the surface, and ignites with a lilac flame. Lithium fizzes steadily and moves slowly. Potassium is more reactive because its outer electron is further from the nucleus and more shielded, so it is lost more easily.
**Explanation**: Students must link observation to position in the group. A common error is saying the difference is due to "more energy" rather than atomic structure.
**Tags**: group-1, reactivity-trend, water-reaction, observation

### Question Q-8.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe the trend in reactivity of the halogens (Group VII) going down the group. Explain this trend in terms of atomic structure.
**Answer**: Reactivity decreases going down Group VII (chlorine > bromine > iodine). As atoms get larger down the group, the outer shell is further from the nucleus and more shielded. The attraction for an incoming electron is weaker, so it is harder to gain an electron, reducing reactivity.
**Explanation**: This is the opposite trend to Group I. A common error is applying the Group I trend to Group VII without recognising the different mechanism (losing vs gaining electrons).
**Tags**: group-7, halogen, reactivity-trend, atomic-structure

### Question Q-8.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Chlorine gas is bubbled through aqueous potassium bromide. (a) State and explain the observation. (b) Write an ionic equation for the reaction. (c) Why does bromine not react with aqueous potassium chloride?
**Answer**: (a) The solution turns orange/brown as bromine is formed. Chlorine is more reactive than bromine and displaces it from potassium bromide.
(b) Cl2(aq) + 2Br⁻(aq) → 2Cl⁻(aq) + Br2(aq)
(c) Bromine is less reactive than chlorine, so it cannot displace chloride ions. A halogen can only displace a less reactive halogen from its salt.
**Explanation**: This requires knowledge of displacement reactions, ionic equations, and reactivity trends. Common errors: wrong equation (not balanced), or stating the wrong colour for bromine solution.
**Tags**: halogen, displacement, ionic-equation, reactivity

### Question Q-8.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Iron is a transition element. (a) State three properties of iron or its compounds that are typical of transition elements. (b) Explain why iron is used as a catalyst in the Haber process. (c) Iron forms two different ions, Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺. How does this relate to the name "transition element"?
**Answer**: (a) Any three: forms coloured compounds (e.g. green Fe²⁺ / brown Fe³⁺ solutions), has variable oxidation states, acts as a catalyst, forms complex ions.
(b) Iron provides an alternative reaction pathway with lower activation energy, increasing the rate without being consumed.
(c) Transition elements can form stable ions with different charges (oxidation states). Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺ demonstrate this variable oxidation state.
**Explanation**: This tests multiple aspects of transition element chemistry. A common error is confusing transition elements with Group I elements, which only form one stable ion.
**Tags**: transition-element, iron, catalyst, variable-oxidation-state, coloured-compounds

### Question Q-8.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student adds aqueous silver nitrate followed by dilute nitric acid to three separate samples containing chloride, bromide and iodide ions. Predict the observations for each and write the ionic equation for the chloride test. How would adding aqueous ammonia help confirm the identity?
**Answer**: Chloride: white precipitate of AgCl. Bromide: cream precipitate of AgBr. Iodide: yellow precipitate of AgI.
Ionic equation for chloride: Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s)
Adding dilute aqueous ammonia: AgCl dissolves, AgBr is partially soluble, AgI is insoluble. This confirms the identity of the halide.
**Explanation**: Students need to know the precipitate colours and solubility tests. A common error is confusing the precipitate colours or not mentioning the acidification step (to remove carbonate interference).
**Tags**: halide-test, silver-nitrate, precipitate, identification
