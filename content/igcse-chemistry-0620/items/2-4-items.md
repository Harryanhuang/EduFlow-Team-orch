# Items: 2.4 — Structures and properties

### Question Q-2.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Why do ionic compounds have high melting and boiling points?
**Answer**: Ionic compounds have a giant lattice structure with strong electrostatic forces of attraction between oppositely charged ions in all directions. A large amount of energy is needed to overcome these forces.
**Explanation**: Strong forces in a giant lattice. A common error is saying "strong bonds" — it's inter-ionic forces, not bonds between molecules.
**Tags**: ionic-compounds, melting-point, giant-lattice

### Question Q-2.4-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Why do simple molecular substances (e.g., CO₂, H₂O) have low melting points?
**Answer**: The molecules are held together by weak intermolecular forces. Only these weak forces need to be overcome during melting, not the strong covalent bonds within each molecule.
**Explanation**: Weak intermolecular forces break, not covalent bonds. A common error is saying "covalent bonds are weak."
**Tags**: simple-molecular, melting-point, intermolecular-forces

### Question Q-2.4-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Diamond and graphite are both forms of carbon. Explain why diamond is hard while graphite is soft and slippery.
**Answer**: In diamond, each carbon atom forms four strong covalent bonds in a giant 3D tetrahedral lattice, making it very hard. In graphite, each carbon atom forms three covalent bonds in layers. The weak forces between layers allow them to slide over each other.
**Explanation**: 4 bonds (3D rigid) vs. 3 bonds (layered + sliding). A common error is saying graphite has "weaker bonds."
**Tags**: diamond, graphite, allotropes, structure

### Question Q-2.4-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why is graphite able to conduct electricity?
**Answer**: Each carbon atom in graphite forms only three covalent bonds, leaving one delocalised electron per atom. These delocalised electrons are free to move between the layers, allowing electrical conduction.
**Explanation**: Delocalised electrons between layers. A common error is saying graphite conducts because it is "a metal."
**Tags**: graphite, conductivity, delocalised-electrons

### Question Q-2.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why does silica (SiO₂) have a high melting point even though it contains only covalent bonds?
**Answer**: Silica has a giant covalent (macromolecular) structure in which each silicon atom is bonded to four oxygen atoms and each oxygen to two silicon atoms in a 3D lattice. All atoms are held by strong covalent bonds, requiring large amounts of energy to break.
**Explanation**: Giant covalent network. A common error is treating SiO₂ as a simple molecule.
**Tags**: silica, macromolecular, melting-point, covalent-network

### Question Q-2.4-06
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Describe the structure of a metal and explain why metals are malleable.
**Answer**: Metals consist a giant lattice of positive ions in a sea of delocalised electrons. The layers of ions can slide over each other without breaking the metallic bonding, making metals malleable (can be hammered into shape) and ductile.
**Explanation**: Sliding layers, not breaking bonds. A common error is saying metals "bend because the bonds are flexible."
**Tags**: metallic-structure, malleable, ductile
