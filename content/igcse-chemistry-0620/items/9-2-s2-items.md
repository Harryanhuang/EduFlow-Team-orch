# Items: 9.2 — Reactivity series extraction (supplementary)


### Question Q-9.2-08
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why aluminium is extracted by electrolysis but iron is extracted by reduction with carbon. Refer to the reactivity series in your answer.
**Answer**: Aluminium is more reactive than carbon (higher in the reactivity series). Carbon cannot displace aluminium from aluminium oxide, so electrolysis is needed to provide enough energy to separate aluminium from oxygen. Iron is less reactive than carbon, so carbon can reduce iron oxide to iron by displacing the iron.
**Explanation**: Students must link the extraction method to position in the reactivity series. A common error is saying electrolysis is "cheaper" — it's actually more expensive but necessary for reactive metals.
**Tags**: reactivity-series, extraction, electrolysis, carbon-reduction

### Question Q-9.2-09
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A metal X reacts vigorously with cold water, producing hydrogen gas and an alkaline solution. Metal Y does not react with water but reacts slowly with dilute acid. Metal Z does not react with water or dilute acid. (a) Place X, Y and Z in order of reactivity. (b) Suggest possible identities for X, Y and Z from the reactivity series. (c) How could you extract metal Z from its oxide?
**Answer**: (a) X > Y > Z (most to least reactive).
(b) X could be sodium or potassium (reacts vigorously with cold water). Y could be zinc or iron (reacts with acid but not cold water). Z could be copper or silver (no reaction with water or dilute acid).
(c) Since Z is unreactive (below hydrogen), it could be extracted by heating its oxide with carbon, or in the case of very unreactive metals, may be found native (as the pure element).
**Explanation**: Students must apply the reactivity series to unknown metals. A common error is placing Y above X because "acid reacts faster than water" — the key is whether they react with cold water at all.
**Tags**: reactivity-series, metal-identification, extraction-method, water-reaction
