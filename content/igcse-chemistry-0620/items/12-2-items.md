# Items: 12.2 — Gas collection, rate experiments, ion tests

### Question Q-12.2-01
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How would you collect a sample of hydrogen gas produced in a reaction?
**Answer**: By downward displacement of water (collecting over water) using an inverted measuring cylinder or gas syringe. Hydrogen is only slightly soluble in water, so this method works well.
**Explanation**: Collect over water. A common error is using upward delivery (hydrogen is lighter than air and would escape).
**Tags**: gas-collection, hydrogen, method

### Question Q-12.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: In a rate experiment, calcium carbonate reacts with hydrochloric acid and the mass of the reaction vessel decreases over time. Explain why and how the rate can be determined.
**Answer**: Carbon dioxide gas is produced and escapes from the open vessel, causing the mass to decrease. The rate can be determined from the gradient of a mass-vs-time graph — a steeper gradient means a faster rate.
**Explanation**: Mass loss = gas escaping. A common error is saying the mass should stay constant.
**Tags**: rate-experiment, mass-loss, graph, CaCO3

### Question Q-12.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How do you test for the presence of ammonium ions (NH₄⁺) in a solution?
**Answer**: Add sodium hydroxide solution and warm gently. Ammonia gas is produced (NH₄⁺ + OH⁻ → NH₃ + H₂O). Test with damp red litmus paper — it turns blue, confirming ammonia.
**Explanation**: NaOH + warm → NH₃ → blue litmus. A common error is not warming the mixture.
**Tags**: ammonium-test, NH4+, litmus

### Question Q-12.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How do you test for the presence of chloride ions (Cl⁻) in a solution?
**Answer**: Add dilute nitric acid followed by aqueous silver nitrate. A white precipitate of silver chloride (AgCl) confirms chloride ions.
**Explanation**: Ag⁺ + Cl⁻ → AgCl (white ppt). Nitric acid removes interfering ions. A common error is adding HCl instead of HNO₃.
**Tags**: chloride-test, Cl-, silver-nitrate

### Question Q-12.2-05
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: How do you distinguish between iron(II) and iron(III) ions using aqueous sodium hydroxide?
**Answer**: Add aqueous NaOH. Iron(II) ions produce a green precipitate of iron(II) hydroxide. Iron(III) ions produce a reddish-brown precipitate of iron(III) hydroxide. Neither precipitate dissolves in excess NaOH.
**Explanation**: Green = Fe²⁺; brown = Fe³⁺. A common error is confusing the colours.
**Tags**: iron-test, Fe2+, Fe3+, NaOH-test

### Question Q-12.2-06
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: How would you test for the presence of water?
**Answer**: Add the liquid to anhydrous copper(II) sulfate (white). If water is present, it turns blue, forming hydrated copper(II) sulfate: CuSO₄(s) + 5H₂O(l) → CuSO₄·5H₂O(s).
**Explanation**: White → blue = water present. A common error is saying pH paper.
**Tags**: water-test, copper-sulfate, anhydrous
