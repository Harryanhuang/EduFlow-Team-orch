# Items: 6.1 — Rate of reaction: factors and collision theory

### Question Q-6.1-01
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how a catalyst increases the rate of reaction.
**Answer**: A catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy. More particles have enough energy to react, so the rate increases. The catalyst is not consumed and can be reused.
**Explanation**: Alternative pathway, lower Ea. A common error is saying it "increases particle energy."
**Tags**: catalyst, activation-energy, rate, pathway

### Question Q-6.1-02
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Sketch and label a reaction profile showing the effect of a catalyst on an exothermic reaction.
**Answer**: The profile shows reactants at a higher energy level than products (exothermic). The uncatalysed curve has a higher peak (activation energy Ea). The catalysed curve has a lower peak (Ea catalysed). ΔH (overall energy change) is the same for both.
**Explanation**: Lower peak, same start and finish. A common error is changing ΔH for the catalysed path.
**Tags**: reaction-profile, catalyst, exothermic, diagram

### Question Q-6.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A student investigates the effect of concentration on the rate of reaction between sodium thiosulfate and hydrochloric acid. Describe what they would observe and how they would measure the rate.
**Answer**: The solution becomes cloudy as sulfur precipitates. The student places the flask over a marked X on paper and times how long until the X is no longer visible. Higher concentration = faster clouding = shorter time = greater rate.
**Explanation**: Cloudiness blocks visibility. A common error is measuring gas volume (no gas is produced here).
**Tags**: rate-experiment, thiosulfate, concentration, method

### Question Q-6.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why powdered calcium carbonate reacts faster with acid than lumps of the same mass.
**Answer**: Powder has a larger surface area exposed to the acid. More frequent collisions between reactant particles per unit time lead to a faster rate. The total amount of product is the same.
**Explanation**: Surface area → more collisions. A common error is saying the powder "has more energy."
**Tags**: surface-area, rate, CaCO3, collision-theory

### Question Q-6.1-05
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: State four factors that affect the rate of a chemical reaction.
**Answer**: (1) Concentration of reactants (or pressure for gases). (2) Temperature. (3) Surface area (particle size) of solid reactants. (4) Presence of a catalyst.
**Explanation**: Concentration, temperature, surface area, catalyst. A common error is including "volume of container."
**Tags**: rate, factors, foundation

### Question Q-6.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why increasing temperature increases the rate of reaction, in terms of collision theory.
**Answer**: Higher temperature means particles have more kinetic energy and move faster. This leads to (1) more frequent collisions per second and (2) more particles having energy ≥ activation energy, so a greater proportion of collisions are successful.
**Explanation**: Both frequency and energy of collisions increase. A common error is only mentioning one factor.
**Tags**: temperature, collision-theory, rate, kinetic-energy

### Question Q-6.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student compares the rate of reaction of magnesium with two different concentrations of hydrochloric acid. The graph of gas volume vs time shows two curves. Which curve represents the higher concentration and why?
**Answer**: The steeper curve represents the higher concentration. Higher concentration means more reactant particles per unit volume, leading to more frequent collisions and a faster rate. The steeper initial slope shows greater rate.
**Explanation**: Steeper = faster = higher concentration. A common error is picking the curve that produces more total gas (same amount of Mg gives same total gas).
**Tags**: rate, concentration, graph, Mg, HCl
