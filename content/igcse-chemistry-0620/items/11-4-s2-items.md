### Question Q-11.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe the structure of proteins and explain how amino acids are joined together.
**Answer**: Proteins are natural polymers (polyamides) made of amino acid monomers. Amino acids are joined by condensation polymerisation — the amine group of one reacts with the carboxylic acid group of another, forming a peptide (amide) link and releasing water. The chain can contain hundreds of amino acids.
**Explanation**: Students must identify the type of polymerisation (condensation) and the linkage (peptide/amide). A common error is saying addition polymerisation.
**Tags**: protein, amino-acid, condensation-polymerisation, peptide-link

### Question Q-11.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Starch and cellulose are both polymers of glucose. Explain how they differ in structure and why these differences are important for their biological functions.
**Answer**: Both are made from glucose monomers joined by condensation. In starch, glucose units are arranged in a coiled/helical structure, making it compact for energy storage. In cellulose, glucose units are arranged in straight chains that form strong fibres held together by hydrogen bonds, providing structural support in plant cell walls.
**Explanation**: Students must contrast storage vs structural function. A common error is saying starch and cellulose are "the same thing" because they both come from glucose.
**Tags**: starch, cellulose, glucose, carbohydrate, structure-function

### Question Q-11.4-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Simple proteins can be broken down into amino acids by hydrolysis. Describe how this process works and identify the conditions needed.
**Answer**: Hydrolysis is the reverse of condensation polymerisation. Water molecules are added to break the peptide (amide) links between amino acids. This requires either heating with dilute acid (acid hydrolysis) or the action of enzymes (enzymatic hydrolysis). Each peptide bond broken releases one amino acid.
**Explanation**: Students must recognise hydrolysis as the reverse of condensation. A common error is confusing hydrolysis with combustion or saying heat alone is sufficient.
**Tags**: hydrolysis, protein, amino-acid, digestion, peptide-link

### Question Q-11.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: The diagram below shows part of a protein chain with three amino acid residues. (a) Identify and draw a circle around one peptide link. (b) Draw the structures of the two amino acids that could have formed the first peptide link. (c) Name the type of polymerisation involved and identify the small molecule produced.
**Answer**: (a) The peptide link is the −CO−NH− group connecting two amino acid residues.
(b) Each amino acid has the general structure H2N−CH(R)−COOH. The specific R groups depend on the amino acids shown.
(c) Condensation polymerisation. Water (H2O) is the small molecule produced.
**Explanation**: This requires identifying structural features and working backwards from polymer to monomer. A common error is not recognising the peptide link structure or drawing the wrong functional groups.
**Tags**: protein, peptide-link, structure, condensation-polymerisation, diagram

### Question Q-11.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare and contrast addition polymerisation (e.g. poly(ethene)) and condensation polymerisation (e.g. nylon or protein) with reference to: (a) the monomers involved, (b) whether a small molecule is produced, (c) the structure of the polymer backbone, and (d) whether the polymer is likely to biodegrade.
**Answer**: (a) Addition: one type of monomer with a C=C double bond. Condensation: monomers with two different functional groups (e.g. −COOH and −OH, or −COOH and −NH2).
(b) Addition: no small molecule produced. Condensation: a small molecule (usually H2O) is released.
(c) Addition: carbon-only backbone (−C−C−C−). Condensation: backbone contains heteroatoms (e.g. −C−O−, −C−N−).
(d) Addition polymers generally do not biodegrade (strong C−C bonds). Condensation polymers may biodegrade (amide and ester links can be hydrolysed).
**Explanation**: This is a comprehensive comparison question. Students must address all four aspects. Common errors: saying addition polymerisation "joins two monomers" or confusing the two types of polymer backbone.
**Tags**: addition-polymer, condensation-polymer, comparison, biodegradation, nylon, protein
