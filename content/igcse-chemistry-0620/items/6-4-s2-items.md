# Items: 6.4 — Catalysts and enzymes (supplementary)


### Question Q-6.4-06
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Describe an experiment to show that manganese(IV) oxide acts as a catalyst in the decomposition of hydrogen peroxide.
**Answer**: 1. Set up a gas syringe or inverted measuring cylinder over water.
2. Add hydrogen peroxide solution to a flask with MnO2.
3. Measure the volume of oxygen gas produced at regular intervals.
4. Filter and dry the MnO2 after the reaction and weigh it — the mass is unchanged.
5. Repeat the experiment with the recovered MnO2 — it still catalyses the reaction.
This shows MnO2 speeds up the reaction and is not consumed.
**Explanation**: Students must show both that the rate increases and that the catalyst is recovered unchanged. A common error is only measuring gas volume without showing the catalyst is recovered.
**Tags**: catalyst, experiment, hydrogen-peroxide, manganese-oxide, rate

### Question Q-6.4-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Enzymes are biological catalysts. (a) Explain what is meant by enzyme specificity. (b) Why does an enzyme work best at a particular temperature (optimum temperature)? (c) What happens to an enzyme at very high temperatures?
**Answer**: (a) Enzyme specificity means each enzyme only catalyses one particular reaction (or type of reaction) because its active site has a specific shape that only fits one type of substrate molecule.
(b) At the optimum temperature, particles have enough energy for frequent successful collisions but the enzyme's shape is not damaged.
(c) At very high temperatures, the enzyme denatures — its active site changes shape and the substrate no longer fits. The reaction stops.
**Explanation**: Students must distinguish between the rate effect of temperature and denaturation. A common error is saying the enzyme "dies" — enzymes are not alive.
**Tags**: enzyme, specificity, optimum-temperature, denaturation, active-site

### Question Q-6.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: The graph below shows how the rate of an enzyme-catalysed reaction changes with pH. (a) Describe the shape of the graph. (b) Explain why the rate drops sharply at very low and very high pH. (c) How would the graph change if the experiment were repeated at a higher temperature? Explain.
**Answer**: (a) Bell-shaped curve: rate increases to a maximum at the optimum pH, then decreases.
(b) At extreme pH, the enzyme's active site changes shape (the acid/base disrupts the bonds holding the enzyme's structure). The substrate no longer fits — the enzyme denatures.
(c) At moderate higher temperature (below denaturation), the whole curve would shift upward (faster rate). At too high a temperature, the curve would flatten as the enzyme denatures.
**Explanation**: Students must explain both the bell shape and the temperature interaction. A common error is saying the substrate is "killed" by pH.
**Tags**: enzyme, pH, denaturation, graph, optimum

### Question Q-6.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the action of a chemical catalyst (e.g. iron in the Haber process) with an enzyme (e.g. amylase). Give two similarities and two differences.
**Answer**: Similarities: (1) Both lower the activation energy of the reaction. (2) Both are not consumed in the reaction and can be reused.
Differences: (1) Enzymes are highly specific — each catalyses only one reaction. Chemical catalysts are less specific. (2) Enzymes only work within a narrow range of temperature and pH. Chemical catalysts often work at high temperatures and are less sensitive to conditions.
**Explanation**: Students must make a structured comparison. Common errors: saying catalysts "provide energy" or that enzymes "only work in living things" (they work in vitro too).
**Tags**: catalyst, enzyme, comparison, specificity, Haber-process
