# Items: 9.1 — Properties and uses of metals

### Question Q-9.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: List three physical properties common to most metals.
**Answer**: (1) Good conductors of electricity. (2) Good conductors of heat. (3) Malleable (can be hammered into sheets) and ductile (can be drawn into wires). Also: shiny/lustrous appearance.
**Explanation**: Conductivity + malleability/ductility. A common error is saying "brittle" (that's non-metals).
**Tags**: metals, physical-properties, conductivity

### Question Q-9.1-02
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Compare the properties of aluminium and copper, giving one use for each based on its properties.
**Answer**: Aluminium: low density, good conductor, resists corrosion (oxide layer) → used for aircraft bodies, overhead power cables, food packaging. Copper: excellent electrical conductor, malleable, ductile → used for electrical wiring, plumbing, cookware.
**Explanation**: Properties determine uses. A common error is saying aluminium is used for wiring because it's "cheaper" — the key property is low density for overhead cables.
**Tags**: aluminium, copper, properties, uses

### Question Q-9.1-03
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student is given samples of pure iron and steel (an alloy of iron and carbon). (a) Describe and explain the difference in hardness between the two samples. (b) Draw a labelled diagram (in words) showing how the atomic arrangement differs between pure iron and steel. (c) Predict what would happen to the properties of steel if the carbon content was increased significantly, and explain why.
**Answer**: (a) Steel is harder than pure iron because carbon atoms are smaller than iron atoms and fit into the spaces between iron atoms, disrupting the regular layers and preventing them from sliding easily over each other. (b) Pure iron: regular, orderly arrangement of iron atoms in layers. Steel: iron atoms in layers with smaller carbon atoms interspersed between them, disrupting the regular pattern. (c) Increasing carbon content would make the steel harder and stronger but also more brittle. More carbon atoms create more disruption to the layer sliding, increasing hardness but reducing ductility.
**Explanation**: The key mechanism is atomic size mismatch disrupting sliding layers. A common error is saying alloys have "stronger chemical bonds" — the metallic bonding is similar, but the structural arrangement changes.
**Tags**: alloy, iron, steel, carbon, hardness, structure, explanation

### Question Q-9.1-04
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Name two alloys of iron and their main additional elements.
**Answer**: Steel (iron + carbon, typically 0.2-2% carbon). Stainless steel (iron + chromium + nickel). Brass is NOT an alloy of iron — it's copper + zinc.
**Explanation**: Steel = Fe + C; Stainless = Fe + Cr + Ni. A common error is confusing brass as an iron alloy.
**Tags**: steel, stainless-steel, iron, alloy

### Question Q-9.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why pure copper is used for electrical wiring rather than a copper alloy.
**Answer**: Pure copper has better electrical conductivity than copper alloys because the regular arrangement of atoms allows electrons to flow more freely. Alloying elements disrupt the structure and increase electrical resistance.
**Explanation**: Pure metal = better conductivity. A common error is saying alloys are "too hard" for wiring.
**Tags**: copper, conductivity, wiring, purity

### Question Q-9.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe the uses of brass, bronze, and stainless steel, relating each to its properties.
**Answer**: Brass (Cu + Zn): malleable, attractive → musical instruments, decorative items. Bronze (Cu + Sn): hard, corrosion-resistant → statues, bearings, ship propellers. Stainless steel (Fe + Cr + Ni): strong, corrosion-resistant → cutlery, surgical instruments, kitchen sinks.
**Explanation**: Properties → uses for each. A common error is swapping brass and bronze compositions.
**Tags**: brass, bronze, stainless-steel, uses, properties

### Question Q-9.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Pure gold (24 carat) is rarely used for jewellery. (a) Explain why 18 carat or 14 carat gold is preferred over pure gold for rings and bracelets. (b) If a ring is marked as 18 carat gold, what fraction of the metal is gold? (c) Name two metals commonly alloyed with gold and explain what each adds to the final product.
**Answer**: (a) Pure gold is very soft and malleable, so it scratches and deforms easily. Alloying makes it harder and more durable for everyday wear. (b) 18 carat means 18/24 = 3/4 (75%) gold. (c) Copper adds a reddish colour and increases hardness; silver or zinc can be added to produce white gold or alter the colour. The other metal atoms disrupt the regular gold lattice, making it harder.
**Explanation**: Purity vs practicality tradeoff. A common error is thinking "24 carat is best" for all uses, or not knowing that carat = purity fraction.
**Tags**: gold, alloy, carat, jewellery, properties
