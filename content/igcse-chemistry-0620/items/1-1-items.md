# Items: 1.1 — States of matter, kinetic particle theory

### Question Q-1.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Describe the arrangement and movement of particles in a solid.
**Answer**: Particles are arranged in a regular, closely packed pattern. They vibrate about fixed positions but do not move from place to place.
**Explanation**: Solids have fixed shape and volume. A common error is saying particles "don't move at all" — they vibrate.
**Tags**: solid, particle-model, arrangement

### Question Q-1.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Why does a liquid take the shape of its container but keep the same volume?
**Answer**: Liquid particles are close together but can slide past each other, allowing the liquid to flow and take the container's shape. The particles are still in contact, so the volume remains constant.
**Explanation**: Flow ≠ expansion. A common error is saying particles in a liquid are far apart.
**Tags**: liquid, particle-model, properties

### Question Q-1.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain, in terms of particles, why heating a gas in a sealed container increases its pressure.
**Answer**: Heating gives gas particles more kinetic energy, so they move faster. They collide with the container walls more frequently and with greater force, increasing pressure.
**Explanation**: Must mention both frequency and force of collisions. A common error is only mentioning one.
**Tags**: gas, pressure, heating, particle-model

### Question Q-1.1-04
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is meant by "changes of state are physical changes"?
**Answer**: A change of state does not alter the chemical composition of the substance — the particles remain the same type. Only the arrangement and energy of the particles change. The process is reversible.
**Explanation**: Physical ≠ chemical. A common error is saying new substances form during melting.
**Tags**: physical-change, state-change, definition

### Question Q-1.1-05
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Name the change of state when a solid turns directly into a gas without becoming a liquid.
**Answer**: Sublimation.
**Explanation**: Solid → gas directly. A common error is confusing it with evaporation.
**Tags**: sublimation, state-change, definition

### Question Q-1.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: During melting, the temperature of a pure substance remains constant even though heat is still being supplied. Explain why.
**Answer**: The energy supplied is used to overcome the attractive forces between particles (latent heat), allowing them to break free from their fixed positions. This energy goes into changing the state, not raising the temperature.
**Explanation**: Energy breaks bonds, not raises KE. A common error is saying "the substance stops absorbing heat."
**Tags**: melting, temperature, latent-heat, explanation

### Question Q-1.1-07
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A pure substance has a melting point of 0°C and a boiling point of 100°C. What state is it in at 25°C?
**Answer**: Liquid. At 25°C the temperature is between the melting point (0°C) and boiling point (100°C), so the substance is in the liquid state.
**Explanation**: Between melting and boiling = liquid. A common error is saying solid because 25°C is "cold."
**Tags**: state, temperature, melting-point, boiling-point

### Question Q-1.1-08
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How can you use melting point to determine whether a sample of ice is pure or impure?
**Answer**: Pure ice melts at exactly 0°C. If the sample melts over a range of temperatures or below 0°C, it is impure. Dissolved impurities lower and broaden the melting point.
**Explanation**: Pure substances have sharp, fixed melting points. A common error is saying impurities raise the melting point.
**Tags**: purity, melting-point, impurities
