### Question Q-4.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain the difference in products between the electrolysis of molten sodium chloride and aqueous sodium chloride.
**Answer**: Molten NaCl: sodium metal at cathode (Na⁺ + e⁻ → Na), chlorine at anode (2Cl⁻ → Cl2 + 2e⁻).
Aqueous NaCl: hydrogen at cathode (H⁺ from water is preferentially discharged), chlorine at anode (Cl⁻ discharged because concentrated).
The difference is at the cathode: water provides H⁺ which is preferentially discharged over Na⁺ in aqueous solution.
**Explanation**: Students must contrast the two cases. A common error is saying the anode product is different — it's chlorine in both cases for concentrated solutions.
**Tags**: electrolysis, molten-vs-aqueous, sodium-chloride, comparison

### Question Q-4.1-06
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: During the electrolysis of dilute sulfuric acid (acidified water), (a) Write the half-equations at each electrode. (b) Explain why the volume of gas at the cathode is twice that at the anode. (c) Write the overall equation.
**Answer**: (a) Cathode: 2H⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → H2(g). Anode: 4OH⁻(aq) → O2(g) + 2H2O(l) + 4e⁻ (or 2H2O(l) → O2(g) + 4H⁺(aq) + 4e⁻).
(b) For every 4 electrons: 2H2 molecules at cathode, 1O2 molecule at anode. Ratio 2:1.
(c) 2H2O(l) → 2H2(g) + O2(g)
**Explanation**: Students must link the mole ratio to the volume ratio (Avogadro's law). A common error is writing the wrong half-equation for the anode in acid.
**Tags**: electrolysis, sulfuric-acid, water, half-equation, gas-volume

### Question Q-4.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student electrolyses aqueous copper(II) sulfate using (a) inert carbon electrodes and (b) copper electrodes. Predict the products for each case and explain why the colour of the solution changes differently.
**Answer**: (a) Carbon electrodes: copper metal deposits at cathode (Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu). Oxygen at anode (OH⁻ from water discharged). The blue colour fades as Cu²⁺ is removed from solution.
(b) Copper electrodes: copper deposits at cathode. Copper anode dissolves (Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻). The concentration of Cu²⁺ stays constant, so the colour does not change. This is the principle of electroplating.
**Explanation**: Students must distinguish inert from active electrodes. A common error is predicting oxygen at the anode in case (b) — the copper electrode itself is oxidised.
**Tags**: electrolysis, copper-sulfate, active-electrode, inert-electrode, electroplating

### Question Q-4.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain how the selective discharge of ions during electrolysis depends on: (a) the position of the ion in the reactivity series, (b) the concentration of the solution, and (c) the nature of the electrode. Give one example for each factor.
**Answer**: (a) Reactivity series: less reactive elements are preferentially discharged. In aqueous Na2SO4, H⁺ (from water) discharges before Na⁺ at the cathode.
(b) Concentration: in dilute NaCl(aq), OH⁻ discharges at the anode (producing O2). In concentrated NaCl(aq), Cl⁻ discharges (producing Cl2).
(c) Electrode nature: with inert (carbon) electrodes in CuSO4(aq), oxygen forms at the anode. With copper electrodes, the copper anode dissolves instead.
**Explanation**: Students must address all three factors with examples. A common error is only mentioning one factor.
**Tags**: electrolysis, selective-discharge, reactivity-series, concentration, electrode

### Question Q-4.1-09
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Calculate the mass of copper deposited at the cathode when a current of 2.0 A is passed through aqueous copper(II) sulfate for 30 minutes. (Faraday constant = 96500 C/mol; Ar: Cu = 63.5)
**Answer**: Charge Q = I × t = 2.0 × (30 × 60) = 3600 C.
Moles of electrons = 3600/96500 = 0.0373 mol.
Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu, so 2 mol e⁻ → 1 mol Cu.
Moles of Cu = 0.0373/2 = 0.0187 mol.
Mass of Cu = 0.0187 × 63.5 = 1.19 g.
**Explanation**: Students must use Q = It and the Faraday constant. A common error is forgetting the 2:1 ratio between electrons and copper.
**Tags**: electrolysis, calculation, faraday, copper-deposition, quantitative
