# Items: 3.4 — Concentration calculations, titration

### Question Q-3.2-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Calculate the concentration in mol/dm³ of a solution containing 4.0 g of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) dissolved in 250 cm³ of solution. (Aᵣ: Na = 23, O = 16, H = 1)
**Answer**: 0.4 mol/dm³
**Explanation**: Mᵣ of NaOH = 40. Moles = 4.0/40 = 0.1 mol. Volume = 250 cm³ = 0.25 dm³. Concentration = 0.1/0.25 = 0.4 mol/dm³. A common error is using cm³ instead of dm³.
**Tags**: concentration, NaOH, titration, calculation

### Question Q-3.2-08
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: 25.0 cm³ of 0.1 mol/dm³ NaOH neutralises 20.0 cm³ of HCl. Calculate the concentration of HCl.
**Answer**: 0.125 mol/dm³
**Explanation**: Moles of NaOH = 0.1 × 0.025 = 0.0025 mol. NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H₂O, 1:1 ratio. Moles of HCl = 0.0025 mol. Concentration = 0.0025/0.020 = 0.125 mol/dm³. A common error is inverting the volumes.
**Tags**: titration, concentration, neutralisation, calculation

### Question Q-3.2-09
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: 25.0 cm³ of 0.05 mol/dm³ H₂SO₄ neutralises 20.0 cm³ of KOH. Calculate the concentration of KOH.
**Answer**: 0.125 mol/dm³
**Explanation**: Moles of H₂SO₄ = 0.05 × 0.025 = 0.00125 mol. H₂SO₄ + 2KOH → K₂SO₄ + 2H₂O, so 1:2 ratio. Moles of KOH = 2 × 0.00125 = 0.0025 mol. Concentration = 0.0025/0.020 = 0.125 mol/dm³. A common error is using 1:1 ratio.
**Tags**: titration, H2SO4, KOH, stoichiometry, calculation

### Question Q-3.2-10
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What indicator would you use in a titration between a strong acid and a strong alkali?
**Answer**: Phenolphthalein (colourless in acid, pink in alkali) or methyl orange (red in acid, yellow in alkali). Both give sharp colour changes for strong acid-strong alkali titrations.
**Explanation**: Sharp endpoint needed. A common error is saying universal indicator (too gradual).
**Tags**: indicator, titration, phenolphthalein

### Question Q-3.2-11
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Why should you rinse the burette with the acid it will contain, not with water?
**Answer**: Rinsing with water would dilute the acid, changing its concentration and giving inaccurate titration results. Rinsing with the acid ensures the concentration is not affected.
**Explanation**: Avoid dilution. A common error is saying water "reacts" with the acid.
**Tags**: titration, technique, accuracy
