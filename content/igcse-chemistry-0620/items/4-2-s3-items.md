# Items: 4.5 — Industrial applications of electrolysis

### Question Q-4.2-09
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe how electroplating works. Give an example.
**Answer**: The object to be plated is the cathode. The plating metal is the anode. The electrolyte contains ions of the plating metal. At the cathode: metal ions gain electrons and deposit as a solid layer. Example: silver plating — silver anode, object as cathode, silver nitrate solution as electrolyte. Ag⁺ + e⁻ → Ag at cathode.
**Explanation**: Object = cathode, plating metal = anode. A common error is reversing the electrodes.
**Tags**: electroplating, silver, cathode, industrial

### Question Q-4.2-10
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: In the electrolysis of brine (concentrated NaCl solution), name the products at each electrode and one use of each product.
**Answer**: Anode: chlorine gas (Cl₂) — used to make bleach, PVC, disinfect water. Cathode: hydrogen gas (H₂) — used to make ammonia, fuel. The remaining solution is sodium hydroxide (NaOH) — used to make soap, paper.
**Explanation**: Cl₂ at anode, H₂ at cathode, NaOH in solution. A common error is saying sodium metal forms (it doesn't in aqueous solution).
**Tags**: brine, chlorine, hydrogen, NaOH, industrial

### Question Q-4.2-11
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Why is aluminium extracted by electrolysis of molten Al₂O₃ and not by reduction with carbon?
**Answer**: Aluminium is above carbon in the reactivity series, so carbon cannot reduce aluminium oxide. Electrolysis is required to force Al³⁺ ions to gain electrons.
**Explanation**: Too reactive for carbon reduction. A common error is saying aluminium "doesn't react with carbon."
**Tags**: aluminium, electrolysis, reactivity-series, extraction
