# Items: 12.3 — Cation and anion identification tests

### Question Q-12.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What colour precipitate does copper(II) ion form with aqueous sodium hydroxide?
**Answer**: Blue precipitate of copper(II) hydroxide.
**Explanation**: Cu²⁺ + 2OH⁻ → Cu(OH)₂ (blue). A common error is saying green (that's Fe²⁺).
**Tags**: copper-test, Cu2+, NaOH-test, precipitate

### Question Q-12.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How would you distinguish between calcium ions (Ca²⁺) and zinc ions (Zn²⁺) using aqueous sodium hydroxide?
**Answer**: Both form white precipitates with NaOH. Add excess NaOH: the zinc hydroxide precipitate dissolves (forming a colourless solution) while calcium hydroxide does not dissolve.
**Explanation**: Zn(OH)₂ dissolves in excess NaOH; Ca(OH)₂ does not. A common error is saying both dissolve.
**Tags**: calcium-test, zinc-test, NaOH-test, excess

### Question Q-12.1-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How do you test for sulfate ions (SO₄²⁻) in a solution?
**Answer**: Add dilute hydrochloric acid followed by aqueous barium chloride (or barium nitrate). A white precipitate of barium sulfate (BaSO₄) confirms sulfate ions.
**Explanation**: Ba²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ → BaSO₄ (white ppt). A common error is using silver nitrate (that's for halides).
**Tags**: sulfate-test, SO42-, barium-chloride

### Question Q-12.1-08
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A solution gives a white precipitate with acidified silver nitrate that dissolves in dilute ammonia solution. Identify the anion.
**Answer**: Chloride ion (Cl⁻). The white precipitate is silver chloride (AgCl), which dissolves in dilute ammonia. (AgBr is cream and dissolves only in concentrated ammonia; AgI is yellow and does not dissolve.)
**Explanation**: AgCl = white + soluble in dilute NH₃. A common error is saying bromide.
**Tags**: halide-test, chloride, silver-nitrate, ammonia

### Question Q-12.1-09
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How do you test for carbonate ions (CO₃²⁻) in a solid?
**Answer**: Add dilute acid. If carbonates are present, effervescence occurs (bubbles of CO₂ gas). Test the gas with limewater — it turns milky/cloudy, confirming carbon dioxide.
**Explanation**: Acid + carbonate → CO₂. A common error is testing with NaOH instead of acid.
**Tags**: carbonate-test, CO32-, limewater
