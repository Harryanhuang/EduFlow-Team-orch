# Items: 8.3 — Noble gases, transition elements

### Question Q-8.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Why are noble gases chemically unreactive?
**Answer**: Noble gases have a full outer shell of electrons (stable electronic configuration). They do not need to gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve stability.
**Explanation**: Full outer shell = stable. A common error is saying they "have no electrons."
**Tags**: noble-gases, unreactive, full-shell

### Question Q-8.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Give one use of helium and explain why it is suitable.
**Answer**: Helium is used in weather balloons and party balloons because it is less dense than air and is non-flammable (unlike hydrogen, which is also light but explosive).
**Explanation**: Light + safe. A common error is saying helium is "chemically active."
**Tags**: helium, applications, density

### Question Q-8.3-03
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Explain why the boiling points of noble gases increase going down Group 18 (helium < neon < argon < krypton < xenon), and predict whether radon would have a higher or lower boiling point than xenon. Justify your answer in terms of intermolecular forces.
**Answer**: Boiling points increase down the group because as atoms get larger, they have more electrons, which leads to stronger London dispersion forces (temporary induced dipoles). More energy is needed to overcome these forces. Radon would have a higher boiling point than xenon because it has even more electrons and is larger, giving even stronger dispersion forces.
**Explanation**: London dispersion forces increase with electron count and atomic size. A common error is attributing the trend to covalent bonding or saying noble gases have no intermolecular forces.
**Tags**: noble-gases, boiling-point, intermolecular-forces, periodic-trend

### Question Q-8.3-04
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A student claims that argon is "completely useless" because it is unreactive. Evaluate this claim by describing at least three practical applications of argon and explaining why its unreactivity is actually essential for each use.
**Answer**: The claim is incorrect. Argon is widely used because of its unreactivity: (1) In filament light bulbs, argon prevents the hot tungsten filament from oxidising and burning out. (2) In welding, argon shields the molten metal from oxygen in air, preventing unwanted oxidation. (3) In double-glazed windows, argon fills the gap between panes as an insulating gas that does not degrade over time. Its unreactivity is the key property that makes these uses possible.
**Explanation**: Noble gases' inertness is valuable, not a drawback. A common error is listing uses without explaining WHY inertness matters.
**Tags**: noble-gases, argon, applications, evaluation
