### Question Q-2.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Predict the electronic configuration and reactivity of an element with atomic number 20.
**Answer**: Electronic configuration: 2,8,8,2. It is in Group 2 (alkaline earth metals) and Period 4. It is reactive, losing 2 electrons to form a 2+ ion. Less reactive than Group 1 elements.
**Explanation**: 20 electrons: 2+8+8+2. Group 2 metals lose 2 electrons. The element is calcium. A common error is saying it gains 6 electrons.
**Tags**: calcium, electronic-configuration, group-2, reactivity

### Question Q-2.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: An element X has the electronic configuration 2,8,5. (a) Which group and period is it in? Explain your reasoning. (b) Is it a metal or non-metal? (c) What type of bonding would you expect in the compound it forms with chlorine?
**Answer**: (a) Group V (5 valence electrons), Period 3 (3 electron shells). The number of valence electrons = group number; the number of shells = period number.
(b) Non-metal — elements on the right side of the Periodic Table with 5 valence electrons are non-metals (this is phosphorus).
(c) Covalent bonding — both X and chlorine are non-metals, so they share electrons.
**Explanation**: Students must link electronic configuration to position. Common errors: confusing group number with total electrons, or saying ionic because "it has electrons to lose".
**Tags**: electronic-configuration, group, period, periodic-table, bonding-type

### Question Q-2.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why the chemical properties of elements in the same group are similar. Use Group I (alkali metals) as an example.
**Answer**: Elements in the same group have the same number of electrons in their outer shell. Chemical reactions involve the loss, gain, or sharing of these outer electrons. Since all Group I elements have 1 outer electron that is easily lost, they all react in similar ways (e.g. vigorously with water to form +1 ions).
**Explanation**: The key is linking outer electron count to chemical behaviour. A common error is saying "they have similar atomic structure" without specifying the outer electrons.
**Tags**: group, chemical-properties, outer-electron, alkali-metal

### Question Q-2.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Element A is in Period 3, Group III. Element B is in Period 2, Group VI. (a) Deduce the electronic configurations of A and B. (b) Predict the formula of the compound formed by A and B. (c) State the type of bonding and give two physical properties you would expect for this compound.
**Answer**: (a) A: 2,8,3 (aluminium). B: 2,6 (oxygen).
(b) A forms A³⁺ ions, B forms B²⁻ ions. Formula: A2B3 (Al2O3).
(c) Ionic bonding. Expected properties: high melting point (strong ionic bonds), conducts electricity when molten or dissolved (ions free to move) but not when solid.
**Explanation**: Students must combine position → configuration → ion charge → formula → bonding → properties. Common errors: wrong ion charges or reversing the formula ratio.
**Tags**: periodic-table, electronic-configuration, ionic-formula, ionic-bonding, properties

### Question Q-2.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: An element has the electronic configuration 2,8,18,18,7. Without naming the element: (a) State its group and period. (b) Predict whether it is a metal or non-metal and explain your reasoning. (c) Predict the formula of its hydride and its oxide. (d) Would you expect it to be more or less reactive than fluorine? Explain.
**Answer**: (a) Group VII (7 valence electrons), Period 5 (5 electron shells).
(b) Non-metal — it has 7 valence electrons and is on the right side of the Periodic Table, so it tends to gain one electron.
(c) Hydride: HX (gains 1 electron to form X⁻). Oxide: X2O (valency 1, like other halogens — though higher oxides are possible).
(d) Less reactive than fluorine. Going down Group VII, reactivity decreases because the outer shell is further from the nucleus and more shielded, making it harder to attract an electron.
**Explanation**: This tests extrapolation of periodic trends to an unnamed element. A common error is saying it's a metal because of the large atomic number.
**Tags**: electronic-configuration, periodic-trends, prediction, halogen, reactivity

### Question Q-2.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: The table below shows properties of four unknown elements W, X, Y, and Z. (W: melts at 98°C, reacts vigorously with water, forms +1 ion; X: melts at 1538°C, conducts electricity, forms +2 and +3 ions; Y: melts at −219°C, does not conduct, does not react with water; Z: melts at 660°C, conducts, forms +3 ion). (a) Identify which group each element belongs to and explain. (b) Which is likely a transition element? (c) Deduce the period order of W, X, and Z (smallest to largest).
**Answer**: (a) W: Group I (low mp, reacts with water, +1 ion — alkali metal). X: Transition element (high mp, conducts, variable oxidation states +2/+3). Y: Group VIII/0 (very low mp, non-conductor, unreactive — noble gas). Z: Group III (conducts, +3 ion, moderate mp — like aluminium).
(b) X — it has variable oxidation states (+2 and +3), which is characteristic of transition elements.
(c) Period order: W (Period 3, sodium) < Z (Period 3, aluminium) < X (Period 4, iron). Actually W and Z could be same period; X is likely a higher period due to higher mp.
**Explanation**: Students must link multiple properties to group identification. A common error is placing Y in Group VII (non-reactive non-metal) rather than recognising the noble gas properties.
**Tags**: element-identification, group, transition-element, deduction, properties
