# Items: 3.2 — Mole calculations, reacting masses, percentage yield

### Question Q-3.2-01
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Calculate the mass of magnesium oxide produced when 24 g of magnesium burns completely in oxygen. (2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO; Aᵣ: Mg = 24, O = 16)
**Answer**: 40 g
**Explanation**: Moles of Mg = 24/24 = 1 mol. From the equation: 2 mol Mg → 2 mol MgO, so 1 mol Mg → 1 mol MgO. Mᵣ of MgO = 24 + 16 = 40. Mass = 1 × 40 = 40 g. A common error is saying 80 g (doubling).
**Tags**: reacting-mass, MgO, stoichiometry, calculation

### Question Q-3.2-02
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: 10 g of calcium carbonate is heated: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂. Calculate the mass of calcium oxide produced. (Aᵣ: Ca = 40, C = 12, O = 16)
**Answer**: 5.6 g
**Explanation**: Mᵣ of CaCO₃ = 100. Moles of CaCO₃ = 10/100 = 0.1 mol. 1:1 ratio → 0.1 mol CaO. Mᵣ of CaO = 56. Mass = 0.1 × 56 = 5.6 g. A common error is using Mᵣ = 44 (CO₂ instead of CaO).
**Tags**: reacting-mass, CaO, thermal-decomposition, calculation

### Question Q-3.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: In a reaction, the theoretical yield is 50 g but the actual yield is 42 g. Calculate the percentage yield.
**Answer**: 84%
**Explanation**: Percentage yield = (actual yield / theoretical yield) × 100 = (42/50) × 100 = 84%. A common error is dividing theoretical by actual.
**Tags**: percentage-yield, calculation

### Question Q-3.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: 6.5 g of zinc reacts with excess hydrochloric acid: Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂. Calculate the volume of hydrogen gas produced at r.t.p. (Aᵣ: Zn = 65; molar volume at r.t.p. = 24 dm³/mol)
**Answer**: 2.4 dm³
**Explanation**: Moles of Zn = 6.5/65 = 0.1 mol. 1:1 ratio → 0.1 mol H₂. Volume = 0.1 × 24 = 2.4 dm³. A common error is using 22.4 dm³ (STP, not r.t.p.).
**Tags**: gas-volume, rtp, zinc, stoichiometry, calculation

### Question Q-3.2-05
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a limiting reactant?
**Answer**: The limiting reactant is the reactant that is completely used up in a reaction, determining the maximum amount of product that can be formed. The other reactant(s) are in excess.
**Explanation**: Runs out first → reaction stops. A common error is saying "the smallest mass is the limiting reactant."
**Tags**: limiting-reactant, definition, excess

### Question Q-3.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: 4 g of hydrogen reacts with 32 g of oxygen: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O. Which is the limiting reactant? (Aᵣ: H = 1, O = 16)
**Answer**: Oxygen
**Explanation**: Moles of H₂ = 4/2 = 2 mol. Moles of O₂ = 32/32 = 1 mol. Ratio is 2:1, and we have exactly 2:1, so neither is in excess — they react completely. If we had more H₂, O₂ would be limiting. In this exact case, both are consumed fully.
**Explanation**: Check the mole ratio against the equation ratio. A common error is comparing masses directly.
**Tags**: limiting-reactant, stoichiometry, water
