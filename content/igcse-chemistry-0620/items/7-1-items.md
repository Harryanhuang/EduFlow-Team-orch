# Items: 7.1 — Properties of acids and bases, pH


### Question Q-7.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Define an acid and a base in terms of proton transfer. Give one example of each.
**Answer**: An acid is a substance that donates protons (H⁺ ions) in solution. Example: hydrochloric acid (HCl) donates H⁺ in water.
A base is a substance that accepts protons (H⁺ ions). Example: sodium hydroxide (NaOH) provides OH⁻ which accepts H⁺ to form water.
**Explanation**: Students must use the proton transfer definition (Brønsted-Lowry). A common error is defining acids as "substances with pH < 7" without explaining the mechanism.
**Tags**: acid, base, proton, definition, Bronsted-Lowry

### Question Q-7.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the pH scale? State the approximate pH values for a strong acid, a weak acid, a neutral solution, a weak base, and a strong base.
**Answer**: The pH scale measures how acidic or alkaline a solution is (0–14).
Strong acid: pH 1–2. Weak acid: pH 3–6. Neutral: pH 7. Weak base: pH 8–11. Strong base: pH 12–14.
**Explanation**: Students must know the ranges. A common error is saying a weak acid has pH 7 or that a strong base has pH 7.
**Tags**: pH, scale, acid, base, strong, weak

### Question Q-7.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain the difference between a strong acid and a weak acid in terms of ionisation. Give one example of each.
**Answer**: A strong acid is completely ionised in water (e.g. HCl → H⁺ + Cl⁻). All HCl molecules dissociate into ions.
A weak acid is only partially ionised in water (e.g. ethanoic acid, CH3COOH ⇌ CH3COO⁻ + H⁺). Most molecules remain undissociated — there is an equilibrium.
**Explanation**: Students must link strength to degree of ionisation. A common error is confusing "strong" with "concentrated."
**Tags**: strong-acid, weak-acid, ionisation, concentration, equilibrium

### Question Q-7.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Write balanced equations for the reactions of: (a) magnesium with dilute hydrochloric acid, (b) calcium carbonate with dilute sulfuric acid, (c) sodium hydroxide with dilute nitric acid. For each, describe one observable result.
**Answer**: (a) Mg(s) + 2HCl(aq) → MgCl2(aq) + H2(g) — effervescence (hydrogen gas), magnesium dissolves.
(b) CaCO3(s) + H2SO4(aq) → CaSO4(aq) + H2O(l) + CO2(g) — effervescence (CO2), solid dissolves.
(c) NaOH(aq) + HNO3(aq) → NaNO3(aq) + H2O(l) — no visible change (neutralisation, temperature increases).
**Explanation**: Students must write balanced equations and describe observations. A common error in (b) is not recognising the reaction may stop due to insoluble CaSO4 coating.
**Tags**: acid-reactions, balancing, observation, metal, carbonate, neutralisation

### Question Q-7.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe the difference between the terms "dilute" and "concentrated" and "strong" and "weak" when describing acids. Can a strong acid be dilute? Explain.
**Answer**: "Concentrated" and "dilute" refer to the amount of acid dissolved in a given volume of solution (concentration).
"Strong" and "weak" refer to the degree of ionisation in water (how completely the acid dissociates).
Yes, a strong acid can be dilute — it means the acid is fully ionised but there are fewer acid molecules per unit volume. For example, dilute HCl is still fully ionised.
**Explanation**: Students must distinguish the two concepts. A common error is using them interchangeably.
**Tags**: dilute, concentrated, strong, weak, acid, concentration

### Question Q-7.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe how you could use a titration to find the concentration of a solution of sulfuric acid using a standard solution of sodium hydroxide. Include the indicator used and the end point.
**Answer**: 1. Pipette a known volume of NaOH into a conical flask.
2. Add a few drops of suitable indicator (phenolphthalein or methyl orange).
3. Fill a burette with the sulfuric acid solution.
4. Add acid from the burette to the flask, swirling, until the indicator changes colour.
5. Record the volume of acid used.
6. Repeat for accuracy.
Use the balanced equation (2NaOH + H2SO4 → Na2SO4 + 2H2O) and the volumes to calculate the acid concentration.
**Explanation**: Students must describe the full titration procedure. A common error is forgetting the 2:1 mole ratio between NaOH and H2SO4.
**Tags**: titration, acid-base, concentration, indicator, procedure
