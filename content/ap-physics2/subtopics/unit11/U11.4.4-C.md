Consider an electric dipole with $+q$ at $(0, d/2)$ and $-q$ at $(0, -d/2)$. Which statement about the potential at point $P$ on the perpendicular bisector (at $(x, 0)$ where $x \gg d$) is correct?

## Options
A) $V = kq/x^2$ (nonzero, same sign as the near charge)
B) $V = 0$ everywhere on the perpendicular bisector
C) $V$ varies with $x$ as $1/x^2$
D) $V$ is maximum on the perpendicular bisector

## Answer
B

## Explanation
Every point on the perpendicular bisector is equidistant from $+q$ and $-q$. Since $V = kq/r_+ + k(-q)/r_-$ and $r_+ = r_-$, the potential is exactly zero everywhere on the bisector. This is a key property: the perpendicular bisector of a dipole is a zero-potential surface.
