A copper wire has a cross-sectional area of $1.0 \text{ mm}^2$ and carries a current of $2.0 \text{ A}$. The free electron density in copper is $8.5 \times 10^{28} \text{ m}^{-3}$. What is the drift velocity of the electrons? ($e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$)

## Options
A) $1.5 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m/s}$
B) $1.5 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m/s}$
C) $1.5 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m/s}$
D) $2.7 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m/s}$

## Answer
C

## Explanation
$nqA = (8.5 \times 10^{28})(1.6 \times 10^{-19})(1.0 \times 10^{-6}) = 1.36 \times 10^{4} \text{ A·s/m}$. $v_d = I/(nqA) = 2.0/13{,}600 = 1.47 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m/s} \approx 1.5 \times 10^{-4} \text{ m/s}$. Drift velocity in copper is extremely slow (mm/min range) — this is why a signal travels at near $c$ even though individual electrons drift slowly.
