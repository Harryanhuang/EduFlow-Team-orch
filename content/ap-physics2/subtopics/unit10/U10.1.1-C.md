A gas expands from state A to state B via two different paths. Path 1: the gas absorbs $800 \text{ J}$ of heat and does $500 \text{ J}$ of work. Path 2: the gas absorbs $600 \text{ J}$ of heat and does $300 \text{ J}$ of work. What is the difference in internal energy between states A and B?

## Options
A) Path 1 gives $\Delta U = 300 \text{ J}$; Path 2 gives $\Delta U = 300 \text{ J}$. No difference.
B) Path 1 gives $\Delta U = 300 \text{ J}$; Path 2 gives $\Delta U = 300 \text{ J}$. Same because $Q - W$ is the same.
C) Path 1 gives $\Delta U = 200 \text{ J}$; Path 2 gives $\Delta U = 300 \text{ J}$. Different paths give different $\Delta U$.
D) $\Delta U$ cannot be determined without knowing the type of gas.

## Answer
A

## Explanation
For Path 1: $\Delta U_1 = Q_1 - W_1 = 800 - 500 = 300 \text{ J}$. For Path 2: $\Delta U_2 = Q_2 - W_2 = 600 - 300 = 300 \text{ J}$. Both paths give the same $\Delta U = 300 \text{ J}$ because internal energy is a state function — it depends only on the initial and final states, not the path. The individual values of $Q$ and $W$ differ between paths, but their difference ($Q - W$) must be the same for any path between the same two states. This is a fundamental consequence of the first law.
