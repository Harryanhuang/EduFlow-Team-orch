Water flows through a long, rough pipe at moderate speed. A student applies Bernoulli's equation to predict the pressure at a downstream point and finds that the predicted pressure is higher than the actual measured pressure. Which is the best explanation for this discrepancy?

## Options
A) Bernoulli's equation does not apply to water.
B) The pipe is not horizontal, so the elevation term was incorrectly set to zero.
C) Viscous effects cause energy loss, which Bernoulli's equation does not account for.
D) The continuity equation was applied incorrectly.

## Answer
C

## Explanation
Bernoulli's equation assumes the fluid is ideal: incompressible, non-viscous, and flowing steadily along a streamline. In a real pipe, viscosity causes friction between the fluid and the pipe walls, leading to energy dissipation (head loss). This means the actual pressure downstream is lower than Bernoulli's prediction. Option A is wrong — Bernoulli's equation does apply to water as an approximation. Option B is possible but the question says the pipe is horizontal. Option D is irrelevant to the pressure discrepancy.
