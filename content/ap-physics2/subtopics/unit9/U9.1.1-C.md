A sealed cylinder contains an ideal gas at uniform pressure. A piston on top is pushed downward, compressing the gas. Immediately after the piston stops, which statement about the gas pressure inside the cylinder is correct?

## Options
A) The pressure is uniform throughout the gas, consistent with Pascal's law.
B) The pressure near the piston is higher than near the bottom, because the gas is compressible.
C) The pressure is uniform throughout, because the cylinder is sealed.
D) Pascal's law guarantees uniform pressure regardless of fluid type.

## Answer
B

## Explanation
Pascal's law states that a pressure change in an enclosed incompressible fluid is transmitted undimmediately to every point. However, a gas is compressible — when the piston pushes down, the gas near the piston compresses first and reaches a higher density and pressure than gas farther away. The pressure gradient persists until the gas molecules redistribute through molecular motion (thermal equilibrium). The key distinction: Pascal's law requires incompressibility. For compressible fluids, pressure changes propagate as waves and do not instantly create uniform pressure. Options A, C, and D all incorrectly assume Pascal's law applies universally to all enclosed fluids.
