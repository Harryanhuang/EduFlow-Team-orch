A container holds three immiscible fluids layered as follows (top to bottom): oil ($\rho_1 = 800 \text{ kg/m}^3$, $h_1 = 5.0 \text{ cm}$), water ($\rho_2 = 1000 \text{ kg/m}^3$, $h_2 = 10.0 \text{ cm}$), and mercury ($\rho_3 = 13{,}600 \text{ kg/m}^3$, $h_3 = 3.0 \text{ cm}$). The top surface is open to the atmosphere ($P_0 = 1.01 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}$). What is the absolute pressure at the bottom? ($g = 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$)

## Options
A) $1.05 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}$
B) $1.10 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}$
C) $1.46 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}$
D) $1.91 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}$

## Answer
A

## Explanation
The absolute pressure at the bottom equals atmospheric pressure plus the hydrostatic pressure from each layer: $P = P_0 + \rho_1 g h_1 + \rho_2 g h_2 + \rho_3 g h_3 = 1.01 \times 10^5 + (800)(9.8)(0.05) + (1000)(9.8)(0.10) + (13{,}600)(9.8)(0.03) = 1.01 \times 10^5 + 392 + 980 + 3{,}998 = 1.01 \times 10^5 + 5{,}370 = 1.064 \times 10^5 \text{ Pa}$. Closest to A. The key insight is that each layer contributes independently to the total pressure at the bottom, and pressure is continuous across each fluid interface.
