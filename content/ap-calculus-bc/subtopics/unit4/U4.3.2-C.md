What is $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}\left(\frac{1}{x}-\frac{1}{\sin x}\right)$?

## Options
A) $0$

B) $\infty$

C) $-\infty$

D) $\frac{1}{6}$

## Answer
A

## Explanation
As $x\to0^+$, this has the indeterminate form $\infty-\infty$. Combine the fractions: $\frac{\sin x-x}{x\sin x}$, which is $\frac{0}{0}$. Apply L'Hôpital's Rule: $\lim\frac{\cos x-1}{\sin x+x\cos x}$, still $\frac{0}{0}$. Apply again: $\lim\frac{-\sin x}{\cos x+\cos x-x\sin x}=\frac{0}{2}=0$. Option B results from not combining the fractions. Option C is a sign error. Option D is a distractor.
