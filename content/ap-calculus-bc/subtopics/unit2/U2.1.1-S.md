Using the limit definition, what is $f'(x)$ for $f(x) = \sqrt{x+1}$?

## Options
A) $\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x+1}}$

B) $\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{x+1}}$

C) $\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$

D) $\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x+1}+1}$

## Answer
A

## Explanation
$f'(x)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{\sqrt{x+h+1}-\sqrt{x+1}}{h}$. Multiply numerator and denominator by the conjugate: $\frac{(\sqrt{x+h+1}-\sqrt{x+1})(\sqrt{x+h+1}+\sqrt{x+1})}{h(\sqrt{x+h+1}+\sqrt{x+1})}=\frac{h}{h(\sqrt{x+h+1}+\sqrt{x+1})}$. Cancel $h$ and let $h\to0$: $\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x+1}}$. Option B misses the $\frac{1}{2}$ factor. Option C uses the wrong radicand ($x$ instead of $x+1$). Option D is algebraically incorrect.
