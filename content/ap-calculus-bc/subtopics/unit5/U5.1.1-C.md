If $f$ is differentiable on $\mathbb{R}$, $f(0)=0$, $f(1)=2$, and $f(3)=6$, what can be concluded about $f'(c)$ for some $c\in(0,3)$?

## Options
A) There exists $c$ with $f'(c)=2$ only.

B) There exist $c_1\in(0,1)$ and $c_2\in(1,3)$ such that $f'(c_1)=2$ and $f'(c_2)=2$.

C) There exists $c\in(0,3)$ with $f'(c)>2$.

D) There exists $c\in(0,3)$ with $f'(c)<1$.

## Answer
B

## Explanation
On $[0,1]$: by MVT, $f'(c_1)=\frac{f(1)-f(0)}{1-0}=\frac{2}{1}=2$ for some $c_1\in(0,1)$. On $[1,3]$: by MVT, $f'(c_2)=\frac{f(3)-f(1)}{3-1}=\frac{4}{2}=2$ for some $c_2\in(1,3)$. So there exist two distinct points with $f'=2$. Option A says "only" — too restrictive (there may be others). Options C and D make claims not supported by the given data.
