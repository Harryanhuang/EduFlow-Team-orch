The function $g$ is defined by $g(x)=x^{1/3}(x-4)$. How many critical points of $g$ are at which $g$ changes from increasing to decreasing (i.e., local maxima)?

## Options
A) 0

B) 1

C) 2

D) 3

## Answer
B

## Explanation
$g(x)=x^{4/3}-4x^{1/3}$. $g'(x)=\frac{4}{3}x^{1/3}-\frac{4}{3}x^{-2/3}=\frac{4}{3}x^{-2/3}(x-1)=\frac{4(x-1)}{3x^{2/3}}$. Critical points: $x=0$ (derivative undefined) and $x=1$ (derivative zero). Sign analysis: $x<0$: $g'<0$ (decreasing). $0<x<1$: $g'<0$ (decreasing). $x>1$: $g'>0$ (increasing). At $x=0$: $g'$ goes negative → negative (no sign change, not an extremum — it's an inflection point with a vertical tangent). At $x=1$: $g'$ goes negative → positive (local minimum). There are **zero** points where $g$ changes from increasing to decreasing. The answer is A, not B. Wait — let me recheck. Actually for $x<0$: numerator $x-1<0$, denominator $3x^{2/3}>0$, so $g'<0$. For $0<x<1$: same, $g'<0$. For $x>1$: $g'>0$. So $g$ is decreasing on $(-\infty,1)$ and increasing on $(1,\infty)$. There is no local maximum at all. The answer is **A) 0**. Option B (1) would be correct only if there were a sign change from $+$ to $-$, which never happens. Option C (2) counts both critical points as extrema, but $x=0$ is not. Option D (3) invents a critical point.
