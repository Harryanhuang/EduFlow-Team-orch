Given $\lim_{x \to 2} f(x) = 6$ and $\lim_{x \to 2} g(x) = 0$, which statement is correct?

## Options
A) $\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \infty$
B) $\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}$ does not exist (or is infinite — cannot be determined from the given information alone).
C) $\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{6}{0}$ which is undefined.
D) $\lim_{x \to 2} [f(x) \cdot g(x)] = 0$ and $\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}$ also exists.

## Answer
B

## Explanation
When $\lim g(x) = 0$, the quotient rule for limits does NOT apply. We cannot determine $\lim f(x)/g(x)$ just from knowing $\lim f(x) = 6$ and $\lim g(x) = 0$. The limit could be $+\infty$, $-\infty$, or might not exist — it depends on how $g(x)$ approaches 0 (from above, below, or both). Option A assumes $+\infty$ without knowing the sign of $g$ near 2. Option C writes "6/0" as if that's a valid mathematical expression. Option D incorrectly claims the quotient limit exists. Only the product rule gives $\lim f(x)g(x) = 6 \times 0 = 0$.
