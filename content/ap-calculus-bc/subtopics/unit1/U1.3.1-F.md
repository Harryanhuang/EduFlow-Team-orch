For a function $f$, which condition is NOT sufficient by itself to guarantee continuity at $x = a$?

## Options
A) $f(a)$ is defined.
B) $\lim_{x \to a} f(x)$ exists.
C) $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$.
D) All three conditions together are required.

## Answer
D

## Explanation
Continuity at $x = a$ requires ALL THREE conditions: (1) $f(a)$ is defined, (2) $\lim_{x \to a} f(x)$ exists, and (3) $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$. Any single condition alone is NOT sufficient. Option A alone fails: $f(2) = 5$ defined but limit might not exist or differ. Option B alone fails: limit exists but $f(a)$ might be undefined. Option C alone fails: stating $\lim = f(a)$ implicitly requires both exist, but in isolation it's the combined condition. Option D correctly states all three are required.
