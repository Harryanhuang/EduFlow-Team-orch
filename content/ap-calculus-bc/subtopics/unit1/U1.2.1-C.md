What is $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sqrt{x+4} - 2}{x}$?

## Options
A) 0
B) $\frac{1}{4}$
C) $\frac{1}{2}$
D) The limit does not exist.

## Answer
B

## Explanation
Direct substitution: $\frac{\sqrt{4}-2}{0} = \frac{0}{0}$. Rationalize by multiplying by $\frac{\sqrt{x+4}+2}{\sqrt{x+4}+2}$:
$\frac{(\sqrt{x+4}-2)(\sqrt{x+4}+2)}{x(\sqrt{x+4}+2)} = \frac{(x+4)-4}{x(\sqrt{x+4}+2)} = \frac{x}{x(\sqrt{x+4}+2)} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x+4}+2}$
$\lim_{x\to0} \frac{1}{\sqrt{x+4}+2} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4}+2} = \frac{1}{4}$. Option A (0) is wrong after cancellation. Option C (1/2) might forget to add 2 in the denominator. Option D is wrong — after rationalization, the limit exists.
