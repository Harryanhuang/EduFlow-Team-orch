What is $\lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{\sqrt{x^2 + 1}}{x}$?

## Options
A) 1
B) -1
C) 0
D) The limit does not exist.

## Answer
B

## Explanation
For $x \to -\infty$, $x < 0$, so $\sqrt{x^2} = |x| = -x$. Factor out $x^2$ from the square root:
$\frac{\sqrt{x^2+1}}{x} = \frac{\sqrt{x^2(1+1/x^2)}}{x} = \frac{|x|\sqrt{1+1/x^2}}{x} = \frac{-x\sqrt{1+1/x^2}}{x} = -\sqrt{1+1/x^2}$
As $x \to -\infty$: $-\sqrt{1+0} = -1$. Option A (1) would be the answer for $x \to +\infty$ (where $|x| = x$). Option C (0) ignores the leading terms. Option D is wrong — the limit exists and equals -1. The key insight: $\sqrt{x^2} = |x|$, not $x$, and the sign of $|x|$ depends on the direction of the limit.
