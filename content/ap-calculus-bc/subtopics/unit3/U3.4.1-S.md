If $y=\dfrac{(x+1)(x+2)}{(x+3)}$, which expression equals $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$?

## Options
A) $y\left(\frac{1}{x+1}+\frac{1}{x+2}-\frac{1}{x+3}\right)$

B) $y\left(\frac{1}{x+1}+\frac{1}{x+2}+\frac{1}{x+3}\right)$

C) $y\left(\frac{1}{x+1}-\frac{1}{x+2}-\frac{1}{x+3}\right)$

D) $\left(\frac{1}{x+1}+\frac{1}{x+2}-\frac{1}{x+3}\right)$

## Answer
A

## Explanation
Take natural log: $\ln y=\ln(x+1)+\ln(x+2)-\ln(x+3)$. Differentiate: $\frac{y'}{y}=\frac{1}{x+1}+\frac{1}{x+2}-\frac{1}{x+3}$. Multiply by $y$: $y'=y\left(\frac{1}{x+1}+\frac{1}{x+2}-\frac{1}{x+3}\right)$. Option B has the wrong sign for the last term. Option C has the wrong sign for the middle term. Option D forgets to multiply by $y$.
