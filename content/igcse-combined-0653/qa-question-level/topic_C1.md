# Topic C1 — States of Matter
## QA Question Level File

**1. [F]** Describe the arrangement and motion of particles in (a) a solid (b) a liquid (c) a gas.

**2. [F]** Name the following changes of state: (a) solid → liquid (b) liquid → gas (c) gas → liquid.

**3. [S]** Use kinetic theory to explain why a gas exerts pressure on the walls of its container.

**4. [S]** A solid is heated from room temperature until it becomes a gas. Describe what happens to the kinetic energy of the particles and the distance between particles.

**5. [S]** Explain why a perfume smell spreads throughout a room more quickly in summer than in winter.

**6. [S]** Draw a heating curve for water from −20°C to 120°C, labelling: (a) ice warming (b) ice melting (c) water warming (d) water boiling.

**7. [C]** A student states: "Evaporation and boiling are the same thing." Evaluate this statement.

**8. [C]** Explain, using kinetic theory, why a gas can be compressed easily but a liquid cannot.

**9. [C]** Brownian motion of smoke particles is observed under a microscope. Explain how this experiment provides evidence for the kinetic theory of matter.

**10. [F]** Name the three changes of state where the substance gains energy from surroundings.

**11. [F]** What is meant by the term 'latent heat'?

**12. [S]** Explain why sweating cools the body down.

**13. [S]** A sealed syringe contains air. Describe what happens to the pressure inside the syringe when the volume is decreased.

**14. [S]** Use kinetic theory to explain why a gas expands when heated at constant pressure.

**15. [S]** Compare the density of solids, liquids, and gases, and explain using kinetic theory.

**16. [C]** A sealed rigid container of gas is heated from 27°C to 327°C. Calculate the factor by which the pressure increases, assuming constant volume.

**17. [C]** A weather balloon filled with helium rises from ground level to high altitude. Using kinetic theory, explain what happens to the volume of the balloon and the pressure inside it.

**18. [C]** Evaluate the following statement: "The Kelvin scale of temperature is more scientifically useful than the Celsius scale."
