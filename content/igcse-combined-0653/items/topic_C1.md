# Topic C1 — States of Matter
## Items File

**Item 1 [F]**
Question: Describe the arrangement and motion of particles in (a) a solid (b) a liquid (c) a gas.
Answer: (a) Solid: particles closely packed in a regular pattern, vibrating in fixed positions. (b) Liquid: particles close together but randomly arranged, moving in all directions. (c) Gas: particles far apart, moving rapidly in all directions.
Difficulty: F
Topic: C1
Explanation: In solids, strong forces of attraction hold particles in fixed positions in a regular lattice; they can only vibrate. In liquids, particles are close together but can move past each other (no fixed shape); they have no regular arrangement. In gases, forces between particles are negligible, so particles move freely and randomly at high speed; the distance between particles is much greater than in liquids.
Tags: kinetic theory, states of matter, particle arrangement, particle motion

**Item 2 [F]**
Question: Name the following changes of state: (a) solid → liquid (b) liquid → gas (c) gas → liquid.
Answer: (a) Melting (or fusion); (b) Boiling (or evaporation); (c) Condensation
Difficulty: F
Topic: C1
Explanation: Melting is the change from solid to liquid, occurring at the melting point. Boiling occurs at the boiling point throughout the liquid; evaporation can occur at any temperature from the surface. Condensation is the change from gas to liquid, occurring when gas cools below its condensation point.
Tags: changes of state, melting, boiling, evaporation, condensation, terminology

**Item 3 [S]**
Question: Use kinetic theory to explain why a gas exerts pressure on the walls of its container.
Answer: Gas particles move rapidly in all directions, colliding with the walls of the container. Each collision exerts a small force on the wall. The total force per unit area from all these collisions is observed as gas pressure. Increasing temperature increases particle speed, so more frequent and energetic collisions, increasing pressure.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C1
Explanation: The pressure exerted by a gas is due to the bombardment of its container walls by rapidly moving particles. Each particle collision transfers momentum to the wall. With millions of collisions per second, the accumulated effect is measurable pressure. If temperature increases, particles gain kinetic energy, move faster, and collide more frequently and forcefully, increasing pressure.
Tags: kinetic theory, gas pressure, particle collisions, temperature and pressure

**Item 4 [S]**
Question: A solid is heated from room temperature until it becomes a gas. Describe what happens to the kinetic energy of the particles and the distance between particles.
Answer: As temperature increases: (1) Kinetic energy of particles increases steadily during heating (particles move faster). (2) During changes of state (melting, boiling), kinetic energy stays constant but potential energy increases. (3) Distance between particles: very close in solid → close in liquid → very far apart in gas. During melting and boiling, distance increases sharply at constant temperature.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C1
Explanation: Heating increases the kinetic energy of particles, making them move faster. During a change of state, the added energy is used to overcome attractive forces between particles (increasing potential energy), not to increase kinetic energy — so temperature (average KE) stays constant. The distance between particles increases significantly during each change of state: solid to liquid (small increase), liquid to gas (very large increase).
Tags: kinetic theory, heating, kinetic energy, potential energy, changes of state

**Item 5 [S]**
Question: Explain why a perfume smell spreads throughout a room more quickly in summer than in winter.
Answer: In summer the room temperature is higher. Higher temperature means perfume particles have higher kinetic energy and move faster, increasing the rate of diffusion. Higher temperature also creates more convection currents in the air, which further disperses the perfume particles more quickly.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C1
Explanation: Diffusion is the movement of particles from an area of high concentration to low concentration due to random motion. The rate of diffusion increases with temperature because particles have more kinetic energy and move faster. Additionally, temperature differences create convection currents that aid mixing. In a warmer room, perfume particles spread faster than in a colder room.
Tags: diffusion, kinetic theory, temperature, rate of diffusion

**Item 6 [S]**
Question: Draw a heating curve for water from −20°C to 120°C, labelling: (a) ice warming (b) ice melting (c) water warming (d) water boiling.
Answer: Curve: −20°C to 0°C (rising, ice warming) → flat at 0°C (ice melting, latent heat) → 0°C to 100°C (rising, water warming) → flat at 100°C (water boiling, latent heat) → above 100°C (rising, steam warming). Flat sections at 0°C (melting) and 100°C (boiling) indicate constant temperature during change of state.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C1
Explanation: A heating curve plots temperature against time (or energy input). During phase changes, temperature remains constant as all added energy goes into breaking intermolecular bonds (potential energy increase). The flat sections are at the melting point (0°C) and boiling point (100°C). The slope of non-flat sections reflects the specific heat capacity of the substance in that state.
Tags: heating curve, changes of state, melting, boiling, latent heat

**Item 7 [C]**
Question: A student states: "Evaporation and boiling are the same thing." Evaluate this statement.
Answer: The statement is INCORRECT. Boiling occurs at a specific temperature (boiling point) throughout the liquid, seen as bubbles throughout. Evaporation occurs at any temperature, only from the surface of the liquid, and results from the escape of high-energy particles from the surface. Both are changes of liquid to gas, but the mechanism and conditions differ.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C1
Explanation: Boiling: occurs at the boiling point, energy input equals latent heat of vaporisation, bubbles form throughout the liquid (e.g., water at 100°C). Evaporation: occurs below the boiling point, only at the liquid surface, happens because the most energetic particles escape; no bubbles form. Evaporation causes cooling (energy is carried away by escaping particles), while boiling requires continuous energy input. Both are forms of vaporisation, but not the same thing.
Tags: evaporation, boiling, changes of state, comparison, evaluation

**Item 8 [C]**
Question: Explain, using kinetic theory, why a gas can be compressed easily but a liquid cannot.
Answer: In a gas, particles are far apart with negligible forces between them. When pressure is applied, particles can be pushed closer together, reducing the volume. In a liquid, particles are already close together with strong attractive forces between them; compressing them would require overcoming these forces, which is very difficult, so liquids are practically incompressible.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C1
Explanation: In gases, there is mostly empty space between particles, so applying pressure reduces the volume as particles are forced closer together. In liquids, particles are already touching each other (close together), and strong intermolecular forces resist further compression. Liquids have a definite volume because particles are already as close as they can be without changing state.
Tags: kinetic theory, compressibility, gas, liquid, intermolecular forces

**Item 9 [C]**
Question: Brownian motion of smoke particles is observed under a microscope. Explain how this experiment provides evidence for the kinetic theory of matter.
Answer: Smoke particles appear to move in random, erratic paths (Brownian motion). This occurs because smoke particles are constantly bombarded by invisible, fast-moving air molecules. The visible smoke particles are large enough to be seen under a microscope but small enough to be affected by collisions from all sides. The randomness of the motion provides evidence that air molecules are in constant random motion — supporting kinetic theory.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C1
Explanation: In 1827, Robert Brown observed pollen grains moving randomly in water. Later explained by Einstein (1905): the visible particles are being struck by millions of tiny, fast-moving water molecules from different directions. Since the collisions are random, the net force on the visible particle changes constantly, causing its erratic path. This directly proves that molecules of a fluid are in constant random motion, confirming kinetic theory. The larger and heavier smoke particles are deflected but not moved in a specific direction.
Tags: Brownian motion, kinetic theory, evidence for particles, random motion

**Item 10 [F]**
Question: Name the three changes of state where the substance gains energy from surroundings.
Answer: (1) Melting — solid to liquid (requires latent heat of fusion). (2) Boiling — liquid to gas (requires latent heat of vaporisation). (3) Evaporation — liquid to gas at any temperature from the liquid surface.
Difficulty: F
Topic: C1
Explanation: Endothermic changes of state require energy input to overcome attractive forces between particles. In melting, energy breaks the rigid lattice of a solid. In boiling/evaporation, energy overcomes nearly all attractive forces between liquid particles, allowing gas particles to separate.
Tags: changes of state, endothermic, melting, boiling, evaporation

**Item 11 [F]**
Question: What is meant by the term 'latent heat'?
Answer: Latent heat is the energy absorbed during a change of state without a change in temperature. It is the energy needed to overcome the attractive forces between particles during melting or boiling.
Difficulty: F
Topic: C1
Explanation: "Latent" means hidden — the temperature does not change during the change of state even though energy is being absorbed. The energy is used to change the arrangement of particles (potential energy), not their kinetic energy. Latent heat of fusion is for melting/solidifying; latent heat of vaporisation is for boiling/condensing.
Tags: latent heat, changes of state, energy, phase change

**Item 12 [S]**
Question: Explain why sweating cools the body down.
Answer: Sweating cools the body because evaporation is an endothermic process. When sweat (water) evaporates from the skin, it absorbs latent heat of vaporisation from the skin surface, removing heat from the body. In humid conditions, evaporation is less effective because the air is already moist, so sweat stays liquid and the body cannot cool efficiently.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C1
Explanation: The latent heat of vaporisation of water is very high (2260 kJ/kg), meaning each gram of sweat that evaporates removes a large amount of heat. This is why sweating is effective at cooling. In hot, humid conditions, the cooling effect is reduced because sweat does not evaporate efficiently.
Tags: evaporation, sweating, cooling, endothermic, latent heat

**Item 13 [S]**
Question: A sealed syringe contains air. Describe what happens to the pressure inside the syringe when the volume is decreased.
Answer: When the volume decreases (piston pushed in), the air particles are compressed into a smaller space. They collide with the walls more frequently because the distance between particles and walls is reduced. Each collision transfers momentum to the piston. More frequent collisions mean greater total force per unit area — pressure increases.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C1
Explanation: This illustrates Boyle's law (for a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature, P × V = constant). Halving the volume doubles the pressure. Particles themselves do not change size — it is the frequency of collisions with the container walls that changes.
Tags: pressure, volume, Boyle's law, gas laws, particle collisions

**Item 14 [S]**
Question: Use kinetic theory to explain why a gas expands when heated at constant pressure.
Answer: When heated, gas particles gain kinetic energy and move faster. At constant pressure, the particles collide with the container walls with more force and more frequently. To keep the pressure constant, the volume must increase so that particles travel further between collisions with the walls, maintaining the same collision frequency and force per collision. Effectively, faster particles need more space to maintain the same pressure.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C1
Explanation: Charles's law: at constant pressure, volume is directly proportional to absolute temperature (V ∝ T). This is because heating particles makes them move faster, increasing their momentum transfer per collision. To keep pressure constant (force per unit area), the volume must increase so collisions are spread over a larger area.
Tags: Charles's law, gas expansion, heating, constant pressure, kinetic theory

**Item 15 [S]**
Question: Compare the density of solids, liquids, and gases, and explain using kinetic theory.
Answer: Solids are most dense because particles are packed closely together in a regular lattice. Liquids are slightly less dense because particles are close but randomly arranged (slightly more space between them). Gases are far less dense because particles are far apart, with mostly empty space between them. Most substances are denser as solids than liquids (water is a notable exception — ice is less dense than liquid water).
Difficulty: S
Topic: C1
Explanation: Density = mass/volume. In solids, particles are most tightly packed. In liquids, particles are close together but not in a fixed lattice, so they can arrange slightly less efficiently. In gases, particles are so far apart that the mass is distributed over a much larger volume. Water's anomalous density is due to hydrogen bonding in ice creating an open lattice structure.
Tags: density, states of matter, kinetic theory, comparison

**Item 16 [C]**
Question: A sealed rigid container of gas is heated from 27°C to 327°C. Calculate the factor by which the pressure increases, assuming constant volume.
Answer: Using the pressure law: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂ (temperatures in Kelvin). T₁ = 27 + 273 = 300 K. T₂ = 327 + 273 = 600 K. P₂/P₁ = T₂/T₁ = 600/300 = 2. The pressure doubles.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C1
Explanation: The pressure law (Gay-Lussac's law): at constant volume, pressure is directly proportional to absolute temperature. Doubling the absolute temperature doubles the pressure. This occurs because particle kinetic energy doubles (at 2× temperature), and with a fixed volume, collisions with the walls are twice as frequent and forceful.
Tags: pressure law, gas law, temperature, pressure, kinetic theory

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: A weather balloon filled with helium rises from ground level to high altitude. Using kinetic theory, explain what happens to the volume of the balloon and the pressure inside it.
Answer: At high altitude, atmospheric pressure decreases. The helium inside the balloon exerts pressure on the flexible balloon skin from inside. With lower external pressure, the balloon expands until the internal pressure equals the external pressure. According to Boyle's law (P₁V₁ = P₂V₂), as external pressure decreases, volume increases. The balloon continues to expand until it reaches an altitude where either the balloon material prevents further expansion or it bursts.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C1
Explanation: Weather balloons expand as they rise because external air pressure decreases with altitude. The balloon is not rigid, so its volume changes to equalise internal and external pressure. The ideal gas law (PV = nRT) confirms that at constant temperature, decreasing pressure requires increasing volume. Weather balloons typically burst at ~30 km altitude.
Tags: Boyle's law, altitude, pressure, gas expansion, weather balloon

**Item 18 [C]**
Question: Evaluate the following statement: "The Kelvin scale of temperature is more scientifically useful than the Celsius scale."
Answer: The statement is CORRECT for many scientific applications. The Kelvin scale is an absolute temperature scale: 0 K (absolute zero) is the lowest possible temperature (particles have zero kinetic energy). In Kelvin, temperature is directly proportional to average particle kinetic energy, making it fundamental to physics. The Celsius scale is an interval scale based on water's properties (0°C = freezing, 100°C = boiling), useful for everyday measurements but arbitrary. When using gas laws (P ∝ T, V ∝ T), temperatures MUST be in Kelvin because the proportional relationships only hold for absolute temperature. Using Celsius can give negative pressures or physically impossible results. However, for everyday use and non-physics contexts, Celsius is perfectly adequate. The Kelvin scale is preferred in physics and chemistry where gas laws or thermodynamic calculations are involved.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C1
Explanation: Absolute zero (0 K = −273.15°C) is the temperature at which all particle motion ceases. No physical system can be cooled below this. The Kelvin scale was defined so that the size of one degree Kelvin equals the size of one degree Celsius, making conversion simple: T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15. In thermodynamics, all equations use absolute temperature, making Kelvin the fundamental scale.
Tags: Kelvin scale, Celsius scale, absolute zero, temperature scales, evaluation
