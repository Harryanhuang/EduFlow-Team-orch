# Topic B2 — Cell Structure and Organisation
## Items File

**Item 1 [F]**
Question: Name three structures found in a plant cell but NOT in an animal cell.
Answer: Cell wall, chloroplasts, large central vacuole
Difficulty: F
Topic: B2
Explanation: Plant cells have a rigid cell wall (made of cellulose), chloroplasts (for photosynthesis), and a large central vacuole (for storage and structural support). Animal cells have none of these structures.
Tags: plant cells, animal cells, cell structure, differences between cells

**Item 2 [F]**
Question: State two differences between a prokaryotic cell and a eukaryotic cell.
Answer: (1) Prokaryotic cells have no membrane-bound nucleus (DNA free in cytoplasm); eukaryotic cells have a membrane-bound nucleus. (2) Prokaryotic cells have no membrane-bound organelles (e.g., mitochondria); eukaryotic cells do.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B2
Explanation: Prokaryotic cells (bacteria) have their genetic material as a circular DNA loop free in the cytoplasm, with no nuclear membrane. Eukaryotic cells (plants, animals, fungi, protoctists) have a membrane-bound nucleus containing linear chromosomes. Also, prokaryotic cells lack membrane-bound organelles like mitochondria and chloroplasts.
Tags: prokaryotic cells, eukaryotic cells, cell structure, differences

**Item 3 [S]**
Question: A cell has a magnification of ×400 and the image size is 2 mm. Calculate the actual size of the specimen. Give your answer in micrometres (μm).
Answer: 5 μm
Difficulty: S
Topic: B2
Explanation: Magnification = image size / actual size. So actual size = image size / magnification = 2 mm / 400 = 0.005 mm. Convert to μm: 0.005 mm × 1000 μm/mm = 5 μm.
Tags: magnification, microscopy, calculations, cell size

**Item 4 [S]**
Question: Describe the function of (a) mitochondria (b) ribosomes (c) cell membrane.
Answer: (a) Mitochondria: site of aerobic respiration, produces ATP (energy). (b) Ribosomes: site of protein synthesis. (c) Cell membrane: controls what enters/exits the cell (selectively permeable).
Difficulty: S
Topic: B2
Explanation: Mitochondria have a double membrane structure and are the powerhouse of the cell — they carry out aerobic respiration to release energy from glucose. Ribosomes are small structures (either free in cytoplasm or on rough ER) that assemble amino acids into proteins. The cell membrane is a phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins that regulate transport.
Tags: cell organelles, function, mitochondria, ribosomes, cell membrane

**Item 5 [S]**
Question: Explain why multicellular organisms need specialised cells rather than all cells being identical.
Answer: Specialised cells allow organisms to carry out complex functions efficiently. Different cells are adapted for different roles (e.g., muscle cells for contraction, nerve cells for transmission), which is more efficient than a single cell type trying to do everything. Specialisation allows larger body size, higher efficiency, and the ability to survive in diverse environments.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B2
Explanation: In multicellular organisms, cells differentiate to form tissues, organs, and organ systems. Each specialised cell type has specific adaptations that make it efficient at its particular function. This division of labour allows the organism to carry out complex life processes that would be impossible for a single cell type.
Tags: cell specialisation, multicellular organisms, differentiation, efficiency

**Item 6 [S]**
Question: Complete: Root hair cell — long projection — absorb water and minerals. Red blood cell — biconcave disc shape — carry oxygen. Nerve cell — long axon — transmit electrical impulses.
Answer: (a) Absorb water and minerals; (b) Biconcave disc shape (no nucleus); (c) Transmit electrical impulses
Difficulty: S
Topic: B2
Explanation: Root hair cells have long projections (microvilli) that increase surface area for water and mineral uptake. Red blood cells have a biconcave disc shape to maximise surface area for oxygen loading/unloading, and lose their nucleus to create more space for haemoglobin. Nerve cells have a long axon to transmit electrical impulses over long distances.
Tags: cell specialisation, adaptations, root hair cell, red blood cell, nerve cell

**Item 7 [C]**
Question: Compare the structure of a sperm cell with a root hair cell. Explain how each adaptation relates to its function.
Answer: Sperm cell: tail/flagellum for swimming to egg, many mitochondria for energy, pointed acrosome containing enzymes to digest through egg membrane. Root hair cell: long projection (villi) to increase surface area for water absorption, large vacuole for water storage. Both are specialised for their specific roles: sperm for mobility and fertilisation, root hair for maximised absorption.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B2
Explanation: The sperm cell is adapted for locomotion and fertilisation: the flagellum propels it through the female reproductive tract, mitochondria provide ATP for the tail movement, and the acrosome releases enzymes to penetrate the egg's zona pellucida. The root hair cell's long thin projection dramatically increases surface area for water and mineral ion uptake from the soil by active transport and osmosis.
Tags: cell specialisation, adaptations, sperm cell, root hair cell, comparison

**Item 8 [C]**
Question: As a cell grows larger, its surface area to volume ratio decreases. Explain why this limits cell size and how this is overcome in multicellular organisms.
Answer: As a cell grows, volume increases faster than surface area, so the SA:V ratio decreases. This limits cell size because: (1) the cell membrane cannot supply enough nutrients or remove waste quickly enough for the large volume; (2) diffusion distances become too large. Multicellular organisms overcome this through cell division (cells divide before becoming too large) and by forming tissues with specialized exchange surfaces (e.g., gut villi, lung alveoli) that increase effective surface area.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B2
Explanation: If a spherical cell doubles in radius, its volume increases by 2³ = 8 times, but its surface area only increases by 2² = 4 times. This means each unit of membrane must service twice as much volume. Cells that become too large cannot get enough nutrients or get rid of wastes efficiently. This is why cells divide when they reach a certain size, and why organisms have specialised exchange surfaces (alveoli, villi, root hairs) rather than relying on simple diffusion through large cells.
Tags: surface area to volume ratio, cell size limitation, cell division, exchange surfaces

**Item 9 [C]**
Question: A student examines a cell under a microscope and observes a cell wall, chloroplasts, and a large central vacuole. The student concludes the cell is a plant cell. Evaluate this conclusion.
Answer: The conclusion is STRONGLY SUPPORTED — all three observed structures are characteristic of plant cells. However, one observation alone is usually sufficient to identify a plant cell (e.g., cell wall or chloroplasts are unique to plant cells). A possible exception: some algae have chloroplasts, and some fungal cells have cell walls but no chloroplasts. Final verdict: the conclusion is correct with very high probability.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B2
Explanation: Cell wall (cellulose), chloroplasts, and large central vacuole are all structures found in plant cells and not in animal cells. Bacterial cells have cell walls but no chloroplasts or large central vacuoles. Fungal cells have cell walls but no chloroplasts. Therefore, the combination of all three structures is conclusive evidence of a plant cell.
Tags: microscopy, plant cell identification, evaluating conclusions, cell structure
