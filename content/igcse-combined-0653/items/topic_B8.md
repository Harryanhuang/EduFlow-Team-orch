# Topic B8 — Excretion
## Items File

**Item 1 [F]**
Question: What is excretion?
Answer: Excretion is the removal of metabolic waste products from the body. These waste products are produced by the chemical reactions of metabolism and, if accumulated, would be toxic to the body.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B8
Explanation: Metabolic reactions produce waste substances that must be removed. Carbon dioxide (from respiration) is excreted by the lungs. Urea (from protein breakdown) is excreted by the kidneys. These waste products are different from egestion (defaecation), which removes undigested food from the digestive system.
Tags: excretion, waste products, metabolism

**Item 2 [F]**
Question: Where is urea produced and how is it removed from the body?
Answer: Urea is produced in the liver from the breakdown of excess amino acids. It is transported in the blood to the kidneys, where it is filtered out and excreted in urine. Urine passes through the ureters to the bladder and is released from the body via the urethra.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B8
Explanation: Deamination in the liver converts toxic ammonia (from amino acid breakdown) into urea, which is less toxic and soluble. Urea in the blood is filtered out by the kidneys in the nephrons. This process maintains blood nitrogen levels within safe limits.
Tags: urea, liver, kidneys, urine, deamination

**Item 3 [F]**
Question: Name two organs involved in excretion.
Answer: (1) Lungs — excrete carbon dioxide and water vapour. (2) Kidneys — excrete urea, excess water, and mineral salts.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B8
Explanation: The lungs remove CO₂ (the main gaseous waste product of aerobic respiration) and water vapour through breathing out. The kidneys filter blood and remove urea, excess water, and mineral ions in the form of urine. The skin also excretes sweat (water, salts, urea).
Tags: excretion, lungs, kidneys, waste removal

**Item 4 [F]**
Question: What is deamination?
Answer: Deamination is the removal of the amino group (NH₂) from an amino acid in the liver. The remaining carbon skeleton is converted into urea (for excretion) or used for energy.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B8
Explanation: When excess protein is consumed, the amino acids cannot be stored. Deamination breaks down the amino group, producing ammonia (toxic) and a carbon skeleton. Ammonia is quickly converted to urea in the urea cycle. The carbon skeleton can be respired for energy or converted to fat.
Tags: deamination, liver, amino acids, urea

**Item 5 [S]**
Question: Draw and label a diagram of the human kidney, showing the cortex, medulla, renal pelvis, and ureter.
Answer: The kidney is bean-shaped. The outer layer is the cortex (darker red). Inside is the medulla (striated). The innermost hollow space is the renal pelvis, which connects to the ureter. The ureter carries urine to the bladder.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B8
Explanation: The kidney's structure is beautifully organised for filtration and reabsorption. The cortex contains the Bowman's capsules and convoluted tubules. The medulla contains the loops of Henle and collecting ducts. The renal pelvis is the funnel-shaped beginning of the ureter, collecting urine.
Tags: kidney, anatomy, cortex, medulla, nephron

**Item 6 [S]**
Question: Describe the structure of a nephron.
Answer: A nephron consists of: (1) Bowman's capsule — cups around a glomerulus of capillaries, where filtration begins. (2) Proximal convoluted tubule — reabsorbs glucose, amino acids, and most water and salts. (3) Loop of Henle — concentrates the tubular fluid. (4) Distal convoluted tubule — fine-tunes salt and water balance. (5) Collecting duct — carries urine to the renal pelvis.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B8
Explanation: Each kidney contains about 1 million nephrons. The nephron is the functional unit of the kidney. Filtration at the Bowman's capsule is non-selective — all small molecules enter the filtrate. Reabsorption in the tubules is selective — only useful substances are recovered.
Tags: nephron, kidney, filtration, reabsorption

**Item 7 [S]**
Question: What is the role of the bladder in the excretory system?
Answer: The bladder stores urine until it can be conveniently expelled from the body. The bladder wall contains smooth muscle (detrusor muscle) that contracts during urination to push urine out through the urethra.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B8
Explanation: The bladder can hold about 500–700 mL of urine. Stretch receptors in the bladder wall send signals to the brain when the bladder is full, creating the urge to urinate. Voluntary control (external sphincter) allows delayed urination until socially appropriate.
Tags: bladder, urinary system, urine storage

**Item 8 [S]**
Question: Explain how the lungs excrete carbon dioxide.
Answer: CO₂ is produced in cells by aerobic respiration and diffuses into the blood plasma. It is carried to the lungs in three ways: (1) dissolved in plasma; (2) as bicarbonate ions (H₂CO₃/HCO₃⁻); (3) bound to haemoglobin (carbaminohaemoglobin). In the alveoli, CO₂ diffuses from the blood (high concentration) into the alveolar air (low concentration) and is breathed out.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B8
Explanation: The bicarbonate buffer system (CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻) is the main way CO₂ is transported. This system also helps maintain blood pH. If CO₂ accumulates (hypoventilation), blood becomes acidic; if blown off too fast (hyperventilation), blood becomes alkaline.
Tags: carbon dioxide, lungs, gas exchange, respiration

**Item 9 [S]**
Question: What is the function of the skin in excretion?
Answer: The skin excretes sweat, which contains water, salts (sodium chloride), and small amounts of urea. This is a minor excretory function that helps regulate body temperature and maintain water and salt balance.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B8
Explanation: Sweat is produced by sweat glands in the dermis and excreted through pores. Its main function is temperature regulation — evaporative cooling. Excretion is secondary. In hot conditions or during exercise, sweat production increases. Sweat contains mainly water, with dissolved salts and trace urea.
Tags: skin, sweat, excretion, temperature regulation

**Item 10 [S]**
Question: Describe what happens to the glomerular filtrate as it passes through the nephron.
Answer: In the Bowman's capsule: water, glucose, amino acids, urea, salts are filtered from blood. In the proximal tubule: all glucose and amino acids are reabsorbed by active transport; most water and salts are reabsorbed by osmosis. In the Loop of Henle: water is reabsorbed from the collecting duct (controlled by ADH), and salts are actively pumped out. In the distal tubule: further fine-tuning of salts and water. The collecting duct: final concentration of urine. The result is dilute filtrate becomes concentrated urine.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B8
Explanation: The nephron processes about 180 litres of filtrate per day, producing only 1–2 litres of urine. More than 99% of the filtrate is reabsorbed. This remarkable efficiency is achieved through selective reabsorption in different tubule segments.
Tags: nephron, filtration, reabsorption, urine concentration

**Item 11 [S]**
Question: Explain why dialysis patients need regular treatment.
Answer: In kidney failure, urea and other waste products accumulate in the blood, becoming toxic. Dialysis uses a semi-permeable membrane to filter blood — waste molecules diffuse from the blood into the dialysis fluid, which contains no urea or waste. Clean blood returns to the body. Dialysis must be regular because kidney failure is permanent and waste accumulates continuously.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B8
Explanation: Haemodialysis: blood is passed through a machine containing a semi-permeable membrane. Dialysis fluid (dialysate) on the other side has carefully controlled concentrations of salts and glucose. Urea diffuses out; necessary substances are maintained. Treatment takes 3–5 hours, typically 3 times per week. Kidney transplantation is the preferred long-term treatment.
Tags: dialysis, kidney failure, filtration, treatment

**Item 12 [S]**
Question: Compare the kidney's role in excretion with the liver's role.
Answer: The liver: deaminates excess amino acids (producing urea), stores glycogen, detoxifies alcohol and drugs, and produces bile. The kidneys: filter blood and remove urea, excess water, and mineral salts as urine. Both organs are essential for homeostasis, but the kidneys are the primary organs of nitrogenous waste excretion.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B8
Explanation: The liver and kidneys work together in nitrogen metabolism. The liver converts ammonia (toxic) to urea (less toxic). The kidneys filter urea from the blood and excrete it. If either organ fails, nitrogenous waste accumulates and death follows without medical intervention.
Tags: liver, kidney, comparison, homeostasis

**Item 13 [C]**
Question: A person has kidney failure. Compare kidney transplantation with dialysis as treatment options, including advantages and disadvantages of each.
Answer: Kidney transplantation: A donated kidney replaces the failed kidneys. Advantages: Normal kidney function restored, no regular hospital visits, no dietary restrictions, better quality of life, more cost-effective long-term. Disadvantages: Major surgery, risk of organ rejection (immune system attacks the new kidney), lifelong immunosuppressant drugs (increased infection risk, side effects), long waiting list for donor kidneys, must take anti-rejection drugs permanently.
Dialysis: Machine or peritoneal cavity filters waste from blood. Advantages: Immediately available for all patients, life-saving when transplantation is not possible, good short-term solution. Disadvantages: Time-consuming (3–5 hours, 3 times/week), strict dietary restrictions, fatigue, risk of infection at catheter site, reduced quality of life, not a cure (patient depends on machine).
Conclusion: Transplantation is the preferred long-term treatment if a suitable donor is available, but dialysis is essential for patients awaiting transplant or when transplantation is not possible.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B8
Explanation: Over 100,000 kidney transplants are performed worldwide annually. Living donor kidneys have better survival rates than deceased donor kidneys. Immunosuppressant drugs (ciclosporin, tacrolimus) have dramatically improved transplant success rates. Peritoneal dialysis uses the patient's own peritoneum as a semi-permeable membrane and can be done at home.
Tags: kidney transplant, dialysis, comparison, treatment options

**Item 14 [C]**
Question: Explain how the kidney maintains blood water potential. Include reference to the role of ADH and the Loop of Henle.
Answer: The kidney maintains water balance through osmoregulation. The Loop of Henle creates a concentration gradient in the medulla (osmotic gradient from 300 to 1200 mOsm/kg) using counter-current multiplication: Na⁺ and Cl⁻ are actively pumped out at the ascending limb, water leaves the descending limb by osmosis, making the tubular fluid increasingly concentrated. The collecting duct is permeable to water only when ADH (antidiuretic hormone) is present. When the body is dehydrated, ADH levels rise, making the collecting duct walls more permeable to water. Water is drawn out of the collecting duct by osmosis into the hypertonic medulla, producing a small volume of concentrated urine. When well-hydrated, ADH levels are low, collecting duct remains impermeable to water, and dilute urine is produced.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B8
Explanation: ADH is produced in the hypothalamus and released from the posterior pituitary gland. It binds to V2 receptors on collecting duct cells, causing aquaporin (water channel) proteins to be inserted into the cell membrane. This increases water reabsorption by up to 20-fold. The counter-current multiplier of the Loop of Henle establishes the osmotic gradient essential for water reabsorption.
Tags: osmoregulation, ADH, Loop of Henle, water balance, kidney

**Item 15 [C]**
Question: A patient has diabetes mellitus. Explain how this affects kidney function and why excessive glucose appears in the urine.
Answer: In diabetes mellitus, blood glucose levels are abnormally high (either due to insufficient insulin or insulin resistance). When blood glucose concentration exceeds the kidney's reabsorption threshold (~10 mmol/L), the proximal convoluted tubule cannot reabsorb all the glucose. The excess glucose remains in the filtrate and is excreted in the urine (glycosuria). The osmotic effect of glucose in the urine also draws out additional water (osmotic diuresis), causing increased urine volume (polyuria) and dehydration, leading to increased thirst (polydipsia).
Difficulty: C
Topic: B8
Explanation: Normally, all filtered glucose is reabsorbed in the proximal tubule via SGLT2 transporters (sodium-glucose co-transporters). In uncontrolled diabetes, the transporters become saturated, and glucose "spills" into the urine. This is why glucose in urine is a diagnostic indicator of diabetes. Long-term, high blood glucose damages kidney blood vessels (diabetic nephropathy).
Tags: diabetes, glycosuria, kidney function, glucose reabsorption

**Item 16 [C]**
Question: Describe the process of counter-current multiplication in the Loop of Henle. Explain how this creates the conditions for water reabsorption.
Answer: The Loop of Henle is a U-shaped tube with a descending limb (permeable to water, impermeable to salts) and an ascending limb (impermeable to water, actively pumping out Na⁺ and Cl⁻). (1) Active pumping at the thick ascending limb moves Na⁺ and Cl⁻ into the medulla, making the surrounding fluid increasingly concentrated (up to 1200 mOsm/kg). (2) Water leaves the descending limb by osmosis (the limb is permeable to water), concentrating the tubular fluid. (3) The concentrated fluid flows around and enters the ascending limb, where salts are pumped out again. (4) This creates a gradient of concentration from cortex (300 mOsm/kg) to inner medulla (1200 mOsm/kg). When ADH increases collecting duct permeability, water moves out by osmosis down this gradient, producing concentrated urine.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B8
Explanation: Counter-current multiplication is a brilliant biological solution to water conservation. The counter-current flow (fluid in opposite directions) maintains the concentration gradient along the entire length of the loop. This allows the kidney to produce urine up to 4 times more concentrated than blood plasma, conserving water in desert animals and humans alike.
Tags: counter-current, Loop of Henle, concentration gradient, water reabsorption

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: Evaluate the statement: "Drinking large amounts of water is always beneficial to kidney function."
Answer: This statement is partially correct but oversimplified. Drinking adequate water is essential for kidney function — dehydration concentrates urine, increasing risk of kidney stones and urinary tract infections. However, excessive water intake can cause hyponatraemia (water intoxication), where blood sodium becomes dangerously diluted, causing cells to swell. In extreme cases, this leads to cerebral oedema (brain swelling) and death. Athletes who drink too much water during endurance events are particularly at risk. The kidneys can only process about 0.8–1.0 litres of water per hour. People with certain kidney diseases (e.g., nephrotic syndrome) may need fluid restriction. Conclusion: moderate, regular water intake (about 2 litres/day for adults) is beneficial; excessive intake is not universally beneficial and can be dangerous.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B8
Explanation: The kidneys have a remarkable capacity to concentrate or dilute urine across a wide range of water intake levels. Minimum urine volume (~300 mL/day) is limited by the need to excrete metabolic waste. Maximum urine output is limited by how fast the kidneys can filter blood (~125 mL/min or 180 L/day, but tubular reabsorption reduces this). Water requirements depend on activity level, climate, and individual physiology.
Tags: water intake, kidney function, hyponatraemia, evaluation

**Item 18 [C]**
Question: A kidney donor and recipient are being matched before a transplant operation. Explain why tissue typing is essential and discuss the mechanisms of transplant rejection.
Answer: Tissue typing ensures the donor and recipient have compatible cell surface antigens (mainly HLA — human leukocyte antigens). If antigens are mismatched, the recipient's immune system recognises the transplanted kidney as foreign (non-self) and mounts an immune attack. This is rejection. Mechanism: (1) Recipient T-cells recognise foreign HLA antigens on donor kidney cells. (2) Cytotoxic T-cells directly attack donor kidney cells. (3) B-cells produce antibodies against donor cell antigens, leading to complement activation and cell destruction. (4) Inflammatory response damages blood vessels supplying the transplant.
Prevention: Tissue typing before transplant, immunosuppressive drugs (ciclosporin, tacrolimus, steroids), and careful monitoring. Living donor kidneys are preferred because HLA matching is typically closer (family members), and the transplant can be planned.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B8
Explanation: HLA antigens are highly variable (thousands of possible combinations), making perfect matches rare except between identical twins. Even with immunosuppression, chronic rejection (gradual failure over years) remains a challenge. ABO blood group compatibility is also essential — type O donors can give to any blood type; type AB recipients can receive from any type.
Tags: tissue typing, transplant rejection, HLA antigens, immunosuppression, immune system
