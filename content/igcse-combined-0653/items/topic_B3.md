# Topic B3 — Movement in and out of Cells
## Items File

**Item 1 [F]**
Question: Define diffusion.
Answer: Diffusion is the net movement of particles from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration, down a concentration gradient.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B3
Explanation: Diffusion occurs because particles have random kinetic motion. Over time, particles spread from where they are more concentrated to where they are less concentrated until equilibrium is reached. No energy is required — it is a passive process.
Tags: diffusion, particle movement, passive transport

**Item 2 [F]**
Question: State two factors that affect the rate of diffusion.
Answer: (1) Concentration difference (steeper gradient = faster diffusion); (2) Temperature (higher temperature = faster diffusion because particles have more kinetic energy).
Difficulty: F
Topic: B3
Explanation: A steeper concentration gradient means more net movement of particles from high to low concentration per unit time. Higher temperature gives particles more kinetic energy, making them move faster and collide more frequently, increasing the diffusion rate.
Tags: diffusion, rate factors, temperature, concentration gradient

**Item 3 [F]**
Question: Give one example of diffusion in the human body.
Answer: Oxygen diffusing from the alveoli (high concentration) into the blood capillaries (low concentration) in the lungs.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B3
Explanation: In the lungs, air in the alveoli has a higher oxygen concentration than the blood in the surrounding capillaries. Oxygen molecules diffuse across the thin alveolar wall and the capillary wall into the blood, where it binds to haemoglobin. This is essential for cellular respiration.
Tags: diffusion, human body, gas exchange, alveoli

**Item 4 [F]**
Question: Define osmosis.
Answer: Osmosis is the net movement of water molecules from a region of higher water potential to a region of lower water potential, across a partially permeable membrane.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B3
Explanation: Water potential measures the tendency of water to move from one area to another. Pure water has a water potential of 0 kPa. Solutions with more solute have a more negative water potential. Water moves down the water potential gradient through the membrane's pores.
Tags: osmosis, water potential, partially permeable membrane, passive transport

**Item 5 [S]**
Question: A plant cell is placed in a dilute solution. State and explain what happens to the cell.
Answer: The cell becomes turgid. Water enters the cell by osmosis through the partially permeable cell surface membrane. The vacuole swells, pushing the cytoplasm against the cell wall. The rigid cell wall prevents the cell from bursting. Turgor pressure helps support the plant.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B3
Explanation: The dilute solution has a higher water potential than the cell contents. Water moves into the cell from the outside solution by osmosis. In plant cells, the cell wall is fully permeable to water and provides structural support, preventing the cell from bursting. The resulting pressure (turgor) is essential for keeping herbaceous plants upright.
Tags: osmosis, plant cells, turgid, turgor pressure, cell wall

**Item 6 [S]**
Question: A red blood cell is placed in distilled water. State and explain what happens.
Answer: The cell haemolyses (bursts). Distilled water has a higher water potential than the cytoplasm inside the red blood cell. Water enters the cell by osmosis, and because animal cells have no cell wall, the cell swells until the membrane ruptures.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B3
Explanation: Animal cells lack a rigid cell wall. When placed in a hypotonic solution (higher water potential outside), water moves into the cell by osmosis. The cell membrane can only stretch so far before rupturing, releasing the cell contents — this is called haemolysis. This is why clinical drip solutions must be isotonic.
Tags: osmosis, animal cells, haemolysis, hypotonic, cell membrane

**Item 7 [S]**
Question: Explain the difference between diffusion and active transport.
Answer: Diffusion is the passive movement of particles from high to low concentration without using energy. Active transport moves particles from low to high concentration (against the concentration gradient) and requires energy from respiration (ATP).
Difficulty: S
Topic: B3
Explanation: Both processes move substances across cell membranes. Diffusion is passive — particles move down their concentration gradient naturally. Active transport uses carrier proteins in the membrane and ATP energy to pump substances against their gradient. Active transport is essential for absorbing nutrients when their concentration outside the cell is already lower than inside.
Tags: diffusion, active transport, concentration gradient, ATP, carrier proteins

**Item 8 [S]**
Question: State two ways in which the small intestine is adapted for efficient absorption of digested food.
Answer: (1) Long length and folded into villi, providing a very large surface area for absorption. (2) Thin lining (one cell thick) so digested food only has to diffuse a short distance to reach the blood capillaries.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B3
Explanation: The small intestine is the main site of nutrient absorption. Villi increase the surface area by about 600 times compared to a flat tube. Each villus has a network of capillaries and a lacteal (lymph vessel) to receive absorbed nutrients. The epithelial cells have microvilli on their apical surface, further increasing surface area. The thin wall means diffusion distance is minimised.
Tags: small intestine, villi, adaptation, absorption, surface area

**Item 9 [S]**
Question: Explain why plant cells do not burst when placed in water, while animal cells do.
Answer: Plant cells have a rigid cell wall made of cellulose outside the cell membrane. When water enters by osmosis, the vacuole swells and pushes the cytoplasm against the cell wall. The cell wall resists expansion, generating turgor pressure that stops further water entry. Animal cells have no cell wall — the membrane stretches and eventually ruptures.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B3
Explanation: The key difference is the presence of the cell wall in plant cells. The wall is fully permeable to water and ions but structurally rigid. When plant cells take up water and the vacuole expands, turgor pressure develops inside the cell, which eventually equals the water potential difference and stops net water entry. Animal cells lack this pressure-generating mechanism, so they swell and burst (haemolyse) in hypotonic solutions.
Tags: cell wall, turgor pressure, plant cells, animal cells, osmosis

**Item 10 [S]**
Question: A potato chip is placed in concentrated salt solution. Describe and explain what happens.
Answer: The chip shrinks (becomes flaccid). The concentrated salt solution has a lower (more negative) water potential than the potato cell cytoplasm. Water leaves the cell by osmosis, from the higher water potential inside to the lower water potential outside. The cytoplasm shrinks away from the cell wall — plasmolysis occurs.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B3
Explanation: In a hypertonic solution (lower water potential outside), water moves out of the cell. In plant cells, the cytoplasm and vacuole shrink as water leaves. The cell membrane pulls away from the cell wall — this is plasmolysis. In extreme cases, this kills the cell. This is why salt-stressed plants wilt and why salt is used to preserve foods.
Tags: osmosis, plasmolysis, flaccid, hypertonic solution, water potential

**Item 11 [S]**
Question: Name the process by which water is absorbed into the blood from the alimentary canal, and explain the mechanism.
Answer: Osmosis. Water is absorbed by osmosis from the lumen of the intestine into the blood. This occurs because the digested food (especially glucose and amino acids) has been absorbed into the epithelial cells, lowering their water potential. Water then moves by osmosis from the higher water potential in the lumen to the lower water potential inside the epithelial cells, and then into the blood capillaries.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B3
Explanation: The absorption of water is linked to the active transport of nutrients. When glucose and amino acids are actively transported into the epithelial cells, the water potential inside the cells decreases. Water then moves from the intestinal lumen (higher water potential) into the epithelial cells (lower water potential) by osmosis. Water is also absorbed into the lacteals in villi due to the osmotic effect of fatty acids and glycerol.
Tags: water absorption, osmosis, small intestine, villi, digestion

**Item 12 [S]**
Question: Explain how oxygen and carbon dioxide are exchanged in the lungs.
Answer: Oxygen diffuses from the alveoli (where its concentration is high) into the blood in the capillaries (where its concentration is low). Carbon dioxide diffuses in the opposite direction — from the blood (high CO₂) into the alveoli (low CO₂). The alveoli have a large surface area, a thin wall (one cell thick), and a rich blood supply, all maximising the rate of diffusion.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B3
Explanation: Gas exchange occurs by diffusion across the respiratory surface. Alveoli provide enormous surface area (about 75 m² in humans) and have walls only one cell thick, minimising diffusion distance. The extensive capillary network maintains a steep concentration gradient for both gases. Ventilation (breathing) renews the air in alveoli, keeping oxygen high and CO₂ low.
Tags: gas exchange, diffusion, alveoli, lungs, respiratory system

**Item 13 [C]**
Question: Explain the role of the root hair cell in water uptake by plants. Include the terms osmosis, diffusion, and active transport in your answer.
Answer: Root hair cells are specialised for water and mineral ion uptake. Water enters by osmosis because the soil solution has a higher water potential than the cell sap in the vacuole. Mineral ions are often absorbed by active transport against their concentration gradient, using ATP from respiration. This lowers the water potential inside the root hair cell, drawing in more water by osmosis. Together, these processes move water from the soil into the plant's vascular tissue.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B3
Explanation: Root hair cells have long projections that push between soil particles, maximising contact with soil water. The high concentration of solutes in the vacuole creates a low (negative) water potential that drives water uptake by osmosis. Active transport pumps mineral ions into the root hair cells, which further lowers water potential and increases water intake. Water moves through the cortex via the apoplast pathway and into the xylem by cohesion-tension.
Tags: root hair cell, active transport, osmosis, water uptake, plant transport

**Item 14 [C]**
Question: Design an experiment to investigate the effect of solution concentration on plant tissue. Describe the method, expected results, and conclusion.
Answer: Method: Cut equal-sized strips of potato. Place them in solutions of different concentrations (e.g., 0%, 5%, 10%, 20% sucrose). Leave for 30 minutes, then remove, blot dry, and weigh each strip. Calculate percentage change in mass. Expected: In distilled water (0%), mass increases (turgid). In high concentration, mass decreases (flaccid). At one concentration (approximately 0.3 M sucrose), mass stays the same — this is the isotonic concentration. Conclusion: Water moves into/out of cells by osmosis depending on the water potential of the surrounding solution.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B3
Explanation: This is the standard potato osmoscope experiment. By measuring mass change, we can infer net water movement. At low external water potential, water leaves the cells by osmosis, causing mass loss. At high external water potential, water enters, causing mass gain. The concentration at which no net change occurs indicates the approximate water potential of the potato cells. Control variables include potato variety, strip size, temperature, and immersion time.
Tags: osmosis experiment, potato, water potential, method, isotonic

**Item 15 [C]**
Question: Compare the transport of oxygen in the blood with the transport of glucose to body cells. In your answer, include the mechanisms of transport and the reasons why each mechanism is necessary.
Answer: Oxygen is transported by diffusion in plasma and by binding to haemoglobin in red blood cells. Because oxygen is a small, non-polar molecule, it can diffuse across cell membranes. Glucose, however, is a large polar molecule that cannot diffuse across membranes. It is transported in plasma and must enter cells via facilitated diffusion (through GLUT transporters, down its concentration gradient) or active transport (against the gradient, using ATP). Both are necessary because glucose is too large and polar for passive diffusion, while oxygen's small size makes diffusion efficient.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B3
Explanation: Oxygen (O₂) is a small non-polar molecule that dissolves in plasma and diffuses freely across membranes, so no special transport mechanism is needed. Haemoglobin increases the oxygen-carrying capacity of blood by about 70 times. Glucose is a large, water-soluble molecule that cannot cross the hydrophobic phospholipid bilayer by simple diffusion. Carrier proteins (GLUT transporters) facilitate glucose entry. When blood glucose is low, active transport can pump glucose into cells against its gradient.
Tags: oxygen transport, glucose transport, haemoglobin, facilitated diffusion, active transport

**Item 16 [C]**
Question: Explain what is meant by water potential. Describe what happens to animal cells when placed in solutions with (a) higher, (b) lower, and (c) equal water potential compared to the cell cytoplasm.
Answer: Water potential (Ψ) measures the tendency of water to move from one area to another. Pure water has Ψ = 0 kPa; adding solute makes it more negative. (a) Higher external Ψ (hypotonic): water enters the cell by osmosis — animal cells swell and may burst (haemolysis). (b) Lower external Ψ (hypertonic): water leaves — animal cells shrink (crenation). (c) Equal Ψ (isotonic): no net water movement — cell shape unchanged.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B3
Explanation: Water always moves from a higher to a lower water potential. In animal cells, the cell membrane is the only barrier. In hypotonic solutions, water enters and the membrane stretches. In severe cases, the membrane ruptures. In hypertonic solutions, water exits and the cell shrivels (crenation). Only in isotonic solutions is the cell stable. Clinical context: IV fluids must be isotonic to avoid damaging blood cells.
Tags: water potential, crenation, haemolysis, hypotonic, hypertonic, isotonic

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: Evaluate the importance of active transport in the kidney in maintaining blood homeostasis. Include reference to osmoregulation and ion balance.
Answer: The kidney uses active transport extensively to regulate blood composition. In the proximal convoluted tubule, sodium ions are actively pumped out of the filtrate into the blood, creating an electrochemical gradient that drives reabsorption of glucose, amino acids, and water by osmosis and facilitated diffusion. The Loop of Henle uses active transport to create a concentration gradient in the medulla, enabling water reabsorption in the collecting duct. This osmoregulation ensures blood water potential remains stable. Additionally, active transport of ions (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻) in the distal tubule fine-tunes blood ion balance.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B3
Explanation: The kidney nephron is a masterpiece of active transport. The Na⁺/K⁺ pump in the basolateral membrane uses ATP to pump Na⁺ out and K⁺ in, establishing a gradient that drives most reabsorption in the proximal tubule. The counter-current multiplier in the Loop of Henle creates a gradient from 300 mOsm/kg (cortex) to 1200 mOsm/kg (medulla), allowing variable water reabsorption depending on hydration state. This maintains blood water potential within narrow limits despite variable fluid intake.
Tags: kidney, active transport, osmoregulation, homeostasis, nephron

**Item 18 [C]**
Question: A student states that "osmosis is just diffusion of water." Evaluate this statement. In your answer, discuss what osmosis has in common with diffusion and how it differs, with reference to membrane properties and water potential.
Answer: The student is partially correct. Like diffusion, osmosis is passive — no energy is used, and particles (water molecules) move down a concentration gradient. Both are forms of particle movement driven by random kinetic motion. However, there are key differences. Diffusion applies to any particle moving down its own concentration gradient. Osmosis specifically refers to water movement and requires a partially permeable membrane that prevents solute movement. The driving force for osmosis is specifically the water potential gradient (not solute concentration alone), and the membrane selectively restricts solute movement. Therefore, osmosis is a specific type of diffusion, not simply diffusion of water — the membrane constraint and water potential concept make it a distinct physical phenomenon.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B3
Explanation: Diffusion: any particles, down own concentration gradient. Osmosis: specifically water, down water potential gradient, across a partially permeable membrane. The key distinction is that osmosis is constrained by the membrane — solutes cannot pass, so water movement is not about solute gradient but about water potential. Water potential (Ψ) combines solute concentration and pressure. The partially permeable membrane makes osmosis fundamentally different from free diffusion: in free diffusion, both solvents and solutes move; in osmosis, only water moves. This distinction matters for understanding clinical conditions (IV fluids, oedema) and plant physiology (wilting, turgor).
Tags: osmosis, diffusion, water potential, evaluation, partially permeable membrane
