# Topic C3 — Bonding and Structure
## Items File

**Item 1 [F]**
Question: What is an ionic bond?
Answer: An ionic bond is the electrostatic force of attraction between positively charged metal ions and negatively charged non-metal ions in an ionic compound.
Difficulty: F
Topic: C3
Explanation: Ionic bonds form when metal atoms lose electrons (to form positive ions) and non-metal atoms gain electrons (to form negative ions). The oppositely charged ions are attracted to each other, forming a giant ionic lattice.
Tags: ionic bond, electrostatic force, ionic compound, lattice

**Item 2 [F]**
Question: State two properties of ionic compounds.
Answer: (1) High melting and boiling points — strong electrostatic forces between ions require a lot of energy to break. (2) They conduct electricity when molten or dissolved in water, but not when solid.
Difficulty: F
Topic: C3
Explanation: In a solid ionic lattice, ions are held in fixed positions and cannot move, so they cannot conduct. When melted or dissolved, ions are free to move and can carry charge.
Tags: ionic compounds, properties, melting point, electrical conductivity

**Item 3 [F]**
Question: Draw the dot-and-cross diagram for sodium chloride (NaCl).
Answer: Na: one electron in outer shell (2, 8, 1). Cl: seven electrons in outer shell (2, 8, 7). Na loses its outer electron → Na⁺. Cl gains one electron → Cl⁻. Ionic transfer: Na gives one electron to Cl. Both achieve full outer shells.
Difficulty: F
Topic: C3
Explanation: Dot-and-cross diagrams show electron transfer using different symbols (dots and crosses) for clarity. NaCl is an ionic compound with strong electrostatic forces between Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions in the lattice.
Tags: dot-and-cross diagram, ionic bonding, electron transfer, sodium chloride

**Item 4 [F]**
Question: What is a covalent bond?
Answer: A covalent bond is formed when two non-metal atoms share a pair of electrons to achieve a full outer shell.
Difficulty: F
Topic: C3
Explanation: Covalent bonds are strong bonds between atoms within molecules. Each atom contributes one electron to the shared pair, so both atoms effectively have a full outer shell.
Tags: covalent bond, electron sharing, non-metals, bonding

**Item 5 [S]**
Question: Draw the dot-and-cross diagram for a molecule of hydrogen (H₂).
Answer: Each H atom has one electron in its outer shell. The two hydrogen atoms share their electrons: H· · H. Each H effectively has two electrons (a full first shell). The shared pair is one covalent bond.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C3
Explanation: In H₂, two electrons are shared between two H atoms. Each H has access to two electrons (one from each atom), giving a full outer shell (duet rule, since shell 1 holds 2 electrons).
Tags: dot-and-cross, covalent bond, hydrogen, molecule

**Item 6 [S]**
Question: What is the difference between a single bond and a double bond?
Answer: Single bond: two atoms share one pair of electrons. Double bond: two atoms share two pairs of electrons (four electrons total). Double bonds are shorter and stronger than single bonds.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C3
Explanation: A single bond (e.g., H–H) consists of one shared pair of electrons. A double bond (e.g., O=O in oxygen) consists of two shared pairs. Double bonds require more energy to break than single bonds.
Tags: single bond, double bond, covalent bonding, bond strength

**Item 7 [S]**
Question: Describe the structure and bonding of diamond.
Answer: Diamond has a giant covalent structure. Each carbon atom forms four covalent bonds to four other carbon atoms, creating a tetrahedral network. The strong covalent bonds throughout the structure give diamond its extreme hardness and very high melting point.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C3
Explanation: Diamond is an allotrope of carbon. The tetrahedral network means there are no free electrons, so diamond is an electrical insulator. The covalent bonds are directional and very strong, requiring high temperatures to break.
Tags: diamond, giant covalent structure, carbon, properties of diamond

**Item 8 [S]**
Question: What is a coordinate bond (dative covalent bond)?
Answer: A coordinate bond is a covalent bond where both electrons are provided by one atom. One atom donates a lone pair of electrons to an empty orbital of another atom.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C3
Explanation: Coordinate bonds are shown with an arrow from the donor to the acceptor. They are found in molecules like carbon monoxide (CO), ammonium ions (NH₄⁺), and in complex ions.
Tags: coordinate bond, dative covalent bond, lone pair, electron pair

**Item 9 [S]**
Question: Compare the electrical conductivity of metals and ionic compounds.
Answer: Metals conduct electricity because delocalised electrons can move through the metal lattice, carrying charge. Ionic compounds only conduct when molten or dissolved (ions free to move). Solid ionic compounds do not conduct.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C3
Explanation: In metals, electrons are the charge carriers and can move even in the solid state. In ionic solutions, ions are the charge carriers and can only move when free to flow.
Tags: electrical conductivity, metals, ionic compounds, charge carriers

**Item 10 [S]**
Question: Explain why graphite conducts electricity but diamond does not.
Answer: Graphite has delocalised electrons between layers. Each carbon atom in graphite forms only three covalent bonds, leaving one free electron per atom. These delocalised electrons can move through the structure, conducting electricity. Diamond has all four carbon bonds used in covalent network, leaving no free electrons — it is an insulator.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C3
Explanation: In graphite, the layers are held together by weak Van der Waals forces, allowing them to slide over each other (explaining why graphite is used as a lubricant). The spare electrons between layers conduct electricity.
Tags: graphite, diamond, electrical conductivity, allotropes of carbon

**Item 11 [S]**
Question: What is a polymer?
Answer: A polymer is a large molecule (macromolecule) formed when many small monomer units join together in a long chain through covalent bonds.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C3
Explanation: Addition polymers (e.g., poly(ethene)) are formed from unsaturated monomers like ethene. Condensation polymers (e.g., proteins, nylon) involve monomers with two functional groups reacting with elimination of water.
Tags: polymer, monomer, addition polymer, macromolecule

**Item 12 [S]**
Question: Describe the bonding and structure of metals.
Answer: Metals consist of a giant metallic lattice. Positive metal ions are held together by a sea of delocalised electrons. The electrons are free to move, explaining metals' high electrical and thermal conductivity. The strong metallic bonds also explain their high melting points.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C3
Explanation: The "electron sea" model explains why metals conduct heat and electricity (mobile electrons transfer energy and charge) and why they are malleable (layers of ions can slide over each other without breaking bonds).
Tags: metallic bonding, metallic lattice, delocalised electrons, electron sea

**Item 13 [C]**
Question: Compare the physical properties of ionic, covalent, and metallic substances.
Answer: Ionic: high melting point (strong electrostatic forces), brittle (layers shift → like charges repel), conducts when molten/dissolved. Covalent molecular: low melting point (weak forces between molecules), soft, does not conduct. Covalent giant (diamond/graphite): very high melting point (strong covalent bonds), hard, only graphite conducts. Metallic: high melting point, malleable/ductile, conducts as solid and liquid.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C3
Explanation: The physical properties depend on the type of bonding and structure. Ionic: strong bonds throughout → high mp. Covalent molecular: weak intermolecular forces → low mp. Covalent giant: strong directional bonds throughout → high mp, very hard. Metallic: delocalised electrons allow malleability and high conductivity.
Tags: comparison, properties, ionic, covalent, metallic, structure

**Item 14 [C]**
Question: Explain why sodium chloride has a high melting point but dissolves in water.
Answer: In the solid lattice, strong ionic bonds hold Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions together, requiring high energy to break (high melting point ~801°C). When placed in water, the polar water molecules attract the ions. Water molecules surround and solvate ions, separating them from each other. The ion-dipole attractions are strong enough to overcome the ionic bonds, allowing dissolution.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C3
Explanation: Dissolution of ionic compounds in water involves ion-dipole interactions. Water is a polar molecule (δ⁺ on H, δ⁻ on O) that can stabilise ions through electrostatic attraction. The lattice energy (energy to separate ions) must be overcome; if the hydration energy released is greater, dissolution is energetically favourable.
Tags: dissolving, ion-dipole, hydration, lattice energy, ionic compounds

**Item 15 [C]**
Question: Evaluate the use of fullerenes in medicine and materials science.
Answer: Fullerenes (C60, C70) are carbon molecules in closed cage or tube structures. Medicine: fullerene derivatives act as antioxidants (scavenge free radicals), drug delivery vehicles (can encapsulate drug molecules), and photosensitisers in photodynamic therapy. Materials: used in lubricants (spherical molecules reduce friction), solar cells (light absorption), and composites (reinforcing materials). Limitations: toxicity of some fullerene derivatives is uncertain; large-scale synthesis is difficult; cost is high.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C3
Explanation: Buckminsterfullerene (C60) was discovered in 1985. Its soccer ball structure (12 pentagons + 20 hexagons) is highly stable. Nanotubes (rolled-up graphite sheets) have extraordinary mechanical strength and electrical properties. Graphene (single layer of graphite) has revolutionised materials science.
Tags: fullerenes, nanotechnology, applications, evaluation, materials science

**Item 16 [C]**
Question: A student says: "Diamond and graphite are both made of carbon, so they must have the same properties." Evaluate this statement.
Answer: The statement is INCORRECT. Diamond and graphite are both pure carbon (allotropes) but have very different properties due to their different bonding and structure. Diamond: each carbon forms four covalent bonds in a tetrahedral network — the strongest directional bonds give extreme hardness, high melting point (~3550°C), and it is an electrical insulator. Graphite: each carbon forms only three covalent bonds, creating flat hexagonal layers with delocalised electrons between them — graphite is soft (layers slide), used as a lubricant, and conducts electricity. Both have high melting points because both require breaking strong bonds.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C3
Explanation: Allotropes are different structural forms of the same element. The bonding and structure determine physical properties, not the identity of the atoms. The C–C bonds are identical in both, but the arrangement differs dramatically.
Tags: allotropes, diamond, graphite, comparison, evaluation

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: Compare the bonding and properties of nylon and poly(ethene).
Answer: Poly(ethene): addition polymer formed by addition polymerisation of ethene monomers. The polymer chain contains only carbon atoms linked by covalent bonds. Nylon: condensation polymer formed by reaction between diamine and dicarboxylic acid monomers. Each link forms with elimination of water. Nylon has hydrogen bonding between chains (from N–H groups); poly(ethene) does not. This gives nylon higher melting point and greater tensile strength.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C3
Explanation: Addition polymers: monomer double bond opens to join chains, no atoms lost. Condensation polymers: two monomers react with elimination of water (or HCl). The intermolecular forces in nylon (H-bonding) are much stronger than Van der Waals in poly(ethene), explaining nylon's superior mechanical properties.
Tags: addition polymer, condensation polymer, nylon, poly(ethene), comparison

**Item 18 [C]**
Question: Explain why metals are malleable but ionic crystals are brittle.
Answer: Metals are malleable because their layers of positive ions can slide over each other without breaking bonds. The delocalised "sea" of electrons adjusts around the new ion positions, maintaining the metallic bonding throughout. In ionic crystals, if layers shift slightly, ions of the same charge come into contact and repel each other, causing the crystal to fracture. There is no equivalent "adjustable" bonding mechanism.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C3
Explanation: Malleability in metals is a consequence of the non-directional nature of metallic bonding. In ionic crystals, the bonds are directional and specific to particular ion pairs — when displaced, repulsive forces between like-charged neighbours cause fracturing.
Tags: malleability, brittleness, metallic bonding, ionic bonding, crystal structure
