# Topic B10 — Reproduction
## Items File

**Item 1 [F]**
Question: What is sexual reproduction?
Answer: Sexual reproduction is the fusion of male and female gametes (sex cells) to form a zygote. The male gamete (sperm) fuses with the female gamete (egg/ovum) in a process called fertilisation. The offspring produced are genetically different from both parents.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B10
Explanation: Sexual reproduction introduces genetic variation through meiosis (producing gametes with different combinations of chromosomes) and the random fusion of gametes during fertilisation. This variation is the raw material for evolution by natural selection.
Tags: sexual reproduction, gametes, fertilisation, variation

**Item 2 [F]**
Question: What is asexual reproduction?
Answer: Asexual reproduction is the production of offspring from a single parent without the fusion of gametes. The offspring are genetically identical to the parent (clones).
Difficulty: F
Topic: B10
Explanation: Asexual reproduction occurs through mitosis only — a single parent divides to produce genetically identical copies. Examples in plants: runners (strawberry), tubers (potato), bulbs (onion). In animals: binary fission in amoeba, budding in Hydra.
Tags: asexual reproduction, mitosis, clones, variation

**Item 3 [F]**
Question: Name the male and female gametes in humans.
Answer: Male gamete: sperm cell. Female gamete: egg cell (ovum).
Difficulty: F
Topic: B10
Explanation: Sperm are small, motile cells produced in the testes. They carry genetic material and a tail for swimming. The ovum is a large, non-motile cell produced in the ovaries. It contains cytoplasm rich in nutrients to support early development.
Tags: gametes, sperm, ovum, reproduction

**Item 4 [F]**
Question: State two differences between the male gamete (sperm) and the female gamete (ovum).
Answer: (1) Sperm is small and motile (has a tail); ovum is large and non-motile. (2) Sperm has little cytoplasm; ovum has abundant cytoplasm containing nutrients for early embryonic development.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B10
Explanation: The sperm's main function is to deliver genetic material to the egg. It is streamlined for locomotion with a flagellum. The ovum's function is to provide nutrients and cellular machinery for early development — it is one of the largest cells in the human body (~0.1 mm diameter, visible to the naked eye).
Tags: sperm, ovum, gamete differences

**Item 5 [S]**
Question: Describe the structure and function of the flower in sexual reproduction in plants.
Answer: The flower contains the reproductive organs. Stamens (male) consist of an anther (produces pollen containing sperm cells) and filament. The carpel/pistil (female) consists of the stigma (receives pollen), style (tube connecting stigma to ovary), and ovary (contains ovules, each containing an egg cell). Petals attract pollinators; sepals protect the flower bud.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B10
Explanation: Flowers may be male, female, or hermaphrodite (both). Wind-pollinated flowers are small, green, and produce large quantities of lightweight pollen. Insect-pollinated flowers are large, coloured, and scented to attract pollinators that transfer pollen.
Tags: flower, pollination, sexual reproduction, plant reproduction

**Item 6 [S]**
Question: Explain the process of pollination in flowering plants.
Answer: Pollination is the transfer of pollen from the anther to the stigma. In self-pollination, pollen lands on the stigma of the same flower. In cross-pollination, pollen is transferred to the stigma of a different flower (by wind, insects, or other animals). After pollination, the pollen grain germinates and grows a pollen tube down the style to the ovary.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B10
Explanation: Pollinators (bees, butterflies, moths, birds, bats) are essential for cross-pollination in many plants. Wind-pollinated plants (grasses, cereals) produce enormous quantities of lightweight pollen. Self-pollination ensures reproduction when pollinators are absent but reduces genetic variation.
Tags: pollination, pollen, cross-pollination, self-pollination

**Item 7 [S]**
Question: Describe the process of fertilisation in flowering plants.
Answer: After pollination, the pollen grain germinates on the stigma and grows a pollen tube down the style to the ovary. The pollen tube enters the ovule through the micropyle. Two male nuclei are released — one fuses with the egg cell nucleus (forming the zygote), the other fuses with two polar nuclei (forming the endosperm nucleus, which becomes food storage tissue). This double fertilisation is unique to flowering plants.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B10
Explanation: Double fertilisation ensures that the endosperm only develops if the egg is fertilised — this is efficient because the endosperm (food store) only forms when a seed can develop. The zygote divides by mitosis to form an embryo, and the ovule develops into a seed.
Tags: fertilisation, double fertilisation, pollen tube, endosperm

**Item 8 [S]**
Question: What is the difference between pollination and fertilisation in plants?
Answer: Pollination is the transfer of pollen from the anther to the stigma. It is a physical process. Fertilisation is the fusion of male and female gametes — the sperm nucleus fuses with the egg cell nucleus inside the ovule. Fertilisation only occurs after successful pollination.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B10
Explanation: Pollination is necessary but not sufficient for fertilisation — pollen must be compatible with the stigma (same species). In self-incompatible plants, self-pollination cannot lead to fertilisation. Fertilisation is the biological reproductive event.
Tags: pollination, fertilisation, plant reproduction

**Item 9 [S]**
Question: Name two male reproductive organs and state their functions.
Answer: (1) Testes — produce sperm cells (spermatogenesis) and the hormone testosterone. (2) Seminal vesicles — produce fructose-rich seminal fluid that provides energy for sperm and transports sperm through the reproductive tract.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B10
Explanation: The testes are located in the scrotum (outside the body) to maintain a temperature ~2°C below body temperature, optimal for sperm production. Testosterone is responsible for male secondary sexual characteristics (deeper voice, facial hair, muscle development).
Tags: testes, seminal vesicles, male reproductive system

**Item 10 [S]**
Question: Describe the menstrual cycle in humans, naming the four main phases.
Answer: The menstrual cycle (~28 days) has four phases: (1) Menstruation (days 1–5): the uterus lining breaks down, causing bleeding. (2) Follicular phase (days 1–13): FSH stimulates follicle development in the ovary; oestrogen thickens the uterus lining. (3) Ovulation (day 14): a mature egg is released from the ovary. (4) Luteal phase (days 15–28): progesterone maintains the uterus lining. If fertilisation does not occur, progesterone falls and menstruation begins.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B10
Explanation: The menstrual cycle is controlled by hormones from the hypothalamus, pituitary gland (FSH, LH), and ovaries (oestrogen, progesterone). Oestrogen peaks just before ovulation, triggering an LH surge that causes ovulation. Progesterone prepares the uterus for implantation. If fertilisation occurs, hCG (human chorionic gonadotrophin) maintains the corpus luteum.
Tags: menstrual cycle, ovulation, hormones, FSH, LH

**Item 11 [S]**
Question: What is the role of oestrogen in the menstrual cycle?
Answer: Oestrogen has two key roles: (1) During the follicular phase, oestrogen (from developing follicles) stimulates the repair and thickening of the uterus lining (endometrium). (2) A peak in oestrogen just before mid-cycle triggers the LH surge, causing ovulation.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B10
Explanation: Oestrogen is produced by the ovarian follicles. It is also responsible for female secondary sexual characteristics (breast development, widening of hips, distribution of body fat). After ovulation, oestrogen and progesterone from the corpus luteum maintain the endometrium.
Tags: oestrogen, menstrual cycle, ovulation, endometrium

**Item 12 [S]**
Question: Explain how a pollen grain is adapted for its function.
Answer: The pollen grain has a tough outer wall (exine) that protects the male gametophyte during transfer. It contains a germ cell that will divide to produce two sperm nuclei. The pollen tube grows through the style, guided by chemical attractants from the ovule, carrying the sperm nuclei to the egg cell.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B10
Explanation: Pollen morphology (shape, size, surface texture) is highly diverse and species-specific, useful for identifying plant species. The exine is extremely resistant to decay, so pollen is well preserved in the fossil record. The intine (inner wall) is flexible, allowing the pollen tube to emerge during germination.
Tags: pollen, adaptation, germination, male gametophyte

**Item 13 [C]**
Question: Compare sexual and asexual reproduction in plants, including advantages and disadvantages.
Answer: Sexual reproduction: Advantages — produces genetic variation, enabling adaptation to changing environments; seeds can be dispersed over long distances. Disadvantages — requires pollination (which may fail), energy-intensive; slower to reproduce. Asexual reproduction (vegetative propagation): Advantages — no need for pollinators, faster, produces many identical offspring suited to stable environments. Disadvantages — no genetic variation, so all offspring are equally vulnerable to diseases; offspring cannot disperse far.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B10
Explanation: Plants use both strategies opportunistically. Many plants reproduce asexually via runners (strawberry), tubers (potato), bulbs (tulip), or rhizomes (iris) in favourable conditions, and sexually via seeds during stress or to colonise new areas. Farmers exploit vegetative propagation to maintain desirable traits.
Tags: sexual vs asexual, plant reproduction, advantages, disadvantages

**Item 14 [C]**
Question: A plant produces many small seeds. Explain how this is an adaptation for survival and dispersal.
Answer: Many small seeds is an adaptation for wind dispersal (anemochory) and for colonising disturbed or nutrient-poor soils. Small seeds are produced in large numbers because: (1) They are lightweight and easily carried by wind over long distances, colonising new areas. (2) They can germinate quickly in open, disturbed ground where large seeds would fail. (3) The sheer numbers compensate for high mortality — most seeds will not reach suitable ground. However, small seeds contain little food reserves, so seedlings are vulnerable to competition and drought until established.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B10
Explanation: Seed size is a trade-off. Orchid seeds are microscopic (contain almost no food) — they must land on a specific fungus to germinate. Coconut seeds are huge (contain abundant food) — they can grow on bare beach sand. The strategy matches the environment.
Tags: seed dispersal, wind dispersal, adaptation, colonisation

**Item 15 [C]**
Question: Evaluate the use of artificial methods of plant propagation in agriculture and horticulture.
Answer: Artificial propagation includes cutting (stem cutting → new plant), grafting (scion attached to rootstock), tissue culture (growing plant cells in sterile medium → new plantlets). Advantages: (1) Produces many identical plants with desirable traits (uniform fruit, disease resistance); (2) Can produce disease-free plants via tissue culture; (3) Faster than seed propagation. Disadvantages: (1) All offspring are clones — vulnerable to the same diseases; (2) Requires technical skill and equipment (especially tissue culture); (3) Cannot produce genetic diversity for breeding programmes; (4) Grafting limits rootstock choice.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B10
Explanation: Commercial horticulture relies heavily on vegetative propagation. Banana and seedless grapes are propagated exclusively vegetatively. Apple orchards use grafted trees (desirable scion on vigorous rootstock). Tissue culture produces millions of identical disease-free plantlets (e.g., oil palm, strawberry).
Tags: artificial propagation, grafting, tissue culture, horticulture, agriculture

**Item 16 [C]**
Question: Explain the role of hormones in the control of the menstrual cycle, including the interactions between FSH, LH, oestrogen, and progesterone.
Answer: The cycle involves complex hormone interactions: (1) FSH (from pituitary) rises at the start → stimulates follicle development in ovary → follicle produces oestrogen. (2) Rising oestrogen inhibits FSH production (negative feedback) but has positive feedback on LH → LH surge. (3) LH surge triggers ovulation (day 14). (4) After ovulation, the corpus luteum produces progesterone. (5) Progesterone inhibits both FSH and LH (negative feedback), maintaining the endometrium. (6) If no fertilisation: corpus luteum degenerates → progesterone falls → endometrium breaks down → menstruation. (7) If fertilisation: hCG maintains corpus luteum → progesterone continues → endometrium is maintained.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B10
Explanation: This hormonal cascade ensures precise timing of ovulation and preparation of the uterus. The positive feedback loop (oestrogen → LH surge) is unusual in biology — most feedback loops are negative. In pregnancy, hCG acts like LH to maintain the corpus luteum until the placenta takes over progesterone production.
Tags: menstrual cycle hormones, FSH, LH, oestrogen, progesterone, feedback

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: Describe how a sperm cell is adapted for fertilising the egg cell.
Answer: Sperm adaptations: (1) Long tail (flagellum) powered by mitochondria for swimming through the female reproductive tract. (2) Streamlined head containing a haploid nucleus with genetic material. (3) Acrosome vesicle at the tip containing digestive enzymes — these are released when the sperm contacts the zona pellucida of the egg, allowing the sperm to penetrate and reach the egg membrane. (4) Many mitochondria (wrapped in a spiral around the flagellum) provide energy for swimming. (5) Haploid nucleus (23 chromosomes) for fusion with the egg nucleus.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B10
Explanation: The acrosome reaction: when sperm contact the zona pellucida (ZP3 proteins), the acrosome membrane fuses with the sperm membrane, releasing enzymes including hyaluronidase (breaks down hyaluronic acid between follicular cells) and acrosin (protease that digests zona pellucida). Only one sperm can fertilise the egg — once the first sperm enters, the zona reaction prevents polyspermy.
Tags: sperm, acrosome, fertilisation, adaptation, zona pellucida

**Item 18 [C]**
Question: A farmer wants to produce a large number of identical plants with a specific flower colour and disease resistance. Evaluate the most appropriate method of propagation for this purpose.
Answer: The farmer should use asexual propagation (vegetative) — specifically stem cuttings or grafting — rather than sexual propagation. Reason: (1) Asexual propagation produces genetically identical clones, ensuring all plants have the same flower colour and disease resistance. (2) Tissue culture could also be used for very large-scale production of identical plants from a small tissue sample. (3) Sexual propagation (seeds) would introduce genetic variation, so flower colour and disease resistance would not be uniform. Evaluation: However, cloning means all plants are equally susceptible to new diseases or environmental changes — no genetic diversity as a buffer. The farmer should also maintain genetic backups and consider introducing some diversity. For very large-scale commercial production, tissue culture is most efficient.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B10
Explanation: Commercial horticulture frequently uses clonal propagation. Strawberry runners produce genetically identical plants. Grafting (scion with desirable fruit from parent with vigorous roots) combines advantages. Tissue culture (micropropagation) can produce thousands of plantlets from a single explant in a year.
Tags: clonal propagation, asexual reproduction, plant breeding, evaluation
