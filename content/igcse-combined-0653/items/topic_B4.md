Difficulty: F

**Item 4 [F]**
Question: State one function of protein in the human body.
Answer: Proteins act as enzymes (biological catalysts) to speed up chemical reactions in the body.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B4
Explanation: Enzymes are globular proteins that catalyse biochemical reactions. Without enzymes, metabolic reactions would occur too slowly to sustain life. Examples include amylase (breaks down starch), pepsin (breaks down proteins in the stomach), and ATP synthase (produces ATP in mitochondria).
Tags: protein, enzyme, function, catalyst

**Item 5 [S]**
Question: Describe the difference between a monomer and a polymer, using starch as an example.
Answer: A monomer is a single small molecule. A polymer is a large molecule made of many monomers linked together. Starch is a polymer made of many glucose monomers joined together. The process is polymerisation.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B4
Explanation: Starch consists of hundreds to thousands of glucose units joined by glycosidic bonds. The individual glucose units are the monomers; the long starch chain is the polymer. This pattern is common in biology: amino acids → proteins, nucleotides → nucleic acids, fatty acids + glycerol → lipids.
Tags: monomer, polymer, starch, polymerisation, macromolecules

**Item 6 [S]**
Question: Name the test used to test for the presence of starch in a food sample and describe the expected result.
Answer: Add iodine solution (iodine in potassium iodide) to the sample. A blue-black colour indicates the presence of starch.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B4
Explanation: Iodine solution is brown/orange in colour. When it contacts starch, the iodine molecules fit into the helical structure of amylose (a component of starch), causing a dramatic colour change to blue-black. This is a simple, non-destructive test suitable for food science practicals.
Tags: starch test, iodine, food tests, identification

**Item 7 [S]**
Question: What is the difference between saturated and unsaturated lipids?
Answer: Saturated lipids have no double bonds between carbon atoms in their fatty acid chains (all carbon atoms bonded to maximum hydrogen atoms) and are usually solid at room temperature. Unsaturated lipids have one or more double bonds in the fatty acid chain and are usually liquid at room temperature (oils).
Difficulty: S
Topic: B4
Explanation: The double bonds in unsaturated fatty acids create kinks in the chain, preventing molecules from packing tightly together. This lowers the melting point, keeping the lipid liquid. Saturated fats (e.g., butter) pack tightly and are solid. Excess saturated fat consumption is linked to cardiovascular disease due to cholesterol deposition in arteries.
Tags: lipids, saturated, unsaturated, fatty acids, health

**Item 8 [S]**
Question: Describe the Benedict's test for reducing sugars and the colour change observed.
Answer: Add Benedict's reagent (blue) to the test solution and heat in a water bath. If reducing sugar is present, the colour changes from blue → green → yellow → orange → brick-red precipitate (precipitate of copper oxide).
Difficulty: S
Topic: B4
Explanation: Reducing sugars (glucose, fructose, maltose, lactose) have a free ketone or aldehyde group that can donate electrons, reducing copper(II) sulphate in Benedict's reagent to copper(I) oxide (brick-red precipitate). Sucrose is NOT a reducing sugar and gives a negative (blue) result with Benedict's test.
Tags: Benedict's test, reducing sugars, food tests, identification

**Item 9 [S]**
Question: Explain why enzymes are specific to their substrates.
Answer: Enzyme specificity is explained by the lock-and-key model: the enzyme's active site has a specific shape that fits only one substrate molecule (the key). The substrate fits into the active site like a key into a lock. If the shape does not match, no reaction occurs.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B4
Explanation: The active site is formed by the folding of the protein chain, creating a unique three-dimensional pocket. Only substrates with complementary shapes and chemical groups can bind. The induced-fit model refines this: the enzyme slightly changes shape as the substrate binds, improving the fit and catalytic efficiency. This specificity is determined by the enzyme's primary amino acid sequence.
Tags: enzyme specificity, lock-and-key, active site, substrate

**Item 10 [S]**
Question: State two properties of enzymes.
Answer: (1) Enzymes are biological catalysts — they speed up reactions without being used up. (2) Enzymes are specific — each enzyme acts on only one particular substrate.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B4
Explanation: Catalysts lower the activation energy of reactions. Because enzymes are not consumed in the reaction, a single enzyme molecule can catalyse many substrate molecules repeatedly. Specificity comes from the unique shape of the active site, which only accepts molecules with complementary shapes (lock-and-key or induced-fit model).
Tags: enzymes, properties, catalyst, specificity

**Item 11 [S]**
Question: Describe the biuret test for protein and the expected colour change.
Answer: Add sodium hydroxide solution followed by copper(II) sulphate solution to the sample. A violet/purple colour indicates the presence of protein.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B4
Explanation: The biuret test detects peptide bonds (the bonds linking amino acids in proteins). Sodium hydroxide denatures proteins and exposes peptide bonds. Copper(II) sulphate then binds to these peptide bonds, producing a violet colour. The intensity of the colour is proportional to the number of peptide bonds, hence to protein concentration.
Tags: biuret test, protein, food tests, peptide bonds

**Item 12 [S]**
Question: Explain why lipid tests require the sample to be dissolved in water first.
Answer: Lipids are non-polar and do not dissolve in water. Emulsification (using ethanol) is needed to disperse lipid droplets before adding water. The ethanol dissolves the lipid, then when water is added, the lipid is forced into tiny droplets that remain suspended in the water, creating a milky emulsion.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B4
Explanation: Water is polar; lipids are non-polar and hydrophobic. When you add ethanol to the lipid sample, it dissolves the lipid. Then, when water is added to the ethanolic solution, the ethanol is diluted and can no longer hold the lipid in solution. The lipid forms an emulsion — tiny droplets suspended in water — producing a milky-white colour. Sudan III dye is an alternative lipid stain that works on solid fat samples directly.
Tags: lipid test, emulsion, hydrophobic, food tests

**Item 13 [C]**
Question: Explain the lock-and-key model of enzyme action. Then evaluate this model, suggesting one way it has been improved.
Answer: The lock-and-key model proposes that the enzyme's active site has a fixed shape that perfectly matches its substrate. Only substrates with the complementary shape can fit into the active site, like a lock and key. Evaluation: The lock-and-key model has been refined by the induced-fit model, which proposes that the enzyme's active site is not rigid but changes shape slightly when the substrate binds, improving the fit. This better explains how some enzymes can accommodate slightly different substrates and how the enzyme is further activated by substrate binding.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B4
Explanation: The lock-and-key model was the first explanation for enzyme specificity (proposed by Emil Fischer, 1894). It successfully explains why each enzyme acts on only one substrate. The induced-fit model (proposed by Daniel Koshland, 1958) adds that substrate binding causes a conformational change in the enzyme, tightening the active site around the substrate. This explains catalytic mechanisms like strain on the substrate bond and orientation effects.
Tags: enzyme model, lock-and-key, induced-fit, evaluation, enzyme mechanism

**Item 14 [C]**
Question: Describe the lock-and-key and induced-fit models of enzyme action. Evaluate each model.
Answer: Lock-and-key: The enzyme active site has a fixed, rigid shape complementary to the substrate. Only correctly shaped substrates can bind. Induced-fit: The active site is flexible; when the substrate approaches, the enzyme changes shape to fit around it, like a glove closing around a hand. Evaluation: Lock-and-key successfully explains specificity but cannot explain how some enzymes show partial activity with similar substrates or how binding increases catalytic rate. Induced-fit better explains these observations and modern structural studies confirm the conformational changes. However, induced-fit does not fully explain all catalytic mechanisms either — both are simplified models of a complex reality.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B4
Explanation: Lock-and-key (Emil Fischer, 1894) was the first model explaining enzyme specificity through structural complementarity. X-ray crystallography later showed that many enzymes do change shape on substrate binding, supporting induced-fit (Koshland, 1958). Modern understanding combines both: enzymes are dynamic molecules whose active site shape is influenced by substrate binding, environmental conditions (pH, temperature), and allosteric regulation.
Tags: enzyme models, induced-fit, lock-and-key, evaluation, biochemistry

The green colour is an intermediate stage between blue (no reduction) and orange/brick-red (high reduction).

**Item 16 [C]**
Question: Explain how enzymes work as catalysts, including reference to the role of the active site and the lowering of activation energy.
Answer: Enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up chemical reactions without being used up. The substrate binds to the enzyme's active site. The active site provides: (1) A complementary shape that holds the substrate in the optimal orientation for reaction. (2) Specific chemical groups that strain or polarise bonds in the substrate. (3) A microenvironment (e.g., hydrophobic or charged) that favours the reaction. These effects lower the activation energy — the energy barrier that must be overcome for the reaction to proceed. With lower activation energy, more substrate molecules have enough energy to react per unit time, increasing the reaction rate.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B4
Explanation: All catalysts (including enzymes) work by providing an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy (Ea). Enzymes lower Ea through several mechanisms: proximity effect (bringing substrates together), orientation effect (aligning substrates correctly), transition state stabilisation (binding the transition state more strongly than the substrate or product), and acid-base catalysis (amino acid side chains acting as acids or bases).
Tags: enzyme catalysis, activation energy, active site, mechanism

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: Describe how temperature affects enzyme activity, explaining the underlying reasons at different temperature ranges.
Answer: At low temperatures, enzyme activity is low because particles have little kinetic energy, so fewer enzyme-substrate collisions occur per unit time. As temperature increases (up to the optimum), kinetic energy increases, more collisions occur, and reaction rate increases — approximately doubling for every 10°C rise (Q₁₀ ≈ 2). Above the optimum temperature, the enzyme's hydrogen bonds and other weak bonds begin to break, causing denaturation — the active site changes shape and can no longer bind the substrate. Denaturation is irreversible for most enzymes. Very high temperatures or extreme pH permanently destroy enzyme function.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B4
Explanation: The rate of enzyme-catalysed reactions doubles with every 10°C rise in temperature up to the optimum (Q₁₀ of approximately 2), due to increased collision frequency and kinetic energy. Most human enzymes have an optimum of about 37°C (body temperature). Beyond this, the rate falls sharply as denaturation begins. Denaturation breaks the tertiary structure of the protein, destroying the active site. Some enzymes from thermophilic bacteria can function at very high temperatures (up to 100°C) because their proteins have additional stabilising bonds.
Tags: enzyme activity, temperature, denaturation, Q10, optimum temperature

**Item 18 [C]**
Question: Evaluate the use of enzyme-based washing powders compared to conventional detergents. Consider their advantages and disadvantages.
Answer: Advantages: (1) Effective at lower temperatures, saving energy; (2) Can remove protein-based stains (blood, grass, egg) more effectively than conventional detergents; (3) Biodegradable. Disadvantages: (1) Can be deactivated by high temperatures or very acidic/alkaline conditions; (2) Some people are allergic to enzyme dust in the powder; (3) May damage delicate fabrics (silk, wool) that contain protein fibres; (4) More expensive to produce. Overall: enzyme detergents are environmentally beneficial (lower wash temperatures) but may not be suitable for all fabrics or wash conditions.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B4
Explanation: Protease enzymes (break down proteins), lipase (break down fats), and amylase (break down starch) are the main types used in biological washing powders. They were first commercialised in the 1960s (Takadiastase from Aspergillus niger). Modern formulations combine multiple enzymes for broad-spectrum stain removal. Environmental benefit: washing at 30°C instead of 60°C can reduce household energy consumption by ~30–40%.
Tags: enzyme applications, washing powder, evaluation, biotechnology, protease
