# Topic B9 — Coordination and Response
## Items File

**Item 1 [F]**
Question: What is the central nervous system (CNS)?
Answer: The central nervous system consists of the brain and spinal cord. It receives information from sensory receptors, processes it, and coordinates responses.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B9
Explanation: The CNS is the body's control centre. The brain processes complex information (thought, memory, emotion) and coordinates unconscious functions. The spinal cord transmits signals between the brain and the rest of the body and coordinates reflex actions.
Tags: CNS, brain, spinal cord, nervous system

**Item 2 [F]**
Question: What is a receptor?
Answer: A receptor is a specialised cell that detects a stimulus (a change in the environment) and converts it into an electrical impulse that can be transmitted along a neurone.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B9
Explanation: Different receptors detect different stimuli: photoreceptors (light), thermoreceptors (temperature), chemoreceptors (chemicals), mechanoreceptors (touch, pressure, sound), proprioceptors (body position).
Tags: receptor, stimulus, sensory neurone

**Item 3 [F]**
Question: What is the function of a motor neurone?
Answer: A motor neurone transmits electrical impulses from the central nervous system (brain or spinal cord) to effectors (muscles or glands), causing a response.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B9
Explanation: Motor neurones have a cell body (in the CNS), a long axon, and branched endings (motor end plates) at the muscle fibres. When an impulse reaches the muscle, it causes contraction.
Tags: motor neurone, CNS, effector, neurone

**Item 4 [F]**
Question: Name two human hormones and state one function of each.
Answer: (1) Insulin — lowers blood glucose levels by promoting the uptake of glucose by liver and muscle cells. (2) Adrenaline — prepares the body for 'fight or flight' by increasing heart rate, blood pressure, and blood glucose.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B9
Explanation: Hormones are chemical messengers produced by endocrine glands, secreted directly into the bloodstream. They travel to target organs where they exert specific effects. Unlike the nervous system (fast, short-lived responses), the hormonal system produces slower but longer-lasting effects.
Tags: hormone, endocrine, insulin, adrenaline

**Item 5 [S]**
Question: Describe the pathway of a simple reflex arc.
Answer: Stimulus → Receptor → Sensory neurone → Relay neurone (in spinal cord) → Motor neurone → Effector (muscle or gland) → Response.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B9
Explanation: A reflex arc is a rapid, automatic response to a stimulus that does not involve the brain. It protects the body from harm. The pathway bypasses conscious brain processing, making the response faster. Examples: knee-jerk reflex, pulling hand away from hot object.
Tags: reflex arc, reflex, neurone pathway

**Item 6 [S]**
Question: Explain why reflex actions are important.
Answer: Reflex actions are fast, automatic responses to stimuli that protect the body from damage without conscious thought. Because they bypass the brain, they are much quicker — important when speed is critical (e.g., pulling away from a hot stove). They also coordinate routine maintenance activities.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B9
Explanation: Reflexes are evolutionarily ancient and present in all animals. They are pre-programmed responses that do not require learning. They protect the body: blinking, pupil reflex, swallowing, coughing, knee-jerk. The speed of reflexes can be measured clinically to assess nervous system function.
Tags: reflex, protection, speed, automatic response

**Item 7 [S]**
Question: What is the role of the pancreas in blood glucose regulation?
Answer: The pancreas monitors blood glucose levels using chemoreceptors (beta and alpha cells in the islets of Langerhans). When blood glucose rises (after a meal), beta cells release insulin, which causes liver and muscle cells to take up glucose and convert it to glycogen for storage. When blood glucose falls (between meals), alpha cells release glucagon, which causes glycogen breakdown and glucose release into the blood.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B9
Explanation: This negative feedback loop maintains blood glucose within narrow limits (4–8 mmol/L). Failure of this system causes diabetes mellitus. Type 1 diabetes: beta cells destroyed by autoimmune attack → no insulin produced → blood glucose rises. Type 2 diabetes: cells become resistant to insulin → compensatory hyperinsulinemia → eventual beta cell exhaustion.
Tags: pancreas, insulin, glucagon, blood glucose, homeostasis

**Item 8 [S]**
Question: Compare the nervous system with the hormonal (endocrine) system.
Answer: Nervous system: electrical impulses transmitted along neurones, fast responses (milliseconds), short-lived effects, precise targeting (specific organ). Hormonal system: chemical messengers (hormones) in blood, slower responses (seconds to hours), longer-lasting effects, widespread effects (multiple target organs). Both coordinate body functions and maintain homeostasis.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B9
Explanation: The nervous and endocrine systems work together. The hypothalamus is both a neural and endocrine organ, connecting the two systems. Stress activates both — the sympathetic nervous system causes immediate responses (increased heart rate), while the HPA axis releases cortisol for longer-term stress management.
Tags: nervous system, hormonal system, comparison, coordination

**Item 9 [S]**
Question: State three features of neurones that make them efficient at transmitting impulses.
Answer: (1) They have a long axon for transmitting impulses over long distances. (2) They are surrounded by a fatty myelin sheath (in some neurones), which insulates the axon and speeds up impulse conduction. (3) They have a resting potential (polarised membrane) that allows electrical impulses to be generated and propagated.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B9
Explanation: The myelin sheath is formed by Schwann cells (in PNS) or oligodendrocytes (in CNS), which wrap around the axon. Myelinated fibres conduct impulses at up to 120 m/s vs 1–2 m/s in unmyelinated fibres. Saltatory conduction (jumping between Nodes of Ranvier) greatly increases speed.
Tags: neurone, axon, myelin sheath, impulse transmission

**Item 10 [S]**
Question: What is a synapse?
Answer: A synapse is a tiny gap between two neurones (or between a neurone and an effector). When an electrical impulse reaches a synapse, it triggers the release of neurotransmitter chemicals (e.g., acetylcholine) from the presynaptic neurone. These chemicals diffuse across the gap and bind to receptors on the next neurone, generating a new impulse.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B9
Explanation: Synapses are the functional connections between neurones. They allow complex processing — one neurone can connect to many others, and one impulse can trigger multiple downstream signals. Synaptic transmission can be excitatory (triggers impulse) or inhibitory (prevents impulse).
Tags: synapse, neurotransmitter, impulse transmission

**Item 11 [S]**
Question: Explain the role of the adrenal glands in the 'fight or flight' response.
Answer: When stimulated by the sympathetic nervous system, the adrenal medulla releases adrenaline (epinephrine) and noradrenaline into the bloodstream. These hormones cause: increased heart rate and force of contraction; bronchodilation (wider airways for more O₂); increased blood pressure; pupil dilation; decreased digestion; increased blood glucose (from glycogen breakdown in the liver); increased blood flow to muscles.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B9
Explanation: The fight-or-flight response prepares the body for immediate physical action. This is an evolutionary adaptation for survival — facing danger required rapid, intense physical response. The adrenal medulla is controlled by the sympathetic nervous system via the sympathetic chain.
Tags: adrenaline, fight or flight, adrenal gland, stress response

**Item 12 [S]**
Question: Describe what happens in the knee-jerk reflex.
Answer: When the patellar tendon is stretched (tapped with a hammer), stretch receptors (proprioceptors) in the quadriceps muscle detect the stretch and send an impulse along the sensory neurone to the spinal cord. Within the spinal cord, the impulse is relayed directly to a motor neurone (via a synapse in the grey matter). The motor neurone sends an impulse to the quadriceps muscle, causing it to contract and extend the leg. This reflex maintains posture and prevents the leg from buckling.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B9
Explanation: The knee-jerk reflex is a monosynaptic reflex — only one synapse is involved (between sensory and motor neurone). It is clinically important: absent or exaggerated reflexes indicate nerve damage or neurological disorders. The brain is informed of the reflex but does not initiate it.
Tags: knee-jerk reflex, proprioceptor, monosynaptic reflex

**Item 13 [C]**
Question: Explain how a synapse ensures one-way transmission of impulses.
Answer: Synapses allow impulses to travel in only one direction because: (1) The presynaptic neurone contains vesicles filled with neurotransmitter. The postsynaptic neurone does not. (2) The impulse arriving at the presynaptic terminal triggers calcium ion influx, causing vesicle fusion and neurotransmitter release. (3) Neurotransmitter receptors are only on the postsynaptic membrane. (4) The neurotransmitter is rapidly broken down by enzymes (e.g., acetylcholinesterase) after the impulse is transmitted, preventing reverse signalling.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B9
Explanation: One-way transmission is essential for organised information flow. If impulses could travel in both directions, chaotic feedback loops would result. The structural and functional specialisation of pre- and post-synaptic neurones ensures unidirectional flow. Enzymatic breakdown of neurotransmitter also limits the duration of synaptic signalling.
Tags: synapse, one-way transmission, neurotransmitter, synaptic transmission

**Item 14 [C]**
Question: A drug blocks acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction. Predict and explain the effects on muscle contraction.
Answer: Acetylcholine (ACh) is the neurotransmitter released by motor neurones at the neuromuscular junction. If receptors are blocked: (1) ACh is released normally when the motor neurone fires. (2) But ACh cannot bind to the blocked receptors on the muscle fibre. (3) No depolarisation occurs at the motor end plate. (4) No action potential is generated in the muscle fibre. (5) The muscle cannot contract → paralysis (flaccid paralysis). This is the mechanism of curare (a plant toxin used as a muscle relaxant in surgery).
Difficulty: C
Topic: B9
Explanation: Normally, ACh binding to nicotinic ACh receptors causes Na⁺ influx and depolarisation (end plate potential). If receptors are blocked (competitive antagonist), ACh cannot act. The motor neurone fires normally, but the muscle does not respond. Surgical muscle relaxants (e.g., rocuronium) work this way and are reversed by anticholinesterase drugs.
Tags: neuromuscular junction, acetylcholine, receptor, paralysis

**Item 15 [C]**
Question: Compare type 1 and type 2 diabetes mellitus, including causes, treatments, and prevalence.
Answer: Type 1 diabetes: Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells → no insulin production. Usually develops in childhood/adolescence. Treatment: insulin injections, blood glucose monitoring, carbohydrate counting, dietary management. Type 2 diabetes: Insulin resistance of body cells + relative insulin deficiency. Usually develops in adults, linked to obesity and sedentary lifestyle. Treatment: dietary changes, increased physical activity, oral hypoglycaemic drugs (e.g., metformin), sometimes insulin. Prevalence: Type 2 is far more common (~90% of cases), linked to the global obesity epidemic.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B9
Explanation: Type 1 is an autoimmune disease — the immune system attacks and destroys beta cells. Type 2 is a metabolic disorder with strong lifestyle risk factors (obesity, physical inactivity, poor diet). Both lead to hyperglycaemia (high blood glucose) with similar symptoms (polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss) and long-term complications (blindness, kidney failure, nerve damage, cardiovascular disease).
Tags: type 1 diabetes, type 2 diabetes, comparison, treatment

**Item 16 [C]**
Question: Explain how thermoregulation works in the human body, including the roles of the hypothalamus, skin, and negative feedback.
Answer: The hypothalamus (in the brain) acts as the body's thermostat. It receives temperature information from thermoreceptors in the skin and hypothalamus itself. If core temperature rises: blood vessels supplying the skin dilate (vasodilation) — more blood reaches the skin surface, radiating heat away; sweat glands produce sweat — evaporation cools the body. If core temperature falls: blood vessels supplying the skin constrict (vasoconstriction) — less blood reaches the skin, conserving heat; hairs on skin are raised by arrector pili muscles — insulating layer of trapped air (goosebumps); shivering generates heat through increased muscle metabolism.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B9
Explanation: Thermoregulation is a classic negative feedback mechanism. The set point is ~37°C. Core temperature above or below this set point triggers responses that return temperature to the set point. Fever occurs when pyrogens (e.g., bacterial toxins) raise the hypothalamic set point, causing shivering and vasoconstriction to raise body temperature.
Tags: thermoregulation, hypothalamus, negative feedback, homeostasis

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: Describe how the structure of the eye is related to its function of focusing light on the retina.
Answer: The eye focuses light through: (1) Cornea — transparent front surface, responsible for ~65% of refractive power (bends light rays). (2) Pupil — adjustable aperture controlled by the iris, regulating light entry. (3) Lens — transparent, flexible disc, fine-focused by ciliary muscles (accommodation). (4) Suspensory ligaments — connect ciliary body to lens; contraction/relaxation changes lens shape. (5) Retina — light-sensitive layer containing photoreceptors (rods and cones) that convert light to electrical impulses.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B9
Explanation: Accommodation: to focus on near objects, ciliary muscles contract, slackening the suspensory ligaments, making the lens thicker (more convex). To focus on distant objects, ciliary muscles relax, tightening ligaments, flattening the lens. Presbyopia (age-related loss of accommodation) occurs because the lens becomes less flexible.
Tags: eye, retina, lens, accommodation, focusing

**Item 18 [C]**
Question: A student touches a hot object and immediately pulls their hand away before consciously registering pain. Explain this sequence of events, including the roles of each component of the reflex arc.
Answer: Sequence: (1) Thermoreceptors in the skin detect heat (stimulus). (2) Sensory neurone converts this to an electrical impulse and transmits it to the spinal cord. (3) In the spinal cord (grey matter), the sensory neurone synapses directly with a relay neurone and with an interneurone. The relay neurone passes the impulse to a motor neurone. (4) The motor neurone transmits the impulse to the biceps muscle (effector). (5) The biceps contracts, pulling the hand away from the hot object (response). (6) Simultaneously, the interneurone inhibits the triceps, preventing antagonistic contraction. After the hand is withdrawn, the sensory neurone also transmits the impulse up the spinal cord to the brain, where the sensation of pain is consciously registered — explaining why pain is felt a fraction of a second after the hand has already moved.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B9
Explanation: This is a polysynaptic reflex — multiple neurones and synapses are involved. The direct sensory-to-motor pathway is the fastest (~0.1 s), allowing rapid withdrawal. The brain receives the pain signal via the sensory neurone's branch that ascends the spinal cord to the thalamus and somatosensory cortex. This dual pathway — rapid reflex + delayed conscious awareness — explains the sequence.
Tags: reflex arc, interneurone, relay neurone, pain, sequence of events
