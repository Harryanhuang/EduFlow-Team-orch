# Topic P2 — Work, Energy, and Power
## Items File

**Item 1 [F]**
Question: State Newton's Second Law of Motion.
Answer: The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the resultant force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass. F = ma.
Difficulty: F
Topic: P2
Explanation: F = ma means a larger force produces greater acceleration; a larger mass requires a larger force for the same acceleration. The units work: N = kg·m/s².
Tags: Newton's second law, force, acceleration, mass

**Item 2 [F]**
Question: What is work done?
Answer: Work done is the energy transferred when a force moves an object in the direction of the force. Work done = force × distance moved in the direction of the force.
Difficulty: F
Topic: P2
Explanation: Work is measured in joules (J). If a force moves an object 5 m with a force of 10 N, work done = 50 J.
Tags: work done, energy transfer, joules, force

**Item 3 [F]**
Question: Calculate the work done when a force of 30 N moves an object 4 m.
Answer: Work = F × d = 30 × 4 = 120 J
Difficulty: F
Topic: P2
Explanation: Work = force × distance (in the direction of force). 30 N × 4 m = 120 J.
Tags: work done, calculation, force, distance

**Item 4 [F]**
Question: What is gravitational potential energy?
Answer: Gravitational potential energy (GPE) is the energy an object has due to its position in a gravitational field.
Difficulty: F
Topic: P2
Explanation: An object raised above the ground has stored energy because gravity can do work on it as it falls.
Tags: gravitational potential energy, GPE, energy

**Item 5 [S]**
Question: Calculate the gravitational potential energy of a 3 kg mass raised 5 m above the ground (g = 10 m/s²).
Answer: GPE = mgh = 3 × 10 × 5 = 150 J
Difficulty: S
Topic: P2
Explanation: GPE = mass × gravitational field strength × height. Work must be done against gravity to raise the object.
Tags: GPE, calculation, potential energy

**Item 6 [S]**
Question: A car travels at constant speed along a flat road. Does the engine do work?
Answer: Yes, the engine must overcome friction and air resistance. Work done = force of friction × distance. Since speed is constant, net force = 0 and acceleration = 0, but the engine provides a force equal and opposite to friction.
Difficulty: S
Topic: P2
Explanation: Even at constant speed, work is done against resistive forces. Without engine power, the car would decelerate due to friction.
Tags: work, constant speed, friction, energy transfer

**Item 7 [S]**
Question: What is the relationship between work done and energy?
Answer: Work done equals the energy transferred. When work is done on an object, its energy changes by an amount equal to the work done.
Difficulty: S
Topic: P2
Explanation: This is the work-energy principle: the work done on an object = change in kinetic energy.
Tags: work, energy, energy transfer

**Item 8 [S]**
Question: Define kinetic energy.
Answer: Kinetic energy is the energy an object has due to its motion. KE = ½mv²
Difficulty: S
Topic: P2
Explanation: Any moving object has kinetic energy. The faster it moves, the more kinetic energy it has.
Tags: kinetic energy, motion, KE

**Item 9 [S]**
Question: Calculate the kinetic energy of a 2 kg object moving at 5 m/s.
Answer: KE = ½ × 2 × 5² = 1 × 25 = 25 J
Difficulty: S
Topic: P2
Explanation: KE = ½mv². ½ × 2 × 25 = 25 J.
Tags: kinetic energy, calculation

**Item 10 [S]**
Question: Define power.
Answer: Power is the rate of doing work — the amount of work done per second. Power = work done / time.
Difficulty: S
Topic: P2
Explanation: Unit: watt (W) = joules per second. A 100 W light bulb does 100 J of work per second.
Tags: power, watt, rate of work

**Item 11 [S]**
Question: A person climbs stairs of height 4 m in 10 s. Their mass is 60 kg. Calculate their power output.
Answer: Work done = mgh = 60 × 10 × 4 = 2400 J. Power = 2400 / 10 = 240 W.
Difficulty: S
Topic: P2
Explanation: Power = work done / time. The person's power output is 240 W.
Tags: power, calculation, work done

**Item 12 [S]**
Question: State the law of conservation of energy.
Answer: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another.
Difficulty: S
Topic: P2
Explanation: In all energy transformations, total energy input = total energy output. This is a fundamental law of physics.
Tags: conservation of energy, energy transformation

**Item 13 [C]**
Question: A pendulum swings from its highest point to its lowest point. Describe the energy transformations.
Answer: At the highest point, the pendulum has maximum gravitational potential energy (GPE) and zero kinetic energy (KE). As it swings down, GPE decreases and KE increases. At the lowest point, KE is maximum and GPE is minimum. Energy is transformed from GPE to KE.
Difficulty: C
Topic: P2
Explanation: In an ideal pendulum (no air resistance), total mechanical energy is conserved: GPE + KE = constant. In a real pendulum, some energy is lost to air resistance as heat.
Tags: energy transformations, pendulum, GPE, KE

**Item 14 [C]**
Question: A ball of mass 0.5 kg is dropped from a height of 10 m. Calculate: (a) its GPE at the top (b) its speed just before hitting the ground.
Answer: (a) GPE = mgh = 0.5 × 10 × 10 = 50 J. (b) At the bottom, GPE = 0, KE = 50 J. KE = ½mv² → 50 = ½ × 0.5 × v² → v² = 200 → v = √200 = 14.1 m/s.
Difficulty: C
Topic: P2
Explanation: By conservation of energy (ignoring air resistance), all GPE is converted to KE. Using v² = 2gh = 2 × 10 × 10 = 200 gives v = 14.1 m/s.
Tags: conservation of energy, GPE, KE, calculation

**Item 15 [C]**
Question: Explain why a roller coaster car reaches its maximum speed at the bottom of the track.
Answer: At the top, the car has maximum GPE (high position) and minimum KE (low speed). As it descends, GPE is converted to KE (speed increases). At the bottom, GPE is minimum (lowest position) and KE is maximum (maximum speed). Energy is conserved: total energy = GPE + KE = constant.
Difficulty: C
Topic: P2
Explanation: The roller coaster demonstrates conversion of GPE to KE. The car's speed is greatest where its GPE is smallest.
Tags: energy conservation, GPE, KE, roller coaster

**Item 16 [C]**
Question: Two cars of the same mass travel at the same speed. One crashes into a barrier and stops. The other rolls up a ramp to rest. Compare the work done by the force in each case.
Answer: Both cars have the same kinetic energy (½mv²). Both must dissipate this energy. The first car: work done by the barrier = change in KE = ½mv² (force × distance of crumple zone). The second car: work done against gravity = ½mv² = mgh, so h = v²/(2g). The average force in the crash depends on the distance over which the car decelerates.
Difficulty: C
Topic: P2
Explanation: Work-energy principle: work done = change in kinetic energy. For both cars, the change is from ½mv² to 0, so the total work done is equal. The difference is the force and distance: barrier (short distance → large force) vs ramp (longer distance → smaller average force).
Tags: work-energy principle, kinetic energy, crash, safety

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: Evaluate the design of a safety helmet in terms of energy transfer.
Answer: In an impact, the kinetic energy of the head must be dissipated safely. The helmet's foam liner deforms (work done = force × distance of compression), converting kinetic energy into elastic potential energy and heat. The longer compression distance (compared to the skull directly hitting a hard surface) reduces the average force on the head (F × d = KE). Modern helmets use: (1) Expanded polystyrene foam — crushes on impact, absorbing energy; (2) Hard outer shell — spreads the impact force over a larger area. Evaluation: Helmets significantly reduce head injury risk by increasing stopping distance, reducing peak force.
Difficulty: C
Topic: P2
Explanation: Work done = change in KE. If stopping distance doubles, average force halves. The foam increases the distance over which the head decelerates, reducing the force. Modern helmets also reduce rotational injuries with slip planes.
Tags: safety helmet, energy dissipation, impact, work done, evaluation

**Item 18 [C]**
Question: A car of mass 1000 kg accelerates from 0 to 30 m/s. Calculate the increase in kinetic energy and explain what happens to this energy when the car brakes to a stop.
Answer: KE increase = ½ × 1000 × 30² = 500 × 900 = 450,000 J = 450 kJ. When braking to a stop, the kinetic energy is converted to heat in the brake pads (and to a lesser extent, heat in the tyres and air). The brake discs and pads become hot — this is the dissipation of kinetic energy as thermal energy.
Difficulty: C
Topic: P2
Explanation: KE increase is from 0 to 450 kJ. During braking, this 450 kJ of KE is transformed into heat (mostly in brakes, also tyre deformation and air resistance). Energy is conserved — it is not "used up" but dissipated.
Tags: kinetic energy, braking, energy dissipation, thermal energy
