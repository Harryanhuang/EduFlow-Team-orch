# Topic C4 — Chemical Formulae and Equations
## Items File

**Item 1 [F]**
Question: What is a chemical formula?
Answer: A chemical formula shows the number and type of atoms in a molecule or formula unit of a compound.
Difficulty: F
Topic: C4
Explanation: For example, H₂O means one molecule of water contains 2 hydrogen atoms and 1 oxygen atom. CO₂ means one carbon and two oxygen atoms.
Tags: chemical formula, notation, compound

**Item 2 [F]**
Question: Write the chemical formulae of: (a) sodium chloride (b) water (c) carbon dioxide.
Answer: (a) NaCl (b) H₂O (c) CO₂
Difficulty: F
Topic: C4
Explanation: The formula shows the elements present and the number of atoms of each. Sodium chloride has one Na and one Cl. Water has two H atoms and one O atom. Carbon dioxide has one C and two O atoms.
Tags: chemical formulae, naming, compounds

**Item 3 [F]**
Question: What is the law of conservation of mass?
Answer: In a chemical reaction, mass is neither created nor destroyed. The total mass of reactants equals the total mass of products.
Difficulty: F
Topic: C4
Explanation: Atoms are rearranged in chemical reactions, not created or destroyed. Therefore, the mass of all reactants must equal the mass of all products.
Tags: conservation of mass, chemical reactions, reactants, products

**Item 4 [F]**
Question: State two examples of physical changes.
Answer: (1) Melting ice → water. (2) Evaporating water → steam.
Difficulty: F
Topic: C4
Explanation: Physical changes do not involve chemical reactions — no new substances are formed. The substance remains the same chemical entity; only its physical state or appearance changes.
Tags: physical change, states of matter

**Item 5 [S]**
Question: Balance the equation: Fe + O₂ → Fe₂O₃.
Answer: 4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃
Difficulty: S
Topic: C4
Explanation: Count atoms: LHS: Fe=1, O=2. RHS: Fe=2, O=3. To balance: multiply Fe by 4 and O₂ by 3 on LHS: 4Fe + 3O₂ → Fe₂O₃. RHS still has Fe=2, so multiply Fe₂O₃ by 2: 4Fe + 3O₂ → 2Fe₂O₃. Check: LHS Fe=4, O=6. RHS Fe=4, O=6. Balanced ✓.
Tags: balancing equations, chemical equations, stoichiometry

**Item 6 [S]**
Question: What is the difference between a chemical reaction and a physical change?
Answer: A chemical reaction produces new substances with different chemical properties. A physical change changes the state or appearance but does not produce new substances.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C4
Explanation: In a chemical reaction, bonds are broken and formed, creating new molecules. In a physical change, bonds are not broken or formed — only intermolecular forces change.
Tags: chemical reaction, physical change, differences

**Item 7 [S]**
Question: What is a catalyst?
Answer: A catalyst is a substance that speeds up a chemical reaction without being used up or changed in the reaction. It provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C4
Explanation: Catalysts are not consumed in reactions — one catalyst molecule can catalyse many reactions. Enzymes are biological catalysts (proteins).
Tags: catalyst, activation energy, enzyme, reaction rate

Difficulty: S

**Item 11 [S]**
Question: What is relative molecular mass (Mr)?
Answer: The relative molecular mass (Mr) is the sum of the relative atomic masses of all atoms in a molecule.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C4
Explanation: For example, H₂O: Mr = (2 × 1) + 16 = 18. CO₂: Mr = 12 + (2 × 16) = 44.
Tags: relative molecular mass, calculation, Mr

Answer: Mr = 40 + 12 + (3 × 16) = 100
Difficulty: S
Topic: C4
Explanation: Ca = 40, C = 12, O₃ = 3 × 16 = 48. Total = 40 + 12 + 48 = 100.
Tags: relative molecular mass, calculation, compounds

**Item 13 [C]**
Question: 10 g of limestone (CaCO₃) is heated and produces 5.6 g of CaO and 4.4 g of CO₂. Show that this obeys the law of conservation of mass.
Answer: Mass of reactants = mass of products. Mass of limestone (reactant) = 10 g. Mass of products = CaO + CO₂ = 5.6 g + 4.4 g = 10.0 g. Since 10 g = 10 g, mass is conserved ✓.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C4
Explanation: The law of conservation of mass applies to all chemical reactions. Atoms are rearranged but not created or destroyed. The total mass before reaction always equals the total mass after.
Tags: conservation of mass, experimental verification, limestone

Tags: balancing equations, neutralisation, acid-base

**Item 15 [C]**
Question: A 2.4 g sample of magnesium is burned in oxygen, forming 4.0 g of magnesium oxide. Calculate the mass of oxygen that reacted.
Answer: Mass of oxygen = mass of products − mass of magnesium = 4.0 g − 2.4 g = 1.6 g
Difficulty: C
Topic: C4
Explanation: By the law of conservation of mass: mass of reactants = mass of products. Mg + O₂ → MgO. Mass of Mg + mass of O₂ = mass of MgO. So mass of O₂ = 4.0 − 2.4 = 1.6 g.
Tags: conservation of mass, magnesium, combustion, calculation

**Item 16 [C]**
Question: Balance: C₂H₆ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O.
Answer: 2C₂H₆ + 7O₂ → 4CO₂ + 6H₂O
Difficulty: C
Topic: C4
Explanation: Balancing the equation 2C₂H₆ + 7O₂ → 4CO₂ + 6H₂O: Count atoms on LHS: C=4, H=12, O=14. Count atoms on RHS: C=4, H=12, O=4+6=14. Balanced ✓. The integer coefficients 2, 7, 4, 6 are found by first balancing with fractions (C₂H₆ + 3.5O₂ → 2CO₂ + 3H₂O) then multiplying all coefficients by 2 to eliminate the fraction.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C4
Explanation: Balancing combustion equations: balance C first, then H, then O. For ethane (C₂H₆), first balance C by making 2CO₂. Then balance H by making 6H₂O. Then count O on RHS (4+6=10) and balance with O₂ on LHS (need 5O₂): C₂H₆ + 3.5O₂ → 2CO₂ + 3H₂O. Multiply by 2 to eliminate the fraction: 2C₂H₆ + 7O₂ → 4CO₂ + 6H₂O. ✓.
Tags: balancing combustion equations, ethane, hydrocarbons

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: 6 g of carbon is burned completely in 16 g of oxygen. Calculate the mass of carbon dioxide produced and identify which reactant is in excess.
Answer: C + O₂ → CO₂. 12 g C reacts with 32 g O₂ to give 44 g CO₂. Ratio: 6 g C needs (6/12) × 32 = 16 g O₂. We have 16 g O₂ exactly — no excess. Mass of CO₂ = 6 + 16 = 22 g.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C4
Explanation: By the law of conservation of mass: mass of CO₂ = mass of C + mass of O₂ used = 6 + 16 = 22 g. In this case, neither reactant is in excess — they are in the exact stoichiometric ratio.
Tags: limiting reagent, stoichiometry, excess reactant, calculation

**Item 18 [C]**
Question: Evaluate the importance of balancing chemical equations in industrial chemical processes.
Answer: Balancing equations is essential in industry for: (1) Predicting quantities — to calculate how much product forms from given amounts of reactants; (2) Economic efficiency — stoichiometric ratios ensure no reactant is wasted; (3) Safety — incorrect ratios in explosive or flammable reactions can be dangerous; (4) Environmental compliance — accurate equations are needed for calculating waste products and emissions; (5) Quality control — ensuring the reaction produces the expected yield.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C4
Explanation: In industry (e.g., Haber process for ammonia, contact process for sulfuric acid), reactions may involve tonnes of chemicals. Accurate balancing is essential for process design, cost control, and safety. Excess reactants waste money; insufficient reactants may not achieve desired yield.
Tags: balancing equations, industrial chemistry, stoichiometry, evaluation
