Question: A car travels 300 m in 20 s. Calculate its average speed.

**Item 2 [F]**
Question: State two differences between a scalar quantity and a vector quantity. Give one example of each.
Answer: (1) Scalar has magnitude only; vector has both magnitude and direction. (2) Scalars cannot be added vectorially; vectors must consider direction. Examples: scalar = speed (20 m/s), vector = velocity (20 m/s north).
Difficulty: F
Topic: P1
Explanation: A scalar quantity only needs a magnitude (number and unit) to be fully described, e.g., mass, speed, temperature, time. A vector quantity needs both magnitude AND direction, e.g., velocity, force, acceleration, displacement. When adding scalars, just add the numbers. When adding vectors, direction matters — vectors in opposite directions subtract.
Tags: scalar, vector, speed, velocity, differences

**Item 3 [S]**
Question: A car accelerates from rest (0 m/s) to 20 m/s in 8 s. Calculate its acceleration.
Answer: 2.5 m/s²
Difficulty: S
Topic: P1
Explanation: Acceleration a = (v − u) / t = (20 − 0) / 8 = 20/8 = 2.5 m/s². The car gains 2.5 m/s of velocity every second.
Tags: acceleration, velocity, kinematics, calculation

**Item 4 [S]**
Question: (a) Draw a D-T graph for a car: stationary, then constant speed, then decelerating to a stop. (b) What does the gradient of a D-T graph represent?
Answer: (a) Four phases: (1) horizontal line — stationary; (2) straight line sloping upward at constant gradient — constant speed; (3) curve with decreasing positive gradient — decelerating; (4) horizontal line at a higher distance value — stationary again (stopped). (b) The gradient of a D-T graph represents speed (or velocity if direction is constant).
Difficulty: S
Topic: P1
Explanation: On a distance-time graph, the gradient (change in distance / change in time) equals speed. A horizontal line (gradient = 0) means stationary. A straight line (constant gradient) means constant speed. A curve with decreasing gradient means the object is slowing down (decelerating). The steeper the gradient, the faster the speed.
Tags: distance-time graph, gradient, speed, graph interpretation, motion

**Item 5 [S]**
Question: A force of 500 N acts on a mass of 25 kg. Calculate the acceleration produced.
Answer: 20 m/s²
Difficulty: S
Topic: P1
Explanation: Using Newton's Second Law: F = ma. Rearranging: a = F/m = 500 N / 25 kg = 20 m/s². Note: 1 N = 1 kg·m/s², so the units work out correctly.
Tags: Newton's second law, force, mass, acceleration, F=ma

**Item 6 [S]**
Question: Draw a V-T graph for a ball thrown vertically upwards and caught again at the same point. Label regions of positive acceleration, zero acceleration, and negative acceleration (deceleration).
Answer: The graph is a straight line with constant negative gradient throughout. (1) Upward motion (v > 0): the ball decelerates under gravity; the line slopes downward from positive v toward zero. (2) At the peak height (v = 0): the ball momentarily stops; this is the point where the line crosses the time axis. (3) Downward motion (v < 0): the ball accelerates downward; the line continues with the same negative gradient below the time axis. Acceleration is constant at approximately −10 m/s² throughout all three phases.
Difficulty: S
Topic: P1
Explanation: The velocity-time graph for vertical projectile motion is a straight line (constant acceleration = −g). When thrown up (positive v), the ball decelerates at g ≈ 10 m/s² until v = 0 at the peak. Then it accelerates downward, with v becoming increasingly negative (moving downward). The gradient is constant and equal to −g throughout.
Tags: velocity-time graph, acceleration, projectile motion, gravity, kinematics

**Item 7 [C]**
Question: Two identical balls collide head-on with equal speeds and bounce off. Explain using the law of conservation of momentum why their velocities are reversed.
Answer: Let each ball have mass m and speed v. Before collision: Ball A momentum = +mv (moving right), Ball B momentum = −mv (moving left). Total momentum = mv − mv = 0. After bouncing off, Ball A has velocity −v (left), Ball B has velocity +v (right). Total momentum = −mv + mv = 0. Momentum is conserved because total before = total after = 0. Since the balls are identical and collide with equal speeds, they transfer equal and opposite momentum, reversing directions.
Difficulty: C
Topic: P1
Explanation: Conservation of momentum states: total momentum before collision = total momentum after collision. For identical balls with equal and opposite velocities, total momentum = 0. Since momentum is a vector, bouncing off with reversed directions gives opposite sign velocities. For total momentum to remain 0, the velocities must be equal in magnitude. Since the balls are identical, momentum conservation alone would suggest they simply exchange velocities. The bouncing indicates an approximately elastic collision where kinetic energy is also conserved (or nearly so).
Tags: momentum, conservation of momentum, collision, elastic collision, vector

**Item 8 [C]**
Question: A car at 20 m/s brakes uniformly at 5 m/s² to rest. Find: (a) braking distance (b) stopping time.
Answer: (a) s = (v² − u²) / (2a) = (0 − 400) / (2 × −5) = −400 / −10 = 40 m. (b) v = u + at: 0 = 20 − 5t → t = 4 s. Alternatively: v² = u² + 2as: 0 = 400 − 10s → s = 40 m. Time: 0 = 20 − 5t → t = 4 s.
Difficulty: C
Topic: P1
Explanation: Using the kinematic equations: v² = u² + 2as gives s = (v² − u²) / (2a) = (0 − 400) / (2 × −5) = 400/10 = 40 m. And v = u + at gives t = (v − u)/a = (0 − 20)/(−5) = 4 s. Both can be verified: average speed = (20 + 0)/2 = 10 m/s, time = 4 s, distance = 10 × 4 = 40 m ✓.
Tags: kinematics, equations of motion, deceleration, stopping distance, braking

**Item 9 [C]**
Question: Newton's third law: for every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. Use this to explain how a fish propels itself through water.
Answer: When a fish pushes water backwards with its tail (action force), the water pushes the fish forwards with an equal and opposite force (reaction force). The forces act on different objects: the fish's tail pushes water back (action on water), and the water pushes fish forward (reaction on fish). This reaction force provides the forward thrust. The streamlined body shape minimises drag so the forward force can accelerate the fish.
Difficulty: C
Topic: P1
Explanation: Newton's Third Law pairs are always equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and act on different bodies. The action is the force exerted by the fish's tail on the water (backwards). The reaction is the force exerted by the water on the fish's tail (forwards). This forward reaction force propels the fish. The fish also uses its fins to control direction. Importantly, the action and reaction forces do NOT cancel because they act on different objects.
Tags: Newton's third law, action and reaction, force pairs, propulsion, physics principles

**Item 10 [F]**
Question: State Newton's First Law of Motion.
Answer: An object will remain at rest or continue moving at a constant speed in a straight line unless acted upon by a resultant force.
Difficulty: F
Topic: P1
Explanation: Newton's First Law (law of inertia) states that objects resist changes in their state of motion. Without a resultant force, there is no acceleration. In everyday life, friction and air resistance provide the external forces that stop moving objects. In space, with no friction, objects continue moving indefinitely.
Tags: Newton's first law, inertia, constant velocity, resultant force

**Item 11 [F]**
Question: A boy pushes a box with a force of 30 N to the right, while his friend pushes it with 20 N to the left. What is the resultant force?
Answer: 10 N to the right
Difficulty: F
Topic: P1
Explanation: Forces in opposite directions subtract. Resultant = 30 − 20 = 10 N to the right (the direction of the larger force). The net effect is the same as a single force of 10 N to the right.
Tags: resultant force, vector addition, force, equilibrium

Answer: 16,000 N

**Item 13 [S]**
Question: A parachutist of mass 80 kg falls with constant velocity. What is the drag force acting on the parachutist?
Answer: 800 N (upward)
Difficulty: S
Topic: P1
Explanation: At constant velocity, acceleration = 0, so resultant force = 0. Weight = mg = 80 kg × 10 m/s² = 800 N downward. Therefore, the drag force (air resistance) must be 800 N upward to balance the weight. At terminal velocity, drag = weight.
Tags: drag, terminal velocity, forces, equilibrium, air resistance

**Item 14 [S]**
Question: State the law of conservation of momentum.
Answer: In a closed system, the total momentum before a collision or explosion is equal to the total momentum after.
Difficulty: S
Topic: P1
Explanation: Momentum is a vector quantity. The law applies to all collisions and explosions. In elastic collisions, kinetic energy is also conserved; in inelastic collisions, kinetic energy is not conserved (some is transformed into heat, sound, or deformation energy), but momentum is always conserved.
Tags: conservation of momentum, momentum, collision, physics principle

**Item 15 [S]**
Question: A car of mass 1200 kg travels at 15 m/s. Calculate its kinetic energy.
Answer: 135,000 J (or 135 kJ)
Difficulty: S
Topic: P1
Explanation: Kinetic energy = ½mv² = ½ × 1200 × 15² = 600 × 225 = 135,000 J. This is the energy the car possesses due to its motion. It must be dissipated (as heat in brakes) to bring the car to rest.
Tags: kinetic energy, energy, mass, velocity, calculation

**Item 16 [C]**
Question: Two trolleys A (2 kg, 3 m/s) and B (3 kg, 2 m/s) collide and stick together. Find their common velocity after the collision.
Answer: 2.4 m/s
Difficulty: C
Topic: P1
Explanation: Total momentum before = (2 × 3) + (3 × 2) = 6 + 6 = 12 kg·m/s. Total mass after = 2 + 3 = 5 kg. Using conservation of momentum: 12 = 5 × v → v = 12/5 = 2.4 m/s. (They stick together — this is a perfectly inelastic collision.)
Difficulty: C
Topic: P1
Explanation: In a perfectly inelastic collision, objects stick together. Total momentum is conserved. Kinetic energy is NOT conserved — some is lost as heat and sound during the collision. The combined object moves at 2.4 m/s.
Tags: conservation of momentum, inelastic collision, kinetic energy, collision

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: A railway truck of mass 5000 kg moving at 8 m/s collides with a stationary truck of mass 3000 kg. They couple together. Calculate the speed after collision.
Answer: 5 m/s
Difficulty: C
Topic: P1
Explanation: Momentum before = 5000 × 8 + 3000 × 0 = 40,000 kg·m/s. Total mass after = 5000 + 3000 = 8000 kg. After coupling: 40,000 = 8000 × v → v = 5 m/s. Kinetic energy before = ½(5000)(64) = 160,000 J. Kinetic energy after = ½(8000)(25) = 100,000 J. 60,000 J lost (to heat, sound, deformation).
Tags: conservation of momentum, inelastic collision, coupling, railway truck

**Item 18 [C]**
Question: Evaluate the safety features in vehicles that rely on Newton's laws of motion.
Answer: Safety features based on Newton's laws: (1) Seat belts — use inertia (Newton's First Law). In sudden stops, passengers continue moving at the car's original speed; seat belts apply a restraining force to decelerate the body safely. (2) Airbags — inflate rapidly on impact, providing a soft surface that increases the time over which the passenger decelerates, reducing the force (F = ma, longer time means smaller average force). (3) Crumple zones — collapse during impact, increasing the time over which the car decelerates, reducing the force on passengers. (4) Padded dashboards — similar to crumple zones. Newton's Second Law (F = ma) is key: for a given change in momentum, increasing the time of impact reduces the average force. Newton's Third Law is also relevant: in collisions, forces between objects are equal and opposite.
Difficulty: C
Topic: P1
Explanation: Momentum change = force × time. To minimise force during a collision, the time must be maximised. Crumple zones, airbags, and seat belts all work by increasing the collision time (and hence decreasing the peak force). In a rigid car hitting a wall, deceleration is instantaneous and forces are enormous. Crumple zones absorb energy and extend the deceleration over a longer time, reducing the average force.
Tags: vehicle safety, Newton's laws, crumple zone, airbag, seat belt, evaluation
