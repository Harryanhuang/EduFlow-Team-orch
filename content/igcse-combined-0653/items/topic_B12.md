# Topic B12 — Variation and Selection
## Items File

**Item 1 [F]**
Question: What is meant by variation?
Answer: Variation is the differences between individuals of the same species. It can be genetic (caused by differences in genes/alleles) or environmental (caused by differences in environment, e.g., nutrition, sunlight).
Difficulty: F
Topic: B12
Explanation: No two individuals of the same species are exactly alike (except identical twins). This variation is essential for evolution — without variation, natural selection cannot act.
Tags: variation, genetic, environmental

**Item 2 [F]**
Question: State two differences between genetic variation and environmental variation.
Answer: Genetic variation: caused by differences in genes/alleles, inherited from parents, not changed by environment. Environmental variation: caused by differences in surroundings/experiences, not inherited, can change over time.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B12
Explanation: Most traits are influenced by both genetic and environmental factors (multifactorial). Height, for example, is influenced by genes (maximum potential height) and environment (nutrition during childhood).
Tags: genetic variation, environmental variation, differences

**Item 3 [F]**
Question: What is natural selection?
Answer: Natural selection is the process by which individuals with characteristics best suited to their environment are more likely to survive and reproduce, passing on their genes to the next generation.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B12
Explanation: Natural selection was proposed by Charles Darwin. Over time, beneficial characteristics become more common in the population. Individuals with harmful characteristics are less likely to survive and reproduce.
Tags: natural selection, Darwin, evolution, survival

**Item 4 [F]**
Question: What is meant by a mutation?
Answer: A mutation is a random change in DNA (genes or chromosomes). Mutations can be harmful, neutral, or (rarely) beneficial. Beneficial mutations increase an organism's chance of survival and can be passed to offspring.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B12
Explanation: Mutations are the ultimate source of all genetic variation. Without mutations, there would be no new alleles for natural selection to act upon.
Tags: mutation, DNA, genetic variation

**Item 5 [S]**
Question: Explain the process of natural selection using an example.
Answer: In a population of beetles: (1) There is variation — some beetles are green, some are brown. (2) Birds eat more green beetles (easier to spot against brown twigs). (3) Brown beetles survive longer and reproduce more, passing on the brown colour genes. (4) Over many generations, the proportion of brown beetles increases.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B12
Explanation: This is a classic example of directional selection. Natural selection acts on existing variation — it does not create new traits, only selects among them.
Tags: natural selection, example, evolution

**Item 6 [S]**
Question: What is selective breeding?
Answer: Selective breeding (artificial selection) is the process by which humans choose individuals with desirable characteristics to breed together, over many generations, to produce offspring with those desirable traits.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B12
Explanation: Selective breeding is used in agriculture and animal husbandry. Examples: dairy cows (high milk yield), wheat varieties (disease resistance), race horses (speed), dogs (various breeds).
Tags: selective breeding, artificial selection, breeding

**Item 7 [S]**
Question: State two examples of selective breeding in agriculture.
Answer: (1) Breeding wheat varieties that are resistant to a specific disease, so crops are less likely to fail. (2) Breeding dairy cows that produce large quantities of milk.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B12
Explanation: Modern wheat varieties have been bred for high yield, disease resistance, and short stems (to resist lodging). dairy farming has used selective breeding to increase average milk yield dramatically over decades.
Tags: selective breeding, agriculture, wheat, dairy

**Item 8 [S]**
Question: Describe the steps in selective breeding.
Answer: (1) Choose individuals with the desired characteristic. (2) Breed them together. (3) Select the best offspring with the desired trait. (4) Repeat over many generations. (5) After many generations, the population will consist mainly of individuals with the desired trait.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B12
Explanation: Each generation, the chosen individuals are bred, and only the best offspring are selected for the next round. This gradually concentrates the desirable alleles in the population.
Tags: selective breeding, steps, process

**Item 9 [S]**
Question: What is meant by variation being continuous or discontinuous?
Answer: Continuous variation: traits show a range of values with no clear categories (e.g., height in humans — most people are around average, fewer are very short or very tall). Discontinuous variation: traits fall into clear, distinct categories with no intermediate values (e.g., blood groups — A, B, AB, O).
Difficulty: S
Topic: B12
Explanation: Continuous variation is usually polygenic (controlled by many genes) and is also influenced by the environment. Discontinuous variation is usually controlled by one or a few genes.
Tags: continuous variation, discontinuous variation, classification

**Item 10 [S]**
Question: How does genetic engineering differ from selective breeding?
Answer: Selective breeding: humans choose existing individuals with desirable traits to breed together — introduces new combinations of existing alleles gradually over many generations. Genetic engineering: humans directly modify an organism's DNA by inserting a new gene from another species, producing a transgenic organism immediately.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B12
Explanation: Selective breeding is a slow, gradual process. Genetic engineering can introduce traits from completely unrelated species immediately — e.g., inserting a bacterial gene for herbicide resistance into soybeans.
Tags: genetic engineering, selective breeding, comparison

**Item 11 [S]**
Question: What is a clone?
Answer: A clone is a genetically identical organism. In biology, clones can be produced naturally (identical twins, asexual reproduction) or artificially (vegetative propagation, embryo splitting, somatic cell nuclear transfer).
Difficulty: S
Topic: B12
Explanation: Natural clones: identical twins are clones formed by the separation of early embryonic cells. Artificial clones: plant tissue culture, animal embryo splitting, Dolly the sheep (somatic cell nuclear transfer).
Tags: clone, identical, asexual reproduction

**Item 12 [S]**
Question: State two advantages and two disadvantages of cloning in agriculture.
Answer: Advantages: (1) Exact copies of high-yield or disease-resistant plants can be produced rapidly. (2) Uniform quality of produce. Disadvantages: (1) Lack of genetic variation — all clones are equally vulnerable to new diseases. (2) Loss of biodiversity if clones replace diverse traditional varieties.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B12
Explanation: Banana is essentially a single clone worldwide (Gros Michel variety, largely replaced by Cavendish due to Panama disease). Potato varieties are also often clonal. This genetic uniformity makes crops vulnerable to epidemics.
Tags: cloning, agriculture, advantages, disadvantages

**Item 13 [C]**
Question: Explain the role of mutations in evolution.
Answer: Mutations are the source of all genetic variation — they create new alleles. Evolution by natural selection cannot occur without variation. Steps: (1) Random mutation occurs in DNA (e.g., a gene that slightly increases disease resistance). (2) If the mutation is beneficial, individuals with it are more likely to survive and reproduce in their environment. (3) These individuals pass the mutated gene to their offspring. (4) Over many generations, the beneficial mutation spreads through the population. (5) Mutations accumulate, leading eventually to speciation. Mutations are random (not directed by need) — a harmful mutation may become neutral if the environment changes.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B12
Explanation: Mutations can occur in any gene. Point mutations change single nucleotides; larger mutations (duplications, insertions, deletions) can have bigger effects. Most mutations are neutral or harmful — beneficial mutations are rare. The rate of mutation is low but sufficient over millions of years to generate enormous genetic diversity.
Tags: mutation, evolution, genetic variation, natural selection

**Item 14 [C]**
Question: Describe how bacteria become resistant to antibiotics. Evaluate the measures to combat antibiotic resistance.
Answer: Mechanism: Random mutation in a bacterial population may give some bacteria resistance to an antibiotic. When the antibiotic is used, susceptible bacteria die. Resistant bacteria survive and reproduce (natural selection), passing on resistance genes. Over time, the population becomes dominated by resistant strains. These genes can spread between bacteria via horizontal gene transfer (plasmids, conjugation).
Evaluation of measures: (1) Rational antibiotic use — reduces selection pressure, slowing resistance development. (2) Completing antibiotic courses — ensures all bacteria are killed, preventing survival of partially resistant strains. (3) Hospital hygiene — reduces transmission of resistant strains. (4) New antibiotic development — ongoing but slow; bacteria evolve resistance to new drugs. (5) Phage therapy — using viruses that target bacteria. (6) Restricting agricultural antibiotic use — prevents selection of resistant bacteria in livestock. Conclusion: Combating resistance requires a multi-pronged strategy; no single measure is sufficient.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B12
Explanation: Antibiotic resistance is one of the most pressing public health challenges. MRSA (methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus), CRE (carbapenem-resistant Enterobacteriaceae), and TB are major threats. The WHO estimates that antibiotic resistance could cause 10 million deaths annually by 2050 if unchecked.
Tags: antibiotic resistance, natural selection, evolution, MRSA

**Item 15 [C]**
Question: Compare natural selection and selective breeding.
Answer: Natural selection: nature (environment) determines which individuals survive and reproduce; occurs over long timescales; produces organisms adapted to natural environments. Selective breeding: humans choose which individuals reproduce; can occur over relatively short timescales; produces organisms with characteristics useful to humans (not necessarily adapted to natural environments).
Similarities: Both involve selection of individuals with desirable traits; both change allele frequencies in populations over time; both require heritable variation.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B12
Explanation: Natural selection and selective breeding are both forms of artificial selection when humans influence survival and reproduction. Both rely on genetic variation and inheritance. The key difference is the agent of selection — nature vs human choice.
Tags: natural selection, selective breeding, comparison

**Item 16 [C]**
Question: A farmer wants to breed a disease-resistant variety of wheat. Describe and evaluate the steps she could take.
Answer: Steps: (1) Obtain a diverse population of wheat plants. (2) Expose them to the disease (or test for disease resistance genes). (3) Select the most resistant plants. (4) Cross-breed the resistant plants. (5) Repeat selection and breeding over many generations. (6) Test the final variety for resistance and yield. Evaluation: This is effective for traits controlled by many genes. However, it is time-consuming (5–15 years for a new variety), and may reduce genetic diversity in the variety. Modern marker-assisted selection (using DNA markers linked to disease resistance genes) accelerates this process. Genetic engineering can insert specific resistance genes directly, bypassing the need for many generations of breeding.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B12
Explanation: Traditional plant breeding has produced all modern crop varieties. Green Revolution varieties (e.g., dwarf wheat developed by Norman Borlaug) used selective breeding to increase yields dramatically. Modern plant breeding uses genomic selection (predicting traits from DNA) to accelerate variety development.
Tags: selective breeding, wheat, disease resistance, evaluation

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: Explain how the evolution of antibiotic resistance in bacteria supports Darwin's theory of natural selection.
Answer: Darwin's theory: individuals with advantageous traits survive and reproduce more successfully (survival of the fittest). The evolution of antibiotic resistance is a textbook example: (1) In any population of bacteria, random mutations occur — some confer antibiotic resistance. (2) In the presence of antibiotics, susceptible bacteria die, while resistant bacteria survive. (3) Resistant bacteria reproduce, passing on resistance genes. (4) Over time, the population evolves to become predominantly resistant. This is natural selection in action: random mutation (variation) → selective pressure (antibiotic) → differential survival and reproduction → evolution of resistance. The speed of bacterial evolution is due to their rapid reproduction (one bacterium can become millions in 24 hours).
Difficulty: C
Topic: B12
Explanation: Bacteria demonstrate evolution by natural selection on a human timescale because of their short generation time (~20 minutes). This makes them ideal model organisms for studying evolution. The evolution of antibiotic resistance is now being tracked in real-time by genomic surveillance.
Tags: antibiotic resistance, Darwin, natural selection, evolution evidence
