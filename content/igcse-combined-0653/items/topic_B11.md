# Topic B11 — Inheritance
## Items File

**Item 1 [F]**
Question: What is a gene?
Answer: A gene is a section of DNA that codes for a specific protein or characteristic. Genes are located on chromosomes in the nucleus of a cell.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B11
Explanation: Genes determine inherited traits. Each gene has a specific sequence of DNA nucleotides. The sequence codes for a specific amino acid sequence, which folds into a protein. Different versions of a gene (alleles) produce different versions of the trait.
Tags: gene, DNA, chromosome, inheritance

**Item 2 [F]**
Question: What is the difference between genotype and phenotype?
Answer: Genotype is the genetic makeup of an organism — the alleles it carries (e.g., BB, Bb, bb). Phenotype is the physical appearance of the organism (e.g., tall, short, brown eyes).
Difficulty: F
Topic: B11
Explanation: The genotype determines the phenotype, but the environment can also influence phenotype (e.g., height is influenced by nutrition as well as genetics). A BB and Bb genotype both produce a "brown eye" phenotype if B codes for brown eyes.
Tags: genotype, phenotype, alleles, inheritance

**Item 3 [F]**
Question: What is meant by a dominant allele?
Answer: A dominant allele is an allele that always shows its effect in the phenotype when present. It is represented by a capital letter. Only one copy of a dominant allele is needed for its characteristic to be expressed.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B11
Explanation: In a heterozygous individual (e.g., Bb), the dominant allele (B) masks the recessive allele (b). The recessive phenotype only appears when both copies are recessive (bb).
Tags: dominant allele, inheritance, genetics

**Item 4 [F]**
Question: What is meant by a recessive allele?
Answer: A recessive allele is an allele whose effect is masked by a dominant allele when present. It is only expressed in the phenotype when an individual has two copies of the recessive allele.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B11
Explanation: A recessive allele can be "carried" by a heterozygous individual (Bb) without showing in the phenotype. Two carriers have a 25% chance of producing offspring showing the recessive phenotype.
Tags: recessive allele, inheritance, carrier

**Item 5 [S]**
Question: What is the difference between mitosis and meiosis?
Answer: Mitosis: one division producing two genetically identical daughter cells (diploid). Used for growth and repair. Meiosis: two divisions producing four genetically different gametes (haploid). Used for sexual reproduction.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B11
Explanation: Meiosis introduces genetic variation through crossing over (exchange of genetic material between homologous chromosomes) and independent assortment (random orientation of chromosome pairs). This variation is essential for evolution.
Tags: mitosis, meiosis, cell division, genetics

**Item 6 [S]**
Question: What is a Punnett square?
Answer: A Punnett square is a diagram used to predict the genotypes and phenotypes of offspring from a genetic cross. The alleles of each parent are placed on the sides, and the possible offspring genotypes are filled in the grid.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B11
Explanation: Punnett squares are used for monohybrid crosses. They predict the probability of offspring having particular genotypes. For example, crossing Bb × Bb gives 25% BB, 50% Bb, 25% bb.
Tags: Punnett square, genetic cross, inheritance

**Item 7 [S]**
Question: In humans, the allele for brown eyes (B) is dominant over the allele for blue eyes (b). Cross a heterozygous brown-eyed parent with a homozygous blue-eyed parent. What are the offspring genotypes and phenotypes?
Answer: Parent 1: Bb (heterozygous brown). Parent 2: bb (homozygous blue). Offspring genotypes: 50% Bb, 50% bb. Offspring phenotypes: 50% brown eyes, 50% blue eyes.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B11
Explanation: Cross: Bb × bb. Gametes: B or b from parent 1; b from parent 2. Offspring: Bb, bb, Bb, bb — each with 50% probability. This cross shows that a brown-eyed parent can have a blue-eyed child if they are heterozygous.
Tags: genetic cross, Punnett square, inheritance, dominant

**Item 8 [S]**
Question: State three ways meiosis introduces genetic variation.
Answer: (1) Crossing over — exchange of genetic material between homologous chromosomes during prophase I. (2) Independent assortment — random orientation of chromosome pairs during metaphase I. (3) Random fertilisation — any sperm can fuse with any egg.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B11
Explanation: Crossing over creates new allele combinations on chromosomes. Independent assortment means each gamete gets a random mix of maternal and paternal chromosomes. Random fertilisation multiplies these possibilities. These three sources together create enormous genetic variation.
Tags: meiosis, genetic variation, crossing over, independent assortment

**Item 9 [S]**
Question: What is the difference between a gene and a chromosome?
Answer: A chromosome is a structure made of DNA that carries many genes. A gene is a specific section of DNA (a sequence of nucleotides) that codes for a particular protein or trait. Chromosomes are visible under a microscope; genes are sub-microscopic.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B11
Explanation: Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs). Each chromosome contains hundreds to thousands of genes. The human genome contains about 20,000–25,000 genes. The Human Genome Project mapped the entire human genome.
Tags: gene, chromosome, DNA, genetics

**Item 11 [S]**
Question: A man with genotype IAi has blood type A. What does this tell you about the alleles IA and i?
Answer: IA is dominant over i. The IA allele codes for the A antigen on red blood cells, so the person has blood group A. The i allele codes for no antigen (O), which is recessive.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B11
Explanation: The ABO blood group system has three alleles: IA (A antigen), IB (B antigen), and i (no antigen). IA and IB are co-dominant over i. This is why a heterozygous IAi genotype produces blood group A.
Tags: blood group, alleles, dominance, ABO system

**Item 12 [S]**
Question: What is a monohybrid cross?
Answer: A monohybrid cross is a genetic cross that follows the inheritance of a single characteristic (controlled by one gene). It involves crossing two parents that differ in one trait.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B11
Explanation: Monohybrid crosses are used to study simple inheritance patterns. Examples: tall × short pea plants, or round × wrinkled seeds. The results of a monohybrid cross can be displayed in a Punnett square.
Tags: monohybrid cross, genetic cross, inheritance

**Item 13 [C]**
Question: In pea plants, tall (T) is dominant over short (t). Two heterozygous tall plants are crossed. Draw a Punnett square and give the ratio of genotypes and phenotypes in the offspring.
Answer: Parents: Tt × Tt. Gametes: T or t from each parent. Offspring genotypes: TT (25%), Tt (50%), tt (25%). Genotypic ratio: 1:2:1. Phenotypic ratio: 3 tall : 1 short.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B11
Explanation: This is a classic Mendelian monohybrid cross. The 3:1 phenotypic ratio is characteristic of a monohybrid cross between two heterozygotes. The 1:2:1 genotypic ratio reflects the underlying allele frequencies.
Tags: Punnett square, monohybrid cross, Mendelian genetics

**Item 14 [C]**
Question: Explain the differences between monohybrid and dihybrid crosses, using a Punnett square in your answer.
Answer: Monohybrid cross: follows one gene (one characteristic). Punnett square is 2×2 = 4 cells. Dihybrid cross: follows two genes (two characteristics) simultaneously. Punnett square is 4×4 = 16 cells. Example: crossing TtRr × TtRr (tall red × tall red flower) gives 16 possible genotypes with 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratios. Dihybrid crosses reveal whether genes are linked (on the same chromosome) or independently assorted.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B11
Explanation: Dihybrid crosses can detect genetic linkage — if two genes are close together on the same chromosome, they tend to be inherited together, producing more parental types and fewer recombinant types than expected from independent assortment.
Tags: dihybrid cross, Punnett square, genetic linkage, inheritance

**Item 15 [C]**
Question: A colour-blind man marries a woman who is a carrier for colour blindness. Colour blindness is caused by a recessive allele on the X chromosome. What are the possible genotypes of their offspring?
Answer: Father: XcY (colour-blind). Mother: XCXc (carrier). Offspring: XcY (colour-blind son, 25%), XCY (normal son, 25%), XCXc (carrier daughter, 25%), XcXc (colour-blind daughter, 25%). So: 50% sons — 50% normal, 50% colour-blind; 50% daughters — 50% normal carrier, 50% colour-blind.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B11
Explanation: Sex-linked genes are located on sex chromosomes (X or Y). Since males are XY, a single recessive allele on the X chromosome is expressed (no second X to mask it). This explains why colour blindness is more common in males (~8% of males vs ~0.5% of females).
Tags: sex-linked inheritance, X chromosome, colour blindness, genetics

**Item 16 [C]**
Question: A couple have four children. Three are girls and one is a boy. Explain this outcome in terms of genetics.
Answer: Sex in humans is determined by sex chromosomes: females are XX, males are XY. Each parent contributes one sex chromosome. The mother always contributes an X chromosome (from her XX pair). The father contributes either X or Y. Each child has a 50% chance of being male (Y from father) or female (X from father). The outcome of three girls and one boy (3:1 ratio) is within the range of statistical variation — the 50:50 probability applies to each pregnancy independently. With only 4 children, deviations from 50:50 are common. Larger samples approach 50:50 (law of large numbers).
Difficulty: C
Topic: B11
Explanation: The probability of exactly 3 girls and 1 boy in 4 children is C(4,3)/2^4 = 4/16 = 25%. This is not unusual. To demonstrate a deviation from 50:50, much larger samples are needed (e.g., 1000 children).
Tags: sex determination, probability, inheritance, sex chromosomes

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: Compare the structure of DNA and RNA.
Answer: DNA: double-stranded helix; deoxyribose sugar; contains base thymine (T); stable molecule. RNA: single-stranded; ribose sugar; contains base uracil (U); less stable. Both contain the bases adenine, guanine, and cytosine.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B11
Explanation: The differences between DNA and RNA reflect their different functions. DNA is the long-term genetic information storage molecule — its double helix and thymine (more stable than uracil) make it suitable for archival storage. RNA is the mobile information carrier — its single-stranded flexibility allows it to fold into various functional shapes.
Tags: DNA, RNA, structure, comparison, genetics

**Item 18 [C]**
Question: Evaluate the statement: "All genetic diseases can be cured using gene therapy."
Answer: This statement is incorrect and overstates current capabilities. Gene therapy (introducing functional genes into cells to treat disease) has shown promise for some single-gene disorders: SCID (severe combined immunodeficiency), inherited blindness (LCA), and some blood disorders. Successes: Adeno-associated virus (AAV) vectors deliver functional copies of defective genes to patient cells. Limitations: (1) Many diseases are polygenic (involving many genes) — gene therapy cannot easily address all involved genes. (2) Viral vectors can cause immune reactions or insert into the wrong genomic location (insertional mutagenesis). (3) Treatment is often somatic (patient only) — not heritable; germline therapy (affecting offspring) raises ethical issues. (4) Some diseases involve complex gene-environment interactions that a single gene cannot fix. (5) Many diseases are not purely genetic (e.g., cancer involves somatic mutations). Conclusion: Gene therapy is a powerful tool for some genetic diseases but cannot cure all genetic diseases.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B11
Explanation: Gene therapy has had setbacks — Jesse Gelsinger (1999, fatal immune reaction to adenovirus vector) and leukaemia cases in early trials slowed the field. Modern AAV vectors are safer. Over 20 gene therapies are now FDA/EMA approved. CRISPR-Cas9 enables precise gene editing and has been used in clinical trials (e.g., sickle cell disease). Germline gene editing (embryos) remains ethically controversial.
Tags: gene therapy, genetic disease, evaluation, CRISPR, ethics
