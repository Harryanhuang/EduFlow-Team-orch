# Topic B5 — Nutrition
## Items File

**Item 1 [F]**
Question: What is photosynthesis?
Answer: Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants make food (glucose) using light energy, carbon dioxide, and water. The word equation is: carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen (in the presence of light and chlorophyll).
Difficulty: F
Topic: B5
Explanation: Photosynthesis occurs in the chloroplasts of plant cells, specifically in the mesophyll layers of leaves. Chlorophyll absorbs light energy, which drives the chemical reactions. The process produces glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆), which the plant uses for energy and growth, and oxygen (O₂), which is released as a byproduct.
Tags: photosynthesis, nutrition, plants, glucose

**Item 2 [F]**
Question: State two sources of light for photosynthesis.
Answer: (1) Natural light from the Sun; (2) Artificial light from incandescent or LED grow lights.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B5
Explanation: In natural conditions, sunlight is the light source for photosynthesis. In controlled environments (greenhouses, indoor farming), artificial grow lights can provide the necessary light energy. Light quality (wavelength) matters — chlorophyll absorbs red and blue light most effectively, which is why plants appear green (green light is reflected).
Tags: photosynthesis, light, energy source

**Item 3 [F]**
Question: What are the two stages of photosynthesis?
Answer: (1) The light-dependent reactions (occur in the thylakoid membranes); (2) The light-independent reactions (occur in the stroma, also called the Calvin cycle).
Difficulty: F
Topic: B5
Explanation: The light-dependent reactions capture light energy to make ATP and NADPH, and split water to release oxygen. The light-independent reactions (Calvin cycle) use ATP and NADPH to fix carbon dioxide and synthesise glucose. Both stages are essential — without light, the light-dependent reactions stop, and without ATP/NADPH, carbon fixation cannot occur.
Tags: photosynthesis, light-dependent, light-independent, Calvin cycle

**Item 4 [F]**
Question: What is a food chain?
Answer: A food chain shows the flow of energy from one organism to the next, starting with a producer (green plant), then herbivores (primary consumers), then carnivores (secondary consumers).
Difficulty: F
Topic: B5
Explanation: In a food chain, arrows represent the direction of energy transfer. Plants are producers — they convert sunlight into chemical energy (glucose) through photosynthesis. Herbivores eat plants, transferring some of this energy. Carnivores eat herbivores. At each level, energy is lost as heat through respiration, so typically only 4–5 trophic levels can be supported.
Tags: food chain, producers, consumers, energy flow

**Item 5 [S]**
Question: Explain why plants need mineral ions for healthy growth. Give two examples.
Answer: Plants need mineral ions for making essential compounds. (1) Nitrate ions (NO₃⁻) are needed to make amino acids and proteins. Without nitrate, plants develop yellowing leaves (chlorosis). (2) Magnesium ions (Mg²⁺) are needed to make chlorophyll — without magnesium, leaves turn yellow between the veins.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B5
Explanation: Mineral ions are absorbed from the soil by the roots via active transport. Nitrogen (as nitrate or ammonium) is needed for nucleic acids, proteins, and chlorophyll. Phosphorus (as phosphate) is needed for ATP and nucleic acids. Potassium regulates water balance and enzyme function. Magnesium is the central atom in chlorophyll. Deficiency symptoms help diagnose nutrient problems in crops.
Tags: mineral ions, nitrate, magnesium, plant nutrition, deficiency

**Item 6 [S]**
Question: Describe the structure of the human digestive system, naming four main regions or organs involved in chemical digestion.
Answer: The digestive system includes: (1) Mouth — amylase in saliva begins starch digestion; (2) Stomach — pepsin and hydrochloric acid digest proteins; (3) Small intestine (duodenum and ileum) — pancreatic amylase, lipase, and trypsin continue digestion; bile emulsifies fats; (4) Large intestine — water absorption.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B5
Explanation: Chemical digestion involves enzymes breaking down large, insoluble food molecules into smaller, soluble ones. Starch → maltose (amylase), proteins → peptides (pepsin, trypsin), fats → fatty acids + glycerol (lipase + bile). The small intestine is the main site of digestion and absorption, aided by secretions from the pancreas and gall bladder.
Tags: digestive system, chemical digestion, enzymes, alimentary canal

**Item 7 [S]**
Question: What is the role of bile in digestion?
Answer: Bile is produced by the liver, stored in the gallbladder, and released into the small intestine. It emulsifies fats — breaking large fat droplets into tiny droplets, increasing the surface area for lipase enzymes to act on.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B5
Explanation: Bile is an alkaline green-yellow liquid containing bile salts (derived from cholesterol) and bile pigments. Emulsification is a physical process — bile salts have hydrophobic and hydrophilic ends, coating fat droplets and keeping them dispersed in water. This increases surface area from a few large droplets to many tiny ones, dramatically increasing the rate of fat digestion by lipase.
Tags: bile, emulsification, fat digestion, liver, small intestine

**Item 8 [S]**
Question: Draw and label a diagram of the human alimentary canal, showing the mouth, oesophagus, stomach, small intestine, and large intestine.
Answer: The alimentary canal is a muscular tube running from the mouth to the anus. In order: Mouth (with teeth and tongue) → Oesophagus (muscular tube, ~25 cm) → Stomach (J-shaped organ) → Small intestine (duodenum + ileum, ~6 m long) → Large intestine (colon, ~1.5 m) → Rectum → Anus.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B5
Explanation: Peristalsis (wave-like muscle contractions) moves food along the oesophagus and intestines. The stomach stores food and begins protein digestion. The small intestine is the main site of chemical digestion and absorption. The large intestine absorbs water and mineral ions, forming solid faeces.
Tags: alimentary canal, digestive system, peristalsis, anatomy

**Item 9 [S]**
Question: Explain how villi in the small intestine are adapted for absorption.
Answer: Villi are finger-like projections lining the small intestine. They are adapted for absorption because: (1) They have a very large surface area for rapid absorption. (2) The epithelial cells have microvilli (further increasing surface area). (3) They have a rich supply of blood capillaries to carry away absorbed nutrients. (4) The epithelial cells are only one cell thick, so substances only diffuse a short distance.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B5
Explanation: The small intestine has a huge internal surface area — about 300 m² — due to villi and microvilli. Each villus contains a capillary network and a lacteal (lymph vessel). Glucose and amino acids enter capillaries; fatty acids and glycerol enter lacteals. The thin epithelium (one cell layer) minimises diffusion distance. Mitochondria in epithelial cells provide ATP for active transport of nutrients.
Tags: villi, absorption, adaptation, small intestine, surface area

**Item 10 [S]**
Question: What is the difference between a food chain and a food web?
Answer: A food chain is a linear sequence showing who eats whom (producer → herbivore → carnivore). A food web is a more complex network of interconnected food chains, showing that most organisms eat more than one type of food and are eaten by more than one predator.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B5
Explanation: Food webs are more realistic because most organisms have multiple food sources and predators. If one organism in a food chain is removed, the chain breaks. In a food web, if one organism is removed, other connections compensate. This makes ecosystems more stable and resilient. Trophic levels (feeding levels) can still be identified in food webs.
Tags: food chain, food web, ecosystem, trophic levels

**Item 11 [S]**
Question: Explain how energy is lost at each stage of a food chain.
Answer: Energy is lost at each trophic level mainly through: (1) Respiration — energy released from glucose to power life processes is lost as heat; (2) Excretion — undigested material is egested as faeces; (3) Movement — energy is used for locomotion. Typically, only about 10% of energy is transferred to the next level.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B5
Explanation: The 10% rule: of all energy ingested by an organism, roughly 90% is lost through respiration (used to power metabolism) and egestion (undigested matter in faeces). This explains why food chains rarely have more than 4–5 trophic levels — there is not enough energy to support another level of consumers.
Tags: energy transfer, food chain, trophic levels, respiration, 10% rule

**Item 12 [S]**
Question: State the balanced chemical equation for photosynthesis.
Answer: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ (in the presence of light and chlorophyll)
Difficulty: S
Topic: B5
Explanation: This equation shows that six molecules of carbon dioxide and six molecules of water, in the presence of light energy and chlorophyll, produce one glucose molecule and six molecules of oxygen. The equation is balanced — equal numbers of each atom on both sides (C: 6, H: 12, O: 18).
Tags: photosynthesis equation, balanced equation, chemical reaction

**Item 13 [C]**
Question: Design an experiment to investigate the effect of light intensity on the rate of photosynthesis. Describe the method, variables, and expected results.
Answer: Method: Place a pondweed (e.g., Elodea) in a beaker of sodium hydrogencarbonate solution (provides CO₂). Place at a fixed distance from a lamp. Count the number of bubbles produced per minute as a measure of oxygen release. Repeat at different distances from the lamp (e.g., 10 cm, 20 cm, 30 cm, 40 cm). Variables: Independent — distance from lamp (light intensity); Dependent — number of bubbles per minute; Control — temperature (water bath), CO₂ concentration, amount of pondweed.
Expected: As distance increases, light intensity decreases (inverse square law), and bubble count decreases — rate of photosynthesis falls.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B5
Explanation: Light intensity affects the light-dependent reactions of photosynthesis. The inverse square law: intensity is inversely proportional to the square of the distance. Doubling the distance reduces intensity to 1/4. Using sodium hydrogencarbonate ensures CO₂ is not a limiting factor. Temperature should be controlled with a water bath to prevent cold water from limiting the rate. Bubbles are oxygen from photosynthesis.
Tags: photosynthesis experiment, light intensity, method, variables, rate

**Item 14 [C]**
Question: Explain why a cactus plant has small, spiky leaves and a thick, fleshy stem.
Answer: Small, spiky leaves reduce surface area, minimising water loss through transpiration. The thick, fleshy stem stores water for use during drought periods. Both adaptations are responses to the dry desert environment where water availability is the main limiting factor for growth.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B5
Explanation: Cacti have evolved to survive in arid (dry) environments. Their spines are modified leaves that reduce transpiration surface area. The succulent stem is specialised for water storage. The waxy cuticle on the stem reduces water loss. Some cacti use CAM photosynthesis (Crassulacean Acid Metabolism), opening stomata only at night to minimise water loss — but this adaptation is not covered in IGCSE Combined Science.
Tags: plant adaptations, desert plants, transpiration, xerophytes, water conservation

**Item 15 [C]**
Question: Compare the energy flows in a food chain and in an ecosystem. Explain why ecosystems are more stable than individual food chains.
Answer: Food chains are linear: energy flows in one direction (producer → consumer → predator) with about 90% lost at each level. Ecosystems are networks of many interconnected food chains — a food web. Ecosystems are more stable because: (1) Multiple food sources mean if one organism declines, consumers can switch to other prey; (2) More trophic connections mean energy pathways are redundant; (3) Decomposers recycle nutrients that producers need, maintaining productivity. In a single food chain, the removal of one species causes cascade effects and possible collapse. In a food web, effects are dampened.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B5
Explanation: Stability in ecosystems depends on biodiversity — more species means more connections and resilience. The pyramid of numbers and biomass represent trophic structure, but food webs show that energy flow is complex and multi-directional. Decomposers (fungi, bacteria) are critical in food webs — they break down dead organic matter, releasing minerals back to the soil for producers. Without decomposers, nutrients would be locked in dead bodies and ecosystems would collapse.
Tags: food web, ecosystem stability, energy flow, biodiversity, trophic structure

**Item 16 [C]**
Question: A farmer grows crops in a field and applies nitrogen fertiliser. Explain the possible consequences for the local ecosystem if excess fertiliser runs off into a nearby lake.
Answer: Excess fertiliser (nitrates and phosphates) entering the lake causes eutrophication. (1) Nitrates/phosphates act as nutrients for algae. (2) Algal bloom occurs — algae multiply rapidly, forming a dense mat on the water surface. (3) This blocks light from underwater plants, which die. (4) Decomposing bacteria break down the dead plants, consuming oxygen. (5) Dissolved oxygen in the water falls, causing fish and other aquatic animals to die. (6) The lake ecosystem collapses — biodiversity decreases dramatically.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B5
Explanation: Eutrophication is a major environmental problem. Natural water bodies have low nutrient levels (oligotrophic). Agricultural runoff adds nitrates and phosphates. The algal bloom shades submerged plants. Bacterial decomposition of dead plant material depletes dissolved oxygen (BOD — biochemical oxygen demand). Fish kills occur when DO falls below ~4 mg/L. Prevention: buffer strips, controlled fertiliser application, wetlands to filter runoff.
Tags: eutrophication, nutrient pollution, algal bloom, dissolved oxygen, ecosystem impact

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: Evaluate the environmental impact of a meat-based diet compared to a plant-based diet.
Answer: A meat-based diet has higher environmental impact than a plant-based diet. Animals are less efficient at converting plant energy into meat — about 10% of feed energy becomes animal tissue. This means more land, water, and fertiliser are needed per unit of food energy. Cattle farming produces significant methane (a greenhouse gas), contributing to climate change. Deforestation for grazing and feed crops reduces biodiversity and carbon sequestration. Plant-based diets require less land, water, and energy, producing fewer greenhouse gases. However, some land (marginal land unsuitable for crops) can only be used for grazing, and livestock provides manure for fertiliser.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B5
Explanation: The 10% trophic efficiency rule applies to livestock. Producing 1 kg of beef requires about 6–10 kg of grain and large amounts of water (~15,000 litres). Livestock accounts for about 14.5% of global greenhouse gas emissions. However, livestock can upcycle inedible plant material (grass, crop residues) into high-quality protein, and grazing animals maintain semi-natural ecosystems. The debate involves land use, water footprint, biodiversity, and ethical considerations.
Tags: environmental impact, diet, food production, sustainability, evaluation

**Item 18 [C]**
Question: Explain how decomposers recycle nutrients in an ecosystem. Include in your answer: the role of decomposition, the nutrients involved, and why this recycling is essential for ecosystem function.
Answer: Decomposers (fungi and bacteria) break down dead organic matter (plant material, animal bodies, waste) through extracellular digestion — they secrete enzymes onto dead material, break it down, and absorb the soluble products. This releases mineral ions (e.g., nitrates, phosphates, magnesium) back into the soil. Producers absorb these minerals for growth. Without decomposers, nutrients would remain locked in dead organisms, and eventually the soil would become depleted and plant growth would stop. The carbon cycle is also maintained: decomposers release CO₂ through respiration, which producers use for photosynthesis. Decomposers are the essential link between dead matter and living organisms, maintaining nutrient cycles and ecosystem productivity.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B5
Explanation: Nutrient cycles (nitrogen, phosphorus, carbon) depend on decomposers. In the nitrogen cycle: decomposers break down proteins and urea to ammonia (ammonification), nitrifying bacteria convert ammonia → nitrite → nitrate (nitrification), and denitrifying bacteria convert nitrate back to nitrogen gas. Phosphorus is released by decomposers breaking down organic phosphates. Without decomposers, the biogeochemical cycles would halt, and ecosystems would collapse within a few years.
Tags: decomposers, nutrient cycling, nitrogen cycle, ecosystem function, soil fertility
