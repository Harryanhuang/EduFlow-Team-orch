# Topic B6 — Gas Exchange and Respiration
## Items File

**Item 1 [F]**
Question: What is aerobic respiration?
Answer: Aerobic respiration is the process of releasing energy from glucose using oxygen. The word equation is: glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water + energy.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B6
Explanation: Aerobic respiration occurs in the mitochondria of cells. It is the most efficient way to release energy from glucose, producing up to 38 ATP molecules per glucose molecule. All multicellular organisms use aerobic respiration most of the time.
Tags: aerobic respiration, mitochondria, energy, oxygen

**Item 2 [F]**
Question: State the balanced symbol equation for aerobic respiration.
Answer: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + energy (ATP)
Difficulty: F
Topic: B6
Explanation: This equation shows that one glucose molecule reacts with six oxygen molecules to produce six molecules of carbon dioxide, six of water, and energy. The equation is balanced: C: 6, H: 12, O: 12+6=18 on each side.
Tags: respiration equation, balanced equation, chemical reaction

**Item 3 [F]**
Question: What is anaerobic respiration?
Answer: Anaerobic respiration is the release of energy from glucose without using oxygen. In plants and yeast: glucose → carbon dioxide + ethanol + energy. In animals: glucose → lactic acid + energy.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B6
Explanation: Anaerobic respiration produces much less energy per glucose molecule (only 2 ATP compared to 38 from aerobic respiration). It occurs when oxygen supply is insufficient — during vigorous exercise, for example. The lactic acid produced in animal muscles causes cramp and must be broken down once oxygen becomes available.
Tags: anaerobic respiration, fermentation, lactic acid, yeast

**Item 4 [F]**
Question: Name the gas exchange surface in humans.
Answer: The alveoli (air sacs) in the lungs.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B6
Explanation: The alveoli are tiny air-filled sacs at the end of the bronchioles. They have very thin walls (one cell thick) surrounded by an extensive network of capillaries. This provides a large surface area (~75 m²) for efficient gas exchange between the air and the blood.
Tags: gas exchange, alveoli, lungs, respiratory system

**Item 5 [S]**
Question: Describe what happens to the diaphragm during inhalation (breathing in).
Answer: During inhalation, the diaphragm contracts and flattens (moves downwards). This increases the volume of the thoracic cavity (chest cavity). The increased volume decreases the air pressure inside the lungs compared to the atmosphere. Air then flows in from high pressure (atmosphere) to low pressure (lungs).
Difficulty: S
Topic: B6
Explanation: The diaphragm is a dome-shaped muscle at the base of the chest cavity. When it contracts, it flattens and moves downwards, increasing the vertical dimension of the thoracic cavity. Simultaneously, the external intercostal muscles contract, raising the ribs. Both actions increase lung volume, decreasing intrapulmonary pressure below atmospheric pressure, and air rushes in.
Tags: diaphragm, inhalation, breathing mechanism, gas exchange

**Item 6 [S]**
Question: Explain why ventilation (breathing) is necessary for gas exchange.
Answer: Ventilation renews the air in the alveoli, maintaining a steep concentration gradient for both oxygen and carbon dioxide. Fresh air (high O₂, low CO₂) replaces stale air (low O₂, high CO₂). Without ventilation, equilibrium would be reached — no net diffusion of either gas — and gas exchange would stop.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B6
Explanation: Diffusion requires a concentration gradient. Stale alveolar air has low O₂ (~16%) and high CO₂ (~5%) compared to fresh air (O₂ ~21%, CO₂ ~0.04%). Ventilation maintains this gradient. Breathing rate increases during exercise to meet higher oxygen demand and remove more CO₂ produced by increased respiration.
Tags: ventilation, gas exchange, concentration gradient, breathing rate

**Item 7 [S]**
Question: Describe what happens to the diaphragm and ribcage during forced exhalation (e.g., blowing out a candle).
Answer: During forced exhalation, the diaphragm relaxes and moves back up into a dome shape. The internal intercostal muscles contract, pulling the ribs downwards and inwards. The volume of the thoracic cavity decreases, increasing the air pressure inside the lungs above atmospheric pressure, forcing air out.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B6
Explanation: Normal breathing out is largely passive — the diaphragm relaxes, and elastic recoil of the lungs and ribcage returns them to their resting position. Forced exhalation (active) additionally contracts the internal intercostal muscles. The abdominal muscles can also contract to push the diaphragm further upwards, increasing the force of exhalation.
Tags: exhalation, forced breathing, diaphragm, ribcage

**Item 8 [S]**
Question: What is the function of the epiglottis?
Answer: The epiglottis is a flap of cartilage at the top of the trachea (windpipe). During swallowing, it closes over the trachea to prevent food from entering the airway (trachea and lungs), directing it to the oesophagus (food pipe) instead.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B6
Explanation: The epiglottis prevents choking — without it, food or drink could enter the respiratory tract. The larynx (voice box) sits at the top of the trachea. When swallowing, the larynx is pulled upwards and the epiglottis folds down to cover the glottis (the opening to the trachea).
Tags: epiglottis, trachea, swallowing, respiratory system

**Item 9 [S]**
Question: State three features of the alveoli that make them efficient for gas exchange.
Answer: (1) Large surface area (about 75 m² in humans) due to many tiny alveoli. (2) Thin walls (only one cell thick) — minimises diffusion distance. (3) Rich blood supply — capillaries maintain steep concentration gradients for O₂ and CO₂.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B6
Explanation: These three features collectively maximise the rate of diffusion. Surface area determines how much gas can be exchanged per unit time. Wall thickness (typically 0.5–1 μm) determines how fast gases can cross. Blood flow removes O₂ and brings CO₂, maintaining concentration gradients. Additional features include the moist surface (gases dissolve in moisture before diffusing) and the short diffusion path.
Tags: alveoli, gas exchange, adaptations, surface area

**Item 10 [S]**
Question: Why does breathing rate increase during exercise?
Answer: During exercise, muscles respire more rapidly to release more energy. This increases oxygen consumption and carbon dioxide production. The increased CO₂ in the blood is detected by chemoreceptors, which send signals to the brain's respiratory centre. This increases breathing rate to: (1) deliver more oxygen to the muscles; (2) remove excess CO₂ faster.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B6
Explanation: Increased respiration rate produces more CO₂, lowering blood pH slightly (more acidic). Chemoreceptors in the carotid bodies and aortic bodies detect this pH change and stimulate the medulla oblongata. The medulla increases both breathing rate and tidal volume to meet metabolic demand.
Tags: breathing rate, exercise, carbon dioxide, control of breathing

**Item 11 [S]**
Question: Compare aerobic and anaerobic respiration in terms of (a) products and (b) energy yield.
Answer: (a) Aerobic: glucose + oxygen → CO₂ + H₂O + energy. Anaerobic (plants/yeast): glucose → ethanol + CO₂ + energy. Anaerobic (animals): glucose → lactic acid + energy. (b) Aerobic: ~38 ATP per glucose (very efficient). Anaerobic: only 2 ATP per glucose (very inefficient).
Difficulty: S
Topic: B6
Explanation: Aerobic respiration uses oxygen as the final electron acceptor in the electron transport chain, allowing complete glucose oxidation. Anaerobic respiration uses an organic molecule as the final acceptor, yielding only partial glucose breakdown. The 38 ATP figure is theoretical — in practice, eukaryotic cells typically produce about 30–32 ATP due to imperfect coupling and membrane leaks.
Tags: aerobic vs anaerobic, energy yield, ATP, comparison

**Item 12 [S]**
Question: What is oxygen debt?
Answer: Oxygen debt is the extra amount of oxygen the body needs after vigorous exercise to break down accumulated lactic acid and restore normal blood oxygen levels.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B6
Explanation: During intense exercise, muscles respire anaerobically, producing lactic acid. This lactic acid accumulates in the muscles and blood. After exercise, continued deep breathing supplies extra oxygen to the liver, where lactic acid is converted back to glucose (via gluconeogenesis) or completely oxidised. The "debt" is the oxygen needed for this recovery process.
Tags: oxygen debt, lactic acid, recovery, exercise physiology

**Item 13 [C]**
Question: A student breathes normally and then breathes as fast and deep as possible for 30 seconds. Predict and explain what happens to the blood pH in each case.
Answer: Normal breathing: Blood pH stays stable (~7.4). Ventilation matches metabolic CO₂ production, maintaining a constant equilibrium. Fast deep breathing: Blood pH rises (becomes more alkaline). Hyperventilation removes CO₂ from the blood faster than it is produced, reducing carbonic acid levels in the blood, raising pH. This can cause dizziness and tingling in the fingers due to vasoconstriction in brain blood vessels.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B6
Explanation: CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻. When CO₂ increases, the equilibrium shifts right, increasing H⁺ ions (lower pH). When CO₂ decreases (hyperventilation), equilibrium shifts left, decreasing H⁺ ions (higher pH). This is the body's most important chemical buffer system. The respiratory centre adjusts breathing to maintain blood pH within narrow limits (7.35–7.45).
Tags: blood pH, hyperventilation, carbonic acid, buffer, respiratory control

**Item 14 [C]**
Question: Design an experiment to compare the rate of respiration in germinating seeds versus dry seeds. Describe the method, variables, and expected results.
Answer: Method: Set up two conical flasks. Flask A: add germinating peas that have been soaked for 24 hours. Flask B: add dry peas of the same mass. Add a manometer (U-tube with coloured liquid) to each flask connected to a flask of potassium hydroxide solution (KOH, which absorbs CO₂). Raise the level of the coloured liquid on one side by adding a syringe. Record how far it drops per unit time as a measure of oxygen uptake.
Variables: Independent — type of seed (germinating vs dry); Dependent — drop in liquid level per minute; Control — temperature, seed mass, apparatus set-up.
Expected: Germinating seeds show a much larger drop (more O₂ absorbed) than dry seeds. This is because germinating seeds are metabolically active and require aerobic respiration; dry seeds are dormant.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B6
Explanation: Respiration rate is measured by O₂ consumption or CO₂ production. KOH absorbs CO₂, so the drop in pressure is due to O₂ consumption. A control with dry seeds shows baseline respiratory demand. Germinating seeds have high metabolic activity — they are synthesising new molecules, growing roots and shoots, and require energy for cell division.
Tags: respiration experiment, germinating seeds, method, variables, rate of respiration

**Item 15 [C]**
Question: Explain the mechanism of gas exchange at the alveolus, including reference to partial pressures and diffusion.
Answer: Alveolar air has higher O₂ partial pressure (~100 mmHg) than blood in pulmonary capillaries (~40 mmHg). O₂ diffuses from alveolus into blood, binding to haemoglobin. Blood in pulmonary capillaries has higher CO₂ partial pressure (~46 mmHg) than alveolar air (~40 mmHg). CO₂ diffuses from blood into the alveolus. The thin alveolar wall (one cell thick) and large surface area (75 m²) ensure rapid diffusion. Blood flow and ventilation maintain these gradients.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B6
Explanation: Diffusion rate is governed by Fick's law: Rate ∝ (Area × Concentration gradient) / Thickness. Alveoli maximise area and concentration gradient, minimise thickness. The partial pressure gradient is the key driving force for gas movement. Haemoglobin in red blood cells increases oxygen carriage ~70-fold compared to plasma alone.
Tags: gas exchange mechanism, partial pressure, diffusion, Fick's law, haemoglobin

**Item 16 [C]**
Question: A person has a lung disease that causes the alveolar walls to become thicker. Explain the effects on gas exchange.
Answer: Thickened alveolar walls increase the diffusion distance for gases. According to Fick's law (Rate ∝ Area × Concentration gradient / Thickness), increasing the diffusion path length reduces the rate of diffusion. This means oxygen uptake and carbon dioxide removal become less efficient. The person would experience breathlessness (dyspnoea) and low blood oxygen levels (hypoxaemia), especially during exercise when demand is high.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B6
Explanation: Conditions like pulmonary fibrosis cause scarring and thickening of alveolar walls. Gas exchange is impaired because: (1) O₂ must diffuse through more tissue; (2) CO₂ removal is also slowed. Blood leaving the lungs would have lower O₂ and higher CO₂ than normal. The body compensates by increasing breathing rate and heart rate. At rest, a moderate increase in wall thickness may not cause symptoms; during exercise, symptoms become apparent.
Tags: gas exchange impairment, diffusion distance, Fick's law, respiratory disease

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: Compare the gas exchange systems in a fish and a human, explaining how each is adapted to its environment.
Answer: Fish: Uses gills — thin lamellae with a large surface area, counter-current blood flow (blood flows opposite to water direction) maximising O₂ uptake. Water has low O₂ (~8 mg/L) compared to air (~210 mg/L), so a highly efficient system is needed. Counter-current exchange can extract up to 80% of dissolved O₂ from water.
Humans: Use lungs — alveoli with thin walls, rich blood supply, large surface area. Air has much higher O₂ (~21%), so less efficient extraction is acceptable. Ventilation (breathing) actively pumps fresh air in and stale air out.
Both: Thin surfaces (one cell thick), large surface area, maintained concentration gradients. Key difference: fish gills require water flow (passive or active) and are optimised for extracting O₂ from a low-concentration medium; human lungs rely on ventilation and handle a high-O₂ medium.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B6
Explanation: The counter-current principle: water flows over the gill lamellae in one direction; blood in the lamellar capillaries flows in the opposite direction. This means blood always meets water with a slightly higher O₂ concentration than the blood, maximising the gradient along the entire length of the lamella. This can extract up to 80% of dissolved O₂, compared to ~50% in a parallel-flow system. Human lungs do not use counter-current flow because air is renewed by ventilation.
Tags: fish gills, human lungs, counter-current flow, adaptation, gas exchange comparison

**Item 18 [C]**
Question: Explain why marathon runners often experience muscle cramps after finishing a race, while a sprinter may experience cramps during the race. Relate your answer to the type of respiration occurring in each case.
Answer: Marathon runners: During the race, aerobic respiration provides most energy, but at very high intensities, some anaerobic respiration occurs, producing lactic acid. After the race, continued deep breathing repays the oxygen debt — oxygen is used in the liver to convert accumulated lactic acid to glucose or to oxidise it completely. The buildup and subsequent breakdown of lactic acid in muscles causes stiffness and cramping.
Sprinters: During a sprint, muscles contract so forcefully that oxygen delivery cannot meet demand, and anaerobic respiration predominates. Lactic acid accumulates rapidly within the muscle itself. Without sufficient oxygen, the muscle cannot clear the lactic acid fast enough, leading to immediate cramping and "burning" sensation during the race.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B6
Explanation: The type of respiration depends on exercise intensity relative to oxygen delivery capacity. Aerobic respiration: sustainable, produces 38 ATP/glucose. Anaerobic respiration: fast, produces only 2 ATP/glucose but does not require oxygen. Lactic acid accumulation causes metabolic acidosis, impairing enzyme function and causing the pain sensation. The oxygen debt repayment period after exercise is called EPOC (Excess Post-exercise Oxygen Consumption).
Tags: marathon, sprint, lactic acid, oxygen debt, aerobic vs anaerobic
