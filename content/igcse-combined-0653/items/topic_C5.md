# Topic C5 — Chemical Reactions
## Items File

**Item 1 [F]**
Question: Name the four main types of chemical reaction.
Answer: (1) Combination (synthesis) — two or more substances combine to form one product. (2) Decomposition — one substance breaks down into two or more products. (3) Displacement — a more reactive element displaces a less reactive element. (4) Combustion — rapid reaction with oxygen, releasing heat and light.
Difficulty: F
Topic: C5
Explanation: These four types cover most chemical reactions. Combination and decomposition are opposites. Displacement involves reactivity series. Combustion is a special type of oxidation.
Tags: types of reactions, combination, decomposition, displacement, combustion

**Item 2 [F]**
Question: What is oxidation?
Answer: Oxidation is the loss of electrons, or the gain of oxygen. In terms of oxygen: when a substance gains oxygen during a reaction.
Difficulty: F
Topic: C5
Explanation: Oxidation always occurs simultaneously with reduction (the other half of a redox reaction). The two processes are coupled — one substance loses electrons (oxidised), another gains them (reduced).
Tags: oxidation, electron transfer, oxygen gain

**Item 3 [F]**
Question: What is reduction?
Answer: Reduction is the gain of electrons, or the loss of oxygen. In terms of oxygen: when a substance loses oxygen during a reaction.
Difficulty: F
Topic: C5
Explanation: OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain (of electrons). Reduction always occurs simultaneously with oxidation.
Tags: reduction, electron transfer, oxygen loss

**Item 4 [F]**
Question: State the reactivity series of metals ( potassium, sodium, calcium, magnesium, aluminium, zinc, iron, copper, silver, gold).
Answer: K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Cu > Ag > Au (most reactive first)
Difficulty: F
Topic: C5
Explanation: Metals higher in the series are more reactive and can displace metals lower in the series from their compounds. Potassium and sodium are so reactive they react violently with water.
Tags: reactivity series, metals, displacement

**Item 5 [S]**
Question: Iron rusts when exposed to air and water. Write the word equation for this reaction.
Answer: Iron + oxygen + water → hydrated iron(III) oxide (rust)
Difficulty: S
Topic: C5
Explanation: Rusting is a slow oxidation reaction. It requires both oxygen and water. Iron forms iron(III) oxide, which is hydrated (contains water molecules in its crystal structure).
Tags: rusting, oxidation, iron, corrosion

**Item 6 [S]**
Question: A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its salt solution. Give an example.
Answer: If zinc is placed in copper(II) sulfate solution: Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu. The zinc (more reactive) displaces copper (less reactive). The blue colour of CuSO₄ fades as copper metal is deposited.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C5
Explanation: In displacement reactions, the more reactive metal loses electrons more readily and reduces the metal ion of the less reactive metal. The more reactive metal dissolves; the less reactive metal is deposited.
Tags: displacement reaction, reactivity series, metal, salt solution

**Item 7 [S]**
Question: What is a decomposition reaction?
Answer: A decomposition reaction is one where a compound breaks down into simpler substances. It can be represented as: AB → A + B.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C5
Explanation: Decomposition often requires energy input (heat, electricity, or light). Thermal decomposition: heating causes a compound to break down (e.g., CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂). Electrolysis: electrical energy decomposes compounds (e.g., water → H₂ + O₂).
Tags: decomposition, thermal decomposition, electrolysis

**Item 8 [S]**
Question: What is thermal decomposition?
Answer: Thermal decomposition is a type of decomposition reaction where a substance breaks down when heated.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C5
Explanation: Example: calcium carbonate → calcium oxide + carbon dioxide when heated strongly. Metal hydroxides and carbonates decompose on heating. The products depend on the reactivity of the metal.
Tags: thermal decomposition, heating, calcium carbonate

**Item 9 [S]**
Question: Give two signs that a chemical reaction has occurred.
Answer: (1) Colour change. (2) Gas being given off (effervescence/bubbling). (3) Precipitate formation (solid forming in a solution). (4) Temperature change (heat given out or absorbed). (5) Change in mass.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C5
Explanation: These observable changes indicate new substances have been formed. Physical changes (melting, boiling) do not involve new substances.
Tags: signs of reaction, chemical change, evidence

**Item 10 [S]**
Question: Explain why unreactivity of gold makes it suitable for jewellery.
Answer: Gold is very low in the reactivity series — it does not react with oxygen, water, or most acids. This means gold jewellery does not tarnish or corrode over time, maintaining its appearance. It is also easy to shape and work with.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C5
Explanation: Gold's unreactivity means it remains chemically unchanged throughout its lifetime. This is why ancient gold artefacts survive for thousands of years.
Tags: gold, reactivity series, corrosion, jewellery

**Item 11 [S]**
Question: What is a combustion reaction?
Answer: Combustion is the rapid reaction of a substance with oxygen, producing heat and light energy. The products are usually carbon dioxide and water (for complete combustion of hydrocarbons).
Difficulty: S
Topic: C5
Explanation: Combustion of methane: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O. The flame provides heat and light energy. Incomplete combustion (limited oxygen) produces carbon monoxide (toxic) and/or carbon (soot).
Tags: combustion, oxidation, burning, energy release

**Item 12 [S]**
Question: Iron is galvanised (coated with zinc). Explain why this protects the iron even if the coating is scratched.
Answer: Zinc is more reactive than iron in the reactivity series. When the coating is scratched, zinc reacts with oxygen and water before iron can. Zinc is oxidised preferentially (sacrificial protection). The iron is only attacked when all the zinc is consumed. Iron would rust first if the coating were tin (less reactive than iron).
Difficulty: S
Topic: C5
Explanation: This is called sacrificial protection. Even when the coating is damaged, the more reactive metal (zinc) continues to protect the iron by corroding instead. Once the zinc is depleted, the iron begins to rust.
Tags: galvanising, sacrificial protection, rusting, reactivity series

**Item 13 [C]**
Question: In the reaction Fe₂O₃ + 2Al → Al₂O₃ + 2Fe: identify which substance is oxidised and which is reduced.
Answer: Iron in Fe₂O₃ is reduced (gains electrons, loses oxygen). Aluminium is oxidised (loses electrons, gains oxygen). Aluminium removes oxygen from iron(III) oxide — this is the thermite reaction.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C5
Explanation: In Fe₂O₃, Fe has +3 oxidation state. In Fe, it has 0 oxidation state (reduction, gain of electrons). In Al₂O₃, Al has +3 oxidation state. In Al, it has 0 oxidation state (oxidation, loss of electrons). The thermite reaction is used for welding railway tracks.
Tags: redox, oxidation, reduction, thermite reaction

**Item 14 [C]**
Question: State the products of complete and incomplete combustion of methane.
Answer: Complete combustion: methane + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water + energy. Incomplete combustion: methane + limited oxygen → carbon monoxide + water + energy (or carbon + water + energy).
Difficulty: C
Topic: C5
Explanation: Complete combustion requires sufficient oxygen: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O. Incomplete combustion (limited O₂): 2CH₄ + 3O₂ → 2CO + 4H₂O. Carbon monoxide (CO) is toxic (binds haemoglobin). Soot (carbon) is unburnt fuel from severely limited oxygen.
Tags: combustion, incomplete combustion, carbon monoxide, methane

**Item 15 [C]**
Question: Electrolysis of molten aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃) produces aluminium. Explain the process.
Answer: In molten Al₂O₃, aluminium ions (Al³⁺) and oxide ions (O²⁻) are free to move. At the cathode (negative): Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al (reduction). At the anode (positive): 2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻ (oxidation). The aluminium collects at the cathode and is tapped off as liquid metal.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C5
Explanation: Electrolysis requires the electrolyte to be molten or in aqueous solution. In Al₂O₃ electrolysis (Hall-Héroult process), cryolite lowers the melting point from 2072°C to ~960°C. Huge amounts of electricity are required. This is why aluminium recycling is so economically and environmentally important.
Tags: electrolysis, aluminium, anode, cathode, Hall-Héroult process

**Item 16 [C]**
Question: Evaluate the use of copper and iron in everyday applications, referring to their reactivity.
Answer: Copper: low in reactivity series (noble). Excellent conductor of electricity, resistant to corrosion, easily shaped. Used for electrical wiring. Iron: higher reactivity. Tends to rust (corrode) unless protected. Used in construction (with protective coatings). Evaluation: Iron is cheaper and stronger but requires protection (paint, galvanising, stainless steel alloying). Copper's corrosion resistance and conductivity make it essential for electrical applications.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C5
Explanation: Iron rusting (Fe → Fe₂O₃) is economically costly — billions spent annually on preventing it. Stainless steel (Fe + Cr + Ni) is iron alloyed to prevent corrosion. Copper roofing lasts centuries because Cu is unreactive. Iron is preferred for structural applications because it is cheaper and stronger.
Tags: reactivity, copper, iron, corrosion, applications, evaluation

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: Explain what happens during the electrolysis of acidified water (dilute sulfuric acid).
Answer: At the cathode (negative): 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂ (hydrogen gas given off). At the anode (positive): 4OH⁻ → O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻ (oxygen gas given off). In dilute acid, the ions present are H⁺ (from acid) and OH⁻ (from water). H⁺ is preferentially discharged at the cathode because it is easier to reduce than SO₄²⁻.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C5
Explanation: Water electrolysis requires energy input. At the cathode, H⁺ ions are discharged preferentially to Na⁺ or other metal ions (which require higher discharge potentials). At the anode, OH⁻ ions are discharged preferentially to SO₄²⁻ or other anions. The volume ratio H₂:O₂ is approximately 2:1, consistent with the formula 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂.
Tags: electrolysis, water, cathode, anode, hydrogen, oxygen

**Item 18 [C]**
Question: A student says: "Aluminium is more reactive than iron, so aluminium should rust faster than iron." Evaluate this statement.
Answer: The statement is INCORRECT in practice. While aluminium is more reactive than iron (higher in the reactivity series), aluminium forms a thin, adherent layer of aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃) on its surface almost instantly when exposed to air. This oxide layer is tightly bonded to the metal and prevents further reaction — it acts as a protective barrier. Iron oxide (rust) is flaky and porous, allowing water and oxygen to continue attacking the underlying iron. Therefore, aluminium appears unreactive despite its higher reactivity. Iron rusts because its oxide layer does not protect the metal. However, under certain conditions (e.g., in saltwater), even aluminium can corrode rapidly.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C5
Explanation: Aluminium oxide forms a dense, adherent layer (passivation) that protects the metal beneath. Iron oxide is bulky, flaky, and non-adherent — it does not form a protective barrier and readily absorbs moisture, so continued oxidation of the underlying iron proceeds. This is why aluminium cans and aircraft (despite being reactive metals) last well in normal conditions. The apparent reactivity differs from actual reactivity because of the difference in oxide layer properties.
Tags: aluminium, iron, reactivity, rusting, passivation, evaluation
