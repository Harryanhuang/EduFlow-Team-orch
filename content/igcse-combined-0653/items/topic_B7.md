# Topic B7 — Transport
## Items File

**Item 1 [F]**
Question: What is the function of the circulatory system?
Answer: The circulatory system transports substances around the body: oxygen and nutrients (from the digestive system and lungs) to all cells, and waste products (CO₂ and urea) from cells to the lungs and kidneys for removal.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B7
Explanation: The circulatory system is the body's transport network. The blood carries dissolved substances: O₂ (bound to haemoglobin in red blood cells), glucose, amino acids, lipids, hormones, waste CO₂ and urea. The heart pumps blood; blood vessels transport it.
Tags: circulatory system, transport, blood, heart

**Item 2 [F]**
Question: Name the three types of blood vessel and state one function of each.
Answer: (1) Arteries — carry blood away from the heart (under high pressure). (2) Veins — carry blood back to the heart (under low pressure). (3) Capillaries — exchange materials between blood and body cells.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B7
Explanation: These three vessel types form the complete circulatory circuit. Arteries have thick muscular walls to withstand high pressure from the pumping heart. Veins have valves to prevent backflow of blood under low pressure. Capillaries are tiny (one cell thick) to allow efficient exchange.
Tags: blood vessels, arteries, veins, capillaries, function

**Item 3 [F]**
Question: What is double circulation?
Answer: In double circulation, blood passes through the heart twice per circuit of the body. Pulmonary circulation: heart → lungs → heart. Systemic circulation: heart → body → heart.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B7
Explanation: Mammals have a double circulatory system. Deoxygenated blood goes from the heart to the lungs, picks up oxygen, and returns to the heart (pulmonary circuit). The heart then pumps this oxygenated blood to the body, where oxygen is delivered, and deoxygenated blood returns to the heart (systemic circuit). This ensures high blood pressure is maintained for systemic circulation.
Tags: double circulation, pulmonary, systemic, heart

**Item 4 [F]**
Question: State two components of blood.
Answer: (1) Plasma — the liquid part of blood, which transports dissolved substances; (2) Red blood cells — carry oxygen from the lungs to body cells.
Difficulty: F
Topic: B7
Explanation: Blood is composed of plasma (~55%) and blood cells (~45%). Plasma is a pale yellow liquid that transports glucose, amino acids, lipids, hormones, waste products (CO₂, urea), and blood cells. Red blood cells transport oxygen bound to haemoglobin. White blood cells fight infection; platelets help with clotting.
Tags: blood, plasma, red blood cells, components

**Item 5 [S]**
Question: Describe the structure of an artery and explain how this relates to its function.
Answer: Arteries have thick walls containing smooth muscle and elastic fibres. The thick walls withstand the high pressure generated when the heart pumps. The elastic fibres allow the artery to stretch and recoil, smoothing out the pulsatile flow. The lumen (inside space) is relatively narrow, maintaining high pressure.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B7
Explanation: The artery wall has three layers: outer connective tissue, thick smooth muscle layer (with elastic fibres), and an inner endothelium. The smooth muscle can contract (vasoconstriction) or relax (vasodilation) to regulate blood flow and pressure. Elastic recoil (Windkessel effect) converts the pulsatile flow from the heart into a smoother flow through the capillaries.
Tags: artery, structure, adaptation, function, blood pressure

**Item 6 [S]**
Question: Describe the structure of a vein and explain how this relates to its function.
Answer: Veins have thin walls with less muscle and elastic fibre than arteries. They contain valves to prevent backflow of blood. The low pressure in veins means thick walls are not needed. Valves ensure blood flows only towards the heart, against gravity.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B7
Explanation: Veins return blood to the heart under low pressure. Their thin walls contain smooth muscle for vasoconstriction/vasodilation. Valves are crucial — without them, blood would pool in the legs due to gravity. Skeletal muscle contractions ("muscle pump") also help push blood through veins towards the heart.
Tags: vein, valves, structure, adaptation, low pressure

**Item 7 [S]**
Question: Explain how water is transported through a plant.
Answer: Water enters root hair cells by osmosis (from the soil, which has higher water potential). It passes through the root cortex via the apoplast or symplast pathway, into the xylem vessels. Cohesion (water molecules attract each other) and adhesion (water sticks to xylem walls) create a continuous column of water. Transpiration pull (suction from evaporation at leaf stomata) pulls the water column up through the xylem.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B7
Explanation: The transpiration stream is driven by evaporation of water from leaf mesophyll cells. This creates negative pressure (tension) in the xylem, pulling water up. Cohesion (water molecules sticking together) maintains the continuity of the water column; adhesion (water sticking to xylem walls) helps counteract gravity. The xylem vessels are dead, hollow tubes that form a continuous pipeline from roots to leaves.
Tags: transpiration, xylem, cohesion, adhesion, water transport

**Item 8 [S]**
Question: What is the function of the coronary arteries?
Answer: Coronary arteries supply oxygen-rich blood to the heart muscle itself (the myocardium). The heart needs a constant supply of oxygen to keep pumping. If these arteries become blocked (e.g., by a blood clot), a heart attack (myocardial infarction) occurs.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B7
Explanation: The coronary arteries branch from the aorta just above the aortic valve. They spread over the surface of the heart, penetrating into the myocardium. Heart muscle cells have high metabolic demands and cannot extract oxygen from blood already inside the heart's chambers, so they need their own blood supply.
Tags: coronary arteries, heart, blood supply, heart attack

**Item 9 [S]**
Question: Describe the role of platelets in blood clotting.
Answer: When a blood vessel is damaged, platelets arrive at the injury site. They release chemicals that initiate the clotting cascade. Platelets aggregate (stick together) to form a temporary platelet plug. They also help activate fibrinogen (a soluble blood protein) into fibrin (insoluble threads). Fibrin threads form a mesh over the wound, trapping red blood cells and forming a clot.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B7
Explanation: Blood clotting involves three stages: (1) Platelet adhesion — platelets stick to the damaged vessel wall. (2) Platelet activation — platelets change shape and release chemicals. (3) Platelet aggregation — platelets stick to each other and to fibrin strands. The clot seals the wound, preventing blood loss and blocking entry of pathogens.
Tags: platelets, blood clotting, fibrin, haemostasis

**Item 10 [S]**
Question: Compare the composition of blood in the pulmonary artery and the aorta.
Answer: The pulmonary artery carries deoxygenated blood (low O₂, high CO₂) from the right ventricle to the lungs. The aorta carries oxygenated blood (high O₂, low CO₂) from the left ventricle to the body.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B7
Explanation: This reflects the pulmonary and systemic circuits. The right side of the heart pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs; the left side pumps oxygenated blood from the lungs to the body. Valves between atria and ventricles (tricuspid and bicuspid) and at the exits of ventricles (pulmonary and aortic valves) ensure one-way flow.
Tags: pulmonary artery, aorta, oxygenated blood, deoxygenated blood

**Item 11 [S]**
Question: Explain why the walls of the left ventricle are thicker than the walls of the right ventricle.
Answer: The left ventricle pumps blood to the entire body (systemic circulation), requiring high pressure to push blood through thousands of kilometres of blood vessels. The right ventricle only pumps blood to the lungs (pulmonary circulation), which are close to the heart and have low resistance. High pressure requires a thicker muscle wall.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B7
Explanation: The left ventricle generates systemic blood pressure (~120 mmHg systolic), which is about 4 times higher than pulmonary pressure (~25 mmHg). Thicker walls generate more force. The right ventricle only needs enough force to overcome the low resistance of the pulmonary circulation.
Tags: left ventricle, right ventricle, muscle thickness, blood pressure

**Item 12 [S]**
Question: Describe the pathway of blood through the human heart, starting from the vena cava and ending at the aorta.
Answer: Vena cava → right atrium → tricuspid valve → right ventricle → pulmonary valve → pulmonary artery → lungs (for oxygenation) → pulmonary veins → left atrium → bicuspid (mitral) valve → left ventricle → aortic valve → aorta → body.
Difficulty: S
Topic: B7
Explanation: This is the complete cardiac cycle. Blood enters the right atrium from the body via the superior and inferior vena cava. It flows into the right ventricle, which pumps it to the lungs. Oxygenated blood returns via pulmonary veins to the left atrium, enters the left ventricle, and is pumped to the body via the aorta. Valves prevent backflow at each stage.
Tags: heart, blood pathway, cardiac cycle, valves

**Item 13 [C]**
Question: A patient has a blocked coronary artery. Explain the consequences and describe how a stent could be used to treat this condition.
Answer: A blocked coronary artery reduces blood flow to the heart muscle. If the blockage is partial, the patient may experience angina (chest pain during exertion) as the heart muscle cannot receive enough oxygen. If the artery is fully blocked, heart muscle cells die — a heart attack. Treatment: A stent is a small metal mesh tube inserted via a catheter through the femoral or radial artery to the blocked coronary artery. The stent is placed at the blockage site and expanded with a balloon, pushing the plaque against the arterial wall and keeping the lumen open. This restores blood flow. Drug-eluting stents slowly release anti-proliferative drugs to prevent restenosis (re-narrowing).
Difficulty: C
Topic: B7
Explanation: Coronary heart disease (CHD) is the leading cause of death globally. Angioplasty + stent insertion (PCI — percutaneous coronary intervention) is the most common treatment. Bare metal stents can cause restenosis (scar tissue regrowth); drug-eluting stents reduce this risk. Bypass surgery (CABG) is used for more extensive blockages.
Tags: coronary artery disease, heart attack, stent, treatment, cardiology

**Item 14 [C]**
Question: Explain the role of cholesterol in cardiovascular disease. Include in your answer: LDL, HDL, and the formation of an atherosclerotic plaque.
Answer: Cholesterol is transported in blood as lipoproteins. LDL (low-density lipoprotein) carries cholesterol from the liver to body cells. High LDL levels cause cholesterol to deposit in artery walls — this is the start of atherosclerosis. HDL (high-density lipoprotein) carries cholesterol back to the liver for excretion ("good cholesterol"). Over time, LDL-cholesterol accumulates beneath the endothelium, triggering an inflammatory response. White blood cells (macrophages) engulf the cholesterol, forming foam cells. These accumulate as a fatty streak → fibrous plaque. The plaque narrows the artery lumen and can rupture, triggering blood clot formation (thrombosis), which can completely block the artery.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B7
Explanation: Atherosclerosis begins in childhood and progresses over decades. Risk factors: high LDL (from saturated fat diet), smoking, hypertension, diabetes, family history. The plaque narrows arteries (reducing blood flow → angina) or ruptures (causing thrombosis → heart attack or stroke). Statins lower LDL cholesterol and have been shown to significantly reduce cardiovascular mortality.
Tags: cholesterol, LDL, HDL, atherosclerosis, cardiovascular disease

**Item 15 [C]**
Question: Explain how the heart's own pacemaker (the sinoatrial node) controls heart rate. Include reference to the autonomic nervous system.
Answer: The sinoatrial (SA) node, located in the right atrium, is the heart's natural pacemaker. It generates electrical impulses at regular intervals (~70 per minute at rest), which spread across both atria, causing coordinated contraction. The impulses then pass through the atrioventricular (AV) node (which briefly delays the signal) to the ventricles, causing ventricular contraction. The autonomic nervous system adjusts heart rate: the sympathetic nervous system releases noradrenaline, increasing heart rate and force of contraction (fight or flight). The parasympathetic system (vagus nerve) releases acetylcholine, decreasing heart rate (rest and digest).
Difficulty: C
Topic: B7
Explanation: The SA node's automaticity comes from its cells' spontaneous depolarisation. Heart rate is continuously adjusted by baroreceptors (detect blood pressure), chemoreceptors (detect O₂/CO₂/pH), and higher brain centres (emotion, exercise). At rest, vagal tone dominates, keeping HR at ~70 bpm. During exercise, sympathetic activation increases HR to 150–200 bpm.
Tags: pacemaker, SA node, autonomic nervous system, heart rate control, cardiac cycle

**Item 16 [C]**
Question: A patient has blood type AB positive. Explain what this means and evaluate the risks of giving this patient blood of other blood types.
Answer: Blood type AB means the red blood cells have both A antigens and B antigens on their surface. The "positive" means the Rhesus D antigen is also present. A person with AB+ blood can receive blood from any type (A, B, AB, O) because they have no antibodies against A or B antigens (unless previously sensitised). However, giving AB+ blood to other types would cause problems: AB+ donor to A recipient would provoke antibodies against B antigens (mild haemolytic reaction); to O recipient, antibodies against both A and B antigens (severe reaction). In emergencies, O-negative blood is the universal donor. AB+ is the universal recipient for red cell transfusion.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B7
Explanation: Blood group antigens are inherited glycoproteins on red blood cell membranes. ABO and Rhesus are the most clinically important blood group systems. Anti-A and anti-B antibodies develop in early childhood against the ABO antigens NOT present on the individual's cells. Rh-negative people can develop anti-D antibodies if exposed to Rh-positive blood (important in pregnancy).
Tags: blood groups, ABO, rhesus, blood transfusion, antigens

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: Compare the transport systems in a mammal and in a plant. In your answer, discuss the mechanisms, substances transported, and limitations of each system.
Answer: Mammals: Closed double circulatory system. The heart pumps blood (containing plasma, red cells, white cells, platelets) through arteries, capillaries, and veins. The system transports O₂, CO₂, nutrients, hormones, waste products, and regulates temperature. Advantages: rapid transport, high pressure, distributes heat; limitations: requires constant energy, blood loss can be fatal.
Plants: Xylem (transports water and mineral ions, driven by transpiration) and phloem (transports sugars and amino acids, driven by pressure flow). Advantages: passive transport (xylem), self-repair (cambium); limitations: slower transport, no dedicated heat distribution, vulnerable to drought and blockages.
Both: Use fluid under pressure, have branching networks, and transport substances throughout the organism. Mammals use a muscular pump (heart); plants rely on evaporation and osmotic pressure. Mammal transport is faster but more energetically costly; plant transport is slower but more energy-efficient.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B7
Explanation: Plant transport vs animal circulatory systems reflect fundamental differences in organism design. Mammals are active, warm-blooded animals with high metabolic rates requiring rapid, pressurised transport. Plants are rooted, relying on evaporation (transpiration) and pressure gradients for transport. The pressure-flow hypothesis explains phloem transport: sucrose is loaded into phloem sieve tubes, water follows by osmosis, generating hydrostatic pressure; unloading at sinks causes mass flow.
Tags: mammal vs plant transport, xylem, phloem, circulatory system, comparison

**Item 18 [C]**
Question: Evaluate the use of artificial pacemakers versus biological approaches to treating heart rhythm disorders. Consider the advantages and limitations of each.
Answer: Artificial pacemakers: Small electronic devices implanted under the skin with leads into the heart chambers. They continuously monitor the heart's rhythm and deliver electrical impulses when needed. Advantages: reliable, immediate, can be programmed to different settings, can respond to activity (rate-responsive pacemakers). Limitations: battery replacement needed every 5–10 years, risk of infection, mechanical failure, patient cannot have MRI with some models.
Biological approaches: Gene therapy (e.g., injecting genes encoding ion channels into the heart to reprogramme cells as pacemakers); cell therapy (transplanting pacemaker-like cells); tissue engineering (creating biological pacemaker tissue). Advantages: no battery, potential for integration with the heart's natural control systems, lower infection risk. Limitations: still experimental, less predictable, difficult to control precisely, long-term stability unknown.
Conclusion: Currently, artificial pacemakers are the standard of care due to reliability. Biological approaches are promising for the future but have not yet matched the precision of electronic devices.
Difficulty: C
Topic: B7
Explanation: Over 1 million pacemakers are implanted worldwide annually. Modern pacemakers are increasingly sophisticated: dual-chamber pacing, rate-adaptive sensors, MRI-conditional devices. Biological pacemakers use viral vectors (e.g., AAV or lentivirus) to deliver genes like HCN4 or TBX18 to create "sinus node-like" cells. Cell therapy uses stem cell-derived pacemaker cells. The field is rapidly advancing.
Tags: artificial pacemaker, gene therapy, biological pacemaker, heart rhythm, treatment evaluation
