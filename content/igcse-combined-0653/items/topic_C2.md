# Topic C2 — Atomic Structure and the Periodic Table
## Items File

**Item 1 [F]**
Question: Describe the structure of an atom.
Answer: An atom consists of a central nucleus (containing protons and neutrons) surrounded by electrons arranged in shells. Protons have a positive charge, electrons have a negative charge, and neutrons are neutral. In a neutral atom, the number of protons equals the number of electrons.
Difficulty: F
Topic: C2
Explanation: The nucleus contains almost all the mass of the atom but occupies a tiny fraction of its volume. Electrons occupy most of the atomic volume but have negligible mass. The number of protons (atomic number) determines the element.
Tags: atomic structure, nucleus, protons, neutrons, electrons

**Item 2 [F]**
Question: What is the relative mass and charge of a proton, neutron, and electron?
Answer: Proton: mass = 1, charge = +1. Neutron: mass = 1, charge = 0. Electron: mass = 1/1836, charge = −1.
Difficulty: F
Topic: C2
Explanation: Protons and neutrons have approximately the same mass (1 atomic mass unit). Electrons are about 1836 times lighter. The charge of a proton (+1) balances the charge of an electron (−1) in a neutral atom.
Tags: subatomic particles, mass, charge, relative mass

**Item 3 [F]**
Question: What is the atomic number and mass number of an atom?
Answer: Atomic number (Z) = number of protons in the nucleus. Mass number (A) = total number of protons + neutrons in the nucleus.
Difficulty: F
Topic: C2
Explanation: In standard notation, mass number is written as a superscript and atomic number as a subscript: ᵃX. For example, ²³Na has 11 protons (atomic number = 11) and 23 − 11 = 12 neutrons.
Tags: atomic number, mass number, isotope, notation

**Item 4 [F]**
Question: What are isotopes?
Answer: Isotopes are atoms of the same element that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons. They have the same atomic number but different mass numbers.
Difficulty: F
Topic: C2
Explanation: Isotopes have identical chemical properties (because chemical behaviour depends on electrons, which are the same) but different physical properties (because mass is different). Example: ¹²C and ¹⁴C are both carbon (6 protons) but have 6 and 8 neutrons respectively.
Tags: isotopes, neutrons, atomic number, mass number

**Item 5 [S]**
Question: Draw the electronic configuration of sodium (Na, atomic number 11) and chlorine (Cl, atomic number 17).
Answer: Sodium (11): 2, 8, 1. Chlorine (17): 2, 8, 7.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C2
Explanation: Electrons fill shells in order: shell 1 (nearest nucleus) holds up to 2 electrons, shell 2 holds up to 8, shell 3 holds up to 18. Elements in the same group have the same number of electrons in their outer shell. Sodium (Group 1) has 1 outer electron; chlorine (Group 7) has 7 outer electrons.
Tags: electronic configuration, shells, electrons, sodium, chlorine

**Item 6 [S]**
Question: What is the pattern of electronic configuration across Period 3 of the periodic table?
Answer: Across Period 3, electrons fill the third shell from Na (2,8,1) to Ar (2,8,8). Each successive element adds one electron to the outer shell: Na(2,8,1), Mg(2,8,2), Al(2,8,3), Si(2,8,4), P(2,8,5), S(2,8,6), Cl(2,8,7), Ar(2,8,8).
Difficulty: S
Topic: C2
Explanation: Period 3 elements all have electron shells 1 and 2 full, with the third shell filling. The number of outer electrons increases from 1 to 8 across the period, which determines chemical properties.
Tags: electronic configuration, Period 3, periodic table, shells

**Item 7 [S]**
Question: Why do elements in the same group of the periodic table have similar chemical properties?
Answer: Elements in the same group have the same number of electrons in their outer shell. Chemical reactions involve losing, gaining, or sharing these outer electrons. Since the number of outer electrons is the same, their chemical behaviour is similar.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C2
Explanation: For example, all Group 1 elements have 1 outer electron and form +1 ions. All Group 7 elements have 7 outer electrons and form −1 ions. The outer electron configuration determines reactivity.
Tags: group, outer electrons, chemical properties, reactivity

**Item 8 [S]**
Question: State the two main features of the periodic table.
Answer: (1) Elements in the same group have the same number of outer electrons and similar chemical properties. (2) Elements in the same period have the same number of electron shells.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C2
Explanation: The periodic table arranges elements by atomic number (increasing protons). This creates recurring patterns: elements with similar properties appear at regular intervals (periodicity). Mendeleev arranged the table by atomic mass, which gave similar properties in the same groups.
Tags: periodic table, groups, periods, periodicity

**Item 9 [S]**
Question: What is a covalent bond?
Answer: A covalent bond is formed when two atoms share electrons to achieve a full outer shell. Each atom contributes one or more electrons to the shared pair.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C2
Explanation: Covalent bonding occurs between non-metal atoms. In a single covalent bond, two electrons are shared. In a double bond, four electrons are shared. The shared electrons count towards the outer shell of both atoms.
Tags: covalent bond, electron sharing, bonding, non-metals

**Item 10 [S]**
Question: What is the difference between an ionic bond and a covalent bond?
Answer: Ionic bond: electrons are transferred from one atom to another, forming positively and negatively charged ions that attract each other. Covalent bond: electrons are shared between atoms, with no electron transfer.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C2
Explanation: Ionic bonding occurs between metals and non-metals (large electronegativity difference). Covalent bonding occurs between non-metals (small electronegativity difference). Ionic compounds form giant lattice structures; covalent compounds form discrete molecules or giant covalent structures.
Tags: ionic bond, covalent bond, electron transfer, comparison

**Item 11 [S]**
Question: How many electrons can the first three electron shells hold?
Answer: Shell 1: up to 2 electrons. Shell 2: up to 8 electrons. Shell 3: up to 8 electrons (for elements up to atomic number 20).
Difficulty: S
Topic: C2
Explanation: Shell 1 holds 2n² = 2 electrons. Shell 2 holds 8 electrons. Shell 3 can technically hold up to 18, but for elements up to calcium (Z=20), the third shell behaves as if it holds 8 (the 18-electron rule is modified by electron sub-shells).
Tags: electron shells, capacity, shell model, electrons

**Item 12 [S]**
Question: Define relative atomic mass (Ar).
Answer: Relative atomic mass (Ar) is the average mass of an element's atoms relative to 1/12 of the mass of a carbon-12 atom.
Difficulty: S
Topic: C2
Explanation: Because most elements exist as mixtures of isotopes, Ar is calculated as a weighted average of all natural isotopes. For example, chlorine has Ar = 35.5 because it is a mixture of ³⁵Cl (75%) and ³⁷Cl (25%): (35×75 + 37×25)/100 = 35.5.
Tags: relative atomic mass, isotopes, average mass, calculation

**Item 13 [C]**
Question: Compare the physical properties of metals and non-metals.
Answer: Metals: shiny (lustrous), good conductors of heat and electricity, malleable (can be hammered into shapes), ductile (can be drawn into wires), high melting and boiling points, dense. Non-metals: dull appearance, poor conductors (insulators), brittle (break when hammered), low melting and boiling points (many are gases at room temperature).
Difficulty: C
Topic: C2
Explanation: The difference arises from bonding and structure. Metals have a giant metallic lattice with delocalised electrons, which explains electrical conductivity and malleability. Non-metals form covalent or ionic structures, with weaker intermolecular forces.
Tags: metals, non-metals, physical properties, comparison

**Item 14 [C]**
Question: Explain the trend in melting points across Period 3 (Na to Ar).
Answer: Na, Mg, Al are metals with high melting points (metallic bonding). Si has a very high melting point (giant covalent structure). P₄, S₈, Cl₂, Ar are simple molecular substances with low melting points (only weak Van der Waals forces between molecules). The peak at Si reflects its giant covalent network.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C2
Explanation: The trend reflects changing bonding types. Na, Mg, Al: metallic bonding (strong). Si: covalent network (very strong). P₄, S₈: simple molecules (weak intermolecular forces, easily broken). Cl₂, Ar: diatomic/monatomic with very weak forces (lowest melting points).
Tags: Period 3, melting point trend, bonding, metallic, covalent, molecular

**Item 15 [C]**
Question: Explain why noble gases are chemically unreactive.
Answer: Noble gases (He, Ne, Ar, etc.) have a full outer shell of electrons. With no unpaired electrons and a stable electron configuration, they have no tendency to lose, gain, or share electrons. Therefore, they do not form chemical bonds readily.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C2
Explanation: Full outer shells are particularly stable. Helium has 2 electrons (full first shell); all others have 8 electrons in the outer shell (full octet). This full outer shell explains why noble gases do not need to react — they are already stable.
Tags: noble gases, unreactive, full outer shell, octet, electronic structure

**Item 16 [C]**
Question: Describe the development of the atomic model from Dalton to Rutherford.
Answer: Dalton (1803): Atoms are solid, indivisible spheres. Thomson (1897): Discovered electrons — atoms must contain negative charges, so proposed the "plum pudding" model (positive sphere with embedded negative electrons). Rutherford (1911): Gold foil experiment showed most alpha particles pass straight through but some deflect at large angles. This proved the positive charge and most mass are concentrated in a tiny nucleus, with electrons orbiting around it — the nuclear model.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C2
Explanation: The gold foil experiment fired alpha particles at thin gold foil. Most passed through, suggesting mostly empty space. Some deflected at large angles, suggesting a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus. Rutherford's student, Bohr, later proposed that electrons orbit in fixed energy levels (shells).
Tags: atomic model, Dalton, Thomson, Rutherford, gold foil experiment, nuclear model

**Item 17 [C]**
Question: The mass spectrum of an element shows peaks at mass numbers 24, 25, and 26 with relative abundances of 76%, 10%, and 14% respectively. Calculate the relative atomic mass.
Answer: Ar = (24×76 + 25×10 + 26×14) / 100 = (1824 + 250 + 364) / 100 = 2438 / 100 = 24.38
Difficulty: C
Topic: C2
Explanation: Relative atomic mass from mass spectrometry is the weighted average of all isotope masses. Each isotope's mass is multiplied by its abundance (as a fraction or percentage), summed, and divided by 100 (for percentage abundances) or 1 (for fractions).
Tags: mass spectrometry, relative atomic mass, isotopes, calculation, weighted average

**Item 18 [C]**
Question: Evaluate the use of carbon-14 dating to determine the age of fossils. Include advantages and limitations.
Answer: Carbon-14 dating measures the proportion of ¹⁴C (radioactive) to ¹²C (stable) in organic material. Living organisms maintain a constant ¹⁴C/¹²C ratio. After death, ¹⁴C decays (half-life = 5730 years). By measuring the remaining ¹⁴C activity, the age of the fossil can be estimated. Advantages: useful for dating organic remains up to ~50,000 years old. Limitations: (1) Only works for organic materials — not metals or rocks; (2) Accuracy decreases beyond ~50,000 years (too little ¹⁴C remains); (3) Assumes the atmospheric ¹⁴C/¹²C ratio has been constant — this is corrected using calibration curves; (4) Contamination can significantly affect results; (5) Requires sophisticated equipment.
Difficulty: C
Topic: C2
Explanation: ¹⁴C is produced in the upper atmosphere by cosmic ray bombardment of nitrogen. It enters the food chain through photosynthesis. The Libby half-life (5568 years) was originally used; modern calibrations use 5730 years. Beyond about 10 half-lives (~57,000 years), the remaining ¹⁴C activity is too low to measure accurately.
Tags: carbon-14 dating, radioactive decay, half-life, radiocarbon, evaluation
