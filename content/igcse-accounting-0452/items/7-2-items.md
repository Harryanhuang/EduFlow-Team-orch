# Items: 7.2 — Capital vs revenue expenditure

### Question Q-7.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the distinction between capital expenditure and revenue expenditure? Why does it matter for financial statements?
**Answer**: Capital expenditure is spending on non-current assets that provides long-term benefits — lasting more than one accounting period. Examples include buying property, machinery, vehicles, or equipment. Revenue expenditure is spending on the day-to-day running of the business — benefits are consumed within the accounting period. Examples include wages, rent, utilities, raw materials, and repairs. The distinction matters because: 1) **Income statement treatment** — revenue expenditure is charged as an expense in the period incurred, reducing profit. Capital expenditure is recorded as a non-current asset and depreciated over its useful life, spreading the cost over multiple periods. 2) **Statement of financial position** — capital expenditure increases non-current assets; revenue expenditure does not appear on the balance sheet (it reduces profit and therefore retained earnings). 3) **Profit measurement** — misclassifying capital as revenue understates profit in the current period (too much expense); misclassifying revenue as capital overstates profit (too little expense).
**Explanation**: The key test is duration of benefit. A common error is thinking capital expenditure only means buying buildings — it includes any expenditure that provides benefits beyond the current period.
**Tags**: capital-expenditure, revenue-expenditure, distinction, foundation

### Question Q-7.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What are the three criteria for classifying expenditure as capital expenditure? Give examples of each.
**Answer**: Expend is capital if it results in: 1) **Acquisition of a non-current asset** — buying a new asset that did not previously exist in the business. Example: purchasing a new delivery van, buying land and buildings, acquiring computer equipment. 2) **Addition (extension) to an existing asset** — adding to or extending an existing asset to increase its capacity. Example: building an extension to a factory, adding a new wing to an office building, installing an additional production line. 3) **Enhancement or improvement to an existing asset** — improving an existing asset so that it produces greater benefits (more output, longer life, better quality). Example: replacing a wooden floor with a concrete floor in a warehouse (increases capacity), upgrading a machine to produce higher quality output, redeveloping a building for a new use. Expenditure that merely maintains or restores an existing asset to its original condition is revenue expenditure, not capital.

**Explanation**: The three criteria are acquisition, addition, and enhancement. A common error is classifying improvements as revenue expenditure — if the expenditure increases the asset's capacity, quality, or useful life, it is capital.
**Tags**: capital-expenditure, criteria, acquisition, addition, enhancement, foundation

### Question Q-7.2-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What types of expenditure are classified as revenue expenditure? Distinguish between repairs, maintenance, and improvements.
**Answer**: Revenue expenditure includes: 1) **Repairs** — restoring an asset to its original working condition after damage or wear. Example: repairing a broken machine part, fixing a damaged roof. 2) **Maintenance** — routine work to keep an asset in good working order. Example: servicing a vehicle, painting a building, replacing worn-out belts on a machine. 3) **Operating costs** — day-to-day running costs. Example: wages, rent, utilities, insurance, raw materials, stationery. 4) **Finance costs** — interest on loans and bank charges. 5) **Selling and distribution costs** — advertising, delivery costs, sales commissions. The key distinction between repairs/maintenance and improvements: repairs and maintenance restore or maintain the asset's existing condition (revenue expenditure); improvements increase the asset's capacity, quality, or useful life beyond its original condition (capital expenditure). For example, repainting a building in its original colour is maintenance (revenue); repainting with a specialized coating that extends the building's life by 10 years could be enhancement (capital).
**Explanation**: The test is whether the expenditure brings the asset back to its original condition (revenue) or takes it beyond its original condition (capital). A common error is classifying all expenditure on non-current assets as capital — the nature of the expenditure, not the asset it relates to, determines the classification.
**Tags**: revenue-expenditure, repairs, maintenance, improvements, standard

### Question Q-7.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How does misclassifying capital expenditure as revenue expenditure (or vice versa) affect the financial statements?
**Answer**: **Capital expenditure classified as revenue expenditure** (expensing a capital item): 1) Current period expenses are overstated → profit is understated. 2) Non-current assets are understated (the asset is not on the balance sheet). 3) Future periods' profit is overstated (because there is no depreciation charge). 4) Total equity is understated (lower profit → lower retained earnings). **Revenue expenditure classified as capital expenditure** (capitalizing an expense): 1) Current period expenses are understated → profit is overstated. 2) Non-current assets are overstated (the expense is recorded as an asset). 3) Future periods' profit is understated (because depreciation is charged on an inflated asset value). 4) Total equity is overstated (higher profit → higher retained earnings). Both errors distort the true financial performance and position of the business. The materiality concept determines whether the error is significant enough to require correction — immaterial errors may be accepted for practicality.
**Explanation**: The direction of the error determines whether profit is overstated or understated. A common error is thinking the total profit over the asset's life is unaffected — it is, but the timing is wrong, which misleads users making decisions based on current period results.
**Tags**: misclassification, financial-statements, overstatement, understatement, standard

### Question Q-7.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Classify the following expenditures and explain why: (a) $500 legal fees for purchasing a building. (b) $3,000 repainting a warehouse. (c) $15,000 installing a new air conditioning system that increases factory output capacity. (d) $200 annual insurance premium for a delivery van. (e) $8,000 replacing the engine of a delivery truck, extending its useful life by 3 years.
**Answer**:
(a) **Capital expenditure** — legal fees directly attributable to the acquisition of a non-current asset are part of the asset's cost. They are necessary to bring the asset to its usable condition.

(b) **Revenue expenditure** — repainting is maintenance that restores the warehouse to its original condition. It does not increase capacity, quality, or useful life.

(c) **Capital expenditure** — the air conditioning system increases factory output capacity (enhancement). It provides benefits beyond the current period by allowing more production.

(d) **Revenue expenditure** — insurance is an operating cost that protects the asset but does not enhance it. Benefits are consumed within the insurance period (one year).

(e) **Capital expenditure** — replacing the engine extends the truck's useful life by 3 years (enhancement). The expenditure provides long-term benefits beyond the original expected life.

**Explanation**: The key test is whether the expenditure provides benefits beyond the current period. Legal fees for asset purchase are capital because they are part of the cost of acquisition. Repairs and maintenance are revenue because they restore, not enhance.

**Tags**: classification, capital-expenditure, revenue-expenditure, application, standard

### Question Q-7.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business spent $50,000 on a new machine, $5,000 on delivery and installation, $3,000 on staff training to operate the machine, and $2,000 on annual maintenance. How should each item be classified and treated?
**Answer**: 1) **Machine cost $50,000** — Capital expenditure. Recorded as a non-current asset at cost and depreciated over its useful life. 2) **Delivery and installation $5,000** — Capital expenditure. These are directly attributable costs necessary to bring the asset to its usable location and condition. Added to the asset cost (total asset cost = $55,000). 3) **Staff training $3,000** — This is debated. Under strict accounting standards, training costs are generally treated as revenue expenditure because the benefit (trained staff) cannot be controlled by the business (staff can leave). However, if the training is essential to bring the machine to its usable condition (e.g. specialized technical training without which the machine cannot operate), some argue it should be capitalized. In IGCSE Accounting, training is typically treated as revenue expenditure. 4) **Annual maintenance $2,000** — Revenue expenditure. Maintenance cost charged as an expense in the income statement for the current period. Total capital expenditure = $55,000 (machine + delivery + installation). Total revenue expenditure = $3,000 (training) + $2,000 (maintenance) = $5,000.
**Explanation**: Directly attributable costs (delivery, installation, legal fees) are part of the asset's cost. A common error is treating only the purchase price as capital — all costs necessary to bring the asset to usable condition are capitalized.

**Tags**: asset-cost, directly-attributable-costs, training, maintenance, standard

### Question Q-7.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business has profit before these items of $100,000. It spent $20,000 on a new machine (capital) but incorrectly charged it as an expense. It also spent $5,000 on building repairs (revenue) but incorrectly capitalized it. Calculate the correct profit and the reported (incorrect) profit. What is the combined effect on the balance sheet?
**Answer**:

**Correct treatment:**
- Profit before items: $100,000
- Machine $20,000 → capital asset (not expensed)
- Repairs $5,000 → revenue expense (deducted)
- Correct profit = $100,000 - $5,000 = $95,000

**Incorrect treatment:**
- Profit before items: $100,000
- Machine $20,000 → incorrectly expensed (deducted)
- Repairs $5,000 → incorrectly capitalized (not expensed)
- Incorrect profit = $100,000 - $20,000 = $80,000

**Effect:**
- Profit is understated by $15,000 ($95,000 correct vs $80,000 reported)
- Non-current assets: the machine ($20,000) is not on the balance sheet, but repairs ($5,000) are incorrectly added. Net effect: non-current assets are understated by $15,000 ($20,000 missing - $5,000 wrongly added).
- Retained earnings: understated by $15,000 (lower profit → lower retained earnings).

The two errors have a combined effect of $15,000 in the same direction — both understate profit and assets. If they had been in opposite directions (e.g. capital correctly treated and revenue correctly expensed), the errors would have offset partially.

**Explanation**: Always analyze each error separately before combining. A common error is treating the two errors as offsetting when they actually reinforce each other (both reduce profit).

**Tags**: misclassification, profit-calculation, balance-sheet, comprehensive, challenge

### Question Q-7.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A property developer spends $200,000 purchasing an old building, $80,000 demolishing it, $500,000 constructing a new office block, and $30,000 on legal fees for the property transfer. How should these costs be classified? What if the developer instead spent $50,000 renovating the existing building to create additional rental units?
**Answer**:

**Scenario 1 — Demolition and rebuild:**
- Purchase of old building $200,000 — Capital expenditure (acquisition of non-current asset)
- Demolition $80,000 — Capital expenditure (necessary cost to prepare the site for the new building, directly attributable to the new asset)
- Construction $500,000 — Capital expenditure (creation of a new non-current asset)
- Legal fees $30,000 — Capital expenditure (directly attributable to the property acquisition)
- Total capitalized cost = $810,000

All costs are capital because they relate to the acquisition and creation of a new asset. The old building's cost is part of the total because it was acquired as part of the development project.

**Scenario 2 — Renovation:**
- Renovation $50,000 to create additional rental units — Capital expenditure (enhancement/addition). The renovation adds new rental units, increasing the building's income-generating capacity beyond its original state. This is not maintenance — it creates additional value.

If the $50,000 had been spent on repainting and repairing the existing building without adding anything new, it would be revenue expenditure (maintenance). The key difference is whether the expenditure creates something new or additional (capital) versus restoring what already exists (revenue).

**Explanation**: Demolition costs are capital when they are part of a plan to create a new asset. A common error is treating demolition as an expense — it is a necessary cost of the new building. Similarly, renovation that adds capacity is capital; renovation that merely restores is revenue.

**Tags**: property-development, demolition, renovation, capital-expenditure, comprehensive, challenge

### Question Q-7.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business reports the following expenditures for the year: purchases of raw materials $120,000; wages $85,000; rent $30,000; purchase of a new computer system $25,000; installation of the computer system $5,000; repair of factory roof damage $12,000; upgrading the production line to increase output by 20% $45,000; advertising $15,000; legal fees for defending a patent infringement lawsuit $8,000; annual software license renewal $3,000. Calculate total capital expenditure and total revenue expenditure. If the business incorrectly classified the production line upgrade as revenue expenditure, how would this affect the reported profit and non-current assets?
**Answer**:

**Classification:**
- Raw materials $120,000 — Revenue (operating cost)
- Wages $85,000 — Revenue (operating cost)
- Rent $30,000 — Revenue (operating cost)
- Computer system $25,000 — Capital (acquisition of non-current asset)
- Installation $5,000 — Capital (directly attributable cost)
- Roof repair $12,000 — Revenue (repair, restores original condition)
- Production line upgrade $45,000 — Capital (enhancement, increases output by 20%)
- Advertising $15,000 — Revenue (operating cost)
- Legal fees (lawsuit defense) $8,000 — Revenue (defending a lawsuit is not acquiring or enhancing an asset; it is a cost of maintaining existing rights)
- Software license renewal $3,000 — Revenue (annual subscription, benefit consumed within one year)

**Totals:**
- Capital expenditure = $25,000 + $5,000 + $45,000 = $75,000
- Revenue expenditure = $120,000 + $85,000 + $30,000 + $12,000 + $15,000 + $8,000 + $3,000 = $273,000

**If upgrade ($45,000) incorrectly classified as revenue:**
- Reported revenue expenditure would be $273,000 + $45,000 = $318,000 (overstated by $45,000)
- Reported capital expenditure would be $75,000 - $45,000 = $30,000 (understated by $45,000)
- Profit would be understated by $45,000 (extra expense charged)
- Non-current assets would be understated by $45,000 (the upgrade is not capitalized)
- Future periods would have overstated profit (no depreciation on the $45,000)

**Explanation**: Always test each item against the three capital criteria (acquisition, addition, enhancement). A common error is treating all legal fees as capital — defending a lawsuit is revenue because it maintains existing rights, not acquires or enhances assets.

**Tags**: comprehensive, classification, capital-expenditure, revenue-expenditure, misclassification, challenge
