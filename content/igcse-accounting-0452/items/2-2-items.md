# Items: 2.2 — Double entry rules

### Question Q-2.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the principle of double entry in accounting?
**Answer**: The double entry principle states that every transaction affects at least two accounts, and the total amount debited must equal the total amount credited. This ensures the accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Capital) always remains in balance.
**Explanation**: Double entry means no transaction is recorded with only one entry. A common error is thinking double entry means "recording the same thing twice" — it means recording both sides of a transaction (what is received and what is given).
**Tags**: double-entry, principle, core

### Question Q-2.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: In a T-account, which side is debit and which side is credit?
**Answer**: The left side of a T-account is the debit (Dr) side and the right side is the credit (Cr) side. This convention is universal in accounting and applies to all T-accounts regardless of the type of account.
**Explanation**: A common error is reversing left and right. A simple way to remember: "debit" is left, "credit" is right — this is the foundation for all journal entries.
**Tags**: t-account, debit, credit

### Question Q-2.2-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Using the DEALER framework, state whether an increase in each of the following is recorded as a debit or a credit: (a) Asset, (b) Liability, (c) Capital, (d) Expense, (e) Revenue.
**Answer**: (a) Asset increase → Debit. (b) Liability increase → Credit. (c) Capital increase → Credit. (d) Expense increase → Debit. (e) Revenue increase → Credit. Conversely, decreases are recorded on the opposite side: asset decrease → Credit, liability decrease → Debit, capital decrease → Debit, expense decrease → Credit, revenue decrease → Debit.
**Explanation**: DEALER: Drawings + Expenses + Assets increase on the debit side; Liabilities + Equity + Revenue increase on the credit side. A common error is recording revenue increase as a debit — revenue increases capital, and capital increases are credits.
**Tags**: dealer-framework, debit-credit, classification

### Question Q-2.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Write the double entry for: a business buys goods for $500 cash.
**Answer**: Debit Purchases $500 (expense increases). Credit Cash $500 (asset decreases). The purchases account records the cost of goods bought for resale, and the cash account records the payment.
**Explanation**: Buying goods for cash increases an expense (purchases → debit) and decreases an asset (cash → credit). A common error is debiting inventory instead of purchases — at the point of purchase, goods are recorded in the purchases account; inventory is adjusted at the end of the period.
**Tags**: journal-entry, cash-purchase, application

### Question Q-2.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Write the double entry for: a business sells goods for $800 on credit to Ahmed.
**Answer**: Debit Ahmed (trade receivables) $800 (asset increases — Ahmed owes the business money). Credit Sales $800 (revenue increases).
**Explanation**: A credit sale creates a receivable (debit) and increases sales revenue (credit). A common error is debiting cash — no cash has been received yet; the customer owes the money.
**Tags**: journal-entry, credit-sale, application

### Question Q-2.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Write the double entry for: the owner withdraws $200 cash for personal use.
**Answer**: Debit Drawings $200 (drawings increase → debit side of DEALER). Credit Cash $200 (asset decreases).
**Explanation**: Drawings reduce the owner's equity and are recorded on the debit side. A common error is treating drawings as an expense — drawings are not an expense; they are a reduction of capital recorded in a separate drawings account.
**Tags**: journal-entry, drawings, application

### Question Q-2.2-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Write the double entry for: a business pays rent of $1,200 by cheque.
**Answer**: Debit Rent expense $1,200 (expense increases). Credit Bank $1,200 (asset decreases — bank balance is an asset).
**Explanation**: Payment by cheque reduces the bank balance (an asset → credit) and records the rent as an expense (→ debit). A common error is crediting "cash" instead of "bank" — a cheque payment affects the bank account, not the cash-in-hand account.
**Tags**: journal-entry, rent, bank-payment

### Question Q-2.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business buys a machine for $3,000, paying $1,000 by cheque and agreeing to pay the remaining $2,000 on credit. Write the double entry.
**Answer**: Debit Machinery (non-current asset) $3,000 (asset increases). Credit Bank $1,000 (asset decreases). Credit Creditor for machinery / Payables $2,000 (liability increases). This is a compound entry with one debit and two credits. Total debits ($3,000) = total credits ($1,000 + $2,000).
**Explanation**: Compound entries affect more than two accounts but must still balance. A common error is recording only the cheque payment and ignoring the credit portion, or recording the full $3,000 as an expense rather than an asset.
**Tags**: compound-entry, application, multi-account

### Question Q-2.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: "A business receives $600 cash from a credit customer. The accountant debits Sales and credits Cash." Is this correct? If not, explain the error and write the correct entry.
**Answer**: Incorrect. When a credit customer pays, the business receives cash (asset increases → debit Cash) and the customer's debt is reduced (asset decreases → credit Trade receivables / the customer's account). The correct entry is: Debit Cash $600, Credit Trade receivables $600. The error: debiting Sales would record a second sale that never occurred, and crediting Cash would record money going out when it actually came in. The accountant has reversed both sides and used the wrong accounts.
**Explanation**: Receiving cash from a debtor affects cash and receivables, not sales. Sales were already recorded when the original credit sale was made. A common error is re-recording sales when payment is received — the sale was recorded at the time of the original credit transaction.
**Tags**: error-identification, correction, application
