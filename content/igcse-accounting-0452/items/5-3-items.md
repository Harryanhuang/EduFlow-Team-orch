# Items: 5.3 — Statement of financial position

### Question Q-5.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a statement of financial position (SOFP)? Explain the structure and the accounting equation it is based on.
**Answer**: A statement of financial position (SOFP, also called a balance sheet) shows the financial position of a business AT a specific point in time (e.g., "as at 31 December 20X4"). It is based on the accounting equation: Assets = Capital (equity) + Liabilities.
Structure:
Non-current assets: Assets held for more than one year (property, vehicles, equipment) shown at carrying amount (cost less accumulated depreciation).
Current assets: Assets expected to be converted to cash within one year (inventory, receivables, prepayments, cash/bank).
Less current liabilities: Amounts owed within one year (payables, accruals, bank overdraft).
= Net current assets (working capital): Current assets minus current liabilities.
= Total assets less current liabilities: Non-current assets + net current assets.
Less non-current liabilities: Long-term loans (repayable after more than one year).
= Net assets: Total assets minus all liabilities.
Capital (equity): Opening capital + Net profit - Drawings. Net assets MUST equal capital.
**Explanation**: A common error is confusing the SOFP with the income statement — the SOFP shows financial position AT a date (a snapshot), while the income statement shows performance OVER a period. The SOFP always balances: net assets = capital.
**Tags**: SOFP, structure, accounting-equation

### Question Q-5.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: How are non-current assets presented in the statement of financial position? Show the presentation for: vehicles at cost $80,000, accumulated depreciation $32,000; equipment at cost $20,000, accumulated depreciation $6,000.
**Answer**: Non-current assets in the SOFP are shown at carrying amount (net book value). The format shows cost, accumulated depreciation, and carrying amount:

Non-current assets:
               Cost      Accum. dep    Carrying amount
  Vehicles     $80,000      $32,000       $48,000
  Equipment    $20,000       $6,000       $14,000
               $100,000     $38,000       $62,000

The total carrying amount of $62,000 appears in the SOFP. The cost and accumulated depreciation columns are shown for information — the key figure that enters the totals is the carrying amount.
**Explanation**: A common error is showing the cost as the SOFP figure — the SOFP figure is the carrying amount (cost minus accumulated depreciation). Accumulated depreciation is a contra-asset account that reduces the asset's book value but does not appear as a liability.
**Tags**: non-current-assets, carrying-amount, SOFP-presentation

### Question Q-5.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What items are included in current assets? A business has: inventory $15,000; receivables $25,000; allowance for receivables $1,250; prepayments $3,000; cash in hand $500; bank $4,200. Show the current assets section of the SOFP.
**Answer**: Current assets are assets expected to be realised (converted to cash) or consumed within one year. They are typically listed in order of liquidity (how quickly they can be converted to cash).

Current assets:
  Inventory: $15,000
  Receivables (net of allowance $1,250): $23,750
  Prepayments: $3,000
  Cash in hand: $500
  Bank: $4,200
  Total current assets: $46,450

Note: Receivables are shown NET of the allowance for receivables. The allowance is a contra-asset — it reduces receivables but is not a separate liability. Prepayments are current assets because they represent amounts paid in advance that will be consumed within the next year.
**Explanation**: A common error is showing receivables at their gross amount ($25,000) without deducting the allowance — the SOFP figure must be net receivables ($23,750). Another error is including non-current assets (e.g., vehicles) in current assets — only assets expected to be converted to cash within 12 months are current.
**Tags**: current-assets, net-receivables, SOFP-presentation

### Question Q-5.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What items are included in current liabilities? A business has: payables $18,000; accruals $2,500; bank overdraft $3,000. Calculate net current assets (working capital) if total current assets are $46,450.
**Answer**: Current liabilities are amounts owed by the business that must be paid within one year.

Current liabilities:
  Payables: $18,000
  Accruals: $2,500
  Bank overdraft: $3,000
  Total current liabilities: $23,500

Net current assets (working capital) = Current assets - Current liabilities = $46,450 - $23,500 = $22,950.

Working capital represents the amount of short-term assets available after settling all short-term obligations. A positive working capital means the business can meet its short-term debts. Bank overdraft is a current liability (not a negative current asset) — it is repayable on demand.
**Explanation**: A common error is not including the bank overdraft in current liabilities — an overdraft is a current liability because it is repayable on demand. Another error is subtracting current assets from current liabilities (giving a negative) instead of current assets minus current liabilities. Working capital = current assets - current liabilities.
**Tags**: current-liabilities, working-capital, SOFP-presentation

### Question Q-5.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is working capital? Why is it important? A business has current assets of $80,000 and current liabilities of $55,000. Calculate working capital. What happens to working capital if the business pays $10,000 to a payable?
**Answer**: Working capital (net current assets) = Current assets - Current liabilities. It measures the short-term liquidity of a business — its ability to meet short-term obligations as they fall due.
Working capital = $80,000 - $55,000 = $25,000.
If the business pays $10,000 to a payable: Current assets (bank) decrease by $10,000 to $70,000. Current liabilities (payables) decrease by $10,000 to $45,000. New working capital = $70,000 - $45,000 = $25,000. Working capital is UNCHANGED because both current assets and current liabilities decreased by the same amount.
**Explanation**: A common error is thinking that paying a payable reduces working capital — it does not, because both sides decrease equally. Working capital only changes when: (1) a non-current asset is bought/sold for cash; (2) profit/loss is made; (3) capital is introduced/withdrawn; or (4) a non-current liability is taken on/repaid.
**Tags**: working-capital, liquidity, analysis

### Question Q-5.3-06
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: How is equity (capital) calculated in the statement of financial position? A sole trader has: opening capital $50,000; net profit for the year $28,000; drawings $12,000. Show the capital section and verify that net assets equal capital.
**Answer**: Capital (owner's equity) for a sole trader:
Opening capital: $50,000
Add net profit for the year: $28,000
Less drawings: ($12,000)
Closing capital: $66,000

The net profit from the income statement is added to capital because it belongs to the owner. Drawings are deducted because the owner has withdrawn funds for personal use.
The SOFP must balance: total net assets = closing capital. If net assets are calculated as $66,000, this equals the closing capital of $66,000 — the SOFP balances.
**Explanation**: A common error is deducting drawings before adding net profit — the order is: opening capital + profit - drawings = closing capital. Another error is treating drawings as an expense in the income statement — drawings are NOT an expense; they are a reduction in capital shown only in the SOFP.
**Tags**: capital, equity, drawings

### Question Q-5.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Distinguish between a bank loan (long-term) and a bank overdraft in the statement of financial position. A business has: a bank loan of $40,000 repayable in 5 years; a bank overdraft of $5,000. Show how each appears in the SOFP.
**Answer**: A bank loan is a non-current liability (long-term liability) because it is repayable after more than one year. It appears in the "non-current liabilities" section.
A bank overdraft is a current liability because it is repayable on demand (within one year). It appears in the "current liabilities" section.

SOFP extract:
  Non-current liabilities:
    Bank loan (repayable in 5 years): $40,000
  Current liabilities:
    Bank overdraft: $5,000

Key difference: The bank loan represents borrowed funds with a fixed repayment schedule (long-term financing). The bank overdraft represents a temporary negative bank balance used for short-term cash management. In exams, a loan described as "repayable in X years" (where X > 1) is always non-current. The portion of a long-term loan due within 12 months may be shown as current — but IGCSE typically treats the full amount as non-current unless stated otherwise.
**Explanation**: A common error is putting both the loan and overdraft under current liabilities — only the overdraft is current. The loan is classified based on when it is repayable, not when it was taken. "Repayable in 5 years" means non-current.
**Tags**: long-term-liabilities, overdraft, classification

### Question Q-5.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Prepare the statement of financial position as at 31 December 20X4 from the following: Non-current assets: Property cost $120,000 (no depreciation); Vehicles cost $60,000, accumulated depreciation $24,000; Equipment cost $30,000, accumulated depreciation $9,000. Current assets: Inventory $18,000; Receivables $22,000 less allowance $1,100; Prepayments $2,500; Bank $6,800. Current liabilities: Payables $15,000; Accruals $3,000; Bank overdraft $2,000. Non-current liabilities: Bank loan $30,000. Capital at 1 Jan 20X4: $130,200. Drawings: $18,000. Calculate net profit.
**Answer**:
Statement of financial position as at 31 December 20X4:

Non-current assets:
  Property: $120,000
  Vehicles ($60,000 - $24,000): $36,000
  Equipment ($30,000 - $9,000): $21,000
  Total non-current assets: $177,000

Current assets:
  Inventory: $18,000
  Receivables (net $22,000 - $1,100): $20,900
  Prepayments: $2,500
  Bank: $6,800
  Total current assets: $48,200

Current liabilities:
  Payables: $15,000
  Accruals: $3,000
  Bank overdraft: $2,000
  Total current liabilities: ($20,000)
  Net current assets: $28,200

Total assets less current liabilities: $177,000 + $28,200 = $205,200
Less non-current liabilities: Bank loan ($30,000)
Net assets: $175,200

Capital:
  Opening capital: $130,200
  Add net profit (balancing figure): $63,000
  Less drawings: ($18,000)
  Closing capital: $175,200

Net profit = Net assets - Opening capital + Drawings = $175,200 - $130,200 + $18,000 = $63,000.
Verification: $130,200 + $63,000 - $18,000 = $175,200. ✓
**Explanation**: In an exam question where net profit is not given directly, it can be derived as the balancing figure: Net profit = Net assets - Opening capital + Drawings. Always verify: opening capital + profit - drawings = closing capital = net assets.
**Tags**: comprehensive, SOFP-preparation, balancing-figure

### Question Q-5.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business provides the following as at 31 December 20X4. Non-current assets: Land and buildings cost $200,000; Motor vehicles cost $50,000, accumulated depreciation $20,000; Fixtures cost $15,000, accumulated depreciation $3,000. Current items: Inventory $12,000; Receivables $18,000 less allowance $900; Prepayments $1,800; Payables $10,000; Accruals $2,200; Bank $4,500. Non-current liability: 10% bank loan $25,000. Capital at 1 Jan 20X4: $160,000; Drawings $20,000. Prepare the SOFP and calculate the net profit for the year.
**Answer**:
Statement of financial position as at 31 December 20X4:

Non-current assets:
  Land and buildings: $200,000
  Motor vehicles ($50,000 - $20,000): $30,000
  Fixtures ($15,000 - $3,000): $12,000
  Total non-current assets: $242,000

Current assets:
  Inventory: $12,000
  Receivables (net $18,000 - $900): $17,100
  Prepayments: $1,800
  Bank: $4,500
  Total current assets: $35,400

Current liabilities:
  Payables: $10,000
  Accruals: $2,200
  Total current liabilities: ($12,200)
  Net current assets: $23,200

Total assets less current liabilities: $242,000 + $23,200 = $265,200
Less non-current liabilities: Bank loan ($25,000)
Net assets: $240,200

Capital:
  Opening capital: $160,000
  Add net profit (balancing figure): $100,200
  Less drawings: ($20,000)
  Closing capital: $240,200

Net profit = $240,200 - $160,000 + $20,000 = $100,200.

Verification: $160,000 + $100,200 - $20,000 = $240,200 = net assets. ✓
**Explanation**: The SOFP must balance — net assets equal closing capital. When capital is given at the start of the year and net profit is not provided, net profit is the balancing figure: Net profit = Net assets - Opening capital + Drawings. Key points: (1) Land and buildings typically have no depreciation. (2) Receivables shown net of allowance. (3) Bank loan is non-current (separate from current liabilities). (4) Bank overdraft would be current, but here the bank has a positive balance ($4,500) so it's a current asset.
**Tags**: comprehensive, SOFP-preparation, capital-calculation
