# Items: 6.4 — Ratio interpretation and limitations

### Question Q-6.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is ratio analysis? What are its main purposes in business?
**Answer**: Ratio analysis is the process of calculating and interpreting relationships between financial statement figures to assess a business's performance, financial position, and trends. Its main purposes are: 1) **Performance evaluation** — assessing profitability, liquidity, and efficiency over time (trend analysis). 2) **Comparison** — comparing the business against competitors (inter-firm comparison) or industry benchmarks. 3) **Planning and budgeting** — using ratios to set targets and forecast future performance. 4) **Decision-making** — helping investors decide whether to invest, lenders decide whether to lend, and managers identify areas for improvement. 5) **Control** — monitoring whether the business is meeting its targets and identifying problems early. Ratio analysis converts raw financial data into meaningful indicators that are easier to understand and compare than absolute figures.
**Explanation**: Ratios make numbers comparable. A common error is thinking ratios tell you everything — they are a starting point for investigation, not a conclusion. Ratios show what has happened, not why it happened.
**Tags**: ratio-analysis, purpose, foundation

### Question Q-6.4-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What are the main limitations of ratio analysis?
**Answer**: Key limitations include: 1) **Historical data** — ratios are based on past financial statements and may not reflect future performance. 2) **Different accounting policies** — businesses may use different methods (e.g. FIFO vs AVCO for inventory, different depreciation methods), making comparisons misleading. 3) **Window dressing** — businesses may manipulate year-end figures to present a better picture (e.g. delaying purchases to reduce payables, collecting receivables early). 4) **Industry differences** — comparing ratios across different industries is meaningless because benchmarks vary dramatically. 5) **Single-point data** — balance sheet figures are a snapshot at one date and may not be representative of the year (e.g. seasonal businesses). 6) **Non-financial factors ignored** — ratios do not capture management quality, employee morale, brand reputation, or market conditions. 7) **Inflation effects** — historical cost accounting distorts ratios during periods of high inflation. 8) **Qualitative context missing** — ratios show numbers but not the reasons behind them.
**Explanation**: Ratios are a tool, not an answer. A common error in exam answers is listing limitations without explaining how each affects interpretation. Always link the limitation to its impact on decision-making.
**Tags**: ratio-analysis, limitations, foundation

### Question Q-6.4-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is window dressing in financial statements? How can it be identified through ratio analysis?
**Answer**: Window dressing refers to actions taken by management to make financial statements look better than the underlying reality, typically at year end. Common techniques and how to spot them: 1) **Delaying purchases of inventory** near year end — reduces payables and current liabilities, artificially improving the current ratio. Check: compare year-end payables with monthly averages; a significant year-end drop is suspicious. 2) **Aggressive receivables collection** before year end — reduces receivables days. Check: compare year-end receivables with prior months. 3) **Repaying short-term loans** just before year end — reduces gearing and improves liquidity ratios, then re-borrowing after year end. Check: compare year-end borrowings with subsequent months. 4) **Selling assets and leasing back** near year end — improves asset turnover and ROCE. Check: look for one-off gains in the income statement. 5) **Capitalizing expenses** that should be expensed — improves profit margins. Check: compare capitalization policy with prior years. Window dressing is harder to detect from year-end figures alone — comparison with monthly or quarterly data and notes to the accounts is needed.
**Explanation**: Window dressing is legal but misleading. A common error is confusing window dressing with fraud — window dressing uses legal accounting choices to present a favorable picture; fraud involves illegal misstatement.
**Tags**: window-dressing, financial-statements, identification, standard

### Question Q-6.4-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why is comparing ratios across different industries misleading? Give examples.
**Answer**: Comparing ratios across industries is misleading because business models, operating cycles, and capital structures differ fundamentally. Examples: 1) **Gross profit margin**: A luxury goods retailer may have a 60% margin, while a supermarket has 5-10%. The supermarket compensates with much higher inventory turnover. Comparing the two margins directly would wrongly suggest the luxury retailer is 'more profitable'. 2) **Current ratio**: A supermarket may have a current ratio below 1:1 because it sells inventory quickly and delays paying suppliers (negative working capital cycle). A manufacturing business may need 2:1 because inventory takes months to produce and sell. 3) **Asset turnover**: A service business (e.g. consultancy) may have very high asset turnover (10+ times) because it needs few physical assets. A capital-intensive business (e.g. airline) may have low asset turnover (0.5-1 times). 4) **Gearing**: A utility company may safely operate at 70% gearing because of stable cash flows, while a technology startup at 30% gearing may be risky due to uncertain revenue. Meaningful comparisons require comparing businesses in the same industry, of similar size, and in similar market conditions.
**Explanation**: Always compare like with like. A common error is ranking businesses across industries by a single ratio without understanding the business model context.
**Tags**: industry-comparison, ratio-analysis, standard

### Question Q-6.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business has improving profitability ratios (gross margin 35% to 40%, net margin 12% to 18%) but deteriorating liquidity ratios (current ratio 2.1:1 to 1.2:1, acid test 1.3:1 to 0.6:1). What could explain this pattern? What are the risks?
**Answer**: This pattern suggests the business is prioritizing profitability at the expense of liquidity. Possible explanations: 1) **Reducing inventory levels aggressively** to cut holding costs — this improves cost efficiency (better gross margin) but reduces current assets, worsening the current ratio and acid test. 2) **Tightening credit terms** to improve margins (fewer discounts offered) but this may reduce sales volume and cash inflows. 3) **Using cash to pay down long-term debt** — improves profitability (less interest expense, better net margin) but reduces cash balances, worsening liquidity. 4) **Paying suppliers more quickly** to secure discounts — improves gross margin but reduces cash. 5) **Investing heavily in non-current assets** — may improve future profitability but ties up cash now. Risks: 1) The business may face cash flow difficulties despite appearing profitable — "profit rich, cash poor". 2) It may struggle to pay suppliers on time if the acid test drops below 0.6:1. 3) It may miss opportunities due to lack of liquid funds. 4) Over-reliance on short-term borrowing to fund operations increases financial risk.
**Explanation**: Profitability and liquidity can move in opposite directions. A common error is assuming improving profitability means the business is doing well overall. Always check both dimensions.
**Tags**: profitability-vs-liquidity, ratio-interpretation, analysis, standard

### Question Q-6.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What non-financial factors should be considered alongside ratio analysis when assessing a business's performance?
**Answer**: Key non-financial factors include: 1) **Management quality and experience** — skilled managers can turn around poor ratios; poor managers can ruin good ratios. 2) **Employee morale and skills** — affects productivity, quality, and customer service. 3) **Brand reputation and customer loyalty** — a strong brand can command premium pricing even with moderate ratios. 4) **Market conditions and competition** — a declining market may make even 'good' ratios concerning; a growing market may mask poor performance. 5) **Technological change** — a business with good current ratios may be vulnerable to disruptive technology. 6) **Environmental and social factors** — regulatory compliance, sustainability practices, and social responsibility affect long-term viability. 7) **Supplier and customer relationships** — strong relationships provide stability that ratios alone cannot show. 8) **Legal and regulatory environment** — changes in laws or regulations may significantly impact future performance. 9) **Product quality and innovation pipeline** — affects future revenue growth. 10) **Economic conditions** — inflation, interest rates, and exchange rates affect ratio interpretation.
**Explanation**: Ratios are quantitative; these factors are qualitative. A common error is relying solely on ratios for investment or lending decisions. The best assessments combine both.
**Tags**: non-financial-factors, ratio-analysis, evaluation, standard

### Question Q-6.4-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Two businesses in the same industry have: Business A — gross margin 45%, net margin 8%, current ratio 0.9:1, ROCE 25%. Business B — gross margin 35%, net margin 15%, current ratio 2.2:1, ROCE 12%. Interpret the differences and suggest what each business might be doing differently.
**Answer**: Both businesses have different strategies. **Business A** has a higher gross margin (45% vs 35%) but lower net margin (8% vs 15%), indicating that A's operating expenses consume 37% of revenue (45% - 8%) while B's consume only 20% (35% - 15%). A likely invests heavily in marketing, staffing, or other overheads — possibly pursuing a premium/differentiation strategy. A's low current ratio (0.9:1) suggests tight liquidity — possibly running a lean operation with minimal working capital. Despite this, A's ROCE of 25% is outstanding, suggesting A generates strong returns from its capital base. **Business B** has a lower gross margin but much better cost control (net margin 15%), a healthy current ratio (2.2:1), and a respectable ROCE of 12%. B likely pursues a cost-leadership strategy — lower margins but better expense management and stronger liquidity. A's strategy: high gross margin, high operating costs, lean liquidity, high returns. B's strategy: moderate gross margin, tight cost control, strong liquidity, moderate returns. Neither is inherently better — A carries more liquidity risk for higher returns; B is more conservative but generates lower ROCE. An investor seeking returns might prefer A; a lender concerned about repayment might prefer B.
**Explanation**: Always look at the gap between gross and net margins to understand operating expense levels. A common error is concluding A is 'better' because of higher ROCE without noting the liquidity risk.
**Tags**: comparison, strategy, ratio-interpretation, challenge

### Question Q-6.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A bank manager is assessing a loan application from a small manufacturing business. The business's ratios are: current ratio 1.4:1, acid test 0.8:1, gross margin 32%, net margin 9%, ROCE 15%, gearing 45%. The economy is entering a recession. What additional information should the bank request, and what concerns should be raised?
**Answer**: In a recession context, the bank should be concerned about: 1) **Liquidity** — current ratio of 1.4:1 is below the 1.5:1 benchmark, and acid test of 0.8:1 means the business cannot cover current liabilities without selling inventory. In a recession, inventory may become harder to sell, further weakening liquidity. 2) **Revenue risk** — recession typically reduces demand for manufacturing output. The bank should request sales order books, customer concentration data, and revenue forecasts. 3) **Gearing at 45%** — moderate but concerning in a recession. The bank should request details of existing loan terms, repayment schedules, and whether existing loans have variable rates. 4) **Cash flow projections** — the bank should request 12-month cash flow forecasts under recession scenarios (e.g. 20% revenue decline). 5) **Security/collateral** — what assets are available as security and their realizable value in a recession. 6) **Customer base** — whether customers are in recession-resistant sectors. 7) **Cost structure** — how much of the cost base is fixed vs variable (fixed costs are harder to reduce in a downturn). The bank should also request: management's recession survival plan, contingency funding arrangements, and sensitivity analysis showing the business's position under various downturn scenarios.
**Explanation**: Ratios are a starting point; the recession context changes everything. A common error is assessing ratios without considering the economic environment — a 1.4:1 current ratio might be acceptable in normal times but risky in a recession.
**Tags**: lending-assessment, recession, ratio-interpretation, risk-analysis, challenge

### Question Q-6.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business's ratios over three years: Gross margin: Y1 38%, Y2 36%, Y3 34%. Net margin: Y1 14%, Y2 12%, Y3 11%. Current ratio: Y1 1.8:1, Y2 1.5:1, Y3 1.3:1. ROCE: Y1 22%, Y2 18%, Y3 14%. Inventory turnover: Y1 7 times, Y2 6 times, Y3 5 times. Receivables days: Y1 38, Y2 42, Y3 48. Provide a comprehensive assessment and recommend actions.
**Answer**:

**Assessment:** All ratios are deteriorating consistently over three years, indicating a business in decline across all dimensions:

1) **Profitability**: Gross margin fell 4 percentage points (38% to 34%) — cost of sales is growing faster than revenue, suggesting rising input costs, price pressure, or a shift to lower-margin products. Net margin fell 3 percentage points (14% to 11%) — operating expenses are also growing faster than revenue, compounding the gross margin problem.

2) **Liquidity**: Current ratio fell from 1.8:1 to 1.3:1 and acid test is likely falling too (below 1:1 by Y3). The business is becoming less able to meet short-term obligations.

3) **Efficiency**: Inventory turnover fell from 7 to 5 times (52 to 73 days) — stock is moving much more slowly. Receivables days increased from 38 to 48 — credit control is weakening. Both trends tie up cash.

4) **Returns**: ROCE fell from 22% to 14% — the business is generating significantly less return on its capital. At 14%, it may still exceed borrowing costs, but the trajectory is concerning.

**Overall diagnosis**: The business faces a combination of pricing pressure (falling gross margin), operational inefficiency (rising expenses, slower inventory and receivables), and deteriorating financial health (falling liquidity and returns). This is not a single-issue problem — it requires a comprehensive response.

**Recommended actions**: 1) **Pricing review** — assess whether prices can be increased without losing volume, or whether product mix should shift to higher-margin items. 2) **Cost of sales analysis** — negotiate with suppliers, seek alternative sources, or improve production efficiency. 3) **Operating expense audit** — identify and eliminate unnecessary costs; aim to reduce expenses as a percentage of revenue. 4) **Inventory management** — review stock levels, discontinue slow-moving products, implement just-in-time ordering. 5) **Credit control** — tighten credit terms, improve collection procedures, consider factoring. 6) **Cash flow management** — prepare detailed cash flow forecasts and identify contingency funding. 7) **Strategic review** — consider whether the business model needs fundamental change if market conditions have shifted permanently.

**Explanation**: When all ratios deteriorate together, the problem is systemic, not isolated. A common error is focusing on one ratio (e.g. "we need to improve receivables") when the business needs a comprehensive turnaround plan.
**Tags**: comprehensive, ratio-interpretation, trend-analysis, recommendations, challenge
