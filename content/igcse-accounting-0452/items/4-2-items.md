# Items: 4.2 — Depreciation reducing balance method

### Question Q-4.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the reducing balance method of depreciation? How does it differ from the straight-line method?
**Answer**: The reducing balance method (also called the diminishing balance method) calculates depreciation by applying a fixed percentage rate to the carrying amount (net book value) of the asset at the beginning of each period. Each year's depreciation = Carrying amount × Depreciation rate. The key difference from straight-line is that reducing balance charges MORE depreciation in the early years of an asset's life and LESS in later years, because the carrying amount reduces each year after depreciation is deducted. Straight-line charges the SAME amount every year. Under reducing balance, the carrying amount never reaches the residual value exactly — it approaches it asymptotically.
**Explanation**: A common error is thinking the reducing balance rate is applied to the original cost each year — it is applied to the carrying amount (cost minus accumulated depreciation) at the START of each period. The cost is only used in year 1; in subsequent years, the reduced balance is the base.
**Tags**: reducing-balance, definition, comparison

### Question Q-4.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the formula for reducing balance depreciation? A vehicle costs $50,000. The business uses a reducing balance rate of 30% per annum. Calculate the depreciation charge for year 1.
**Answer**: Reducing balance formula: Depreciation charge = Carrying amount at start of year × Depreciation rate. For year 1, carrying amount at start = cost. Year 1 depreciation = $50,000 × 30% = $15,000. Carrying amount at end of year 1 = $50,000 - $15,000 = $35,000. In year 2, depreciation = $35,000 × 30% = $10,500 (not $50,000 × 30% — the base is the REDUCED amount). Carrying amount at end of year 2 = $35,000 - $10,500 = $24,500. In year 3, depreciation = $24,500 × 30% = $7,350. Carrying amount at end of year 3 = $24,500 - $7,350 = $17,150.
**Explanation**: A common error is applying the rate to the original cost every year — that would produce the same charge as straight-line, defeating the purpose of reducing balance. The entire point is that the base (carrying amount) shrinks each year, so the depreciation charge also shrinks.
**Tags**: reducing-balance, formula, calculation

### Question Q-4.2-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A machine costs $80,000. Depreciation is charged at 25% per annum using the reducing balance method. Show the carrying amount and accumulated depreciation at the end of each of the first 4 years.
**Answer**: Year 1: Depreciation = $80,000 × 25% = $20,000. Accumulated depreciation $20,000. Carrying amount $80,000 - $20,000 = $60,000. Year 2: Depreciation = $60,000 × 25% = $15,000. Accumulated depreciation $20,000 + $15,000 = $35,000. Carrying amount $60,000 - $15,000 = $45,000. Year 3: Depreciation = $45,000 × 25% = $11,250. Accumulated depreciation $35,000 + $11,250 = $46,250. Carrying amount $45,000 - $11,250 = $33,750. Year 4: Depreciation = $33,750 × 25% = $8,438 (rounded). Accumulated depreciation $46,250 + $8,438 = $54,688. Carrying amount $33,750 - $8,438 = $25,312. Notice the pattern: each year's depreciation is 75% of the previous year's (because the rate is 25%, so the balance retains 75% each year).
**Explanation**: A common error is showing the carrying amount reaching zero or a residual value after a set number of years — under reducing balance, the carrying amount never reaches zero (unless the rate is 100%). The asset retains some book value indefinitely, which is why the rate must be carefully chosen to approximate the expected residual value.
**Tags**: reducing-balance, accumulated-depreciation, multi-year-table

### Question Q-4.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business buys a delivery van for $40,000 on 1 January 20X1. It is depreciated at 35% per annum using the reducing balance method. Show: (a) the depreciation expense in the statement of profit or loss for years 1, 2, and 3, (b) the carrying amount at the end of year 3, (c) the total accumulated depreciation at the end of year 3.
**Answer**: Year 1: Depreciation = $40,000 × 35% = $14,000. Carrying amount = $40,000 - $14,000 = $26,000. Year 2: Depreciation = $26,000 × 35% = $9,100. Carrying amount = $26,000 - $9,100 = $16,900. Year 3: Depreciation = $16,900 × 35% = $5,915. Carrying amount = $16,900 - $5,915 = $10,985. (a) Depreciation expense in statement of profit or loss: Year 1 = $14,000, Year 2 = $9,100, Year 3 = $5,915. (b) Carrying amount at end of year 3 = $10,985. (c) Accumulated depreciation = $14,000 + $9,100 + $5,915 = $29,015 (alternatively: $40,000 - $10,985 = $29,015).
**Explanation**: A common error is thinking the total depreciation over 3 years under reducing balance should equal the straight-line total — it does not. Reducing balance is front-loaded: in year 1, $14,000 is charged (vs. straight-line which might charge much less). The statement of profit or loss shows a DECLINING expense pattern, which better matches the declining productivity/value of assets like vehicles.
**Tags**: reducing-balance, profit-or-loss, declining-charge

### Question Q-4.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A computer costs $12,000. The business uses reducing balance depreciation at 40% per annum. How many full years of depreciation must be charged before the carrying amount falls below $2,000?
**Answer**: Work year by year: Year 1: Carrying amount = $12,000 × (1 - 0.40) = $12,000 × 0.60 = $7,200. Year 2: Carrying amount = $7,200 × 0.60 = $4,320. Year 3: Carrying amount = $4,320 × 0.60 = $2,592. Year 4: Carrying amount = $2,592 × 0.60 = $1,555. The carrying amount falls below $2,000 at the end of year 4 ($1,555). So 4 full years of depreciation must be charged. Alternative formula approach: Carrying amount after n years = Cost × (1 - rate)^n. We need $12,000 × 0.60^n < $2,000, so 0.60^n < 2,000/12,000 = 0.1667. Testing: 0.60^3 = 0.216 (too high), 0.60^4 = 0.1296 (below 0.1667). Therefore n = 4 years.
**Explanation**: A common error is trying to use a straight-line approach (dividing the depreciable amount by annual charge) for a reducing balance problem. Reducing balance requires either year-by-year iteration or the formula Cost × (1 - rate)^n. The exponential decay formula is faster for "how many years" questions.
**Tags**: reducing-balance, reverse-calculation, exponential-decay

### Question Q-4.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Equipment costs $18,000 on 1 April 20X3. It is depreciated at 20% per annum using the reducing balance method. The financial year ends 31 December. Calculate the depreciation charge for the year ended 31 December 20X3 and for the year ended 31 December 20X4.
**Answer**: The equipment was owned for 9 months in 20X3 (April to December). Full year 20X3 depreciation = $18,000 × 20% = $3,600. Time-apportioned for 9 months: $3,600 × 9/12 = $2,700. Carrying amount at 31 December 20X3 = $18,000 - $2,700 = $15,300. For the year ended 31 December 20X4, the full year depreciation is calculated on the carrying amount at the start of the year. Carrying amount at 1 January 20X4 = $15,300. Full year 20X4 depreciation = $15,300 × 20% = $3,060. Carrying amount at 31 December 20X4 = $15,300 - $3,060 = $12,240.
**Explanation**: A common error is calculating the first year at the full rate and not time-apportioning. The reducing balance method still requires time apportionment for partial years, just like straight-line. The carrying amount at the end of the first partial year becomes the base for the NEXT full year's depreciation — do not recalculate from cost.
**Tags**: reducing-balance, time-apportionment, partial-year

### Question Q-4.2-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A machine is depreciated at 30% per annum using the reducing balance method. After 2 years, the accumulated depreciation is $13,020. What was the original cost of the machine? What is the carrying amount at the end of year 3?
**Answer**: Let the cost = C. Carrying amount after 1 year = C × (1 - 0.30) = 0.70C. Carrying amount after 2 years = 0.70C × 0.70 = 0.49C. Accumulated depreciation after 2 years = Cost - Carrying amount = C - 0.49C = 0.51C. We are told 0.51C = $13,020. So C = $13,020 / 0.51 = $25,529 (rounded to nearest dollar). Verification: Year 1 dep = $25,529 × 30% = $7,659. Carrying = $25,529 - $7,659 = $17,870. Year 2 dep = $17,870 × 30% = $5,361. Accumulated = $7,659 + $5,361 = $13,020. Carrying amount after 3 years = $25,529 × 0.70^3 = $25,529 × 0.343 = $8,756. Alternatively: Year 3 dep = $17,870 - $5,361 = $12,509 × 30% = $3,753 (wait — recalculate from carrying at end of year 2: $12,509 × 30% = $3,753). Carrying = $12,509 - $3,753 = $8,756.
**Explanation**: A common error is setting up the equation as C × rate × years = accumulated depreciation — that is the straight-line approach. Under reducing balance, accumulated depreciation = C × (1 - (1-rate)^n). Always verify by working forward year by year to confirm the accumulated depreciation matches the given figure.
**Tags**: reducing-balance, reverse-calculation, find-cost

### Question Q-4.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business buys a vehicle for $45,000 on 1 January 20X1. Depreciation is charged at 40% per annum on a reducing balance basis. On 30 September 20X3 the vehicle is sold for $10,000. Calculate the profit or loss on disposal and show the relevant entries in the statement of profit or loss and statement of financial position for the year ended 31 December 20X3.
**Answer**: Year 1 (20X1): Depreciation = $45,000 × 40% = $18,000. Carrying amount = $45,000 - $18,000 = $27,000. Year 2 (20X2): Depreciation = $27,000 × 40% = $10,800. Carrying amount = $27,000 - $10,800 = $16,200. Year 3 (20X3, to 30 September = 9 months): Full year depreciation on carrying amount = $16,200 × 40% = $6,480. Time-apportioned for 9 months = $6,480 × 9/12 = $4,860. Carrying amount at disposal = $16,200 - $4,860 = $11,340. Sale proceeds = $10,000. Loss on disposal = $11,340 - $10,000 = $1,340 loss.
Statement of profit or loss for year ended 31 Dec 20X3: Depreciation expense $4,860 (9 months) + Loss on disposal $1,340 = total charge $6,200. Statement of financial position at 31 Dec 20X3: No vehicle (disposed). The accumulated depreciation at disposal = $18,000 + $10,800 + $4,860 = $33,660. Check: Cost $45,000 - Accumulated $33,660 = Carrying $11,340.
**Explanation**: A common error is charging a full year's depreciation in the year of disposal — depreciation is only charged up to the date of disposal (9 months here). Another common error is comparing sale proceeds with original cost instead of carrying amount at the disposal date. Profit/loss = Sale proceeds minus carrying amount at disposal.
**Tags**: reducing-balance, asset-disposal, profit-or-loss-on-disposal

### Question Q-4.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: "A business should always use the reducing balance method for vehicles and the straight-line method for buildings." Evaluate this statement and explain the accounting principle that determines which depreciation method is most appropriate.
**Answer**: The statement reflects the matching concept — the depreciation method should reflect how the asset's economic benefits are consumed. Vehicles: lose a large proportion of their value in the first few years; are more productive and reliable when new; require increasing maintenance as they age, so higher early depreciation (reducing balance) matches higher early benefits and lower later depreciation matches declining benefits — this is appropriate. Buildings: provide consistent utility over many decades; their value does not decline significantly year on year (and may even appreciate); the pattern of benefit consumption is relatively even — so straight-line, which charges equal amounts each year, is appropriate. The key principle is NOT simplicity or tax advantage — it is the matching concept. The selected method must reflect the pattern of economic benefit consumption. If a vehicle is used equally throughout its life and well-maintained, straight-line could also be justified. If a building's value declines rapidly (e.g., a specialised factory building), reducing balance may be more appropriate. Neither method is inherently correct for any asset type — the choice depends on the specific circumstances and the expected pattern of benefit consumption.
**Explanation**: A common error is stating that one method is "better" than the other. Examiners look for evaluation based on the matching concept — the method must reflect the pattern of economic benefit consumption. Simply saying "reducing balance is better for vehicles because it gives more depreciation early" is insufficient — you must explain WHY that pattern matches the asset's actual usage.
**Tags**: evaluation, reducing-balance-vs-straight-line, matching-concept
