# Items: 2.1 — Source documents

### Question Q-2.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What are source documents in accounting? Give three examples.
**Answer**: Source documents are the original written records that provide evidence that a transaction has taken place. They are the basis for recording entries in the books of account. Examples: invoice, credit note, cheque, receipt, payment voucher, purchase order.
**Explanation**: Source documents are the first step in the accounting process — without them there is no evidence to support entries in the books. A common error is thinking that source documents are the same as books of account — source documents are the evidence; books of account are where the data is recorded.
**Tags**: source-documents, definition, evidence

### Question Q-2.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is an invoice? Who issues it and when?
**Answer**: An invoice is a document issued by a seller to a buyer when goods are sold (or services provided) on credit. It lists the goods or services, quantities, prices, and total amount due. The invoice serves as a request for payment and as evidence of the transaction for both parties.
**Explanation**: An invoice is issued for credit transactions, not necessarily for cash sales. A common error is thinking an invoice means payment has been made — an invoice requests payment; it does not confirm payment. A receipt confirms payment.
**Tags**: invoice, credit-sale, source-documents

### Question Q-2.1-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: When is a credit note issued and by whom?
**Answer**: A credit note is issued by a seller to a buyer to reduce the amount the buyer owes. Common situations: goods returned by the buyer, goods damaged in transit, an overcharge on the original invoice, or an allowance granted for faulty goods. The credit note states the amount by which the buyer's debt is reduced.
**Explanation**: A credit note reduces the buyer's payable (debt). A common error is thinking a credit note means the buyer will receive cash — it reduces the amount owed, not a cash refund. The seller credits the buyer's account in the sales ledger.
**Tags**: credit-note, returns, source-documents

### Question Q-2.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a debit note? How does it differ from a credit note?
**Answer**: A debit note is a document sent by a buyer to a seller requesting a reduction in the amount owed, usually because goods have been returned or were incorrectly supplied. It differs from a credit note in who issues it: a credit note is issued by the seller, while a debit note is issued by the buyer. Both relate to the same underlying event (e.g., returned goods) but from opposite perspectives.
**Explanation**: A common error is confusing the issuer — credit notes come from the seller, debit notes from the buyer. Some businesses use debit notes internally to authorise a credit note request.
**Tags**: debit-note, credit-note, distinction

### Question Q-2.1-05
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the difference between a cheque and a receipt as source documents?
**Answer**: A cheque is a written order instructing a bank to pay a specified amount from the drawer's account to the payee. It is evidence of an intention to pay. A receipt is a document acknowledging that payment has been received. A cheque is issued before or at the time of payment; a receipt is issued after payment is received.
**Explanation**: A cheque is a payment instruction; a receipt is a payment confirmation. A common error is treating a cheque as proof of payment — the cheque may bounce, whereas a receipt confirms money actually changed hands.
**Tags**: cheque, receipt, payment

### Question Q-2.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Identify the source document that would be produced for each of the following: (a) Ali returns defective goods to his supplier. (b) Mei receives goods from a customer but finds the invoice amount is too high. (c) A business pays rent by cheque. (d) A customer pays cash and is given a written acknowledgment.
**Answer**: (a) Credit note (issued by the supplier to reduce Ali's debt). (b) Debit note (issued by Mei to request a reduction). (c) Cheque counterfoil / payment voucher (evidence of bank payment). (d) Receipt (evidence of cash received by the seller).
**Explanation**: Match the scenario to the document type. A common error is answering "invoice" for (a) or (b) — an invoice records the original sale, not a return or adjustment.
**Tags**: application, identification, source-documents

### Question Q-2.1-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why are source documents important in accounting? Give three reasons.
**Answer**: 1) They provide evidence that a transaction occurred, supporting the entries in the books of account. 2) They contain details (date, amount, parties, description) needed for accurate recording. 3) They serve as an audit trail — if a discrepancy is found, source documents can be checked to verify or correct the records. They also help prevent fraud by requiring documentary support for every entry.
**Explanation**: Source documents are the foundation of the accounting trail. A common error is saying they are "for filing" only — they are active tools for verification and accuracy.
**Tags**: importance, audit-trail, accuracy

### Question Q-2.1-08
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A customer receives a credit note from a supplier. In the customer's books, does the credit note reduce purchases, trade payables, or both? Explain.
**Answer**: The credit note reduces trade payables because the customer owes less to the supplier. It also reduces purchases (or returns outwards) because the goods returned are no longer part of the purchase total. In practice, the customer debits trade payables (reducing the liability) and credits purchases returns/returns outwards.
**Explanation**: The credit note has a dual effect in the customer's books. A common error is thinking it only reduces payables — it must also affect the purchases figure to keep the double entry balanced.
**Tags**: credit-note, double-entry, application

### Question Q-2.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business cannot find the source document for a $2,000 payment recorded in its books. What are the risks and what should the accountant do?
**Answer**: Risks: 1) The payment may not have actually occurred — the entry could be an error or fraud. 2) Without a source document, the entry cannot be verified during an audit, potentially leading to a qualification of the accounts. 3) The business cannot prove the payment was legitimate if challenged by tax authorities. Action: The accountant should investigate by checking bank statements for a corresponding payment, contacting the supposed payee, reviewing authorisation records, and if no evidence is found, reversing the entry. The incident should be reported to management.
**Explanation**: Missing source documents undermine the reliability of financial records. A common error is simply saying "record it anyway" — every entry must be supported by evidence.
**Tags**: evaluation, missing-document, risk
