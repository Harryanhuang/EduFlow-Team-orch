# Items: 5.5 — Partnership accounts appropriation

### Question Q-5.5-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a partnership agreement (Articles of Partnership)? Why is it important?
**Answer**: A partnership agreement (also called Articles of Partnership) is a written legal document that sets out the terms under which partners will run the business together. It typically covers: profit-sharing ratios, capital contributions by each partner, partners' salaries, interest on capital, interest on drawings, procedures for admitting new partners or handling retirement/death, and how disputes will be resolved. It is important because it prevents disagreements, provides a clear framework for decision-making, and — if the Partnership Act 1890 applies by default — ensures partners' intentions override statutory default rules (equal profit sharing, no interest on capital, no salaries).
**Explanation**: Without a written agreement, the Partnership Act 1890 default provisions apply. A common error is thinking a partnership agreement is optional — while legally it can be verbal, exam answers should stress the risks of relying on default provisions, especially when capital contributions or effort levels differ.
**Tags**: partnership-agreement, articles-of-partnership, legal-framework

### Question Q-5.5-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is an appropriation account? How does it differ from an income statement?
**Answer**: An appropriation account is a financial statement that shows how the net profit of a partnership is distributed (appropriated) among the partners. It starts with the net profit figure from the income statement and then deducts appropriations — interest on capital, partners' salaries, and interest on drawings (added back) — to arrive at the residual profit, which is divided in the profit-sharing ratio. It differs from the income statement in that: 1) The income statement calculates profit from business operations (revenue minus expenses). 2) The appropriation account distributes that profit — appropriations are NOT expenses. 3) Items like partners' salaries and interest on capital appear in the appropriation account, not the income statement.
**Explanation**: The key distinction is that appropriations distribute profit; they do not generate it. A common error is treating partners' salaries as business expenses in the income statement — they are distributions of profit and belong only in the appropriation account.
**Tags**: appropriation-account, profit-distribution, income-statement

### Question Q-5.5-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: How is interest on capital calculated and treated in partnership accounts? Why is it allowed?
**Answer**: Interest on capital is calculated by applying the agreed rate (e.g. 5% per annum) to each partner's capital balance. If capital changes during the year (e.g. additional capital introduced mid-year), interest is calculated on a time-weighted basis — for example, 5% on $10,000 for 6 months = $250. Interest on capital is recorded in the appropriation account as a deduction from net profit BEFORE the residual profit is shared. It is allowed to compensate partners who have invested more capital, recognizing that capital has an opportunity cost. This ensures that partners with larger capital contributions receive a return on their investment before profit is shared equally or in another ratio.
**Explanation**: Interest on capital is an appropriation, not an expense. A common error is calculating interest on the average capital when the agreement specifies opening capital, or forgetting to time-apportion when capital changes mid-year.
**Tags**: interest-on-capital, appropriation, calculation

### Question Q-5.5-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How is interest on drawings treated in partnership accounts? What is its purpose?
**Answer**: Interest on drawings is charged to partners on the amounts they have withdrawn from the business during the year. It is calculated at the agreed rate, typically using the average period method — if no specific dates are given, drawings are assumed to have been made on average at the midpoint of the year, so interest is charged for 6 months. In the appropriation account, interest on drawings is ADDED BACK to the net profit (increasing the amount available for distribution), because it represents money that partners effectively owe back to the partnership. Its purpose is to discourage excessive withdrawals (which reduce the capital available for the business) and to penalize partners who take out more than their fair share, ensuring fairness between partners who draw different amounts.
**Explanation**: Interest on drawings increases distributable profit. A common error is deducting it like an expense — it must be added back to net profit in the appropriation account. When specific dates are given, calculate from the date of each drawing to the year end.
**Tags**: interest-on-drawings, appropriation, calculation

### Question Q-5.5-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What are partners' salaries in partnership accounts? How are they treated?
**Answer**: Partners' salaries are amounts paid to partners for their work in the business, as specified in the partnership agreement. They are NOT expenses of the business — they are appropriations of profit. In the appropriation account, partners' salaries are deducted from net profit BEFORE the residual profit is shared. This recognizes that some partners may contribute more time or effort to running the business and should be compensated for this before profit is shared in the agreed ratio. If the partnership makes a loss, salaries are still shown in the appropriation account (unless the agreement specifies otherwise), resulting in a larger loss allocation to partners in the residual sharing.
**Explanation**: A partner's salary is not an employment salary — partners are not employees of the partnership. A common error is treating partner salaries as operating expenses in the income statement. They belong only in the appropriation account.
**Tags**: partner-salary, appropriation, profit-sharing

### Question Q-5.5-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Distinguish between a partner's capital account and current account. When is each used?
**Answer**: The capital account records the partner's permanent investment in the partnership — the original capital contributed plus any additional capital introduced. It normally remains unchanged unless the partner agrees to change the capital (e.g. bringing in more capital or withdrawing some). The current account records the partner's accumulated profits minus drawings, salaries, interest on capital and interest on drawings. Each year, the partner's share of profit (after appropriations) is credited to the current account, and drawings, interest on drawings are debited. The current account balance fluctuates annually. The fixed capital method uses both accounts (capital stays fixed; profits go to current account). The fluctuating capital method uses only one account where capital AND profits/drawings are all recorded together. The method used depends on the partnership agreement.
**Explanation**: The distinction matters for presentation in the statement of financial position. A common error is recording profit share or drawings directly in the capital account when the fixed capital method is specified. Always check whether the question uses fixed or fluctuating capital.
**Tags**: capital-account, current-account, fixed-capital, fluctuating-capital

### Question Q-5.5-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: How does the appropriation account change when a new partner is admitted or an existing partner retires?
**Answer**: When a new partner is admitted or a partner retires, the appropriation account must reflect the change for the relevant period. If the change occurs mid-year, the year is split into two periods — pre-admission/retirement and post-admission/retirement. For each period: 1) Net profit is apportioned on a time basis (unless actual figures are available). 2) Interest on capital, salaries, and other appropriations are calculated based on the partners active in each period. 3) Residual profit for each period is shared using the profit-sharing ratio that applied during that period. Additionally, goodwill may need to be calculated and adjusted among partners' capital/current accounts, and asset revaluations may be required. The new partnership agreement terms apply from the date of change.
**Explanation**: Time-apportionment is key when a partnership changes mid-year. A common error is applying the new profit-sharing ratio to the whole year's profit instead of splitting at the date of change. Always check whether goodwill and revaluation adjustments are required alongside the appropriation.
**Tags**: admission, retirement, appropriation, goodwill, time-apportionment

### Question Q-5.5-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare and contrast the financial reporting of a partnership with that of a sole trader. What additional accounts and considerations arise?
**Answer**: Both sole traders and partnerships prepare an income statement (trading and profit & loss) and a statement of financial position. However, partnerships have additional reporting requirements: 1) An appropriation account showing how net profit is divided among partners — sole traders take all profit as drawings so no appropriation is needed. 2) Separate capital and current accounts for each partner — a sole trader has a single capital account. 3) More complex treatment of drawings — partnership drawings may carry interest, and interest on drawings must be appropriated. 4) Profit-sharing arrangements must be documented and consistently applied. 5) On admission or retirement of partners, additional calculations for goodwill, revaluation of assets, and adjustment of capital balances are required. 6) Partnership accounts must comply with the terms of the partnership agreement, which may specify different treatments for different partners. The fundamental accounting principles (double entry, accruals, prudence) are the same, but the ownership structure creates additional complexity in how equity is presented and how profit is allocated.
**Explanation**: The core financial statements are similar; the differences arise from multiple owners. A common error is presenting partnership capital as a single figure instead of showing each partner's capital and current account separately.
**Tags**: partnership, sole-trader, comparison, financial-statements

### Question Q-5.5-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Prepare a full appropriation account from the following scenario: A partnership of Alex and Ben has net profit of $85,000 for the year. Their partnership agreement specifies: capital balances — Alex $60,000, Ben $40,000; interest on capital at 5% p.a.; Alex receives a salary of $10,000 p.a.; interest on drawings — Alex $1,200, Ben $800; residual profit is shared 3:2. Show the appropriation account and calculate each partner's total appropriation.
**Answer**:
Appropriation Account for the year ended:
- Net profit: $85,000
- Add: Interest on drawings — Alex $1,200, Ben $800 = $2,000
- Total available for appropriation: $87,000
- Less appropriations:
  - Interest on capital: Alex $3,000 (5% × $60,000), Ben $2,000 (5% × $40,000) = $5,000
  - Partner's salary: Alex $10,000 = $10,000
  - Total appropriations: $15,000
- Residual profit: $87,000 - $15,000 = $72,000
- Shared 3:2 — Alex $43,200 (3/5 × $72,000), Ben $28,800 (2/5 × $72,000)

Each partner's total appropriation:
- Alex: interest on capital $3,000 + salary $10,000 + residual share $43,200 = $56,200
- Ben: interest on capital $2,000 + residual share $28,800 = $30,800
- Total: $56,200 + $30,800 = $87,000 (matches available for appropriation)

**Explanation**: Always verify that total appropriations equal net profit plus interest on drawings. A common error is deducting interest on drawings instead of adding it back, or miscalculating the residual profit after appropriations. Work systematically: start with net profit, add interest on drawings, deduct interest on capital and salaries, share the remainder.
**Tags**: appropriation-account, preparation, calculation, comprehensive
