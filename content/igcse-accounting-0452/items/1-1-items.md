# Items: 1.1 — The purpose of accounting

### Question Q-1.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is accounting?
**Answer**: Accounting is the systematic process of recording, classifying, summarising, interpreting and communicating financial information about a business.
**Explanation**: Accounting is more than just recording numbers — it involves classifying transactions into categories, summarising them into reports, and interpreting the results to help users make decisions. A common error is defining accounting only as "recording transactions" and missing the analysis and communication aspects.
**Tags**: purpose-of-accounting, definition, core

### Question Q-1.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the difference between bookkeeping and accounting?
**Answer**: Bookkeeping is the routine process of recording financial transactions in the books of account. Accounting goes further — it includes classifying, summarising, interpreting and communicating financial information to help users make decisions. Bookkeeping is a subset of accounting.
**Explanation**: A common error is treating bookkeeping and accounting as the same thing. Bookkeeping is the mechanical recording stage; accounting adds analysis, interpretation and reporting. Exam answers that only say "bookkeeping records and accounting analyses" are incomplete — accounting also includes classification, summarisation and communication.
**Tags**: bookkeeping, accounting, difference, core

### Question Q-1.1-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: State three main purposes of accounting in a business.
**Answer**: 1) To provide financial information that helps owners and managers make informed business decisions. 2) To monitor and measure the financial performance and position of the business (profitability, liquidity, solvency). 3) To meet legal and regulatory requirements such as tax returns, statutory filings and compliance with accounting standards.
**Explanation**: Accounting serves both internal decision-making and external compliance purposes. A common error is listing only one purpose (e.g., "to calculate profit") — exam answers should cover decision-making, performance monitoring and compliance.
**Tags**: purpose-of-accounting, decision-making, compliance

### Question Q-1.1-04
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Why is accounting described as an information system?
**Answer**: Accounting is an information system because it takes raw financial data (transactions) as input, processes it through recording, classifying and summarising, and outputs useful financial reports (income statement, statement of financial position) that different users need for decision-making.
**Explanation**: The input-process-output model applies: transactions are input, accounting procedures process them, and financial statements are the output. A common error is describing accounting only as record-keeping without recognising the system of processing data into useful information.
**Tags**: information-system, input-process-output, accounting

### Question Q-1.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A small business owner says, "I don't need accounting records — I know how my business is doing." Explain two risks of this approach.
**Answer**: 1) Without records the owner cannot accurately determine profit or loss, making it impossible to know whether the business is truly profitable after all expenses. 2) The business may fail to meet tax obligations or legal reporting requirements, resulting in penalties or legal action. Additionally, without records the owner cannot make informed decisions about pricing, cost control or expansion, and cannot provide evidence to banks when seeking loans.
**Explanation**: Intuition is not a substitute for systematic records. A common error is only mentioning tax — the decision-making and financing risks are equally important in exam answers.
**Tags**: application, risk, record-keeping

### Question Q-1.1-06
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Name the two key financial statements that accounting produces and state what each shows.
**Answer**: 1) The income statement (or profit and loss account) shows the business's revenue, expenses and resulting profit or loss over a period of time. 2) The statement of financial position (or balance sheet) shows the business's assets, liabilities and capital at a specific point in time.
**Explanation**: These are the primary outputs of the accounting process. A common error is confusing the time dimension — the income statement covers a period (e.g., "for the year ended"), while the statement of financial position is a snapshot ("as at" a specific date).
**Tags**: financial-statements, income-statement, statement-of-financial-position

### Question Q-1.1-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain how accounting information helps a business control its costs.
**Answer**: Accounting records track every expense by category (e.g., rent, wages, materials). By comparing these costs over time or against budgets, managers can identify which costs are rising, investigate the causes, and take action such as finding cheaper suppliers or reducing waste. Without systematic cost tracking, overspending may go unnoticed until the business faces a cash shortage.
**Explanation**: Cost control depends on having detailed, classified expense data. A common error is saying "accounting reduces costs" — accounting provides the information; management action reduces costs.
**Tags**: cost-control, management, application

### Question Q-1.1-08
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: List three types of financial information that accounting records provide.
**Answer**: 1) Revenue information — how much the business has earned from sales or services. 2) Expense information — what the business has spent on operations (rent, wages, materials, etc.). 3) Asset and liability information — what the business owns and owes at a given date.
**Explanation**: These three categories cover the core financial data. A common error is listing non-financial information (e.g., "number of employees") — accounting specifically deals with financial data measured in monetary terms.
**Tags**: financial-information, revenue, expenses

### Question Q-1.1-09
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A new business starts trading. Explain why the owner needs accounting records from day one, even if the business is very small.
**Answer**: From day one the business incurs transactions (purchases, sales, payments) that must be tracked to: 1) Know whether the business is making a profit or loss. 2) Monitor cash flow to avoid running out of money. 3) Build a complete record for tax purposes — missing early transactions can lead to incorrect tax returns. 4) Establish a financial baseline for future comparison. Starting late means gaps in records that are difficult to reconstruct.
**Explanation**: Early records form the foundation for all future reporting. A common error is assuming small businesses can "catch up" later — memory-based reconstruction is unreliable and auditors require documentary evidence.
**Tags**: application, start-up, record-keeping
