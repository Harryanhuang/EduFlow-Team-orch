# Items: 1.4 — The accounting equation

### Question Q-1.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: State the accounting equation.
**Answer**: Assets = Liabilities + Capital (A = L + C). This equation shows that a business's resources (assets) are financed either by borrowing (liabilities) or by the owner's investment (capital).
**Explanation**: The equation must always balance. A common error is writing it as A = L - C or C = A + L — both are wrong. The correct form shows that assets equal the total claims against the business (external claims by creditors plus the owner's claim).
**Tags**: accounting-equation, definition, core

### Question Q-1.4-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Rearrange the accounting equation to show how capital is calculated.
**Answer**: Capital = Assets - Liabilities (C = A - L). This shows that capital (owner's equity) is the residual interest in the assets of the business after deducting all liabilities.
**Explanation**: This form is useful when you know total assets and total liabilities but need to find the owner's equity. A common error is writing C = L - A — this would give a negative capital when assets exceed liabilities, which is illogical.
**Tags**: accounting-equation, rearrangement, capital

### Question Q-1.4-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A business has assets of $120,000 and liabilities of $45,000. Calculate the owner's capital.
**Answer**: Capital = Assets - Liabilities = $120,000 - $45,000 = $75,000.
**Explanation**: Direct application of C = A - L. A common error is adding instead of subtracting, or confusing which figure is assets and which is liabilities.
**Tags**: calculation, accounting-equation, capital

### Question Q-1.4-04
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A business has assets of $80,000 and capital of $55,000. Calculate the liabilities.
**Answer**: From A = L + C, rearranging: L = A - C = $80,000 - $55,000 = $25,000.
**Explanation**: The equation can be rearranged to find any one unknown. A common error is writing L = A + C — this would give $135,000, which would make the equation unbalanced ($80,000 ≠ $135,000 + $55,000).
**Tags**: calculation, accounting-equation, liabilities

### Question Q-1.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business buys a machine for $10,000 cash. State the effect on the accounting equation.
**Answer**: One asset (machinery) increases by $10,000 and another asset (cash) decreases by $10,000. Total assets remain unchanged. Liabilities and capital are unaffected. The equation remains balanced.
**Explanation**: This is an asset exchange transaction — total assets do not change, only the composition changes. A common error is thinking the equation is disrupted because cash goes down — the increase in machinery offsets it.
**Tags**: transaction-analysis, asset-exchange, application

### Question Q-1.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: The owner contributes an additional $20,000 cash to the business. State the effect on the accounting equation.
**Answer**: Assets (cash) increase by $20,000 and Capital increases by $20,000. The equation remains balanced: both sides increase by the same amount. Liabilities are unaffected.
**Explanation**: Capital introduction increases both assets and capital. A common error is recording this as a liability — it is the owner's investment, not a loan, so it increases capital not liabilities.
**Tags**: capital-introduction, transaction-analysis, application

### Question Q-1.4-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business buys goods on credit for $5,000. State the effect on the accounting equation.
**Answer**: Assets (inventory) increase by $5,000 and Liabilities (trade payables) increase by $5,000. Capital is unaffected. The equation remains balanced: A increases by $5,000 and L + C also increases by $5,000 (all from L).
**Explanation**: Credit purchases increase both what the business owns (stock) and what it owes. A common error is thinking this affects capital — the purchase itself does not create profit or loss, it is simply an exchange of a promise to pay for goods.
**Tags**: credit-purchase, transaction-analysis, application

### Question Q-1.4-08
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business repays a bank loan of $15,000 by cheque. State the effect on the accounting equation.
**Answer**: Assets (bank balance) decrease by $15,000 and Liabilities (bank loan) decrease by $15,000. Capital is unaffected. The equation remains balanced: both sides decrease by $15,000.
**Explanation**: Repaying a liability reduces both assets and liabilities. A common error is thinking the repayment is an expense that reduces capital — loan repayment is not an expense (interest would be, but the principal repayment is a balance sheet transaction only).
**Tags**: loan-repayment, transaction-analysis, application

### Question Q-1.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business starts with assets $50,000, liabilities $20,000. During the year: (1) owner adds $10,000 capital, (2) goods bought on credit $8,000, (3) goods sold on credit for $12,000 that cost $7,000, (4) owner takes drawings of $3,000 cash. Calculate the closing capital using the accounting equation.
**Answer**: Opening capital = A - L = $50,000 - $20,000 = $30,000. Effect of each transaction: (1) Capital +$10,000. (2) No effect on capital — assets (inventory) +$8,000 and liabilities (payables) +$8,000. (3) Revenue $12,000 less cost $7,000 = profit $5,000, which increases capital by $5,000. (4) Drawings reduce capital by $3,000. Closing capital = $30,000 + $10,000 + $5,000 - $3,000 = $42,000.
**Explanation**: Each transaction must be analysed for its effect on capital. Key points: transaction (2) is an asset/liability swap with no capital effect; transaction (3) generates profit which increases capital; drawings are not expenses but reduce capital directly. A common error is treating transaction (2) as increasing capital, or forgetting that profit increases capital.
**Tags**: multi-transaction, calculation, application, profit
