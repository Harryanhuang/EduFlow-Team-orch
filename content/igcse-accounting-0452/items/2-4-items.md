# Items: 2.4 — The ledger

### Question Q-2.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the ledger in accounting?
**Answer**: The ledger is a book (or collection of accounts) in which all transactions are classified and recorded by account. Each account in the ledger shows all the transactions relating to one particular item (e.g., cash, rent, sales) in one place. It is the principal book of account in the double entry system.
**Explanation**: The ledger organises transactions from the day books and cash book into individual accounts. A common error is thinking the ledger is the first place transactions are recorded — transactions are first recorded in books of original entry, then posted to the ledger.
**Tags**: ledger, definition, core

### Question Q-2.4-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Describe the structure of a T-account in the ledger.
**Answer**: A T-account has the account name written at the top. The left side is the debit (Dr) side and the right side is the credit (Cr) side. Each entry on either side shows the date, the name of the corresponding account (the "other side" of the double entry), and the amount. T-accounts are used to calculate the balance on each account.
**Explanation**: The T-account is a visual representation of a ledger account. A common error is putting the wrong account name on the left side — the entry on the debit side shows the name of the account that was credited, and vice versa, because it represents the other half of the double entry.
**Tags**: t-account, structure, ledger

### Question Q-2.4-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is "posting" in the context of the ledger?
**Answer**: Posting is the process of transferring (copying) transaction amounts from the books of original entry (day books, cash book) into the appropriate accounts in the ledger. For each transaction, the debit amount is posted to the debit side of one account and the credit amount is posted to the credit side of another account, maintaining the double entry.
**Explanation**: Posting moves data from chronological records (day books) to classified records (ledger accounts). A common error is posting to the wrong side or to the wrong account.
**Tags**: posting, ledger, process

### Question Q-2.4-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: The following entries appear in the Rent account: Debit side — Jan 1: Bank $600, Feb 1: Bank $600, Mar 1: Bank $600. Credit side — Mar 31: Income statement $1,800. What does the credit entry on Mar 31 represent?
**Answer**: The credit entry on Mar 31 of $1,800 represents the transfer of the total rent expense to the income statement at the end of the accounting period. The three debit entries total $1,800 ($600 x 3), which is the total rent paid. At the end of the period, expense accounts are closed by transferring their totals to the income statement (credit the expense account, debit the income statement).
**Explanation**: This is the "balancing off" process for expense accounts. A common error is thinking the credit entry is a refund — it is a bookkeeping transfer to close the account for the period.
**Tags**: balancing-off, income-statement, application

### Question Q-2.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What do "balance c/f" (carried forward) and "balance b/f" (brought forward) mean in the ledger?
**Answer**: Balance c/f (carried forward) is the balancing figure inserted at the end of an accounting period to make the two sides of an account equal. It is written on the side with the smaller total. Balance b/f (brought forward) is the same amount written on the opposite side at the start of the next period, representing the opening balance. This process is called "balancing off" an account.
**Explanation**: The c/f and b/f mechanism ensures continuity between periods. A common error is putting c/f and b/f on the same side — c/f goes on the lighter side to balance, b/f appears on the opposite side as the opening balance.
**Tags**: balance-c/f, balance-b/f, balancing-off

### Question Q-2.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A trade receivables account shows: Debit side total $12,000, Credit side total $8,000. What is the balance and what type of balance is it?
**Answer**: The balance is $4,000 debit balance ($12,000 - $8,000). A debit balance in a trade receivables account means customers owe the business $4,000. This is normal — receivables are assets and should have debit balances. The balance c/f of $4,000 would be entered on the credit side to balance the account, and brought forward as a debit balance b/f.
**Explanation**: The side with the larger total determines the balance type. A common error is reversing the calculation ($8,000 - $12,000) or misidentifying the balance type.
**Tags**: balance-calculation, debit-balance, application

### Question Q-2.4-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A supplier's account in the purchases ledger shows a credit balance of $3,500. Is this correct? Explain.
**Answer**: Yes, this is correct. A supplier is a creditor, and creditors have credit balances because the business owes them money (a liability). A credit balance of $3,500 means the business owes the supplier $3,500. If a supplier's account showed a debit balance, it would indicate an abnormal situation — perhaps an overpayment or goods returned but not yet credited.
**Explanation**: Liabilities (payables/creditors) naturally have credit balances in the ledger. A common error is thinking a supplier account should have a debit balance because we "pay" the supplier — the balance represents what we owe, not what we've paid.
**Tags**: credit-balance, supplier, payables

### Question Q-2.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: The following entries are in the Motor Vehicles account: Debit — Jan 1: Balance b/f $25,000, Jun 15: Bank $18,000. Credit — Dec 31: Disposal $10,000. Balance the account and state the closing balance.
**Answer**: Debit side total = $25,000 + $18,000 = $43,000. Credit side (before balancing) = $10,000. Balance c/f = $43,000 - $10,000 = $33,000 (entered on credit side). Closing balance b/f = $33,000 (debit balance). This represents the net book value of motor vehicles at year end.
**Explanation**: Add both sides, find the difference, and enter the balancing figure on the smaller side. A common error is forgetting to include the opening balance in the calculation, or placing the balance c/f on the wrong side.
**Tags**: balancing-off, calculation, non-current-assets

### Question Q-2.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: During posting to the ledger, a bookkeeper correctly debits the Purchases account with $450 but credits the Cash account with only $45. What will be the effect on the trial balance? What type of error is this?
**Answer**: The trial balance will not balance — total debits will exceed total credits by $405 ($450 - $45). This is a recording error where the debit and credit amounts don't match. The error will be detected when the trial balance is prepared because the difference of $405 will show as an imbalance. To correct it, the Cash account needs an additional credit entry of $405.
**Explanation**: When debit and credit amounts don't match, the trial balance detects it. A common error is calling this an "error of principle" — it is simply a recording error (incorrect amount), not an error of principle (which involves using the wrong type of account).
**Tags**: error, trial-balance, posting
