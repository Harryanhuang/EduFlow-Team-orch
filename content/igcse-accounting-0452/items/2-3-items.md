# Items: 2.3 — Books of original entry

### Question Q-2.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What are books of original entry (also called day books or journals)?
**Answer**: Books of original entry are books in which transactions are first recorded, before being posted to the ledger. They include the sales day book, purchases day book, returns inwards day book, returns outwards day book, and the general journal. They serve as an intermediate step between source documents and the ledger.
**Explanation**: Transactions flow: source documents → books of original entry → ledger. A common error is thinking books of original entry are the same as the ledger — day books record transactions chronologically; the ledger organises them by account.
**Tags**: books-of-original-entry, day-books, definition

### Question Q-2.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What type of transactions are recorded in the sales day book (sales journal)?
**Answer**: The sales day book records credit sales of goods only. Each entry shows the date, the customer's name, the invoice number, and the amount. Cash sales are NOT recorded in the sales day book — they go directly into the cash book. The total of the sales day book is posted to the sales account in the ledger.
**Explanation**: The sales day book only records sales on credit, not cash sales. A common error is including cash sales in the sales day book — cash sales bypass the day book and go straight to the cash book.
**Tags**: sales-day-book, credit-sales

### Question Q-2.3-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What type of transactions are recorded in the purchases day book (purchases journal)?
**Answer**: The purchases day book records credit purchases of goods only. Each entry shows the date, the supplier's name, the invoice number, and the amount. Cash purchases are NOT recorded in the purchases day book — they go directly into the cash book. The total of the purchases day book is posted to the purchases account in the ledger.
**Explanation**: Like the sales day book, the purchases day book only records credit purchases. A common error is including cash purchases — they are recorded in the cash book, not the purchases day book.
**Tags**: purchases-day-book, credit-purchases

### Question Q-2.3-04
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the difference between the returns inwards day book and the returns outwards day book?
**Answer**: The returns inwards day book (sales returns journal) records goods returned by customers to the business — these are credit sales being reversed. The returns outwards day book (purchases returns journal) records goods returned by the business to its suppliers — these are credit purchases being reversed. Returns inwards reduces sales; returns outwards reduces purchases.
**Explanation**: "Inwards" means goods coming back into the business (from customers); "outwards" means goods going out (to suppliers). A common error is confusing which book records which type of return.
**Tags**: returns-inwards, returns-outwards, distinction

### Question Q-2.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the general journal used for? Give three examples of transactions recorded in it.
**Answer**: The general journal records transactions that do not fit into the other day books. Examples: 1) Purchase or sale of a non-current asset on credit. 2) Correction of errors. 3) Depreciation charges. 4) Opening entries. 5) Adjusting entries such as accruals and prepayments. It is also used for unusual or infrequent transactions.
**Explanation**: The general journal is a "catch-all" for non-routine entries. A common error is using it for ordinary credit purchases or sales — those go in the purchases or sales day book.
**Tags**: general-journal, application

### Question Q-2.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: For each of the following transactions, state which book of original entry it should be recorded in: (a) Credit sale of goods to Lee, (b) Cash purchase of inventory, (c) Credit purchase of a machine, (d) Goods returned by customer Sara, (e) Goods returned to supplier Omar.
**Answer**: (a) Sales day book — credit sale of goods. (b) Cash book — cash transaction (not a day book entry). (c) General journal — purchase of non-current asset on credit (not goods, so not in purchases day book). (d) Returns inwards day book — goods returned by a customer. (e) Returns outwards day book — goods returned to a supplier.
**Explanation**: Match the transaction type to the appropriate book. A common error is putting (c) in the purchases day book — a machine is a non-current asset, not goods for resale, so it goes in the general journal.
**Tags**: application, identification, book-selection

### Question Q-2.3-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Why are cash sales not recorded in the sales day book? Where are they recorded instead?
**Answer**: Cash sales are not recorded in the sales day book because the sales day book is designed for credit sales — transactions where a customer owes money and will pay later. Cash sales involve immediate payment, so they are recorded directly in the cash book. Recording them in the sales day book would create a fictitious receivable.
**Explanation**: The sales day book total is posted to trade receivables control — recording cash sales there would overstate what customers owe. A common error is thinking all sales go through the sales day book.
**Tags**: cash-sales, day-book-limitation

### Question Q-2.3-08
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Describe the process of posting from the sales day book to the ledger.
**Answer**: Individual entries in the sales day book are posted daily to the personal accounts of customers in the sales ledger (debit each customer's account). At the end of the period (usually monthly), the total of the sales day book is posted to the sales account in the nominal ledger (credit sales). This ensures both individual customer balances and the total sales figure are updated.
**Explanation**: There are two levels of posting: individual entries to customer accounts (debit) and the total to the sales account (credit). A common error is only posting the total and forgetting individual customer accounts, or only posting individuals and forgetting the total.
**Tags**: posting, sales-day-book, ledger

### Question Q-2.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business records a credit sale of a non-current asset (an old vehicle sold on credit to Ali) in the sales day book. Is this correct? Explain the error and the correct treatment.
**Answer**: Incorrect. The sales day book is for credit sales of goods (inventory) only. Selling a non-current asset on credit is not a sale of goods — it should be recorded in the general journal. The correct entry: Debit Ali (receivable) with the sale amount, Credit Disposal of non-current asset account. Recording it in the sales day book would overstate sales revenue and create an incorrect entry in the trading section of the income statement.
**Explanation**: The sales day book is strictly for goods sold on credit. A common error is thinking any credit sale goes in the sales day book — the key is whether what is sold is "goods" (inventory) or something else.
**Tags**: error-identification, general-journal, evaluation
