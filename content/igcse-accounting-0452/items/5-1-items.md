# Items: 5.1 — Income statement: trading section

### Question Q-5.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is an income statement (statement of profit or loss)? Describe the structure of a trading entity's income statement, including the trading section.
**Answer**: An income statement (statement of profit or loss) is a financial statement that shows the financial performance of a business over a period of time — whether it made a profit or a loss. For a trading entity, the structure is:
Trading section: Sales (revenue) - Cost of goods sold (COGS) = Gross profit. COGS = Opening inventory + Purchases - Closing inventory.
The trading section shows the profit earned from the core trading activity (buying and selling goods).
Below the trading section: Gross profit - Expenses (wages, rent, insurance, etc.) + Other income = Net profit (or net loss).
Gross profit measures the profit from buying and selling before considering overheads. Net profit measures what remains after ALL expenses are deducted.
**Explanation**: A common error is including operating expenses (rent, wages, etc.) in the trading section — these appear BELOW the gross profit line. The trading section only includes items directly related to buying and selling goods.
**Tags**: income-statement, structure, trading-section

### Question Q-5.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the formula for cost of goods sold (COGS)? Calculate COGS for a business with: opening inventory $15,000, purchases $85,000, closing inventory $20,000.
**Answer**: COGS formula: Opening inventory + Purchases - Closing inventory.
For the given figures: $15,000 + $85,000 - $20,000 = $80,000.
COGS represents the cost of the goods that were actually sold during the period. Opening inventory is added because these goods were available for sale. Purchases are added because more goods were bought. Closing inventory is subtracted because these goods were NOT sold — they remain in stock and their cost should not be included in COGS.
If there are additional adjustments: Purchases should include carriage inwards (transport costs to bring goods to the business) and exclude returns outwards (goods returned to suppliers). Sales should exclude returns inwards (goods returned by customers).
**Explanation**: A common error is adding closing inventory instead of subtracting it — closing inventory represents goods NOT sold, so their cost must be removed from the total. Think of it as: total goods available for sale ($15,000 + $85,000 = $100,000) minus what is left unsold ($20,000) = what was sold ($80,000).
**Tags**: COGS, formula, calculation

### Question Q-5.1-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Prepare the trading section of the income statement for a business with the following: Sales $250,000, opening inventory $30,000, purchases $150,000, carriage inwards $5,000, returns inwards $8,000, returns outwards $3,000, closing inventory $35,000.
**Answer**:
Trading section for the year ended 31 December 20X4:

Sales ($250,000 - $8,000 returns inwards) = $242,000
Less cost of goods sold:
  Opening inventory: $30,000
  Purchases ($150,000 - $3,000 returns outwards): $147,000
  Carriage inwards: $5,000
  Less closing inventory: ($35,000)
  COGS: $30,000 + $147,000 + $5,000 - $35,000 = $147,000

Gross profit = $242,000 - $147,000 = $95,000

Key adjustments: Net sales = Sales - Returns inwards. Net purchases = Purchases - Returns outwards + Carriage inwards. Carriage inwards is a cost of acquiring goods and is added to purchases. Carriage outwards (delivery to customers) is an operating expense, NOT part of COGS.
**Explanation**: A common error is including carriage outwards in COGS — carriage outwards is a selling/distribution expense and appears BELOW the gross profit line. Only carriage inwards (cost of bringing goods into the business) is part of the cost of purchases.
**Tags**: trading-section, COGS-adjustments, carriage

### Question Q-5.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is markup? A business buys goods for $80 and sells them for $120. Calculate the markup amount and markup percentage.
**Answer**: Markup is the percentage increase on the COST price to arrive at the selling price. It measures how much profit is added to the cost of goods.
Markup amount = Selling price - Cost price = $120 - $80 = $40.
Markup percentage = (Markup amount / Cost price) × 100% = ($40 / $80) × 100% = 50%.
The business marks up the cost by 50% to arrive at the selling price. If cost is $80 and markup is 50%, selling price = $80 + (50% × $80) = $80 + $40 = $120.
Markup is always based on cost as the denominator. A 50% markup means the selling price is 150% of cost.
**Explanation**: A common error is calculating markup as a percentage of selling price — that is margin, not markup. Markup = Gross profit / Cost of goods sold. Margin = Gross profit / Sales (revenue). The denominator is different, so the percentages are different even though the gross profit amount is the same.
**Tags**: markup, definition, calculation

### Question Q-5.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is gross profit margin (margin)? A business has sales of $200,000 and cost of goods sold of $140,000. Calculate the gross profit and the margin percentage.
**Answer**: Gross profit margin (margin) is the percentage of sales revenue that is gross profit. It measures how much of each dollar of sales remains after deducting the cost of goods sold.
Gross profit = Sales - COGS = $200,000 - $140,000 = $60,000.
Margin percentage = (Gross profit / Sales) × 100% = ($60,000 / $200,000) × 100% = 30%.
For every $1 of sales, 30 cents is gross profit. The remaining 70 cents covers the cost of goods sold.
Margin is always based on sales (revenue) as the denominator. A 30% margin means gross profit is 30% of sales.
**Explanation**: A common error is calculating margin as a percentage of cost — that is markup, not margin. Margin = Gross profit / Sales revenue. The denominator is sales, not cost. A 30% margin does NOT mean a 30% markup — the markup would be $60,000 / $140,000 = 42.9%.
**Tags**: margin, gross-profit, calculation

### Question Q-5.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business has a markup of 25%. If sales are $50,000, calculate: (a) the cost of goods sold, (b) the gross profit, (c) the gross profit margin.
**Answer**: Markup of 25% means selling price = Cost + 25% of cost = 125% of cost.
(a) COGS: Sales = 125% of cost. $50,000 = 1.25 × Cost. Cost = $50,000 / 1.25 = $40,000.
(b) Gross profit = Sales - COGS = $50,000 - $40,000 = $10,000. (Alternatively: 25% × $40,000 = $10,000.)
(c) Margin = Gross profit / Sales = $10,000 / $50,000 = 20%.

Relationship between markup and margin: If markup is 1/n, then margin is 1/(n+1). With 25% markup (= 1/4), margin = 1/5 = 20%.
Verification: Cost $40,000, markup 25% → selling price = $40,000 × 1.25 = $50,000. Gross profit $10,000, margin = $10,000/$50,000 = 20%.
**Explanation**: A common error is calculating 25% of sales ($50,000 × 25% = $12,500) as the gross profit — markup is on COST, not sales. Sales is the result of applying markup to cost, not the base for markup. Always work backwards: if sales = cost × (1 + markup%), then cost = sales / (1 + markup%).
**Tags**: markup, margin, conversion

### Question Q-5.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business has a gross profit margin of 40%. If the cost of goods sold is $72,000, calculate: (a) sales, (b) gross profit.
**Answer**: Margin of 40% means gross profit = 40% of sales. Also, COGS = Sales - Gross profit = Sales - 40% of Sales = 60% of Sales.
(a) COGS = 60% of Sales. $72,000 = 0.60 × Sales. Sales = $72,000 / 0.60 = $120,000.
(b) Gross profit = Sales - COGS = $120,000 - $72,000 = $48,000. (Alternatively: 40% × $120,000 = $48,000.)
Verification: Margin = $48,000 / $120,000 = 40%. Markup = $48,000 / $72,000 = 66.67%.
**Explanation**: A common error is calculating sales as $72,000 × 1.40 = $100,800 — this confuses margin with markup. A 40% margin means COGS is 60% of sales (not that sales is 140% of cost). When working with margin, always express COGS as a percentage of sales: COGS% = 100% - margin%. Then Sales = COGS / COGS%.
**Tags**: margin, reverse-calculation, find-sales

### Question Q-5.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A trader's income statement for the year shows: Sales $180,000; Opening inventory $22,000; Purchases $110,000; Carriage inwards $4,000; Closing inventory (destroyed by fire before counting). The business applies a uniform markup of 33⅓% on cost. Calculate the value of the closing inventory that was destroyed.
**Answer**: Markup of 33⅓% = 1/3 on cost. This means Sales = Cost + 1/3 × Cost = 4/3 × Cost.
COGS = Sales × 3/4 = $180,000 × 3/4 = $135,000.
Alternatively: If markup is 33⅓%, margin is 1/4 = 25%. Gross profit = 25% × $180,000 = $45,000. COGS = $180,000 - $45,000 = $135,000.
COGS = Opening inventory + Purchases + Carriage inwards - Closing inventory.
$135,000 = $22,000 + $110,000 + $4,000 - Closing inventory.
$135,000 = $136,000 - Closing inventory.
Closing inventory = $136,000 - $135,000 = $1,000.
**Explanation**: This is a classic "inventory destroyed" question. The method is: (1) Use markup/margin to calculate COGS from sales. (2) Use the COGS formula to work back to the missing closing inventory. The key skill is converting between markup and margin: 33⅓% markup = 25% margin, and Sales = 4/3 × Cost (or Cost = 3/4 × Sales).
**Tags**: markup, missing-figure, inventory-destroyed

### Question Q-5.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business provides the following for the year ended 31 December 20X4: Sales $320,000; Returns inwards $12,000; Opening inventory $28,000; Purchases $180,000; Returns outwards $7,000; Carriage inwards $9,000; Carriage outwards $11,000; Wages $45,000 (of which $3,000 relates to next year); Rent $18,000 (accrued $2,000 at year end). The business's gross profit margin is 37.5%. Calculate: (a) net sales, (b) COGS, (c) closing inventory, (d) gross profit, (e) net profit.
**Answer**: (a) Net sales = Sales - Returns inwards = $320,000 - $12,000 = $308,000.
(b) Margin 37.5% = 3/8. Gross profit = 37.5% × $308,000 = $115,500. COGS = $308,000 - $115,500 = $192,500.
(c) COGS = Opening inventory + Net purchases + Carriage inwards - Closing inventory.
Net purchases = $180,000 - $7,000 = $173,000.
$192,500 = $28,000 + $173,000 + $9,000 - Closing inventory.
$192,500 = $210,000 - Closing inventory.
Closing inventory = $210,000 - $192,500 = $17,500.
(d) Gross profit = $115,500 (from b).
(e) Operating expenses: Carriage outwards $11,000 + Wages ($45,000 - $3,000 prepaid) $42,000 + Rent ($18,000 + $2,000 accrued) $20,000 = $73,000.
Net profit = Gross profit - Operating expenses = $115,500 - $73,000 = $42,500.
**Explanation**: A common error in comprehensive questions is not making all adjustments before calculating expenses — wages need the prepayment deducted and rent needs the accrual added. Also, carriage outwards is an operating expense (not in COGS). The gross profit margin applies to NET sales (after returns inwards), not gross sales. Always work in order: net sales → gross profit (from margin) → COGS → closing inventory (reverse) → operating expenses → net profit.
**Tags**: comprehensive, trading-section, markup-margin
