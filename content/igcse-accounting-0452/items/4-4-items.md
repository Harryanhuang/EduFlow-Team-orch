# Items: 4.4 — Irrecoverable debts and allowances for receivables

### Question Q-4.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is an irrecoverable (bad) debt? Show the journal entries to write off an irrecoverable debt of $500 owed by a customer.
**Answer**: An irrecoverable debt (also called a bad debt) is a receivable that is now considered to have no chance of being collected — the customer has died, gone bankrupt, disappeared, or refused to pay and legal action has failed. The debt must be written off because it no longer meets the definition of an asset (future economic benefit).
Journal entries to write off: (1) Debit Irrecoverable debts expense (statement of profit or loss) $500, Credit Receivables (trade receivables account / individual customer's account) $500.
This removes the uncollectible amount from the receivables balance and recognises the expense in the period it is identified. Irrecoverable debts are an operating expense — they appear in the statement of profit or loss. They do NOT affect revenue (sales) because the original sale was valid — the customer simply cannot pay.
**Explanation**: A common error is debiting the Sales account to "reverse" the sale — the sale was real and should not be reversed. The expense is recorded separately as "Irrecoverable debts expense" in the statement of profit or loss.
**Tags**: irrecoverable-debt, write-off, journal-entry

### Question Q-4.4-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A business has receivables totaling $45,000 at 31 December 20X4. It confirms that customer A ($3,000) is bankrupt and will not pay. Show the impact on the statement of profit or loss and statement of financial position after writing off this irrecoverable debt.
**Answer**: Write off customer A: Debit Irrecoverable debts expense $3,000, Credit Receivables $3,000.
Statement of profit or loss: Irrecoverable debts expense $3,000 (deducted from gross profit as an operating expense).
Statement of financial position: Receivables at 31 December 20X4 = $45,000 - $3,000 = $42,000. This is the net receivables after removing the uncollectible amount.
Note: The write-off reduces both profit (by $3,000 expense) and assets (receivables reduced by $3,000). This reflects that the business has lost an economic resource.
**Explanation**: A common error is thinking the receivables should still show $45,000 and the bad debt shown separately — once written off, the receivable is removed from the receivables balance. The statement of financial position should only show receivables that are expected to be collected.
**Tags**: irrecoverable-debt, financial-statements, write-off

### Question Q-4.4-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business wrote off a debt of $2,000 as irrecoverable in 20X3. In 20X4, the customer unexpectedly pays $1,200 in full settlement. Show the journal entries to record the recovery of a previously written-off debt.
**Answer**: When a previously written-off debt is partially recovered, two methods are acceptable:

**Full reinstatement method** (three entries, clearly separating SPL from balance sheet):
Step 1 — Reinstate the FULL original debt: Debit Receivables $2,000, Credit Irrecoverable debts recovered (SPL income) $2,000. (This is a pure reversal — the receivable returns to the books.)
Step 2 — Record the cash actually received: Debit Cash $1,200, Credit Receivables $1,200. (Balance sheet only: cash increases, receivable decreases.)
Step 3 — Write off the remaining unrecovered balance: Debit Irrecoverable debts expense (SPL expense) $800, Credit Receivables $800. (The remaining $2,000 - $1,200 = $800 will not be collected, so it is re-written off.)
SPL impact: Irrecoverable debts recovered $2,000 (income) - Irrecoverable debts expense $800 (expense) = Net income $1,200.

**Simplified method** (one entry):
Debit Cash $1,200, Credit Irrecoverable debts recovered (SPL income) $1,200.
SPL impact: Irrecoverable debts recovered $1,200 (income). Same net result as the full reinstatement method.

Both methods give the same SPL net income of $1,200 (= cash received). The full reinstatement method is preferred in exams because it shows the full picture: the debt that was reversed, the cash collected, and the balance that remains uncollectible.
**Explanation**: A common error is crediting Sales when a bad debt is recovered — this is not a sale, it is a recovery of a previously written-off amount. The correct credit is to Irrecoverable debts recovered (income), which appears in the statement of profit or loss as other income.
**Tags**: irrecoverable-debt, recovery, previously-written-off

### Question Q-4.4-04
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is an allowance for receivables (allowance for doubtful debts)? Why do businesses make an allowance? Distinguish between a specific allowance and a general allowance.
**Answer**: An allowance for receivables is an estimate of the amount of receivables that may not be collected in the future. It is based on the prudence concept — expenses and losses should be recognised as soon as they are anticipated, not waited for. The allowance reduces receivables to their estimated recoverable amount in the statement of financial position.
Specific allowance: Made against an identified individual receivable where there is known uncertainty about collection (e.g., a customer is in financial difficulty but not yet confirmed as irrecoverable). It is calculated based on the specific circumstances — e.g., 50% of that customer's balance.
General allowance: Applied to the total receivables balance (or a group of receivables) based on past experience or industry norms — e.g., 5% of total receivables. It covers the risk that some unknown receivables may become irrecoverable.
**Explanation**: A common error is thinking an allowance writes off a debt — it does not. An allowance is an ESTIMATE. The receivables are still on the books. An irrecoverable debt write-off removes the debt entirely. An allowance reduces the carrying amount but keeps the receivable on the books.
**Tags**: allowance-for-receivables, definition, specific-vs-general

### Question Q-4.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: At 31 December 20X4, receivables total $60,000. The business makes a specific allowance of $2,000 against customer B (who is in financial difficulty) and a general allowance of 3% against the remaining receivables. Calculate the total allowance and show the net receivables in the statement of financial position.
**Answer**: First write off any irrecoverable debts (none given, so receivables = $60,000).
Specific allowance = $2,000 (against customer B).
Remaining receivables after specific allowance = $60,000 - $2,000 = $58,000.
General allowance = 3% × $58,000 = $1,740.
Total allowance = $2,000 + $1,740 = $3,740.
Net receivables in statement of financial position = $60,000 - $3,740 = $56,260.
Alternatively shown as: Receivables $60,000 less allowance for receivables $3,740 = $56,260.
**Explanation**: A common error is calculating the general allowance on the full $60,000 instead of the remaining balance after the specific allowance. The specific allowance is deducted first because it represents a known reduction; the general allowance applies to the remainder where the risk is unknown. Always apply the general allowance to receivables net of specific allowances.
**Tags**: allowance-for-receivables, specific-allowance, general-allowance

### Question Q-4.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business has an existing allowance for receivables of $4,000 at 1 January 20X4. At 31 December 20X4, the business calculates that the required allowance is $5,500. Show: (a) the income statement charge for the year, (b) the closing balance of the allowance, (c) the journal entry.
**Answer**: (a) Income statement charge = Increase in allowance = $5,500 - $4,000 = $1,500 additional expense. This is charged to the statement of profit or loss as an increase in the allowance for receivables.
(b) Closing allowance in the statement of financial position = $5,500.
(c) Journal entry: Debit Increase in allowance for receivables (SPL expense) $1,500, Credit Allowance for receivables $1,500.
The allowance account (a contra-asset account) now shows a credit balance of $5,500 ($4,000 brought forward + $1,500 increase). In the statement of financial position, receivables are shown net of this $5,500 allowance.
**Explanation**: A common error is charging the full $5,500 to the income statement — the business already has $4,000 from last year, so only the INCREASE ($1,500) is charged this year. The income statement shows the CHANGE in the allowance, not the absolute amount.
**Tags**: allowance-for-receivables, increase-in-allowance, income-statement-charge

### Question Q-4.4-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business has an existing allowance for receivables of $3,000 at 1 January 20X4. At 31 December 20X4, after writing off $1,000 of irrecoverable debts, the remaining receivables are $40,000. The business decides to reduce the general allowance from 5% to 4% of receivables. Show: (a) the irrecoverable debts expense, (b) the change in allowance, (c) the total charge to the statement of profit or loss.
**Answer**: (a) Irrecoverable debts written off = $1,000. This is charged to the statement of profit or loss as irrecoverable debts expense.
(b) New allowance = 4% × $40,000 = $1,600. Existing allowance = $3,000. Decrease in allowance = $3,000 - $1,600 = $1,400. A decrease in allowance is credited to the statement of profit or loss (it reduces the expense / is income).
(c) Total charge to statement of profit or loss: Irrecoverable debts expense $1,000 - Decrease in allowance $1,400 = net credit of $400 (net income of $400).
Journal entries: (1) Dr Irrecoverable debts expense $1,000, Cr Receivables $1,000. (2) Dr Allowance for receivables $1,400, Cr Decrease in allowance (SPL) $1,400.
**Explanation**: A common error is treating the decrease in allowance as an additional expense — it is the opposite. A decrease means the business now expects to collect MORE than previously estimated, so the reduction is income (a credit to SPL). Always check: increase in allowance = expense; decrease in allowance = income.
**Tags**: allowance-for-receivables, decrease-in-allowance, income-statement

### Question Q-4.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: At 1 January 20X4, a business had receivables of $52,000 and an allowance for receivables of $2,600 (5% of receivables). During the year: credit sales $180,000; cash received from receivables $170,000; irrecoverable debts written off $3,000. At 31 December 20X4, the business increases the general allowance to 6% of remaining receivables. Calculate: (a) closing receivables before allowance, (b) the new allowance, (c) the total charge to the statement of profit or loss for irrecoverable debts and change in allowance.
**Answer**: (a) Closing receivables before allowance: Opening $52,000 + Credit sales $180,000 - Cash received $170,000 - Written off $3,000 = $59,000.
(b) New allowance = 6% × $59,000 = $3,540. Existing allowance = $2,600. Increase = $3,540 - $2,600 = $940.
(c) Total charge to statement of profit or loss: Irrecoverable debts expense $3,000 + Increase in allowance $940 = $3,940 total expense.
Statement of financial position at 31 Dec 20X4: Receivables $59,000 less allowance $3,540 = net receivables $55,460.
**Explanation**: A common error is not reconstructing the receivables T-account — closing receivables must be calculated from opening + sales - cash - write-offs. Another error is calculating the new allowance on the opening receivables ($52,000) instead of the closing balance ($59,000). Always calculate the receivables balance at the reporting date first, then apply the allowance percentage.
**Tags**: allowance-for-receivables, comprehensive, receivables-reconstruction

### Question Q-4.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: At 31 December 20X4, a business has receivables of $80,000 before any adjustments. The following matters are identified: (i) Customer C ($4,000) is bankrupt — write off in full. (ii) Customer D ($6,000) has disputed the balance — make a specific allowance of 50%. (iii) After (i) and (ii), make a general allowance of 3% of the remaining receivables. (iv) The existing allowance at 1 January 20X4 was $2,000. Show: (a) all adjustments, (b) the income statement charges/credits, (c) the net receivables in the statement of financial position.
**Answer**: (a) Adjustments:
Step 1 — Write off Customer C: Receivables = $80,000 - $4,000 = $76,000. Irrecoverable debts expense = $4,000.
Step 2 — Specific allowance for Customer D: 50% × $6,000 = $3,000. Remaining for general allowance = $76,000 - $3,000 = $73,000.
Step 3 — General allowance: 3% × $73,000 = $2,190.
Total allowance = Specific $3,000 + General $2,190 = $5,190.
(b) Income statement: Irrecoverable debts expense $4,000. Change in allowance: New allowance $5,190 - Existing allowance $2,000 = Increase of $3,190. Total charge = $4,000 + $3,190 = $7,190 expense.
(c) Statement of financial position: Receivables $76,000 less allowance $5,190 = net receivables $70,810.
**Explanation**: A common error is calculating the general allowance before deducting the specific allowance from the receivables base. The correct order is: (1) write off irrecoverable debts, (2) deduct specific allowances, (3) apply general allowance to the remainder. Another error is charging the full $5,190 to the income statement — only the CHANGE ($3,190 increase) is charged because $2,000 was already provided last year.
**Tags**: irrecoverable-debt, specific-allowance, general-allowance, comprehensive
