# Items: 4.5 — Accruals, prepayments and inventory valuation

### Question Q-4.5-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is an accrual (accrued expense)? Show the journal entries for an accrual of $800 for electricity at the year end.
**Answer**: An accrual (accrued expense) is an expense that has been incurred (used up) during the accounting period but has not yet been paid or invoiced by the year-end date. It is recognised under the matching concept — expenses must be matched to the period in which they were incurred, regardless of when cash is paid.
Journal entries: Debit Electricity expense (statement of profit or loss) $800, Credit Accruals (current liability in statement of financial position) $800.
This records the expense in the correct period and shows the amount owed as a liability. Accruals appear under current liabilities (trade payables and accruals) in the statement of financial position.
**Explanation**: A common error is thinking an accrual is only recorded when the invoice arrives — it is recorded when the expense is incurred, even if the invoice has not yet been received. This ensures the matching concept is applied correctly.
**Tags**: accruals, definition, journal-entry

### Question Q-4.5-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a prepayment (prepaid expense)? A business pays $12,000 on 1 October 20X4 for one year's insurance in advance. The financial year ends 31 December 20X4. Show the prepayment calculation and entries at year end.
**Answer**: A prepayment (prepaid expense) is an amount paid during the accounting period that relates to a future period — the benefit has not yet been consumed. It is recognised under the matching concept — only the portion of the payment that relates to the current period should be expensed.
Annual insurance = $12,000. Monthly cost = $12,000 / 12 = $1,000. Months in current year = October, November, December = 3 months. Expense for current year = 3 × $1,000 = $3,000. Prepayment = $12,000 - $3,000 = $9,000 (9 months × $1,000).
Journal entries at year end: (1) Record the full payment: Debit Insurance expense $12,000, Credit Cash $12,000. (2) Adjust for prepayment: Debit Prepayments (current asset) $9,000, Credit Insurance expense $9,000. Net insurance expense in SPL = $3,000. Prepayment of $9,000 appears under current assets in the statement of financial position.
**Explanation**: A common error is expensing the full $12,000 in the current year because it was all paid in cash — the expense recognised must reflect the period of benefit, not the timing of payment.
**Tags**: prepayments, definition, time-apportionment

### Question Q-4.5-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: At 31 December 20X4, a business's rent account shows payments of $10,000 during the year. The rent for the year should be $12,000. The business had no accruals or prepayments at the start of the year. Show the accrual entry, the rent expense in the statement of profit or loss, and the statement of financial position presentation.
**Answer**: Rent incurred for the year = $12,000. Rent paid during the year = $10,000. Accrual (rent owed but not yet paid) = $12,000 - $10,000 = $2,000.
Journal entry: Debit Rent expense (SPL) $2,000, Credit Accruals (current liability) $2,000.
Statement of profit or loss: Rent expense = $10,000 (paid) + $2,000 (accrued) = $12,000. The full year's expense is charged regardless of how much was paid.
Statement of financial position: Accruals of $2,000 shown under current liabilities (amount owed at year end).
**Explanation**: A common error is showing only $10,000 (cash paid) as the rent expense — the matching concept requires the full $12,000 to be charged. The unpaid $2,000 is a liability and must be accrued. Always calculate expense = cash paid + closing accrual - opening accrual.
**Tags**: accruals, spl-presentation, matching-concept

### Question Q-4.5-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: At 1 January 20X4, a business had prepaid insurance of $2,400 (brought forward). During the year, insurance payments of $15,000 were made. At 31 December 20X4, the prepaid insurance for the next year is $3,000. Show the insurance expense in the statement of profit or loss and the journal entry to record the prepayment at year end.
**Answer**: Insurance expense for the year = Cash paid during the year + Opening prepayment - Closing prepayment. = $15,000 + $2,400 - $3,000 = $14,400.
The $2,400 opening prepayment relates to the current year (it was paid last year but benefits this year). The $3,000 closing prepayment relates to next year (paid this year but not consumed yet).
Statement of profit or loss: Insurance expense = $14,400.
Journal entry for closing prepayment: Debit Prepayments (current asset) $3,000, Credit Insurance expense $3,000.
Statement of financial position: Prepayments of $3,000 shown under current assets.
**Explanation**: A common error is ignoring the opening prepayment — the expense for the year includes the opening prepayment because it relates to this year's benefit. The formula is: expense = cash paid + opening prepayment - closing prepayment. The closing prepayment is carried forward as an asset.
**Tags**: prepayments, year-carryforward, expense-calculation

### Question Q-4.5-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: At 1 January 20X4, a business had an accrual for electricity of $600. During the year, electricity bills of $4,800 were paid. At 31 December 20X4, the electricity used but not yet billed is estimated at $900. Show the electricity expense in the statement of profit or loss and all relevant journal entries.
**Answer**: Electricity expense for the year = Cash paid + Closing accrual - Opening accrual = $4,800 + $900 - $600 = $5,100.
The $600 opening accrual relates to last year's usage paid this year — it is not this year's expense. The $900 closing accrual is this year's usage not yet paid — it IS this year's expense.
Journal entries:
(1) To clear opening accrual: Debit Accruals $600, Credit Cash $600 (this is part of the $4,800 paid).
(2) To record closing accrual: Debit Electricity expense (SPL) $900, Credit Accruals $900.
Net electricity expense in SPL: $4,800 paid - $600 (opening accrual cleared) + $900 (closing accrual) = $5,100.
Statement of financial position: Accruals of $900 under current liabilities.
**Explanation**: A common error is thinking the expense is simply the cash paid ($4,800) — the accruals adjust the expense to the matching concept amount. Always use the formula: expense = cash paid + closing accrual - opening accrual. If there is no opening or closing accrual, the expense equals cash paid.
**Tags**: accruals, year-carryforward, expense-formula

### Question Q-4.5-06
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: How should inventory be valued in financial statements? Explain "lower of cost and net realisable value (NRV)" and give an example.
**Answer**: Inventory should be valued at the LOWER of cost and net realisable value (NRV). This follows the prudence concept — inventory should not be stated at more than its recoverable value.
Cost includes: purchase price, import duties, transport to bring inventory to its present location, and any directly attributable costs. Trade discounts are deducted from cost.
NRV is the estimated selling price minus the costs to complete and sell the item (e.g., sales commission, packaging for sale). It is NOT the same as market value or replacement cost.
Example: An item costs $50 to purchase. Due to damage, it can only be sold for $40 with selling costs of $5. NRV = $40 - $5 = $35. Lower of cost ($50) and NRV ($35) = $35. The item is valued at $35, not $50. The $15 write-down is an expense in the statement of profit or loss.
If NRV is higher than cost, inventory is valued at cost — the business does not recognise unrealised profit on inventory.
**Explanation**: A common error is valuing inventory at NRV when NRV is higher than cost — the rule is LOWER of cost and NRV. NRV above cost does not justify an upward revaluation; only a downward write-down is recorded (prudence).
**Tags**: inventory-valuation, lower-of-cost-and-NRV, prudence

### Question Q-4.5-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business uses the FIFO (first in, first out) method for inventory valuation. During the month: 1 March opening inventory 100 units at $5 each; 10 March purchased 200 units at $6 each; 15 March purchased 150 units at $7 each; 20 March sold 280 units. Calculate the value of closing inventory and the cost of goods sold.
**Answer**: Total units available = 100 + 200 + 150 = 450. Units sold = 280. Closing inventory units = 450 - 280 = 170 units.
Under FIFO, the first units purchased are assumed to be sold first. Closing inventory consists of the MOST RECENT purchases.
COGS (280 units): 100 units @ $5 = $500 (from opening inventory) + 180 units @ $6 = $1,080 (from 10 March purchase, leaving 20 units @ $6 unsold). COGS = $500 + $1,080 = $1,580.
Closing inventory (170 units): 20 units @ $6 = $120 (remaining from 10 March) + 150 units @ $7 = $1,050 (all from 15 March). Closing inventory value = $120 + $1,050 = $1,170.
Verification: Total cost of goods available = (100 × $5) + (200 × $6) + (150 × $7) = $500 + $1,200 + $1,050 = $2,750. COGS $1,580 + Closing inventory $1,170 = $2,750. Matches.
**Explanation**: A common error is mixing up FIFO and LIFO — under FIFO, the closing inventory consists of the LATEST (most recent) purchases, not the earliest. COGS uses the OLDEST purchases first. Always think of it as a queue: first in, first out means what remains at the end is what came in last.
**Tags**: inventory-valuation, FIFO, COGS

### Question Q-4.5-08
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business uses the weighted average cost (AVCO) method for inventory valuation. During the month: 1 March opening inventory 100 units at $5 each; 10 March purchased 200 units at $6 each; 15 March purchased 150 units at $7 each; 20 March sold 280 units. Calculate the value of closing inventory and the cost of goods sold using weighted average cost.
**Answer**: Total units available = 100 + 200 + 150 = 450 units. Total cost = (100 × $5) + (200 × $6) + (150 × $7) = $500 + $1,200 + $1,050 = $2,750.
Weighted average cost per unit = $2,750 / 450 units = $6.111... (recurring).
Closing inventory = 170 units × $6.111 = $1,039 (rounded to nearest dollar).
COGS = 280 units × $6.111 = $1,711 (rounded).
Verification: $1,039 + $1,711 = $2,750. (If rounding causes a $1 difference, adjust COGS: COGS = $2,750 - $1,039 = $1,711.)
**Explanation**: A common error is calculating a new weighted average after each purchase (perpetual AVCO) when the question implies periodic AVCO. Under periodic AVCO, one average is calculated for ALL purchases in the period. Under perpetual AVCO, a new average is calculated after each purchase batch. IGCSE exams typically use periodic AVCO unless stated otherwise. Always check which method the question requires.
**Tags**: inventory-valuation, weighted-average-cost, AVCO

### Question Q-4.5-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: At 31 December 20X4, a business has the following: (a) Accrual for wages at 1 Jan 20X4: $1,500. Wages paid during the year: $48,000. Accrual for wages at 31 Dec 20X4: $2,000. (b) Prepayment for rates at 1 Jan 20X4: $800. Rates paid during the year: $6,000. Prepayment for rates at 31 Dec 20X4: $1,200. (c) Inventory: opening inventory $8,000, purchases $52,000, closing inventory valued at lower of cost and NRV = $9,500. Show: (a) the wages expense, (b) the rates expense, (c) the cost of goods sold, and (d) the total operating expenses in the statement of profit or loss for the year ended 31 December 20X4.
**Answer**: (a) Wages expense = Cash paid + Closing accrual - Opening accrual = $48,000 + $2,000 - $1,500 = $48,500.
(b) Rates expense = Cash paid + Opening prepayment - Closing prepayment = $6,000 + $800 - $1,200 = $5,600.
(c) COGS = Opening inventory + Purchases - Closing inventory = $8,000 + $52,000 - $9,500 = $50,500.
(d) Total operating expenses in SPL: COGS $50,500 + Wages $48,500 + Rates $5,600 = $104,600.
Statement of financial position at 31 Dec 20X4:
Current assets: Prepayments $1,200 + Inventory $9,500.
Current liabilities: Accruals $2,000.
**Explanation**: A common error in comprehensive questions is applying the wrong formula — using the accrual formula for prepayments or vice versa. Remember: accruals ADD the closing amount and SUBTRACT the opening (expense = paid + closing accrual - opening accrual); prepayments ADD the opening and SUBTRACT the closing (expense = paid + opening prepayment - closing prepayment). COGS follows a different formula: opening inventory + purchases - closing inventory. Each type has its own pattern.
**Tags**: comprehensive, accruals, prepayments, inventory-valuation
