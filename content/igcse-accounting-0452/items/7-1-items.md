# Items: 7.1 — Accounting concepts application

### Question Q-7.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the going concern concept? How does it affect the preparation of financial statements?
**Answer**: The going concern concept assumes that a business will continue to operate for the foreseeable future (at least 12 months) and will not be forced to close or liquidate its assets. It affects financial statement preparation in several ways: 1) Non-current assets are recorded at historical cost less depreciation, not at their break-up (liquidation) value, because they are expected to be used over their useful lives rather than sold immediately. 2) Expenses are allocated over the periods they benefit (depreciation), rather than written off immediately. 3) Long-term liabilities are classified as non-current, because the business is expected to continue and meet them over time. 4) Prepayments are recorded as assets because the business will continue to benefit from them. If the going concern assumption is doubtful, the financial statements must disclose this and assets may need to be restated at their realizable (break-up) value.
**Explanation**: Going concern is the fundamental assumption underlying all financial statements. A common error is thinking going concern means the business is profitable — it means the business will continue operating, regardless of profitability. An unprofitable business can still be a going concern if it has sufficient cash or funding to continue.
**Tags**: going-concept, accounting-concepts, foundation

### Question Q-7.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the prudence (conservatism) concept? How does it apply to inventory valuation and the treatment of doubtful debts?
**Answer**: The prudence concept requires that assets and profits should not be overstated, and liabilities and losses should not be understated. When there is uncertainty, the more cautious approach should be taken — anticipate losses but not gains. Applications: 1) **Inventory valuation** — inventory is valued at the lower of cost and net realizable value (NRV). If NRV falls below cost (e.g. damaged or obsolete stock), the loss is recognized immediately. If NRV is above cost, the gain is not recognized until the inventory is sold. 2) **Doubtful debts (allowance for receivables)** — an allowance is created for receivables that may not be collected, even though the exact amount is uncertain. This anticipates a possible loss. 3) **Contingent liabilities** — if a loss is probable, it is provided for. 4) **Contingent assets** — if a gain is possible, it is not recognized until it is virtually certain. The prudence concept prevents businesses from presenting an overly optimistic picture of their financial position.
**Explanation**: Prudence means "anticipate no profit, provide for all losses." A common error is confusing prudence with pessimism — prudence does not mean deliberately understating assets; it means being cautious when there is genuine uncertainty.
**Tags**: prudence, conservatism, accounting-concepts, foundation

### Question Q-7.1-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the accruals (matching) concept? How does it apply to the treatment of accruals and prepayments?
**Answer**: The accruals concept (also called the matching concept) states that revenue and expenses should be recorded in the period in which they are earned or incurred, regardless of when cash is received or paid. This ensures that the financial statements reflect the true financial performance of the period. Applications: 1) **Accruals (outstanding expenses)** — expenses incurred but not yet paid are recorded as a liability and charged to the income statement of the current period (e.g. electricity used in December but billed and paid in January). 2) **Prepayments** — expenses paid in advance are recorded as an asset and only the portion relating to the current period is charged to the income statement (e.g. insurance paid for 12 months in advance — only one month's expense is charged this year). 3) **Accrued revenue** — revenue earned but not yet received is recorded as an asset and recognized in the current period's income statement. 4) **Revenue received in advance** — cash received before the service is provided is a liability until the service is delivered. The accruals concept ensures that each period's profit reflects all revenues earned and all expenses incurred in that period.
**Explanation**: Accruals and matching are closely related but not identical — accruals refers to timing of recognition, matching refers to linking expenses to the revenues they helped generate. A common error is thinking accruals only means recording unpaid bills — it also means deferring expenses paid in advance.
**Tags**: accruals, matching, accounting-concepts, standard

### Question Q-7.1-04
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the consistency concept? Why is it important and what happens when a business changes its accounting policy?
**Answer**: The consistency concept requires that once a business adopts an accounting method or policy, it should continue to use it from one period to the next. This is important because: 1) It allows meaningful comparison of financial results over time (trend analysis). 2) It prevents businesses from manipulating profits by switching between methods (e.g. changing depreciation methods to boost profit in a poor year). 3) It ensures that changes in ratios and figures reflect genuine changes in performance, not changes in accounting treatment. When a business changes its accounting policy (e.g. from reducing balance to straight-line depreciation, or from FIFO to AVCO for inventory), it must: 1) Disclose the change in the notes to the financial statements. 2) Explain the reason for the change. 3) Show the financial effect of the change (what profit would have been under the old method). The change should be justified by a genuine improvement in the relevance or reliability of the information, not by a desire to manipulate results.
**Explanation**: Consistency does not mean a business can never change — it means changes must be justified and disclosed. A common error is thinking that changing an accounting policy is always wrong — it is acceptable if it provides more relevant or reliable information, but must be transparently disclosed.
**Tags**: consistency, accounting-policy, standard

### Question Q-7.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the materiality concept? How does it affect decisions about what to include in financial statements and how to present items?
**Answer**: The materiality concept states that financial statements should include all items that are significant enough to influence the decisions of users. An item is material if its omission or misstatement could affect the economic decisions of users. Applications: 1) **Small-value items** — a $10 calculator used for 5 years could theoretically be depreciated over 5 years (matching concept), but it is immaterial — it is expensed immediately for practicality. 2) **Rounding** — financial statements often round to the nearest thousand or million because individual dollars are immaterial. 3) **Disclosure decisions** — a contingent liability of $50,000 for a large company may not require detailed disclosure, but the same amount for a small partnership might be material and require full disclosure. 4) **Aggregation** — small expense items may be grouped together rather than shown separately. Materiality is relative — what is material for one business may not be material for another. It depends on the size of the business, the size of the item relative to total assets or profit, and the nature of the item.
**Explanation**: Materiality is about significance, not precision. A common error is thinking materiality has a fixed dollar threshold — it is relative to the business's size and the user's needs. Always consider both the size and the nature of the item.
**Tags**: materiality, accounting-concepts, standard

### Question Q-7.1-06
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: What is the substance over form concept? Give two examples of how it applies in practice.
**Answer**: The substance over form concept requires that transactions are recorded based on their economic reality (substance) rather than their legal form. This ensures that financial statements present a true and fair view. Examples: 1) **Hire purchase (finance lease) assets** — legally, the asset belongs to the finance company until the final payment. In substance, the business controls and benefits from the asset, so it is recorded as a non-current asset with a corresponding liability, and depreciated over its useful life. 2) **Sale with agreement to repurchase** — a business may "sell" inventory to another party with an agreement to buy it back at a fixed price later. Legally this looks like a sale, but in substance it is a loan secured against inventory. The inventory should remain on the seller's balance sheet and the cash received recorded as a liability. 3) **Revenue recognition** — a business may deliver goods but retain the right to recall them. Legally the goods have been delivered, but in substance the risks and rewards of ownership have not transferred, so revenue should not be recognized.
**Explanation**: Substance over form prevents businesses from using legal structures to misrepresent their financial position. A common error is recording transactions based solely on legal documentation without considering the economic reality.
**Tags**: substance-over-form, accounting-concepts, standard

### Question Q-7.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business has consistently used FIFO for inventory valuation for 10 years. This year, management wants to switch to AVCO because they believe it gives a more accurate picture of inventory cost. The change would reduce the reported profit by $15,000. Discuss whether this change is acceptable and what disclosures are required.
**Answer**: The change is potentially acceptable but must meet specific criteria and disclosures. **Acceptability**: The consistency concept requires that accounting policies remain the same, but a change is permitted if it provides more relevant and reliable information. Management's belief that AVCO gives a more accurate picture is a valid reason if supported by evidence (e.g. inventory items are interchangeable and AVCO better reflects the flow of costs). The change should not be motivated by a desire to manipulate profit. **Required disclosures**: 1) The nature of the change (from FIFO to AVCO) must be stated in the notes. 2) The reason for the change must be explained (more accurate reflection of inventory cost). 3) The financial effect must be quantified — profit under FIFO would have been $15,000 higher. 4) If practicable, comparative figures for the prior year should be restated under AVCO to allow meaningful comparison. **Evaluation**: The change is acceptable if the primary motive is improved accuracy, not profit manipulation. However, switching from FIFO to AVCO after 10 years of FIFO requires strong justification, as users have become accustomed to FIFO-based trend analysis. The $15,000 reduction in profit should be assessed in the context of total profit — if it represents a small percentage, the impact on decision-making may be limited. If it is significant, users need clear disclosure to understand the change.
**Explanation**: Consistency does not prohibit change — it requires transparency. A common error is stating that the change is "not allowed" because of consistency. The key is justification and disclosure.
**Tags**: consistency, accounting-policy-change, FIFO, AVCO, evaluation, challenge

### Question Q-7.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate how the concepts of prudence, going concern, and accruals interact when preparing financial statements for a business that has received a large order (revenue) but the customer's financial position is uncertain. Which concepts apply and how do they influence the treatment?
**Answer**: Three concepts interact in this scenario: **Prudence**: The business should not recognize revenue if there is significant uncertainty about whether the customer will pay. Even if goods have been delivered, if the customer's financial position is doubtful, the business should consider whether the revenue meets the criteria for recognition (probable inflow of economic benefits). If collection is doubtful, an allowance for receivables should be created. **Going concern**: This concept assumes the business will continue operating. If the large order represents a significant portion of the business's revenue and the customer's uncertainty threatens the business's ability to continue, the going concern assumption may be in doubt. The business would need to assess whether it has sufficient alternative revenue sources or funding to continue for the next 12 months. **Accruals**: If revenue is recognized (because the goods have been delivered and the performance obligation is satisfied), it should be recorded in the period of delivery regardless of when cash is received. However, prudence tempers this — if collection is not probable, the revenue should not be recognized even though delivery has occurred. **Interaction**: Accruals says "record it when earned"; prudence says "but only if collection is probable"; going concern asks "if this order is critical, can we survive without it?" The correct treatment is: 1) If delivery has occurred and collection is probable — recognize revenue and receivable (accruals). 2) If delivery has occurred but collection is doubtful — do not recognize revenue, or recognize with a full allowance for the receivable (prudence). 3) If the order's uncertainty threatens the business's survival — disclose going concern issues.
**Explanation**: Accounting concepts often interact and sometimes conflict. A common error is applying one concept in isolation — the correct treatment requires balancing all relevant concepts. Prudence tempers accruals; going concern provides the broader context.
**Tags**: concept-interaction, prudence, going-concern, accruals, revenue-recognition, challenge

### Question Q-7.1-09
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A sole trader's financial statements show: non-current assets recorded at historical cost less depreciation; a personal car used 60% for business and 40% for personal use recorded as a business asset at full value; inventory valued at selling price; a potential lawsuit (probable loss estimated at $25,000) not disclosed; and a change from straight-line to reducing balance depreciation with no disclosure. Identify which accounting concepts have been violated in each case and explain the correct treatment.
**Answer**:

1) **Non-current assets at historical cost less depreciation** — This is CORRECT. It follows the going concern concept (assets will be used over their useful lives) and the accruals concept (cost is allocated over the periods benefited). No violation.

2) **Personal car recorded at full value** — Violates the **business entity concept**, which requires that the owner's personal affairs are kept separate from the business. Only the 60% business use portion should be recorded as a business asset. The personal portion should be excluded or treated as drawings.

3) **Inventory valued at selling price** — Violates the **prudence concept**. Inventory must be valued at the lower of cost and net realizable value. Valuing at selling price anticipates a profit before sale, which is not prudent. The correct treatment is cost (or NRV if lower).

4) **Probable lawsuit loss not disclosed** — Violates the **prudence concept**. A probable loss should be provided for (recorded as a liability and expense). It may also violate the **materiality concept** if $25,000 is significant relative to the business's size.

5) **Depreciation method changed with no disclosure** — Violates the **consistency concept**. While changing the method is permissible if justified, the change must be disclosed in the notes, including the reason and financial effect.

**Explanation**: Each concept addresses a different aspect of faithful representation. A common error is identifying the violation without explaining the correct treatment. Always state both what was wrong and how it should be corrected.

**Tags**: concept-violation, business-entity, prudence, consistency, materiality, comprehensive, challenge
