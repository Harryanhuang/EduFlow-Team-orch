# Items: 6.2 — Profitability ratios

### Question Q-6.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the gross profit margin? How is it calculated and what does it measure?
**Answer**: The gross profit margin measures the percentage of revenue that remains as gross profit after deducting the cost of sales. It is calculated as: Gross profit margin = (Gross profit / Revenue) × 100%. A higher margin indicates that the business is retaining more from each dollar of revenue to cover operating expenses and generate net profit. An increase in the margin could result from higher selling prices, lower cost of sales (cheaper materials or more efficient production), or a change in sales mix towards higher-margin products. A decrease could result from price cuts, rising costs, or increased competition.
**Explanation**: Gross profit margin is expressed as a percentage. A common error is using net profit instead of gross profit in the numerator, or reversing the formula (cost of sales / revenue). Always use gross profit and revenue from the income statement.

### Question Q-6.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the net profit margin (mark-up on cost)? How does it differ from the gross profit margin?
**Answer**: The net profit margin measures the percentage of revenue that remains as net profit after deducting ALL expenses (cost of sales plus operating expenses). It is calculated as: Net profit margin = (Net profit / Revenue) × 100%. It differs from the gross profit margin in that: 1) Gross profit margin only considers cost of sales; net profit margin considers all expenses. 2) The gap between the two margins represents the proportion of revenue consumed by operating expenses. 3) A business can have a healthy gross profit margin but a low or negative net profit margin if operating expenses are too high. The net profit margin reflects the overall efficiency of the business in converting revenue into profit.
**Explanation**: Net profit margin is always lower than gross profit margin (unless there are no operating expenses, which is rare). A common error is confusing net profit with gross profit — net profit is after ALL expenses.
**Tags**: net-profit-margin, profitability, calculation, foundation

### Question Q-6.2-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is ROCE (return on capital employed)? How is it calculated and why is it important?
**Answer**: ROCE measures how efficiently a business uses its capital to generate profit. It is calculated as: ROCE = (Operating profit / Capital employed) × 100%. Where capital employed = total assets minus current liabilities (or equivalently, non-current assets plus working capital, or equity plus non-current liabilities). ROCE is important because: 1) It shows the return generated on the money invested in the business. 2) It can be compared to alternative investments (e.g. bank interest rates) to assess whether the business is using capital effectively. 3) It can be compared across businesses and industries. 4) A ROCE higher than the cost of borrowing indicates the business is creating value; lower than borrowing costs indicates it is destroying value. 5) Trends in ROCE reveal whether the business is becoming more or less efficient at using its capital.
**Explanation**: ROCE is one of the most important profitability ratios. A common error is using net profit after interest and tax instead of operating profit (profit before interest and tax) in the numerator. ROCE measures the return on ALL capital, so the profit figure should be before interest and tax deductions.
**Tags**: ROCE, return-on-capital-employed, profitability, calculation, standard

### Question Q-6.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the difference between markup and margin? Given a cost price and selling price, calculate both.
**Answer**: Markup and margin are both measures of profitability but use different bases: **Markup** = (Gross profit / Cost of sales) × 100%. It expresses profit as a percentage of cost. **Margin** = (Gross profit / Revenue (selling price)) × 100%. It expresses profit as a percentage of selling price. For example, if a product costs $60 and sells for $100: Gross profit = $40. Markup = ($40 / $60) × 100% = 66.7%. Margin = ($40 / $100) × 100% = 40%. The markup is always higher than the margin for the same transaction. They are mathematically related: Markup = Margin / (1 - Margin); Margin = Markup / (1 + Markup). A common error is confusing the two or using the wrong denominator.
**Explanation**: The key is the denominator: markup uses cost, margin uses selling price. A common error is thinking a 50% markup equals a 50% margin — a 50% markup on $100 gives a selling price of $150, but the margin is only 33.3% ($50 / $150).
**Tags**: markup, margin, gross-profit, calculation, standard

### Question Q-6.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business has revenue of $350,000, cost of sales $210,000, operating expenses $85,000. Calculate the gross profit margin, net profit margin, and ROCE if capital employed is $250,000. Comment on the results.
**Answer**:
Gross profit = $350,000 - $210,000 = $140,000
Gross profit margin = ($140,000 / $350,000) × 100% = 40.0%
Net profit = $140,000 - $85,000 = $55,000
Net profit margin = ($55,000 / $350,000) × 100% = 15.7%
ROCE = ($55,000 / $250,000) × 100% = 22.0%
(Using operating profit for ROCE: operating profit = $55,000, assuming no interest or other non-operating items)

Comment: The gross profit margin of 40% is strong — the business retains $0.40 from each dollar of revenue after cost of sales. Operating expenses consume 24.3% of revenue ($85,000 / $350,000), leaving a net profit margin of 15.7%. The ROCE of 22% is good — the business generates $0.22 of profit for every $1.00 of capital employed, which likely exceeds the cost of borrowing. The gap between gross and net margins (40% - 15.7% = 24.3%) shows operating expenses are significant but manageable.
**Explanation**: Always show the working clearly. ROCE should use operating profit (before interest and tax). If interest is given, add it back to net profit to get operating profit. A common error is using net profit after interest for ROCE.
**Tags**: gross-profit-margin, net-profit-margin, ROCE, calculation, analysis, standard

### Question Q-6.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business's gross profit margin has remained constant at 35% over three years, but its net profit margin has declined from 18% to 12% to 8%. What could explain this? What actions should the business take?
**Answer**: If gross profit margin is constant but net profit margin is declining, the problem lies in operating expenses increasing faster than revenue. Possible causes: 1) Rising overhead costs (rent, utilities, insurance) that have not been controlled. 2) Increasing administrative expenses (staff costs, professional fees). 3) Higher distribution or selling expenses (marketing, delivery costs). 4) Increased finance costs (interest on loans) if the business has taken on more debt. 5) One-off expenses (legal fees, restructuring costs). Actions: 1) Review all expense categories and identify which ones are growing fastest. 2) Negotiate better terms with suppliers of overhead services. 3) Reduce discretionary spending (marketing with low return, unnecessary travel). 4) Consider whether revenue growth justifies expense growth (economies of scale should reduce expense as a percentage of revenue). 5) Review staffing levels and operational efficiency.
**Explanation**: The constant gross margin means pricing and cost of sales are stable — the problem is operational efficiency. A common error is suggesting price increases or cost of sales reductions when gross margin is already stable.
**Tags**: profit-margin-decline, expense-analysis, evaluation, standard

### Question Q-6.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the profitability of two businesses: Business A has revenue of $500,000, gross profit $200,000, net profit $75,000, capital employed $400,000. Business B has revenue of $300,000, gross profit $150,000, net profit $60,000, capital employed $200,000. Which business is more profitable and why?
**Answer**:
**Business A:**
- Gross profit margin = ($200,000 / $500,000) × 100% = 40.0%
- Net profit margin = ($75,000 / $500,000) × 100% = 15.0%
- ROCE = ($75,000 / $400,000) × 100% = 18.75%

**Business B:**
- Gross profit margin = ($150,000 / $300,000) × 100% = 50.0%
- Net profit margin = ($60,000 / $300,000) × 100% = 20.0%
- ROCE = ($60,000 / $200,000) × 100% = 30.0%

Business B is more profitable on all three measures despite having lower revenue. Its gross profit margin (50% vs 40%) shows better pricing or lower cost of sales relative to revenue. Its net profit margin (20% vs 15%) shows it controls operating expenses more effectively. Its ROCE (30% vs 18.75%) shows it generates a much higher return on capital invested — for every $1 of capital, B generates $0.30 of profit compared to A's $0.19. Business B uses half the capital to generate 80% of A's profit, indicating much more efficient capital utilization. Business A should consider whether its larger scale is justified by its lower returns, and whether it can reduce costs or improve efficiency.
**Explanation**: Always compare all three ratios, not just one. A common error is concluding A is more profitable because it has higher revenue or higher absolute profit — profitability ratios reveal efficiency, not scale.
**Tags**: comparison, profitability-ratios, ROCE, evaluation, challenge

### Question Q-6.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business has the following data: Revenue $600,000, cost of sales $360,000, operating expenses $144,000, non-current assets $350,000, current assets $180,000, current liabilities $80,000. Calculate all profitability ratios. If the business could reduce cost of sales by 5% without changing revenue, how would each ratio change?
**Answer**:

**Current position:**
- Gross profit = $600,000 - $360,000 = $240,000
- Gross profit margin = ($240,000 / $600,000) × 100% = 40.0%
- Net profit = $240,000 - $144,000 = $96,000
- Net profit margin = ($96,000 / $600,000) × 100% = 16.0%
- Capital employed = $350,000 + ($180,000 - $80,000) = $450,000
- ROCE = ($96,000 / $450,000) × 100% = 21.3%

**After 5% cost of sales reduction:**
- New cost of sales = $360,000 - (5% × $360,000) = $342,000
- New gross profit = $600,000 - $342,000 = $258,000
- New gross profit margin = ($258,000 / $600,000) × 100% = 43.0%
- New net profit = $258,000 - $144,000 = $114,000
- New net profit margin = ($114,000 / $600,000) × 100% = 19.0%
- New ROCE = ($114,000 / $450,000) × 100% = 25.3%

**Impact:**
- Gross profit margin improved by 3.0 percentage points (40.0% to 43.0%)
- Net profit margin improved by 3.0 percentage points (16.0% to 19.0%)
- ROCE improved by 4.0 percentage points (21.3% to 25.3%)
- The net profit margin improvement equals the gross margin improvement because operating expenses are unchanged — the full $18,000 saving flows through to net profit.

**Explanation**: A cost of sales reduction flows through to all three ratios. A common error is not recognizing that the net profit improvement equals the gross profit improvement when expenses are fixed.

**Tags**: profitability-ratios, sensitivity-analysis, calculation, challenge

### Question Q-6.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business's profitability data over three years: Revenue: Year 1 $400,000, Year 2 $480,000, Year 3 $520,000. Cost of sales: Year 1 $240,000, Year 2 $312,000, Year 3 $364,000. Operating expenses: Year 1 $100,000, Year 2 $115,000, Year 3 $130,000. Capital employed: Year 1 $300,000, Year 2 $350,000, Year 3 $400,000. Calculate all profitability ratios for each year and analyze the trend. Is the business improving or deteriorating?
**Answer**:

**Year 1:**
- Gross profit = $160,000 | Gross margin = 40.0%
- Net profit = $60,000 | Net margin = 15.0%
- ROCE = ($60,000 / $300,000) × 100% = 20.0%

**Year 2:**
- Gross profit = $168,000 | Gross margin = ($168,000 / $480,000) × 100% = 35.0%
- Net profit = $53,000 | Net margin = ($53,000 / $480,000) × 100% = 11.0%
- ROCE = ($53,000 / $350,000) × 100% = 15.1%

**Year 3:**
- Gross profit = $156,000 | Gross margin = ($156,000 / $520,000) × 100% = 30.0%
- Net profit = $26,000 | Net margin = ($26,000 / $520,000) × 100% = 5.0%
- ROCE = ($26,000 / $400,000) × 100% = 6.5%

**Trend analysis:** All three ratios are deteriorating significantly. Revenue grew 30% (Year 1 to Year 3) but profitability collapsed. Gross margin fell from 40% to 30% — cost of sales grew 52% while revenue grew only 30%, suggesting rising material costs, price pressure, or a shift to lower-margin products. Net margin fell from 15% to 5% — operating expenses grew 30% (in line with revenue) but the falling gross margin meant much less profit to cover them. ROCE collapsed from 20% to 6.5% — partly because profit fell and partly because capital employed grew 33%. The business is expanding but becoming much less efficient. Concerns: the business may be pursuing revenue growth at the expense of profitability ("buying revenue" with lower prices or higher costs). The ROCE of 6.5% may be below the cost of capital, meaning the business is destroying value. Actions: investigate why cost of sales is growing faster than revenue, review pricing strategy, and consider whether the capital expansion is justified by returns.

**Explanation**: Revenue growth with falling margins is a classic warning sign. A common error is concluding the business is "doing well" because revenue is growing — the ratios show the opposite. Always look at what's driving each component.

**Tags**: comprehensive, profitability-ratios, trend-analysis, ROCE, evaluation, challenge
