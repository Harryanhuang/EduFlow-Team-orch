# Items: 5.6 — Limited companies financial statements

### Question Q-5.6-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a limited company? How does it differ from a sole trader in terms of legal status, liability, and ownership?
**Answer**: A limited company is a business entity that is legally separate from its owners (shareholders). Key differences from a sole trader: 1) **Legal status**: A limited company is a separate legal person — it can own property, sue, and be sued in its own name. A sole trader and their business are the same legal entity. 2) **Liability**: Shareholders have limited liability — they can only lose the amount they invested (the value of their shares). A sole trader has unlimited liability — personal assets are at risk for business debts. 3) **Ownership**: A limited company is owned by shareholders who hold shares. A sole trader owns the business entirely. 4) **Management**: A limited company is managed by directors appointed by shareholders. A sole trader makes all decisions personally. 5) **Financing**: A limited company can raise capital by issuing shares. A sole trader cannot issue shares.
**Explanation**: The key concept is separate legal personality. A common error is thinking limited liability means the company cannot go bankrupt — the company can, but shareholders' personal assets are protected.
**Tags**: limited-company, sole-trader, legal-status, liability, shares

### Question Q-5.6-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is share capital? Distinguish between ordinary shares and preference shares.
**Answer**: Share capital is the money raised by a company through the issue of shares to investors (shareholders). **Ordinary shares**: 1) Carry voting rights — shareholders can vote at general meetings. 2) Dividends are variable — paid only if the company makes enough profit and the amount is decided by directors. 3) Last to be paid in liquidation — ordinary shareholders receive any remaining assets after all other claims are settled. 4) No fixed rate of return. **Preference shares**: 1) Usually do not carry voting rights. 2) Receive a fixed dividend rate (e.g. 8% preference shares) before ordinary shareholders receive any dividend. 3) Have priority over ordinary shares in liquidation. 4) Dividends are cumulative (if the company cannot pay in one year, unpaid dividends accumulate and must be paid before ordinary dividends in future years).
**Explanation**: The key difference is risk and reward. Ordinary shareholders are true owners with voting power but uncertain returns. Preference shareholders are closer to lenders — fixed income, priority, but no control. A common error is thinking preference shares are debt — they are equity, and dividends are appropriations, not expenses.
**Tags**: share-capital, ordinary-shares, preference-shares, equity

### Question Q-5.6-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What are debentures? How does debenture (loan) interest differ from dividends on shares?
**Answer**: Debentures are long-term loans secured against the company's assets. Debenture holders are lenders to the company, not owners. They receive a fixed rate of interest regardless of whether the company makes a profit. Debenture interest differs from share dividends in four key ways: 1) **Legal obligation**: Debenture interest is a legal obligation — the company MUST pay it even if it makes a loss. Dividends are only paid if the company has sufficient distributable profits. 2) **Accounting treatment**: Debenture interest is a finance charge in the income statement (an expense). Dividends are an appropriation of profit shown after net profit in the appropriation account. 3) **Tax treatment**: Debenture interest is tax-deductible (reduces taxable profit). Dividends are paid from after-tax profit and are not tax-deductible. 4) **Security**: Debentures are secured against company assets; shares represent ownership with no security.
**Explanation**: The critical distinction is debt vs equity. A common error is treating debenture interest as an appropriation — it is an expense and appears in the income statement, reducing profit before tax.
**Tags**: debentures, loan-interest, dividends, debt-vs-equity

### Question Q-5.6-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is directors' remuneration? How is it treated in a company's financial statements?
**Answer**: Directors' remuneration is the total pay and benefits received by the directors of a company for their services. It typically includes: directors' salaries, bonuses, benefits in kind (e.g. company car, health insurance), and pension contributions. In the financial statements: 1) Directors' remuneration is treated as an **expense** in the income statement (usually under administrative expenses). 2) It is deducted when calculating profit before tax. 3) It is NOT an appropriation of profit — unlike partners' salaries in a partnership, directors are employees of the company. 4) The total amount of directors' remuneration must be disclosed in the notes to the financial statements (this is a legal requirement for published accounts).
**Explanation**: Directors are employees of the company, so their remuneration is an expense. A common error is treating directors' remuneration as an appropriation (like partners' salaries) — this is only correct for partnerships, not for limited companies.
**Tags**: directors-remuneration, expense, income-statement

### Question Q-5.6-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How is corporation income tax treated in a limited company's financial statements?
**Answer**: Corporation income tax (or corporate income tax) is the tax that a limited company pays on its taxable profit. In the financial statements: 1) Corporation tax is shown as a separate line item in the income statement, deducted from profit before tax to arrive at profit after tax (profit for the year). 2) It is calculated based on taxable profit, which may differ from accounting profit due to tax adjustments (e.g. disallowable expenses, tax allowances on assets). 3) The unpaid corporation tax appears as a current liability in the statement of financial position until it is paid. 4) Corporation tax is NOT shown in the appropriation account — dividends are paid from profit AFTER tax. 5) In the appropriation account, the starting figure is profit after tax, not profit before tax.
**Explanation**: Corporation tax is a major expense. A common error is confusing corporation tax (paid by the company) with personal income tax (paid by individuals). In exams, the tax rate or amount is usually given. Always deduct tax before showing appropriations.
**Tags**: corporation-tax, income-tax, profit-after-tax, current-liability

### Question Q-5.6-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a company's appropriation account? How does it differ from a partnership's appropriation account?
**Answer**: A company's appropriation account shows how the profit after tax is distributed. It starts with profit after tax (profit for the year), then shows: dividends paid or proposed (ordinary and preference), and transfers to reserves (e.g. general reserve, retained earnings). The company's appropriation account differs from a partnership's in several ways: 1) **Starting point**: Company — profit after tax. Partnership — net profit (before tax, as partnerships do not pay corporation tax). 2) **Items**: Company — dividends to shareholders and transfers to reserves. Partnership — interest on capital, partners' salaries, interest on drawings, residual profit share. 3) **Directors' salaries**: Company — treated as an expense in the income statement. Partnership — treated as an appropriation. 4) **Tax**: Company shows corporation tax in the income statement. Partnerships do not. 5) **Reserves**: Companies may transfer profit to reserves. Partnerships distribute all profit to partners.
**Explanation**: The structure reflects the different ownership model. A common error is including directors' salaries in the company appropriation account — they are an expense, not an appropriation.
**Tags**: appropriation-account, company, partnership, dividends, reserves

### Question Q-5.6-07

### Question Q-5.6-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: What are the key features of published financial statements for a limited company? How do they differ from the financial statements of a sole trader or partnership?
**Answer**: Published financial statements for a limited company typically include: 1) **Statement of comprehensive income** (income statement) — showing revenue, cost of sales, gross profit, expenses, finance costs, profit before tax, tax, and profit after tax. 2) **Statement of financial position** (balance sheet) — showing non-current assets, current assets, equity (share capital, share premium, reserves, retained earnings), non-current liabilities, and current liabilities. 3) **Statement of changes in equity** — showing movements in share capital, share premium, reserves, and retained earnings during the period. 4) **Statement of cash flows** — showing cash generated from operating, investing, and financing activities. 5) **Notes to the accounts** — providing detailed breakdowns, accounting policies, and disclosures (e.g. directors' remuneration, contingencies, related party transactions). Key differences from sole trader/partnership accounts: a) More structured format following legal requirements (e.g. Companies Act). b) Equity section shows share capital and reserves instead of a single capital account. c) Additional statements (cash flows, changes in equity). d) Extensive notes and disclosures. e) Must comply with accounting standards (e.g. IFRS).
**Explanation**: The format is prescribed by law and accounting standards. A common error is presenting company equity like a sole trader's capital account — companies have share capital, share premium, reserves, and retained earnings as separate components.

**Tags**: published-financial-statements, company-accounts, format, disclosure, challenge

### Question Q-5.6-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A company has the following: issued ordinary share capital $400,000 (shares of $1 each), 6% preference share capital $100,000, share premium account $80,000, general reserve $50,000, retained earnings at start of year $120,000. Profit after tax for the year is $95,000. Directors propose a final ordinary dividend of 5 cents per share and pay the preference dividend. Prepare the statement of changes in equity showing all movements.
**Answer**:

**Statement of Changes in Equity for the year ended:**

| | Share capital $ | Share premium $ | General reserve $ | Retained earnings $ | Total $ |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Balance at start of year | 400,000 | 80,000 | 50,000 | 120,000 | 650,000 |
| Profit for the year | — | — | — | 95,000 | 95,000 |
| Preference dividend (6% × 100,000) | — | — | — | (6,000) | (6,000) |
| Ordinary dividend (5c × 400,000) | — | — | — | (20,000) | (20,000) |
| **Balance at end of year** | **400,000** | **80,000** | **50,000** | **189,000** | **719,000** |

**Workings:**
- Ordinary dividend = 5 cents × 400,000 shares = $20,000
- Preference dividend = 6% × $100,000 = $6,000
- Retained earnings end = $120,000 + $95,000 − $6,000 − $20,000 = $189,000
- Total equity = $400,000 + $80,000 + $50,000 + $189,000 = $719,000

**Explanation**: All equity movements flow through retained earnings except profit (which adds) and dividends (which reduce). Share capital and share premium only change when new shares are issued. A common error is deducting dividends from share capital instead of retained earnings, or forgetting that the ordinary dividend is calculated per share (5c × number of shares).

**Tags**: statement-of-changes-in-equity, dividends, retained-earnings, comprehensive, challenge
