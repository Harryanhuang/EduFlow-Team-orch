# Items: 6.1 — Liquidity ratios

### Question Q-6.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the current ratio? How is it calculated and what does it measure?
**Answer**: The current ratio is a liquidity ratio that measures a business's ability to pay its short-term debts (current liabilities) using its short-term assets (current assets). It is calculated as: Current ratio = Current assets / Current liabilities. A ratio of 1.5:1 to 2:1 is generally considered healthy for most businesses — it means the business has $1.50 to $2.00 of current assets for every $1.00 of current liabilities. A ratio below 1:1 suggests the business may struggle to meet short-term obligations. A ratio that is too high may indicate idle resources (e.g. excess inventory or receivables not being collected). The ratio is expressed as a number (e.g. 1.8) or as a ratio (e.g. 1.8:1).
**Explanation**: The current ratio includes all current assets, including inventory. A common error is reversing the formula (current liabilities / current assets). Always check whether inventory should be included — if the question asks for the acid test ratio, inventory is excluded.
**Tags**: current-ratio, liquidity, calculation, foundation

### Question Q-6.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the acid test (quick) ratio? How does it differ from the current ratio?
**Answer**: The acid test ratio (also called the quick ratio) is a stricter measure of liquidity that excludes inventory from current assets, because inventory may not be quickly or easily converted to cash. It is calculated as: Acid test ratio = (Current assets - Inventory) / Current liabilities. Alternatively: Acid test ratio = (Cash + Receivables + Short-term investments) / Current liabilities. The acid test differs from the current ratio by excluding inventory, providing a more conservative view of immediate liquidity. A ratio of 1:1 is generally considered acceptable — the business can meet its short-term obligations without selling inventory. For businesses that turn inventory over quickly (e.g. supermarkets), a lower acid test may be acceptable.
**Explanation**: The acid test is more stringent. A common error is including prepaid expenses in the numerator — prepayments cannot be converted to cash and should be excluded. Only the most liquid assets (cash, receivables, short-term investments) are included.
**Tags**: acid-test-ratio, quick-ratio, liquidity, calculation, foundation

### Question Q-6.1-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A business has current assets of $180,000 (inventory $65,000, receivables $75,000, cash $40,000) and current liabilities of $90,000. Calculate the current ratio and acid test ratio. Comment on the results.
**Answer**:
Current ratio = $180,000 / $90,000 = 2.0:1
Acid test ratio = ($180,000 - $65,000) / $90,000 = $115,000 / $90,000 = 1.28:1

Comment: The current ratio of 2.0:1 is at the upper end of the acceptable range (1.5:1 to 2:1), suggesting the business has sufficient current assets to cover short-term debts. However, a ratio of 2.0:1 may indicate some idle resources — particularly the $65,000 in inventory, which represents 36% of current assets. The acid test ratio of 1.28:1 is above the 1:1 benchmark, confirming the business can meet its immediate obligations even without selling inventory. The gap between the two ratios (2.0 vs 1.28) indicates inventory is a significant portion of current assets, which may be a concern if inventory is slow-moving.
**Explanation**: Always comment on both ratios and what the gap between them reveals about inventory levels. A common error is calculating the acid test without properly excluding inventory, or commenting on only one ratio.
**Tags**: current-ratio, acid-test-ratio, calculation, analysis, standard

### Question Q-6.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the working capital cycle (cash conversion cycle)? How is it calculated and why does it matter?
**Answer**: The working capital cycle (also called the cash conversion cycle) measures the number of days it takes for a business to convert its investment in inventory and other current assets into cash received from customers. It is calculated as: Working capital cycle = Inventory turnover days + Receivables collection days - Payables payment days. Where: Inventory turnover days = (Inventory / Cost of sales) × 365; Receivables collection days = (Receivables / Revenue) × 365; Payables payment days = (Payables / Cost of purchases) × 365. A shorter cycle is generally better — it means the business receives cash from customers faster than it pays suppliers. A long or negative cycle may indicate cash flow problems. The cycle matters because it directly affects the business's cash flow and need for external financing.
**Explanation**: The key insight is that the working capital cycle links three separate ratios into one measure of cash flow efficiency. A common error is adding payables days instead of subtracting them — remember: payables delay cash outflows, which shortens the cycle.
**Tags**: working-capital-cycle, cash-conversion-cycle, calculation, standard

### Question Q-6.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is overtrading? What liquidity symptoms indicate a business may be overtrading?
**Answer**: Overtrading occurs when a business expands its operations too quickly without having sufficient long-term finance to support the growth. Liquidity symptoms of overtrading include: 1) **Deteriorating current ratio** — current liabilities grow faster than current assets as the business stretches its resources. 2) **Low or declining acid test ratio** — the business struggles to maintain liquid assets. 3) **Lengthening receivables collection period** — the business offers extended credit to attract sales but cannot collect quickly. 4) **Increasing inventory levels** — the business buys more stock to support growth but cannot sell it fast enough. 5) **Extending payables payment period** — the business delays paying suppliers to conserve cash, damaging supplier relationships. 6) **Frequent use of bank overdraft** — the business relies on short-term borrowing to fund day-to-day operations. 7) **High revenue growth but low cash balances** — the business is growing on paper but has no cash.
**Explanation**: Overtrading is a growth-without-finance problem. A common error is confusing overtrading with general poor management — the key is that growth itself creates the liquidity pressure because long-term finance has not been increased to match.
**Tags**: overtrading, liquidity, symptoms, analysis, standard

### Question Q-6.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What measures can a business take to improve its liquidity position? Evaluate the advantages and disadvantages of each.
**Answer**: Key measures: 1) **Reduce receivables collection period** — offer early payment discounts, tighten credit control, factor receivables. Advantage: improves cash inflows quickly. Disadvantage: discounts reduce profit margins; strict credit terms may lose customers. 2) **Reduce inventory levels** — use just-in-time ordering, sell obsolete stock, improve demand forecasting. Advantage: frees up cash and reduces storage costs. Disadvantage: risk of stockouts and lost sales. 3) **Extend payables payment period** — negotiate longer credit terms with suppliers. Advantage: retains cash longer. Disadvantage: may damage supplier relationships; lose early payment discounts. 4) **Sale and leaseback of non-current assets** — sell assets and lease them back. Advantage: generates immediate cash. Disadvantage: long-term lease costs may exceed sale proceeds. 5) **Increase long-term finance** — issue shares, take a long-term loan. Advantage: provides stable funding. Disadvantage: cost of capital; dilution of ownership. 6) **Reduce drawings or dividends** — retain more cash in the business. Advantage: immediate improvement. Disadvantage: owners/shareholders receive less return.
**Explanation**: Each measure has trade-offs. A common error is recommending a single solution — in practice, businesses use a combination. The best choice depends on the cause of the liquidity problem.
**Tags**: liquidity-improvement, evaluation, measures, standard

### Question Q-6.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business's liquidity ratios over three years are: Year 1 — current ratio 1.8:1, acid test 1.1:1; Year 2 — current ratio 1.5:1, acid test 0.9:1; Year 3 — current ratio 1.3:1, acid test 0.7:1. Analyze the trend and identify possible causes and concerns.
**Answer**: Both ratios show a clear declining trend over three years, indicating worsening liquidity. The current ratio has fallen from 1.8:1 (healthy) to 1.3:1 (below the 1.5:1 benchmark), while the acid test has fallen from 1.1:1 (acceptable) to 0.7:1 (concerning — the business cannot cover current liabilities without selling inventory). The gap between the two ratios has narrowed (0.7 in Year 1, 0.6 in Year 2, 0.6 in Year 3), suggesting inventory is becoming a larger proportion of the remaining current assets. Possible causes: 1) Current liabilities increasing faster than current assets (e.g. trade payables growing due to delayed payments). 2) Cash balances declining (e.g. used to fund expansion or cover losses). 3) Receivables growing more slowly than payables. Concerns: The business may face difficulty paying suppliers on time, may need to increase short-term borrowing, and could be approaching overtrading. Immediate action should focus on improving cash inflows (tighter credit control, faster collections) and managing payables.
**Explanation**: Trend analysis is more valuable than a single year's figure. A common error is stating the ratios without interpreting the trend or linking causes to the specific pattern of decline.
**Tags**: trend-analysis, liquidity, current-ratio, acid-test-ratio, challenge

### Question Q-6.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether a supermarket chain and a furniture retailer would have different 'acceptable' current and acid test ratios. Explain why.
**Answer**: Yes, acceptable ratios differ significantly by industry. A supermarket chain typically has: 1) **Low current ratio** (possibly below 1:1) — this is acceptable because supermarkets sell inventory very quickly (high inventory turnover), generate large amounts of cash daily, and often have strong bargaining power to delay paying suppliers (long payables period). Their working capital cycle may even be negative — they receive cash from customers before paying suppliers. 2) **Low acid test ratio** — supermarkets hold minimal receivables (most sales are cash/card), so the acid test is naturally low but not concerning. A furniture retailer typically has: 1) **Higher current ratio** (1.5:1 to 2:1) — furniture has slower inventory turnover, meaning more capital is tied up in stock. Receivables may also be significant if the retailer offers credit terms. 2) **Higher acid test ratio needed** — because inventory cannot be quickly converted to cash, the business needs more liquid assets to cover short-term obligations. The key principle is that ratio benchmarks are industry-specific. Comparing ratios across industries without context leads to incorrect conclusions.
**Explanation**: Context matters. A common error is applying the same benchmark (1.5:1 to 2:1 for current ratio) to all businesses. Always consider the industry, business model, and working capital cycle when interpreting ratios.
**Tags**: industry-comparison, current-ratio, acid-test-ratio, evaluation, challenge

### Question Q-6.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business has the following information for two years: Revenue: Year 1 $800,000, Year 2 $1,200,000. Cost of sales: Year 1 $480,000, Year 2 $780,000. Current assets: Year 1 $200,000 (inventory $80,000), Year 2 $280,000 (inventory $130,000). Current liabilities: Year 1 $120,000 (payables $60,000), Year 2 $200,000 (payables $110,000). Receivables: Year 1 $90,000, Year 2 $120,000. Calculate all relevant liquidity ratios and the working capital cycle for each year. Comment on the business's liquidity position and whether it shows signs of overtrading.
**Answer**:

**Year 1:**
- Current ratio = $200,000 / $120,000 = 1.67:1
- Acid test = ($200,000 - $80,000) / $120,000 = 1.0:1
- Inventory days = ($80,000 / $480,000) × 365 = 60.8 days
- Receivables days = ($90,000 / $800,000) × 365 = 41.1 days
- Payables days = ($60,000 / $480,000) × 365 = 45.6 days
- Working capital cycle = 60.8 + 41.1 - 45.6 = 56.3 days

**Year 2:**
- Current ratio = $280,000 / $200,000 = 1.4:1
- Acid test = ($280,000 - $130,000) / $200,000 = 0.75:1
- Inventory days = ($130,000 / $780,000) × 365 = 61.0 days
- Receivables days = ($120,000 / $1,200,000) × 365 = 36.5 days
- Payables days = ($110,000 / $780,000) × 365 = 51.5 days
- Working capital cycle = 61.0 + 36.5 - 51.5 = 46.0 days

**Comment:** Revenue grew 50% ($800,000 to $1,200,000) but liquidity deteriorated: current ratio fell from 1.67:1 to 1.4:1, acid test from 1.0:1 to 0.75:1. Current liabilities grew 67% while current assets grew only 40%. Inventory increased 63% and payables 83% — the business is stretching supplier payments to fund growth. Receivables days improved (41.1 to 36.5), but this is overshadowed by the rapid growth in payables. The working capital cycle improved from 56.3 to 46.0 days, but this is driven by extending payables, not improved efficiency. These are classic overtrading symptoms — revenue growth outpacing the long-term finance available. The business should consider raising long-term capital and managing its working capital more carefully.

**Explanation**: Always calculate all three component ratios before commenting. The working capital cycle improvement here is misleading — it comes from paying suppliers more slowly, not from better operations. A common error is concluding the business is 'improving' based solely on the shorter cycle without analyzing the components.

**Tags**: comprehensive, liquidity-ratios, working-capital-cycle, overtrading, calculation, challenge
