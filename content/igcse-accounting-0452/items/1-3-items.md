# Items: 1.3 — Assets, liabilities and capital

### Question Q-1.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a non-current asset? Give three examples.
**Answer**: A non-current asset is an asset that a business intends to keep for more than one year and uses in the operations of the business to generate income. Examples: machinery, buildings, vehicles, equipment, land. Non-current assets are not held for resale.
**Explanation**: The key test is intention to keep for more than one year, not the physical size or cost. A common error is classifying any expensive item as non-current — a cheap laptop used for 3 years is non-current; an expensive batch of inventory for resale is current.
**Tags**: non-current-assets, definition, classification

### Question Q-1.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a current asset? Give three examples.
**Answer**: A current asset is an asset that is expected to be converted into cash, sold, or consumed within one year (or within the normal operating cycle of the business). Examples: cash, bank balance, inventory (stock of goods), trade receivables (debtors), prepaid expenses.
**Explanation**: The one-year time frame is the key distinguishing feature. A common error is including non-current assets like equipment in the current assets list. Inventory is a current asset even though it may take months to sell.
**Tags**: current-assets, definition, examples

### Question Q-1.3-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the difference between a current liability and a non-current liability?
**Answer**: A current liability is a debt that must be repaid within one year (e.g., trade payables, bank overdraft, short-term loans). A non-current liability is a debt that is not due for repayment until more than one year has passed (e.g., a mortgage repayable over 20 years, a five-year bank loan). The distinction is based on the time until payment is due.
**Explanation**: The one-year boundary is the same test used for assets. A common error is confusing the type of debt with its timing — a bank loan can be either current or non-current depending on when repayment is due.
**Tags**: liabilities, current, non-current, classification

### Question Q-1.3-04
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is capital (owner's equity) in accounting?
**Answer**: Capital (or owner's equity) is the amount the owner has invested in the business. It equals the owner's initial contribution plus any profits retained in the business, minus any drawings (withdrawals) the owner has taken out. In the accounting equation: Capital = Assets - Liabilities.
**Explanation**: Capital is not the same as cash — it represents the owner's claim on the business's net assets. A common error is equating capital with the cash the owner put in — capital includes accumulated profits and excludes drawings.
**Tags**: capital, owners-equity, definition

### Question Q-1.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Classify each of the following as current asset, non-current asset, current liability, non-current liability, or capital: (a) Delivery van, (b) Trade payables, (c) Inventory, (d) Bank loan repayable in 5 years, (e) Owner's contribution.
**Answer**: (a) Non-current asset — a vehicle used in operations for more than one year. (b) Current liability — amounts owed to suppliers, normally due within one year. (c) Current asset — inventory is expected to be sold within one year. (d) Non-current liability — repayment is due in more than one year. (e) Capital — the owner's investment in the business.
**Explanation**: Classification depends on both the nature of the item and the time frame. A common error is classifying trade payables as non-current — they are short-term obligations to suppliers.
**Tags**: classification, application, assets, liabilities

### Question Q-1.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the difference between trade receivables and trade payables?
**Answer**: Trade receivables (debtors) are amounts owed to the business by customers who have bought goods on credit — they are a current asset because the business expects to receive cash. Trade payables (creditors) are amounts the business owes to suppliers who have provided goods on credit — they are a current liability because the business must pay cash in the future.
**Explanation**: Receivables = money coming in (asset); payables = money going out (liability). A common error is reversing these or thinking both are assets because they both involve "trade."
**Tags**: trade-receivables, trade-payables, distinction

### Question Q-1.3-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business buys a machine for $50,000. After three years of depreciation, its net book value is $35,000. Is the machine shown at $50,000 or $35,000 in the statement of financial position? Explain.
**Answer**: The machine is shown at its net book value of $35,000 (cost of $50,000 less accumulated depreciation of $15,000). The statement of financial position reports non-current assets at net book value, not at original cost. However, notes to the accounts typically show both cost and accumulated depreciation separately.
**Explanation**: Net book value reflects the portion of the asset's cost that has not yet been allocated as an expense. A common error is showing assets at original cost without deducting accumulated depreciation.
**Tags**: net-book-value, depreciation, statement-of-financial-position

### Question Q-1.3-08
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why drawings are not treated as a business expense in accounting.
**Answer**: Drawings are withdrawals of cash or other assets by the owner for personal use. They are not expenses incurred in earning revenue — they represent a reduction of the owner's capital (equity), not a cost of operations. Expenses appear in the income statement; drawings appear in the capital section of the statement of financial position as a deduction from capital.
**Explanation**: The business entity concept separates the owner's personal transactions from the business's transactions. A common error is treating drawings as an expense because cash leaves the business — the key distinction is whether the outflow was for business operations or personal use.
**Tags**: drawings, expense, business-entity-concept

### Question Q-1.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business has the following balances: Cash $8,000, Inventory $15,000, Trade receivables $12,000, Machinery $45,000, Trade payables $10,000, Bank loan $30,000. Calculate total current assets, total non-current assets, total current liabilities, total non-current liabilities, and capital.
**Answer**: Current assets = Cash $8,000 + Inventory $15,000 + Trade receivables $12,000 = $35,000. Non-current assets = Machinery $45,000. Current liabilities = Trade payables $10,000. Non-current liabilities = Bank loan $30,000. Capital = Total assets - Total liabilities = ($35,000 + $45,000) - ($10,000 + $30,000) = $80,000 - $40,000 = $40,000.
**Explanation**: Apply the accounting equation step by step. A common error is including the bank loan in current liabilities without checking whether it is due within one year — the question states it as a bank loan (implying long-term). Always check the description carefully.
**Tags**: calculation, application, accounting-equation
