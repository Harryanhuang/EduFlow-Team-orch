# Items: 3.1 — Trial balance

### Question Q-3.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a trial balance?
**Answer**: A trial balance is a list of all the ledger accounts and their balances at a specific date. It has two columns: debit balances in the left column and credit balances in the right column. The total of the debit column should equal the total of the credit column, confirming that the double entry has been maintained.
**Explanation**: A trial balance is not a financial statement — it is a checking tool used to verify arithmetical accuracy. A common error is confusing a trial balance with the statement of financial position — a trial balance lists all account balances (including revenue and expenses), while a statement of financial position only shows assets, liabilities and capital.
**Tags**: trial-balance, definition, core

### Question Q-3.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the main purpose of preparing a trial balance?
**Answer**: The main purpose is to check that the total of all debit balances equals the total of all credit balances, confirming that the double entry bookkeeping has been arithmetically correct. It also serves as a convenient summary of all ledger balances from which the financial statements can be prepared.
**Explanation**: A trial balance checks mathematical accuracy, not completeness or correctness of classification. A common error is saying "a trial balance proves the accounts are correct" — it only proves that debits equal credits; many types of errors can still exist.
**Tags**: trial-balance, purpose, accuracy

### Question Q-3.1-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Which of the following account types normally have debit balances and which have credit balances? (a) Assets, (b) Liabilities, (c) Capital, (d) Expenses, (e) Revenue, (f) Drawings.
**Answer**: Debit balances: (a) Assets, (d) Expenses, (f) Drawings. Credit balances: (b) Liabilities, (c) Capital, (e) Revenue. In the trial balance, each account is placed in the column matching its normal balance type.
**Explanation**: This follows the DEALER framework. A common error is putting revenue in the debit column — revenue increases capital and capital increases are credits, so revenue has a credit balance.
**Tags**: debit-balance, credit-balance, classification

### Question Q-3.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A trial balance shows: Purchases $12,000 (Dr), Sales $45,000 (Cr), Rent $3,600 (Dr), Capital $20,000 (Cr), Bank $8,000 (Dr), Trade payables $6,400 (Cr), Trade receivables $7,200 (Dr), Drawings $4,000 (Dr), Inventory $5,600 (Dr), Bank loan $10,000 (Cr). Verify whether the trial balance agrees.
**Answer**: Debit column total = $12,000 + $3,600 + $8,000 + $7,200 + $4,000 + $5,600 = $40,400. Credit column total = $45,000 + $20,000 + $6,400 + $10,000 = $81,400. The trial balance does NOT agree — credits exceed debits by $41,000. There must be an error in the bookkeeping (an imbalance in double entry, or an omitted debit).
**Explanation**: When the trial balance does not agree, look for missing entries. Here, the $45,000 sales (credit) has no corresponding debit entry listed — the missing debit would be cash/receivables. A common error is only adding one column and assuming it balances.
**Tags**: trial-balance, calculation, verification

### Question Q-3.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Which types of errors can a trial balance detect? Give two examples.
**Answer**: A trial balance can detect errors that cause debits not to equal credits. Examples: 1) Single entry error — recording only the debit side of a transaction without the corresponding credit (or vice versa). 2) Transposition error — posting different amounts to the debit and credit sides (e.g., debit $540, credit $450). 3) Calculation error — incorrect balancing of an account leading to a wrong balance being extracted.
**Explanation**: The trial balance only catches mathematical imbalances. A common error is thinking it can detect all errors — it cannot detect errors where debits still equal credits despite being wrong.
**Tags**: trial-balance, detectable-errors

### Question Q-3.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: List four types of errors that a trial balance CANNOT detect and explain why each does not affect the agreement of debits and credits.
**Answer**: 1) Error of omission — a complete transaction is not recorded at all, so neither debit nor credit is entered; both sides remain equal. 2) Error of commission — a transaction is posted to the wrong account but the correct side (e.g., crediting supplier A instead of supplier B); debits and credits are still equal. 3) Error of principle — a transaction is posted to the wrong type of account but with correct debit/credit sides (e.g., capital expenditure recorded as expense); the amounts still balance. 4) Compensating errors — two or more errors cancel each other out mathematically; the totals happen to agree.
**Explanation**: All these errors maintain debit = credit equality despite being wrong. A common error is listing "error of original entry" without explaining — this is where the wrong amount is used in both debit and credit entries, so they still balance.
**Tags**: trial-balance, undetectable-errors, explanation

### Question Q-3.1-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A bookkeeper purchases a non-current asset (a machine for $5,000 cash) but records it as: Debit Purchases $5,000, Credit Cash $5,000. Will the trial balance detect this error? Explain.
**Answer**: No. The trial balance will still agree because debits ($5,000) equal credits ($5,000). This is an error of principle — the machine (a non-current asset) should have been debited to the Machinery account, not the Purchases account (which is for goods/inventory). The debit/credit sides are correct, but the wrong account type was used. The trial balance cannot detect this.
**Explanation**: The trial balance only checks that debits equal credits, not whether the right accounts were chosen. A common error is saying "yes it will be detected because Purchases is wrong" — the trial balance has no way of knowing which accounts should have been used.
**Tags**: error-of-principle, undetectable, application

### Question Q-3.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A trial balance disagrees because the debit column exceeds the credit column by $1,080. Investigation reveals two errors: (1) a rent payment of $360 was recorded as Debit Rent $360 with no credit entry; (2) a cash sale of $360 was recorded as Debit Cash $360, Debit Sales $360 (instead of Credit Sales $360). Explain how these errors produce the $1,080 imbalance and what corrections are needed.
**Answer**: Error 1: The rent payment debit was recorded but no credit was made. Debits exceed credits by $360. Error 2: The cash sale should have been Debit Cash $360, Credit Sales $360. Cash debit is correct, but Sales was debited $360 instead of credited $360. This means debits are $360 too high AND credits are $360 too low, so debits exceed credits by $720 from this error. Combined: $360 + $720 = $1,080 debit excess. Corrections: 1) Credit Cash $360 to complete the rent entry. 2) Credit Sales $720 to reverse the incorrect debit ($360) and record the correct credit ($360).
**Explanation**: When an entry is on the wrong side (debit instead of credit), it creates a double imbalance: the wrong column gains an amount the right column loses. A common error is counting only one side of the wrong-side error, underestimating its contribution to the difference.
**Tags**: error-correction, trial-balance, wrong-side-entry

### Question Q-3.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: "If a trial balance agrees, the accounts must be correct." Do you agree? Justify your answer with reference to the limitations of a trial balance.
**Answer**: Disagree. An agreeing trial balance only proves that total debits equal total credits. It does NOT prove: 1) All transactions have been recorded (error of omission — a missing transaction affects neither side). 2) Transactions are in the correct accounts (error of commission — wrong account, correct side). 3) The correct account types have been used (error of principle — capital vs revenue). 4) The original amounts were correct (error of original entry — wrong amount on both sides). 5) Errors have not cancelled out (compensating errors). 6) Complete reversals of entry (debit recorded as credit and vice versa for the same amount). Financial statements prepared from an agreeing but erroneous trial balance will still contain errors.
**Explanation**: This is a fundamental limitation of the trial balance. A common error is agreeing with the statement — the entire topic of "errors not revealed by trial balance" exists because an agreeing trial balance does not guarantee correctness.
**Tags**: evaluation, trial-balance, limitations
