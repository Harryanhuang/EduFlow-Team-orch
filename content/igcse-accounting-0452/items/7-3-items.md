# Items: 7.3 — Accounting policy changes

### Question Q-7.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is an accounting policy? Give three examples of accounting policies a business might choose.
**Answer**: An accounting policy is the specific principle, basis, convention, rule or practice adopted by a business in preparing and presenting its financial statements. It is the method chosen from available alternatives under accounting standards. Examples: 1) **Inventory valuation** — choosing between FIFO (first in, first out) and AVCO (weighted average cost) for valuing inventory. 2) **Depreciation method** — choosing between straight-line (equal charge each year) and reducing balance (higher charge in early years) for depreciating non-current assets. 3) **Receivables allowance** — choosing the basis for calculating the allowance for doubtful debts (e.g. percentage of receivables, specific review of individual debts, or historical loss rates). Each policy choice affects the reported profit and asset values, so consistency in applying the chosen policy is essential.
**Explanation**: A common error is confusing accounting policies with accounting concepts. Concepts (e.g. going concern, prudence) are fundamental principles that must always be followed. Policies are the specific methods chosen within those concepts. A business must follow concepts but has some choice in policies.
**Tags**: accounting-policy, concepts, foundation

### Question Q-7.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Why might a business change its accounting policy? Give two legitimate reasons and one illegitimate reason.
**Answer**: Legitimate reasons: 1) **Improved relevance and reliability** — the new policy provides more useful information to users. For example, switching from FIFO to AVCO because inventory items are interchangeable and AVCO better reflects the average cost flow. 2) **Change in accounting standard** — a new or revised accounting standard requires or permits the change. For example, a new standard requiring all leases to be recognized on the balance sheet. 3) **Change in business circumstances** — the old policy is no longer appropriate. For example, switching depreciation methods because the pattern of economic benefits from an asset has changed. Illegitimate reason: **Profit manipulation** — changing a policy solely to improve reported profit or meet a target, such as switching from reducing balance to straight-line depreciation to reduce the depreciation charge and boost profit in a poor year. This is misleading because it does not reflect genuine economic change.
**Explanation**: The test is whether the change provides better information, not whether it improves profit. A common error is accepting any change as long as it is disclosed — disclosure alone does not make a change legitimate.
**Tags**: accounting-policy-change, legitimate, illegitimate, foundation

### Question Q-7.3-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business changes from FIFO to AVCO for inventory valuation. Opening inventory under FIFO was $40,000; under AVCO it would have been $36,000. Closing inventory under FIFO is $50,000; under AVCO it would be $44,000. Cost of sales under FIFO was $200,000. Calculate the adjusted cost of sales under AVCO and explain the effect on profit.
**Answer**: Under FIFO: Opening inventory $40,000 + Purchases - Closing inventory $50,000 = Cost of sales $200,000. So Purchases = $200,000 + $50,000 - $40,000 = $210,000. Under AVCO: Opening inventory $36,000 + Purchases $210,000 - Closing inventory $44,000 = Cost of sales $202,000. Alternatively: The difference in cost of sales = change in opening inventory difference + change in closing inventory difference = ($40,000 - $36,000) - ($50,000 - $44,000) = $4,000 - $6,000 = -$2,000. So cost of sales under AVCO = $200,000 + $2,000 = $202,000. Effect on profit: Cost of sales is $2,000 higher under AVCO, so profit is $2,000 lower. The change reduces reported profit by $2,000.
**Explanation**: Always adjust both opening and closing inventory when recalculating cost of sales. A common error is only adjusting the closing inventory — the opening inventory change also affects cost of sales because it is the starting point of the calculation.
**Tags**: FIFO, AVCO, policy-change, cost-of-sales, calculation, standard

### Question Q-7.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business changes depreciation from reducing balance (25%) to straight-line (10%) on a machine that cost $80,000 and has been depreciated for 3 years. Calculate the depreciation charge for Year 4 under both methods and the difference.
**Answer**: 
**Reducing balance (25%):**
- Year 1: 25% × $80,000 = $20,000 | NBV = $60,000
- Year 2: 25% × $60,000 = $15,000 | NBV = $45,000
- Year 3: 25% × $45,000 = $11,250 | NBV = $33,750
- Year 4 charge: 25% × $33,750 = $8,438

**Straight-line (10% of cost):**
- Year 4 charge: 10% × $80,000 = $8,000 (assuming no residual value and 10-year life)

**Difference:** $8,438 - $8,000 = $438 lower charge under straight-line in Year 4.
The change from reducing balance to straight-line reduces the Year 4 depreciation by $438, increasing profit by that amount. Note that in earlier years the reducing balance charge was much higher ($20,000, $15,000, $11,250 vs $8,000 each year under straight-line), so the cumulative effect is that reducing balance has already charged $46,250 vs $32,000 under straight-line — a difference of $14,250.

**Explanation**: The direction of change matters. Switching from reducing balance to straight-line typically reduces the charge in later years (increasing profit) but the cumulative depreciation up to the change date is already different. A common error is only comparing the current year without considering the cumulative effect.
**Tags**: depreciation, policy-change, reducing-balance, straight-line, calculation, standard

### Question Q-7.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What disclosures are required when a business changes an accounting policy? Why are these disclosures important?
**Answer**: Required disclosures: 1) **Nature of the change** — what policy was changed and what the new policy is (e.g. "changed from FIFO to AVCO for inventory valuation"). 2) **Reason for the change** — why the new policy provides more relevant and reliable information, or reference to the new accounting standard that requires the change. 3) **Financial effect** — the amount of the adjustment for the current period and each prior period presented. This includes the effect on revenue, profit, assets, liabilities, and equity. 4) **Restated comparatives** — if practicable, the financial statements for prior periods should be restated under the new policy to allow meaningful comparison. These disclosures are important because: 1) Users need to understand why figures have changed — was it genuine performance or just a change in method? 2) Trend analysis depends on consistent measurement; changes distort trends unless adjusted. 3) Transparency prevents management from hiding poor performance behind policy changes. 4) Users can assess whether the change is genuinely an improvement or profit manipulation.
**Explanation**: Disclosure is not optional — it is a requirement under accounting standards. A common error is stating the change and reason without quantifying the financial effect.
**Tags**: disclosure, accounting-policy-change, transparency, standard

### Question Q-7.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business has used the reducing balance method for depreciating its delivery vehicles for 5 years. It now wants to switch to straight-line because management believes it will result in a more consistent charge. Vehicles cost $150,000 total, accumulated depreciation under reducing balance is $75,000. Under straight-line, accumulated depreciation would have been $60,000. Discuss whether the change is justified and what the financial impact would be.
**Answer**: **Justification**: Management's desire for a "more consistent charge" is a potentially valid reason if the pattern of economic benefits from vehicles is more even over time (straight-line) rather than front-loaded (reducing balance). However, the change should be justified by the pattern of benefits, not by a desire to manipulate profit. **Financial impact**: The accumulated depreciation difference is $75,000 - $60,000 = $15,000 more charged under reducing balance. This means: 1) Net book value under reducing balance = $150,000 - $75,000 = $75,000. Under straight-line = $150,000 - $60,000 = $90,000. 2) If the change is applied prospectively (from the current year forward), the NBV of $75,000 will be depreciated using straight-line over the remaining useful life. 3) Prior periods are NOT restated — the $15,000 difference remains in accumulated depreciation. 4) The current year's depreciation charge will be based on $75,000 NBV over remaining life, which will be different from what it would have been under continuing reducing balance. **Disclosure required**: Nature of change, reason, and effect on current year's depreciation charge. The cumulative difference of $15,000 should be disclosed.
**Explanation**: Prospective application means the change affects the current and future periods only — prior periods are not restated. A common error is restating prior accumulated depreciation when the change is prospective.
**Tags**: depreciation-policy-change, justification, prospective, standard

### Question Q-7.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business changed from straight-line to reducing balance depreciation in Year 3, claiming the new method better reflects the asset's usage pattern. Profit before depreciation was $100,000 each year. Asset cost $60,000, no residual value, 5-year life. Calculate profit under both methods for all 5 years and evaluate whether the change improved or distorted the financial picture.
**Answer**:

**Straight-line (12,000 per year = $60,000/5):**
- Y1: Profit = $100,000 - $12,000 = $88,000
- Y2: Profit = $88,000
- Y3: Profit = $88,000
- Y4: Profit = $88,000
- Y5: Profit = $88,000
- Total: $440,000

**Reducing balance (40% to fully depreciate in 5 years approx):**
Using 40% rate:
- Y1: Dep = 40% × $60,000 = $24,000 | Profit = $76,000
- Y2: Dep = 40% × $36,000 = $14,400 | Profit = $85,600
- Y3: Dep = 40% × $21,600 = $8,640 | Profit = $91,360
- Y4: Dep = 40% × $12,960 = $5,184 | Profit = $94,816
- Y5: Dep = remaining $7,776 | Profit = $92,224
- Total: $440,000

**Evaluation:** The change in Year 3 increased profit from $88,000 to $91,360 (by $3,360). In Year 4, profit increased from $88,000 to $94,816. The change front-loaded depreciation (higher in early years, lower in later years). If the asset genuinely loses value more quickly in early years (e.g. technology), reducing balance is more appropriate. If the change was made in Year 3 specifically to boost declining profits, it is manipulation. The total profit over 5 years is the same ($440,000) — the change only affects timing.

**Explanation:** The total is the same under both methods — only timing differs. A common error is concluding the change "improved" performance without recognizing that total profit is unchanged.

**Tags**: depreciation-policy-change, profit-distortion, evaluation, challenge

### Question Q-7.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare the effects of changing from FIFO to AVCO during a period of rising prices versus falling prices. How does the price trend affect the direction and magnitude of the policy change impact?
**Answer**: During **rising prices**: FIFO assigns the oldest (cheapest) costs to cost of sales and the newest (most expensive) costs to closing inventory. AVCO assigns average costs to both. Therefore: 1) FIFO gives lower cost of sales → higher profit. 2) FIFO gives higher closing inventory → higher current assets and total assets. 3) Switching from FIFO to AVCO during rising prices: cost of sales increases, profit decreases, closing inventory decreases. During **falling prices**: FIFO assigns the oldest (most expensive) costs to cost of sales and the newest (cheapest) costs to closing inventory. Therefore: 1) FIFO gives higher cost of sales → lower profit. 2) FIFO gives lower closing inventory → lower assets. 3) Switching from FIFO to AVCO during falling prices: cost of sales decreases, profit increases, closing inventory increases. The **magnitude** of the impact depends on: 1) The rate of price change — faster changes create larger differences. 2) The volume and turnover of inventory — faster turnover reduces the difference because inventory is replaced more frequently. 3) The size of the inventory balance relative to total assets. The key principle is that the direction of the price trend determines whether the change increases or decreases profit and assets.
**Explanation:** The price trend is critical — the same policy change has opposite effects depending on whether prices are rising or falling. A common error is assuming FIFO always gives higher profit — it only does so during rising prices.
**Tags**: FIFO, AVCO, rising-prices, falling-prices, policy-change, challenge

### Question Q-7.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business has changed three accounting policies in the same year: (1) FIFO to AVCO for inventory, (2) reducing balance to straight-line for depreciation, and (3) increasing the receivables allowance from 3% to 5%. Revenue is $500,000, profit before these changes is $60,000. The changes reduce profit by $8,000 (AVCO), increase profit by $5,000 (depreciation), and reduce profit by $4,000 (allowance). Calculate the adjusted profit and evaluate whether management's combination of changes is likely to be viewed positively or negatively by users.
**Answer**:

**Adjusted profit:**
- Profit before changes: $60,000
- AVCO change: -$8,000
- Depreciation change: +$5,000
- Allowance change: -$4,000
- Net effect: -$7,000
- Adjusted profit: $53,000

**Evaluation:** Three policy changes in one year is unusual and may raise concerns. Individually: (1) FIFO to AVCO could be justified if inventory items are interchangeable. (2) Reducing balance to straight-line could be justified if the asset usage pattern is even. (3) Increasing the allowance from 3% to 5% is a change in estimate, not a policy change — it should be justified by evidence of deteriorating customer credit quality. Combined effect: the changes reduce profit by $7,000 (11.7% reduction). This is a significant impact. Users may view this negatively if: (a) the changes appear designed to "take a bath" — reducing profit in an already poor year to make future years look better by comparison. (b) there is no clear justification for each change. (c) the timing coincides with a change in management (new management often blames predecessors for poor results and takes all losses in their first year). Users would want detailed disclosures for each change, including the rationale and quantified effect. If the changes are genuinely improvements in measurement, they should be viewed positively in the long term, even though current profit is lower.

**Explanation:** Multiple policy changes in one year are a red flag. A common error is treating an allowance percentage change as a policy change — it is a change in estimate, which is handled differently (prospectively, with disclosure of the effect but no restatement).

**Tags**: multiple-policy-changes, big-bath, profit-manipulation, comprehensive, challenge
