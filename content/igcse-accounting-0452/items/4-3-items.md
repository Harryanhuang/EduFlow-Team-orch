# Items: 4.3 — Disposal of non-current assets

### Question Q-4.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is meant by the "disposal" of a non-current asset? Give three examples of how a non-current asset may be disposed of.
**Answer**: Disposal of a non-current asset means removing the asset from the business's accounting records when it is no longer in use. This occurs when the asset is sold, scrapped (thrown away because it is no longer usable), traded in for a new asset, given away, or destroyed. When an asset is disposed of, two things must be removed from the books: (1) the original cost of the asset (credit the asset account), and (2) the accumulated depreciation on that asset up to the date of disposal (debit the accumulated depreciation account). Any difference between the net carrying amount and the proceeds received is recorded as a profit or loss on disposal.
**Explanation**: A common error is thinking disposal only means selling an asset for cash — an asset can also be disposed of by scrapping (zero proceeds), trading in, or destruction. In all cases, the cost and accumulated depreciation must be removed from the books.
**Tags**: disposal, definition, non-current-assets

### Question Q-4.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What are the journal entries to record the disposal of a non-current asset? A machine costing $10,000 with accumulated depreciation of $7,000 is sold for $4,000 cash. Show all journal entries.
**Answer**: The journal entries for any disposal are: (1) Debit Disposal account, Credit Non-current asset account — with the original cost of the asset. (2) Debit Accumulated depreciation account, Credit Disposal account — with the accumulated depreciation to the date of disposal. (3) Debit Cash (or proceeds), Credit Disposal account — with the sale proceeds received. (4) Balance the disposal account: if credit side is greater, there is a profit (Debit Disposal, Credit Profit on disposal in statement of profit or loss); if debit side is greater, there is a loss (Debit Loss on disposal in statement of profit or loss, Credit Disposal account).
For the machine: (1) Dr Disposal $10,000, Cr Machinery (cost) $10,000. (2) Dr Accumulated depreciation $7,000, Cr Disposal $7,000. (3) Dr Cash $4,000, Cr Disposal $4,000. Disposal account: Debits = $10,000, Credits = $7,000 + $4,000 = $11,000. Credit exceeds debit by $1,000 — profit on disposal. (4) Dr Disposal $1,000, Cr Profit on disposal (SPL) $1,000.
**Explanation**: A common error is trying to calculate profit/loss as proceeds minus cost — it is proceeds minus carrying amount (cost minus accumulated depreciation). Here: $4,000 - ($10,000 - $7,000) = $4,000 - $3,000 = $1,000 profit. The disposal account is a temporary clearing account that must balance to zero.
**Tags**: disposal, journal-entry, profit-on-disposal

### Question Q-4.3-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: How do you calculate the profit or loss on disposal of a non-current asset? Equipment costing $25,000 has accumulated depreciation of $18,000. It is sold for $5,500. Calculate the profit or loss on disposal.
**Answer**: Profit or loss on disposal = Sale proceeds minus Carrying amount at the date of disposal. Carrying amount = Cost minus Accumulated depreciation. For the equipment: Carrying amount = $25,000 - $18,000 = $7,000. Sale proceeds = $5,500. Loss on disposal = $7,000 - $5,500 = $1,500 loss. The loss appears in the statement of profit or loss as an expense (or deducted from other income). A profit would appear as income (or a negative expense). If the asset was scrapped with zero proceeds, the loss would be the full carrying amount ($7,000).
**Explanation**: A common error is comparing proceeds with the original cost ($5,500 - $25,000 = -$19,500) — this is wrong because $18,000 of depreciation has already been charged to profit or loss in previous years. The loss on disposal only reflects the difference between what the asset was "worth" on the books ($7,000) and what was actually received ($5,500).
**Tags**: disposal, profit-or-loss-calculation, carrying-amount

### Question Q-4.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business bought a vehicle for $36,000 on 1 January 20X2. Depreciation is charged at 25% per annum using the straight-line method, residual value $6,000, useful life 4 years. On 31 December 20X3 (end of year 2) the vehicle is sold for $19,000. Calculate the profit or loss on disposal and show the disposal account.
**Answer**: Annual depreciation = ($36,000 - $6,000) / 4 = $7,500 per year. The asset was owned for exactly 2 years (1 Jan 20X2 to 31 Dec 20X3). Accumulated depreciation at disposal = $7,500 × 2 = $15,000. Carrying amount at disposal = $36,000 - $15,000 = $21,000. Sale proceeds = $19,000. Loss on disposal = $21,000 - $19,000 = $2,000 loss.
Disposal account:
Debit side: Vehicle (cost) $36,000 | Profit on disposal —
Credit side: Accumulated depreciation $15,000 | Cash (proceeds) $19,000 | Loss on disposal (SPL) $2,000
Total debits: $36,000. Total credits: $15,000 + $19,000 + $2,000 = $36,000. Account balances.
**Explanation**: A common error is forgetting that the accumulated depreciation must be removed from the books separately from the cost. The disposal account shows both the cost going out (debit) and the accumulated depreciation being cleared (credit). The loss balances the account — it is not an extra entry but the difference that makes debits equal credits.
**Tags**: disposal, straight-line, disposal-account

### Question Q-4.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A machine costs $50,000 on 1 January 20X1. It is depreciated at 30% per annum using the reducing balance method. On 30 June 20X3 the machine is sold for $15,000. Calculate the profit or loss on disposal. Show the entries that appear in the statement of profit or loss for the year ended 31 December 20X3.
**Answer**: Year 1 (20X1): Depreciation = $50,000 × 30% = $15,000. Carrying amount = $50,000 - $15,000 = $35,000. Year 2 (20X2): Depreciation = $35,000 × 30% = $10,500. Carrying amount = $35,000 - $10,500 = $24,500. Year 3 (to 30 June = 6 months): Full year on $24,500 × 30% = $7,350. Time-apportioned 6/12 = $3,675. Carrying amount at disposal = $24,500 - $3,675 = $20,825. Accumulated depreciation at disposal = $15,000 + $10,500 + $3,675 = $29,175. Sale proceeds = $15,000. Loss on disposal = $20,825 - $15,000 = $5,825 loss.
Statement of profit or loss for year ended 31 Dec 20X3: Depreciation expense $3,675 (6 months) + Loss on disposal $5,825 = total charge $9,500.
**Explanation**: A common error is charging a full year's reducing balance depreciation in the year of disposal — depreciation must be time-apportioned to the date of disposal (6 months here). Another error is using the carrying amount at the start of the year without charging the partial year depreciation first — the carrying amount at disposal must include depreciation up to the disposal date.
**Tags**: disposal, reducing-balance, time-apportionment

### Question Q-4.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What happens when a non-current asset is traded in (part-exchanged) for a new asset? A business part-exchanges an old vehicle (cost $28,000, accumulated depreciation $19,600) as part of the purchase of a new vehicle priced at $35,000. The dealer allows $10,000 for the old vehicle. Calculate the profit or loss on disposal and show the net cash payment required.
**Answer**: The trade-in allowance ($10,000) is treated as the "sale proceeds" of the old asset. Carrying amount of old vehicle = $28,000 - $19,600 = $8,400. Profit on disposal = $10,000 - $8,400 = $1,600 profit. The profit appears in the statement of profit or loss.
Net cash payment: The new vehicle costs $35,000. The dealer allows $10,000 for the old vehicle. Net cash payable = $35,000 - $10,000 = $25,000.
Journal entries: (1) Dr Disposal $28,000, Cr Vehicle (cost) $28,000. (2) Dr Accumulated depreciation $19,600, Cr Disposal $19,600. (3) Dr New vehicle $35,000, Cr Cash $25,000, Cr Disposal (trade-in) $10,000. (4) Dr Disposal $1,600, Cr Profit on disposal (SPL) $1,600.
**Explanation**: A common error is treating the trade-in as two separate transactions (sell old + buy new) without recognising that the trade-in allowance IS the proceeds. The profit on disposal is real and must be recorded — it is not cancelled out by the purchase of the new asset. The new asset is recorded at its full cost ($35,000), not the net cash paid.
**Tags**: disposal, trade-in, part-exchange

### Question Q-4.3-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business has a policy of depreciating vehicles at 25% per annum on a reducing balance basis. The following vehicles were on the register at 1 January 20X4: Vehicle A cost $30,000 (bought 1 Jan 20X2), Vehicle B cost $22,000 (bought 1 July 20X3). On 31 March 20X4, Vehicle A was sold for $14,000. No depreciation is charged in the year of disposal. Calculate: (a) the accumulated depreciation on Vehicle A at the date of disposal, (b) the profit or loss on disposal of Vehicle A, (c) the total depreciation charge for the year ended 31 December 20X4.
**Answer**: (a) Vehicle A: No depreciation in year of disposal (per policy). At 1 Jan 20X4, Vehicle A has been depreciated for 2 years (20X2 and 20X3). Year 1: $30,000 × 25% = $7,500. Carrying = $22,500. Year 2: $22,500 × 25% = $5,625. Carrying = $16,875. Accumulated depreciation at disposal (31 Mar 20X4) = $7,500 + $5,625 = $13,125 (no depreciation in 20X4 per policy). (b) Carrying amount = $30,000 - $13,125 = $16,875. Proceeds $14,000. Loss = $16,875 - $14,000 = $2,875 loss. (c) Vehicle B only: at 1 Jan 20X4, Vehicle B owned for 6 months (Jul-Dec 20X3). Year 20X3 dep = $22,000 × 25% × 6/12 = $2,750. Carrying at 1 Jan 20X4 = $22,000 - $2,750 = $19,250. Year 20X4 full year dep = $19,250 × 25% = $4,813. Total depreciation charge for 20X4 = $4,813 (Vehicle B only, Vehicle A disposed before year end and no dep in year of disposal per policy).
**Explanation**: A common error is applying the standard time-apportionment rule when the question states "no depreciation in the year of disposal" — the policy overrides the usual approach. Always read the depreciation policy carefully. This is a common IGCSE exam technique: the policy may differ from the default.
**Tags**: disposal, no-depreciation-policy, multi-asset

### Question Q-4.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A company's non-current asset register shows the following at 1 January 20X4: Equipment cost $60,000, accumulated depreciation $24,000. The equipment was purchased on 1 January 20X2 with a 5-year useful life and no residual value, using straight-line depreciation. On 1 October 20X4, one item of equipment (original cost $12,000, purchased 1 January 20X2) is sold for $4,500. Calculate: (a) the accumulated depreciation on the disposed item at the date of disposal, (b) the profit or loss on disposal, (c) the carrying amount of the remaining equipment at 31 December 20X4.
**Answer**: First identify the specific disposed item: Cost $12,000, purchased 1 Jan 20X2. Annual depreciation on this item = $12,000 / 5 = $2,400 per year. (a) Disposed on 1 Oct 20X4 = 9 months into 20X4. Depreciation for 20X4 on this item = $2,400 × 9/12 = $1,800. Prior years (20X2 + 20X3): $2,400 × 2 = $4,800. Total accumulated depreciation on disposed item = $4,800 + $1,800 = $6,600. (b) Carrying amount = $12,000 - $6,600 = $5,400. Proceeds $4,500. Loss = $5,400 - $4,500 = $900 loss. (c) Remaining equipment: Original total cost $60,000 - disposed $12,000 = $48,000 remaining cost. Original total accumulated at 1 Jan 20X4 = $24,000. Verification: $24,000 / $60,000 = 40% depreciated = 2 years of 20% rate — consistent. Disposed item's accumulated at 1 Jan 20X4 = $12,000 × 2/5 = $4,800. Remaining accumulated at 1 Jan 20X4 = $24,000 - $4,800 = $19,200. Remaining equipment annual depreciation = $48,000 / 5 = $9,600. 20X4 full year depreciation on remaining = $9,600. Accumulated at 31 Dec 20X4 = $19,200 + $9,600 = $28,800. Carrying amount = $48,000 - $28,800 = $19,200.
**Explanation**: A common error is not isolating the specific disposed item's depreciation from the total. When a question says "one item" from a pool, you must pro-rate the accumulated depreciation to identify the disposed item's share. The key is that each item depreciates independently: $12,000 / 5 = $2,400 per year for the specific item, not a share of the pool's total.
**Tags**: disposal, multi-step, asset-register

### Question Q-4.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: How does the treatment of disposal of non-current assets appear in the financial statements? A company has the following for the year ended 31 December 20X4: Opening balance of non-current assets at cost $100,000, opening accumulated depreciation $40,000. During the year: purchased new equipment $20,000; disposed of equipment (cost $15,000, accumulated depreciation $9,000) for $7,500; depreciation charge for the year $12,000. Show the cost, accumulated depreciation, and carrying amount sections of the statement of financial position and the disposal impact on the statement of profit or loss.
**Answer**:
Non-current assets — Cost: Opening balance $100,000 + Additions $20,000 - Disposals $15,000 = Closing balance $105,000.
Non-current assets — Accumulated depreciation: Opening balance $40,000 + Charge for the year $12,000 - Eliminated on disposal $9,000 = Closing balance $43,000.
Carrying amount at 31 Dec 20X4: $105,000 - $43,000 = $62,000.
Verification: Opening carrying amount = $100,000 - $40,000 = $60,000. Movement: Additions $20,000 - Disposals (carrying amount) $6,000 - Depreciation charge $12,000 = Net change $2,000. Closing = $60,000 + $2,000 = $62,000. Matches.

Statement of profit or loss impact: Depreciation expense $12,000 + Profit on disposal ($7,500 - $6,000 = $1,500 profit, so net charge is reduced by $1,500). The profit on disposal of $1,500 appears as other income (or netted against depreciation expense).

**Explanation**: A common error in the statement of financial position is adding the disposed asset's cost to the closing balance or forgetting to eliminate its accumulated depreciation. The accumulated depreciation on the disposed asset ($9,000) is SUBTRACTED from the accumulated depreciation column — it is being removed from the books, not added. In the reconciliation: Closing carrying amount = Opening + Additions - Disposals (at carrying amount) - Depreciation charge.
**Tags**: disposal, financial-statements, reconciliation
