# Items: 1.2 — Users of accounting information

### Question Q-1.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Who are the users of accounting information? Classify them into two groups.
**Answer**: Users of accounting information are classified into: Internal users — owners, managers, employees who work within the business and use information for day-to-day decisions. External users — investors, banks/creditors, government/tax authorities, suppliers, customers, and the public who are outside the business but have an interest in its financial affairs.
**Explanation**: The key distinction is whether the user works inside the organisation. A common error is classifying investors as internal users — even though they own shares, they are external unless they are also involved in management.
**Tags**: users-of-accounting, internal, external, classification

### Question Q-1.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What financial information does the owner of a business need and why?
**Answer**: The owner needs information about profitability (profit or loss), financial position (assets and liabilities), and cash flow. This helps the owner assess whether the business is making a return on their investment, whether their capital is growing or declining, and whether the business can meet its obligations. The owner also needs this information to decide whether to expand, withdraw funds, or close the business.
**Explanation**: Owners focus on return on investment and capital preservation. A common error is only saying "owners need to know profit" — they also need liquidity, solvency and cash flow information.
**Tags**: owner, information-needs, profitability

### Question Q-1.2-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Why does a bank need accounting information before lending money to a business?
**Answer**: A bank needs accounting information to assess: 1) The business's ability to repay the loan (profitability and cash flow). 2) The value of assets available as security (collateral). 3) The existing level of debt (gearing) to determine if the business is already over-borrowed. 4) Liquidity ratios to check short-term payment ability. Without this information the bank cannot evaluate credit risk.
**Explanation**: Banks are primarily concerned with repayment ability, not profit maximisation. A common error is saying "banks want to know if the business is profitable" — profitability is relevant only insofar as it supports loan repayment.
**Tags**: bank, creditor, lending, credit-risk

### Question Q-1.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How do employees use accounting information? Give two examples.
**Answer**: 1) Employees use accounting information to assess job security — if the business is profitable and growing, their jobs are more secure. 2) Employees or their trade unions use profit data when negotiating wage increases and bonuses, arguing that employees should share in the business's success. In some businesses, employees may also participate in profit-sharing schemes based on accounting results.
**Explanation**: Employees are internal users with a direct stake in the business's performance. A common error is forgetting that employees are accounting information users at all — many students only list owners, managers and external parties.
**Tags**: employees, internal-users, wages

### Question Q-1.2-05
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What accounting information does the government (tax authority) need from a business?
**Answer**: The government needs information about the business's revenue, expenses and profit to calculate the correct amount of tax owed. It may also need information about specific taxable items such as sales tax collected, payroll taxes, and import duties. The government uses this to verify that the business is paying the correct tax amount and complying with tax laws.
**Explanation**: Government use of accounting information is primarily for tax assessment and compliance. A common error is confusing government's need (tax calculation) with management's need (decision-making).
**Tags**: government, tax-authority, compliance

### Question Q-1.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A supplier is considering offering trade credit to a new business customer. What accounting information would the supplier want to see and why?
**Answer**: The supplier would want to see the business's statement of financial position (to assess assets vs liabilities and net worth), recent income statement (to check profitability), and liquidity position (current ratio). This information shows whether the customer can pay their debts on time. The supplier may also check payment history with other suppliers through credit reference agencies.
**Explanation**: Suppliers are concerned about trade receivables becoming irrecoverable. A common error is focusing only on the customer's sales volume — a high-sales business with poor cash management may still default.
**Tags**: supplier, trade-credit, application

### Question Q-1.2-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Explain why managers need more detailed accounting information than owners.
**Answer**: Managers need detailed, frequent (often weekly or monthly) reports broken down by department, product, or region to make operational decisions such as pricing, cost control, resource allocation, and performance evaluation. Owners typically need summary-level information (annual reports) to assess overall investment returns. Managers run the business day-to-day and need granular data; owners evaluate the business as a whole.
**Explanation**: The level of detail reflects the different roles — managers operate, owners invest. A common error is assuming owners and managers need the same information — in large businesses they are different people with different needs.
**Tags**: manager, detail, operational-decision

### Question Q-1.2-08
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Why might a potential investor (someone who does not yet own shares) want accounting information about a business?
**Answer**: A potential investor wants accounting information to assess whether the business offers a good return on investment compared to alternatives. They look at profitability trends, dividend history, growth prospects, and financial stability to decide whether to invest. Without this information they cannot evaluate the risk and return of the investment.
**Explanation**: Potential investors are external users evaluating whether to commit capital. A common error is confusing potential investors with existing owners — both use accounting information but for different decisions (invest vs. continue holding).
**Tags**: investor, investment-decision, external-users

### Question Q-1.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: "All users need the same accounting information." Do you agree? Justify your answer with reference to three different user groups.
**Answer**: Disagree. Different users have different needs: 1) A bank focuses on liquidity and gearing to assess loan repayment risk — it cares less about detailed revenue breakdowns. 2) A manager needs detailed cost analysis by department for operational control — summary profit figures are insufficient. 3) A tax authority needs accurate profit figures and allowable expense details for tax computation — growth projections are irrelevant. While all users rely on the same financial statements, each group extracts and interprets different information. This is why businesses produce both general-purpose financial statements and specialised management reports.
**Explanation**: The syllabus emphasises that users have different information needs. A common error is agreeing with the statement — the whole topic exists to show that needs differ. Strong answers reference specific user groups with specific information requirements.
**Tags**: evaluation, users, information-needs
