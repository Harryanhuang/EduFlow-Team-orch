# Items: 2.6 — The petty cash book

### Question Q-2.6-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is petty cash? Give three examples of petty cash expenditures.
**Answer**: Petty cash is a small amount of cash kept by a business for minor, everyday expenses. Examples: postage stamps, office supplies (pens, paper), taxi fares, tea/coffee for the staff room, small repairs, delivery charges. Petty cash avoids the need to write cheques or make bank transfers for very small amounts.
**Explanation**: Petty cash is for small, frequent payments where writing a cheque would be impractical. A common error is including large purchases in petty cash — petty cash is for minor amounts only, and what qualifies as "minor" depends on the business's imprest amount.
**Tags**: petty-cash, definition, examples

### Question Q-2.6-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the imprest system of petty cash?
**Answer**: The imprest system is a method of managing petty cash where a fixed amount (the imprest amount) is set aside at the beginning of a period. As petty cash payments are made during the period, the cash balance reduces. At the end of the period, the total amount spent is reimbursed (topped up) from the main cash/bank account, restoring the imprest amount for the next period.
**Explanation**: The imprest system ensures control — the amount available at any time is fixed and predictable. A common error is thinking the imprest amount is spent and not replenished — the key feature is that it is restored each period.
**Tags**: imprest-system, petty-cash, definition

### Question Q-2.6-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: How is the reimbursement amount calculated under the imprest system?
**Answer**: The reimbursement amount equals the total of all petty cash payments (supported by vouchers/receipts) made during the period. When the business reimburses petty cash, it pays an amount equal to what was spent, restoring the fund to the original imprest amount. Formula: Reimbursement = Total petty cash payments = Opening imprest amount - Closing cash balance.
**Explanation**: The reimbursement restores the fund to its fixed level. A common error is reimbursing the difference between the imprest amount and the closing balance without verifying against vouchers — this could mask missing cash.
**Tags**: reimbursement, imprest-system, calculation

### Question Q-2.6-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: The petty cash imprest amount is $200. During the week, payments were made as follows: postage $35, stationery $28, cleaning $42, delivery $15, travel $20. How much is needed to reimburse the petty cash fund? What is the closing cash balance before reimbursement?
**Answer**: Total payments = $35 + $28 + $42 + $15 + $20 = $140. Closing cash balance = $200 - $140 = $60. Reimbursement needed = $140 (to restore the fund to $200). After reimbursement, the petty cash balance returns to $200.
**Explanation**: Under the imprest system, reimbursement always equals total payments. A common error is reimbursing the closing balance ($60) instead of the payments total ($140).
**Tags**: imprest-system, calculation, reimbursement

### Question Q-2.6-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What are analysis columns in a petty cash book? Why are they useful?
**Answer**: Analysis columns are separate columns in the petty cash book that categorise payments by expense type (e.g., postage, stationery, travel, cleaning). Each payment is recorded in the total column and also in the relevant analysis column. They are useful because they automatically total each category, making it easy to post the correct amount to each expense account in the ledger at the end of the period.
**Explanation**: Analysis columns save time at period end — instead of adding up each category manually, the column total can be posted directly. A common error is recording payments only in the total column and not in the analysis column — this defeats the purpose of having analysis columns.
**Tags**: analysis-columns, petty-cash, posting

### Question Q-2.6-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A petty cash book has the following analysis columns for a month: Postage $150, Stationery $90, Travel $200, Cleaning $120, Sundries $40. Total payments column shows $600. What ledger entries are made from this information?
**Answer**: Each analysis column total is debited to the corresponding expense account in the ledger: Debit Postage expense $150, Debit Stationery expense $90, Debit Travel expense $200, Debit Cleaning expense $120, Debit Sundries expense $40. The credit entry of $600 is made to the main cash/bank account (to reimburse the petty cash fund). The total of all analysis columns ($600) matches the total payments column, confirming the postings are complete.
**Explanation**: The analysis columns provide the breakdown for ledger posting. A common error is crediting each individual expense — expenses are always debited; the credit goes to the cash/bank account.
**Tags**: posting, analysis-columns, ledger

### Question Q-2.6-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: The petty cash book shows opening balance $150. Payments total $110. A reimbursement of $110 is received from the main cash book. What is the closing balance? Is the fund in balance?
**Answer**: Opening balance $150 - Payments $110 = Cash in hand $40. Reimbursement $110 received: $40 + $110 = $150. Closing balance = $150. The fund is in balance — the closing balance equals the imprest amount of $150. The reimbursement has correctly restored the fund.
**Explanation**: Verify that closing balance = imprest amount after reimbursement. A common error is adding payments to the opening balance instead of subtracting — payments reduce the cash in hand.
**Tags**: imprest-system, verification, application

### Question Q-2.6-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: The petty cash imprest is $300. At month end, cash in hand is $72 but the vouchers total only $218. There is a shortage of $10. How should this be recorded and what might be the cause?
**Answer**: Expected cash = $300 - $218 = $82. Actual cash = $72. Shortage = $10. The shortage should be recorded as an additional petty cash payment (debit an expense account such as "petty cash shortage" or "cash losses" $10, credit petty cash $10). This increases total payments to $228. Reimbursement = $228. Possible causes: a payment was made without a voucher, cash was stolen, or a voucher was lost. The shortage should be investigated.
**Explanation**: Cash shortages must be recorded to keep the imprest system in balance. A common error is ignoring the shortage and reimbursing only $218 — this would leave the fund at $290 instead of $300 next period.
**Tags**: shortage, imprest-system, application

### Question Q-2.6-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate the advantages and disadvantages of the imprest system compared to giving the petty cashier a lump sum once a year with no regular reimbursement.
**Answer**: Advantages of imprest system: 1) Regular reconciliation (each period) ensures discrepancies are spotted quickly. 2) The fixed amount limits the maximum loss from theft or error. 3) Analysis columns provide timely expense data for the ledger each period. 4) The petty cashier cannot spend more than the imprest amount without authorisation. Disadvantages: 1) Requires regular administrative effort to verify vouchers and reimburse. 2) If expenses exceed the imprest amount mid-period, the petty cashier cannot make payments. Compared to an annual lump sum, the imprest system offers much better control and timeliness of information, though it requires more frequent oversight.
**Explanation**: The imprest system is a classic internal control mechanism. A common error is focusing only on convenience without recognising the control and monitoring benefits.
**Tags**: evaluation, imprest-system, advantages, disadvantages
