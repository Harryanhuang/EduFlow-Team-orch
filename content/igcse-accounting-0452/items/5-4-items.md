# Items: 5.4 — Capital adjustments

### Question Q-5.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Distinguish between capital expenditure and revenue expenditure. Classify each of the following: (a) purchase of a delivery van; (b) fuel for the van; (c) extension to a building; (d) repairs to machinery; (e) installation costs for a new machine.
**Answer**: Capital expenditure is spending on acquiring or improving non-current assets — benefits last more than one accounting period. It is recorded as an asset in the statement of financial position (not as an expense in the income statement).
Revenue expenditure is spending on the day-to-day running of the business — benefits are consumed within one period. It is recorded as an expense in the income statement.
(a) Purchase of delivery van: Capital expenditure (acquiring a non-current asset).
(b) Fuel for the van: Revenue expenditure (ongoing operating cost).
(c) Extension to a building: Capital expenditure (improving/enlarging a non-current asset).
(d) Repairs to machinery: Revenue expenditure (maintaining existing condition, not improving).
(e) Installation costs for a new machine: Capital expenditure (cost necessary to bring the asset to working condition — part of the asset's cost).
**Explanation**: A common error is classifying repairs as capital expenditure — repairs maintain the existing condition but do not improve the asset, so they are revenue expenditure. However, if the expenditure ENHANCES the asset beyond its original condition (e.g., an upgrade that increases capacity), it is capital expenditure. Installation costs are capital because they are part of bringing the asset to its intended use.
**Tags**: capital-expenditure, revenue-expenditure, classification

### Question Q-5.4-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: How is additional capital introduced by the owner recorded? The owner introduces $15,000 cash and a personal vehicle valued at $20,000 into the business. Show the journal entries and the effect on the statement of financial position.
**Answer**: When the owner introduces additional capital, the business records the assets received and credits the capital account.
Journal entries:
(1) For cash: Debit Cash $15,000, Credit Capital $15,000.
(2) For vehicle: Debit Motor vehicles $20,000, Credit Capital $20,000.
Total capital introduced = $15,000 + $20,000 = $35,000.
Effect on SOFP: Assets increase by $35,000 (cash + vehicle) and capital increases by $35,000. The accounting equation remains balanced.
Capital introduced is not revenue — it does not appear in the income statement. It is added to the owner's capital in the SOFP (or in the statement of changes in capital).
**Explanation**: A common error is treating capital introduced as sales revenue — it is an injection of owner's funds, not income. The owner is investing in the business, not selling to it. Capital introduced increases both assets and capital equally.
**Tags**: capital-introduced, journal-entry, SOFP-effect

### Question Q-5.4-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What are drawings? How do drawings differ from expenses? The owner takes $3,000 cash for personal use and takes goods costing $500 from inventory for personal use. Show the journal entries and the effect on financial statements.
**Answer**: Drawings are withdrawals of assets (cash or goods) by the owner for personal use. They reduce the owner's capital but are NOT business expenses — they do not appear in the income statement.
Journal entries:
(1) Cash drawings: Debit Drawings $3,000, Credit Cash $3,000.
(2) Goods drawings: Debit Drawings $500, Credit Purchases (or inventory) $500.
Total drawings = $3,500.
Effect: In the SOFP, drawings are deducted from capital (opening capital + net profit - drawings = closing capital). Goods drawings reduce purchases in the income statement (they are not an additional expense — they reduce the cost of goods available for sale).
**Explanation**: A common error is recording goods drawings as an expense — they reduce the purchases figure in the income statement, not add an expense. The goods are no longer available for sale, so the purchases figure must be reduced. Drawings are NOT deducted from profit to calculate net profit — they are deducted AFTER net profit in the capital section.
**Tags**: drawings, journal-entry, goods-drawings

### Question Q-5.4-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Prepare a statement of changes in capital for a sole trader: Opening capital $80,000; additional capital introduced $12,000; net profit $35,000; cash drawings $8,000; goods drawings $2,000. Calculate closing capital and show where it appears in the SOFP.
**Answer**:
Statement of changes in capital for the year:

  Opening capital: $80,000
  Add capital introduced: $12,000
  Add net profit: $35,000
  Less cash drawings: ($8,000)
  Less goods drawings: ($2,000)
  Closing capital: $117,000

Verification: $80,000 + $12,000 + $35,000 - $8,000 - $2,000 = $117,000.
The closing capital of $117,000 appears in the equity section of the SOFP. It must equal net assets (total assets minus total liabilities).
Goods drawings reduce purchases in the income statement, so the net profit of $35,000 already reflects the reduction in purchases. The drawings deduction in the capital statement is the total amount withdrawn by the owner, regardless of whether it was cash or goods.
**Explanation**: A common error is deducting goods drawings twice — once from purchases and once from capital. Goods drawings ARE deducted from purchases in the income statement (affecting net profit), and the total drawings (cash + goods) are then deducted from capital in the capital statement. These are two different entries with different purposes: the purchases adjustment ensures COGS is correct; the capital adjustment reduces owner's equity.
**Tags**: statement-of-changes-in-capital, closing-capital, SOFP

### Question Q-5.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: In a partnership, what is the difference between a partner's fixed capital account and current account? Partner A has a fixed capital of $30,000 and a current account credit balance of $8,000. Partner B has a fixed capital of $20,000 and a current account debit balance of $2,000. Show the equity section of the SOFP.
**Answer**: In a partnership with fixed capital accounts:
Fixed capital account: Records the permanent capital contribution by each partner. This balance normally does not change unless a partner introduces additional permanent capital or withdraws capital (with agreement).
Current account: Records the partner's share of profit, interest on capital, drawings, salary, etc. The balance fluctuates each year. A credit balance means the partner has undrawn profits; a debit balance means the partner has overdrawn (drawn more than their share).

Equity section of SOFP:
  Fixed capital accounts:
    Partner A: $30,000
    Partner B: $20,000
    Total fixed capital: $50,000
  Current accounts:
    Partner A (credit): $8,000
    Partner B (debit): ($2,000)
    Total current accounts: $6,000
  Total equity: $56,000

**Explanation**: A common error is not showing Partner B's debit current account as a deduction — a debit balance in a current account means the partner owes the partnership money (overdrawn), so it reduces total equity. Another error is mixing up fixed capital and current account — fixed capital is permanent; the current account fluctuates with profit and drawings.
**Tags**: partnership, fixed-capital, current-account

### Question Q-5.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A new partner is admitted to a partnership. Existing partners A ($40,000 capital) and B ($30,000 capital) share profits 2:1. Partner C is admitted with a capital contribution of $25,000. Goodwill is valued at $15,000. C does not bring goodwill separately; it is to be raised in the books. Show the capital accounts after admission.
**Answer**: Goodwill of $15,000 is shared between existing partners in their OLD profit-sharing ratio (2:1):
Partner A: $15,000 × 2/3 = $10,000.
Partner B: $15,000 × 1/3 = $5,000.

Capital accounts after admission:
  Partner A: $40,000 + $10,000 (goodwill) = $50,000
  Partner B: $30,000 + $5,000 (goodwill) = $35,000
  Partner C: $25,000 (capital introduced)
  Total capital: $110,000

Goodwill appears as a non-current asset in the SOFP if it is to be retained. If goodwill is to be written off immediately, it is deducted from the partners' capital accounts in the NEW profit-sharing ratio.
Journal entries for raising goodwill: Debit Goodwill $15,000, Credit A's capital $10,000, Credit B's capital $5,000.
**Explanation**: A common error is sharing goodwill in the new profit-sharing ratio — goodwill is the value of the EXISTING business, so it belongs to the existing partners in their OLD ratio. C does not share in goodwill because C is joining an established business. Always check whether goodwill is to be retained (shown as asset) or written off (deducted from capital).
**Tags**: partnership, admission-of-partner, goodwill

### Question Q-5.4-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A partner retires from a partnership. Partners A ($30,000 capital), B ($25,000 capital), and C ($20,000 capital) share profits 3:2:1. C retires. Goodwill is valued at $18,000 and is to be raised in the books. C is to be paid the full amount due. Show: (a) C's amount due, (b) the journal entries, (c) the remaining partners' capital balances after C's retirement.
**Answer**: (a) C's amount due: Capital $20,000 + Share of goodwill $18,000 × 1/6 = $3,000. Total due to C = $23,000.
(b) Journal entries:
  Raise goodwill: Debit Goodwill $18,000; Credit A's capital ($18,000 × 3/6) $9,000; Credit B's capital ($18,000 × 2/6) $6,000; Credit C's capital ($18,000 × 1/6) $3,000.
  Pay C: Debit C's capital $23,000, Credit Cash $23,000.
(c) Remaining partners' capital:
  A: $30,000 + $9,000 = $39,000
  B: $25,000 + $6,000 = $31,000
  Total: $70,000

**Explanation**: A common error is not including C's share of goodwill in the amount due — the retiring partner is entitled to their share of goodwill because they helped build the business. Goodwill is shared in the OLD profit-sharing ratio (3:2:1) because it represents value created by all partners up to the retirement date. After C leaves, A and B may agree a new profit-sharing ratio.
**Tags**: partnership, retirement-of-partner, goodwill

### Question Q-5.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Partners A and B share profits 3:2. Their capital accounts show $50,000 and $35,000 respectively. Their current accounts (before appropriation) show credit balances of $12,000 and $5,000. The partnership agreement provides: interest on capital 5% p.a.; partner salary to A of $8,000; residual profits/losses shared 3:2. Net profit for the year before appropriations is $40,000. Drawings during the year: A $10,000, B $8,000. Prepare the profit appropriation and calculate each partner's closing current account balance.
**Answer**:
Profit appropriation for the year:

  Net profit: $40,000
  Less appropriations:
    Interest on capital: A ($50,000 × 5%) $2,500; B ($35,000 × 5%) $1,750. Total: $4,250
    Partner salary: A $8,000
    Total appropriations: $12,250
  Residual profit: $40,000 - $12,250 = $27,750
  Share of residual profit: A ($27,750 × 3/5) $16,650; B ($27,750 × 2/5) $11,100

Each partner's share of total profit:
  A: Interest $2,500 + Salary $8,000 + Residual $16,650 = $27,150
  B: Interest $1,750 + Residual $11,100 = $12,850
  Total: $40,000 ✓

Closing current account:
  A: Opening $12,000 + Profit share $27,150 - Drawings $10,000 = $29,150
  B: Opening $5,000 + Profit share $12,850 - Drawings $8,000 = $9,850

**Explanation**: The profit appropriation account distributes net profit among partners according to the partnership agreement. Interest on capital and salaries are appropriations of profit (not expenses in the income statement). They are deducted from net profit to find the residual profit to be shared. Each partner's total share (interest + salary + residual) is credited to their current account, and drawings are deducted. The closing current account balance reflects undrawn profits.
**Tags**: partnership, profit-appropriation, current-account

### Question Q-5.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A sole trader converts to a partnership. The sole trader's SOFP shows: Non-current assets (carrying amount) $60,000; Current assets $25,000; Current liabilities $10,000; Capital $75,000. The business is revalued: property increases by $20,000; vehicles decrease by $5,000. Goodwill is agreed at $30,000. The sole trader and a new partner agree capital in the ratio 2:1. Show: (a) the revaluation surplus, (b) the total value of the business including goodwill, (c) each partner's opening capital in the partnership.
**Answer**: (a) Revaluation surplus: Property increase $20,000 - Vehicles decrease $5,000 = Net revaluation gain $15,000.
Adjusted capital = $75,000 + $15,000 = $90,000.
(b) Total value including goodwill = Adjusted capital + Goodwill = $90,000 + $30,000 = $120,000.
(c) Partners' capital in ratio 2:1: Total capital $120,000.
  Existing partner (2/3): $120,000 × 2/3 = $80,000.
  New partner (1/3): $120,000 × 1/3 = $40,000.
The existing partner's capital of $80,000 = Revalued capital $90,000 + Goodwill share $20,000 - Cash/asset adjustment ($30,000).
The new partner introduces $40,000 as capital.

Partnership SOFP opening:
  Non-current assets: Revalued $75,000 + Goodwill $30,000 = $105,000
  Current assets: $25,000
  Current liabilities: ($10,000)
  Net assets: $120,000
  Capital: Partner 1 $80,000 + Partner 2 $40,000 = $120,000 ✓

**Explanation**: Conversion from sole trader to partnership involves: (1) revaluing assets to current values (revaluation surplus goes to the sole trader's capital). (2) Recognising goodwill (which also increases the sole trader's capital). (3) Determining the new partner's capital contribution. (4) Setting up the partnership capital accounts. The key is that the sole trader benefits from both revaluation surplus AND goodwill before the new partner joins.
**Tags**: comprehensive, conversion-to-partnership, revaluation
