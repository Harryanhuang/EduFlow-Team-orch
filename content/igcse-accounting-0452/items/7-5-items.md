# Items: 7.5 — Comprehensive Scenario

### Question Q-7.5-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Describe the structure of a comprehensive IGCSE Accounting scenario question and what skills it tests.
**Answer**: A comprehensive scenario question presents a realistic business situation requiring students to apply multiple accounting concepts together. It typically: 1) Provides background information about a business (type, size, transactions). 2) Requires preparation of financial statements (income statement, statement of financial position, or cash flow). 3) Includes adjustments (depreciation, accruals, prepayments, bad debts). 4) Requires calculations and analysis (ratios, cost calculations). 5) Asks for evaluation or recommendation based on results. Skills tested: recording transactions correctly, applying accounting concepts and principles, preparing complete financial statements, performing calculations accurately, interpreting results, making informed recommendations, presenting information clearly and logically.
**Explanation**: Comprehensive questions test integration, not isolated knowledge. A common error is treating each part independently — the recommendation should be based on the calculations and statements prepared.
**Tags**: comprehensive-scenario, overview, exam-technique

### Question Q-7.5-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A sole trader's trial balance at 31 December 20X5 shows: Sales $450,000; Purchases $280,000; Opening inventory $35,000; Rent expense $18,000; Insurance expense $6,000; Equipment $120,000; Accumulated depreciation (1 Jan 20X5) $48,000; Trade receivables $65,000; Allowance for receivables (1 Jan 20X5) $3,000; Cash $15,000; Capital $200,000; Drawings $40,000; Trade payables $54,000. Additional information: (a) Closing inventory $42,000. (b) Depreciate equipment at 20% straight-line. (c) Increase allowance for receivables to 5% of trade receivables. (d) Accrued rent $2,000; prepaid insurance $500. Prepare the income statement for the year ended 31 December 20X5 and calculate profit.
**Answer**: Cost of sales = Opening inventory $35,000 + Purchases $280,000 - Closing inventory $42,000 = $273,000. Gross profit = Sales $450,000 - Cost of sales $273,000 = $177,000.

Operating expenses:
- Rent expense: $18,000 + accrued rent $2,000 = $20,000
- Insurance expense: $6,000 - prepaid insurance $500 = $5,500
- Depreciation of equipment: $120,000 × 20% = $24,000
- Increase in allowance for receivables: ($65,000 × 5%) - $3,000 = $3,250 - $3,000 = $250

Total expenses = $20,000 + $5,500 + $24,000 + $250 = $49,750

Profit for the year = Gross profit $177,000 - Total expenses $49,750 = $127,250
**Explanation**: This is a standard trial balance adjustment question. First calculate cost of sales and gross profit. Then adjust each expense: accruals increase expenses, prepayments reduce expenses, depreciation is charged for the year, and only the movement in allowance for receivables is taken to the income statement. A common error is to use the total allowance balance as the expense instead of the increase.
**Tags**: financial-statements, trial-balance, adjustments

### Question Q-7.5-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A and B are partners sharing profits 3:2 after interest on capital of 8% p.a. A's capital $100,000; B's capital $60,000. They admit C as a partner. C brings $40,000 capital and $15,000 goodwill. New profit-sharing ratio A:B:C = 2:1:1. Goodwill is not to be retained in the partnership books. Calculate: (a) each partner's interest on capital, (b) distribution of remaining profit (assume total profit before appropriations $80,000), (c) how C's goodwill premium is distributed, (d) each partner's total share of profit.
**Answer**: (a) Interest on capital: A = $100,000 × 8% = $8,000; B = $60,000 × 8% = $4,800; C = $40,000 × 8% = $3,200. Total interest = $16,000. (b) Remaining profit: $80,000 - $16,000 = $64,000. Distribution 2:1:1: A = $32,000; B = $16,000; C = $16,000. (c) Goodwill $15,000 distributed to old partners in old ratio (3:2): A = $15,000 × 3/5 = $9,000; B = $15,000 × 2/5 = $6,000. C pays this privately or through capital adjustment. (d) Total shares: A = $8,000 + $32,000 + $9,000 = $49,000; B = $4,800 + $16,000 + $6,000 = $26,800; C = $3,200 + $16,000 = $19,200. Total = $95,000 ($80,000 profit + $15,000 goodwill).
**Explanation**: Goodwill is distributed to old partners in old ratio, not new ratio. A common error is distributing goodwill in the new profit-sharing ratio.
**Tags**: partnership, admission, goodwill, profit-sharing

### Question Q-7.5-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A manufacturer's data for the year: Raw materials purchased $180,000; Opening raw materials $25,000; Closing raw materials $30,000; Direct wages $120,000; Direct expenses $15,000; Factory overheads $60,000; Opening work in progress $10,000; Closing work in progress $8,000; Opening finished goods $45,000; Closing finished goods $50,000; Sales $500,000; Administration expenses $35,000; Selling expenses $28,000. The manufacturer adds 20% mark-up to cost of production for transfer price. Prepare the manufacturing account and calculate gross profit and net profit.
**Answer**: Manufacturing account: Raw materials consumed: $25,000 + $180,000 - $30,000 = $175,000. Prime cost: $175,000 + $120,000 + $15,000 = $310,000. Factory overheads: $60,000. Total production cost: $370,000. Add opening WIP $10,000 - closing WIP $8,000 = $372,000 cost of production. Transfer price (20% mark-up): $372,000 × 1.20 = $446,400. Unrealized profit: $446,400 - $372,000 = $74,400. Income statement: Sales $500,000. Cost of sales at cost = Opening FG $45,000 + Cost of production $372,000 - Closing FG $50,000 = $367,000. Gross profit: $500,000 - $367,000 = $133,000. Net profit: $133,000 - $35,000 - $28,000 = $70,000.
**Explanation**: The key is using cost of production (not transfer price) for the cost of sales calculation. Transfer price and unrealized profit are internal adjustments. A common error is using transfer price as cost of sales.
**Tags**: manufacturing, cost-of-production, mark-up, gross-profit

### Question Q-7.5-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Two businesses in the same industry report: Business X: Current ratio 2.5:1, ROCE 18%, Gross profit margin 40%, Net profit margin 12%, Inventory turnover 8 times. Business Y: Current ratio 1.2:1, ROCE 22%, Gross profit margin 35%, Net profit margin 15%, Inventory turnover 12 times. Compare performance and recommend which business a bank should lend to.
**Answer**: Liquidity: X (2.5:1) is stronger than Y (1.2:1). Y may struggle to meet short-term obligations. However, X may have too much idle capital. Profitability: Y outperforms X on ROCE (22% vs 18%) and net margin (15% vs 12%), despite lower gross margin (35% vs 40%). This means Y controls operating expenses better. Efficiency: Y has much higher inventory turnover (12 vs 8), suggesting better inventory management and possibly faster sales. Overall: Y is more profitable and efficient but has weaker liquidity. X is more liquid but less profitable. Recommendation: Lend to Y with conditions. Y's superior profitability and efficiency indicate strong operational performance. The low current ratio is a risk but may reflect efficient use of working capital. Mitigation: require collateral, shorter repayment terms, or a covenant to maintain minimum current ratio.
**Explanation**: No single ratio tells the full story. A common error is recommending X based on liquidity alone, ignoring Y's superior profitability and efficiency.
**Tags**: ratio-analysis, comparison, lending-recommendation

### Question Q-7.5-06
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Nova Traders began business on 1 January 20X8. During January 20X8 the following transactions took place: Owner introduced capital in cash $60,000; Bought equipment for cash $18,000; Bought goods on credit from Delta Supplies $24,000; Bought goods for cash $6,000; Credit sales $40,000; Cash sales $10,000; Returns inwards from credit customers $2,000; Received cash from credit customers $30,000; Paid Delta Supplies $20,000 by cheque; Rent paid by cheque $4,000; Owner withdrew cash for personal use $5,000; A cheque for $6,000 was entered in the cash book as wages; Discount received from Delta Supplies $1,000; Closing inventory at 31 January 20X8 was $8,000. Additional information: (1) Rent owing at 31 January 20X8 was $500. (2) Of the $6,000 cheque entered as wages, $5,000 was actually drawings by the owner. Only $1,000 related to wages. Prepare: (1) The journal entry to correct the drawings error. (2) The income statement for the month ended 31 January 20X8. (3) The statement of financial position at 31 January 20X8.
**Answer**: (1) Correction journal: Dr Drawings $5,000; Cr Wages $5,000. (2) Income statement: Net sales = Credit $40,000 + Cash $10,000 - Returns $2,000 = $48,000. Cost of sales = $0 + $24,000 + $6,000 - $8,000 = $22,000. Gross profit = $26,000. Other income: Discount received $1,000. Expenses: Rent $4,500 + Wages $1,000 = $5,500. Profit = $26,000 + $1,000 - $5,500 = $21,500. (3) SOFP: Non-current assets: Equipment $18,000. Current assets: Inventory $8,000 + Receivables $8,000 + Cash $41,000 = $57,000. Total assets = $75,000. Capital: $60,000 + $21,500 - $10,000 = $71,500. Current liabilities: Payables $3,000 + Rent owing $500 = $3,500. Total = $75,000.
**Explanation**: Two drawings effects: the original cash withdrawal of $5,000 and the $5,000 hidden inside the cheque posted as wages. After correction, wages = $1,000, total drawings = $10,000, profit = $21,500, closing capital = $71,500. A common mistake is correcting wages but forgetting drawings must then total $10,000.
**Tags**: complete-cycle, error-correction, financial-statements

### Question Q-7.5-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: P, Q, and R share profits 4:3:3. At dissolution: assets realised: Land $180,000 (book value $150,000); Equipment $45,000 (book value $60,000); Inventory $25,000 (book value $35,000). Trade receivables collected $28,000 (book value $32,000). Trade payables paid $40,000. Dissolution costs $5,000. Capital balances: P $80,000; Q $55,000; R $45,000. Current accounts: P $12,000 (Cr); Q $8,000 (Cr); R $3,000 (Dr). Calculate: (a) profit or loss on realisation, (b) each partner's share, (c) final payment to each partner.
**Answer**: (a) Assets realised: $180,000 + $45,000 + $25,000 + $28,000 = $278,000. Book value: $150,000 + $60,000 + $35,000 + $32,000 = $277,000. Dissolution costs $5,000. Loss on realisation: $278,000 - $277,000 - $5,000 = -$4,000 (loss). (b) Loss shared 4:3:3: P = $1,600; Q = $1,200; R = $1,200. (c) Final payments: P = $80,000 + $12,000 - $1,600 = $90,400. Q = $55,000 + $8,000 - $1,200 = $61,800. R = $45,000 - $3,000 - $1,200 = $40,800. Total = $193,000. Check: Cash available = $278,000 - $5,000 - $40,000 = $233,000. $193,000 + $40,000 = $233,000. Correct.
**Explanation**: Dissolution requires tracking realisation of each asset, costs, and distribution. A common error is forgetting that a debit current account (R's $3,000) reduces what the partner receives.
**Tags**: partnership, dissolution, realisation

### Question Q-7.5-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business changed its inventory valuation method from FIFO to AVCO on 1 January 20X5. Under FIFO, inventory at 31 Dec 20X4 was $85,000; under AVCO it would have been $78,000. 20X5 purchases $320,000; sales $500,000. Closing inventory 31 Dec 20X5: FIFO $92,000; AVCO $88,000. Operating expenses $120,000 (both years). Prepare: (a) income statements for 20X5 under both methods, (b) comparative analysis showing the effect of the change, (c) disclose the change in accounting policy with justification.
**Answer**: (a) FIFO: COGS = $85,000 + $320,000 - $92,000 = $313,000. GP = $187,000. NP = $67,000. AVCO: COGS = $78,000 + $320,000 - $88,000 = $310,000. GP = $190,000. NP = $70,000. (b) Effect: NP under AVCO is $3,000 higher. Opening inventory reduction ($85,000 - $78,000 = $7,000) partially offsets closing inventory difference ($92,000 - $88,000 = $4,000), net effect $3,000 increase. (c) Disclosure: "Changed inventory valuation from FIFO to AVCO from 1 January 20X5. The change provides a more consistent measure of inventory cost in a period of volatile prices. Effect on NP: increase of $3,000. Comparative figures restated."
**Explanation**: Policy changes require disclosure and restatement. A common error is not adjusting the opening inventory to the new basis when comparing.
**Tags**: accounting-policy-change, FIFO, AVCO, comparative-analysis

### Question Q-7.5-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: An investor has $200,000 to invest and is considering two opportunities. Opportunity A (buy existing business): Purchase price $200,000. Last year's financials: Sales $300,000; Gross profit $120,000; Net profit $45,000. Assets at fair value: Equipment $80,000; Inventory $35,000; Receivables $25,000. Goodwill valued at $60,000. Opportunity B (start new business): Initial investment $200,000 (equipment $100,000; working capital $100,000). Projected Year 1: Sales $180,000; Gross profit margin 35%; Operating expenses $50,000. Projected Year 2: Sales $250,000; Gross profit margin 38%; Operating expenses $65,000. Projected Year 3: Sales $320,000; Gross profit margin 40%; Operating expenses $80,000. Calculate for each: (a) Year 1 profit, (b) payback period, (c) ROCE, (d) qualitative factors. Recommend which to choose.
**Answer**: Opportunity A: (a) Year 1 profit $45,000. (b) Payback: $200,000 / $45,000 = 4.4 years. (c) ROCE: $45,000 / $200,000 = 22.5%. (d) Established customer base, proven track record. Risk: why selling? Opportunity B: (a) Year 1: $180,000 × 35% - $50,000 = $13,000. Year 2: $250,000 × 38% - $65,000 = $30,000. Year 3: $320,000 × 40% - $80,000 = $48,000. (b) Cumulative: Y1 $13,000; Y2 $43,000; Y3 $91,000. Payback exceeds 3 years; average $30,667; payback ≈ 6.5 years. (c) Average ROCE: $30,667 / $200,000 = 15.3%. (d) Growth potential, no legacy issues. Risk: unproven. Recommendation: A has better returns (22.5% vs 15.3% ROCE), faster payback (4.4 vs 6.5 years), and lower risk. However, B's Year 3 profit ($48,000) exceeds A's ($45,000), suggesting growth trajectory.
**Explanation**: Investment appraisal requires both quantitative and qualitative analysis. A common error is looking only at first-year profit without considering payback, growth trajectory, and risk.
**Tags**: investment-appraisal, ROCE, payback, comprehensive-analysis
