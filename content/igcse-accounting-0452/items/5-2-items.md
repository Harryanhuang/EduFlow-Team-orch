# Items: 5.2 — Income statement with adjustments

### Question Q-5.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What are year-end adjustments in the preparation of financial statements? List the common types of year-end adjustments and explain why they are necessary.
**Answer**: Year-end adjustments are entries made at the end of an accounting period to ensure that the financial statements reflect the correct amounts under the matching concept and prudence concept. They adjust revenue and expenses to the amounts that relate to the period, regardless of when cash was paid or received.
Common types: (1) Accruals — expenses incurred but not yet paid. (2) Prepayments — expenses paid but not yet incurred. (3) Depreciation — allocation of non-current asset cost over useful life. (4) Irrecoverable debts — receivables confirmed as uncollectible. (5) Allowance for receivables — estimate of potentially uncollectible receivables. (6) Inventory valuation — closing inventory at lower of cost and NRV.
These adjustments ensure that the income statement shows the true profit or loss for the period and the statement of financial position shows the correct assets and liabilities.
**Explanation**: A common error is thinking year-end adjustments are optional — they are essential for preparing financial statements that give a true and fair view. Without adjustments, the income statement would show cash-based figures rather than accrual-based figures.
**Tags**: year-end-adjustments, definition, accounting-concepts

### Question Q-5.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A business has a trial balance showing rent paid $8,400 and insurance paid $3,600. At year end, rent is accrued $1,200 and insurance is prepaid $600. Show the adjusted expenses in the income statement and the statement of financial position entries.
**Answer**: Rent adjustment: Trial balance amount $8,400 + Accrual $1,200 = Adjusted rent expense $9,600 in income statement. Accrual $1,200 shown as current liability in statement of financial position.
Insurance adjustment: Trial balance amount $3,600 - Prepayment $600 = Adjusted insurance expense $3,000 in income statement. Prepayment $600 shown as current asset in statement of financial position.
Total impact on net profit: Rent accrual ADDS $1,200 to expense; insurance prepayment REDUCES expense by $600. Net additional expense = $1,200 - $600 = $600 reduction in net profit.
**Explanation**: A common error is not adjusting the trial balance figure — the trial balance shows CASH PAID, not the expense for the year. The income statement expense = trial balance amount + closing accrual - closing prepayment. Always adjust each item individually.
**Tags**: accruals, prepayments, adjusted-expenses

### Question Q-5.2-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A business has machinery at cost $50,000 in the trial balance. Depreciation is charged at 20% per annum straight-line. Show the depreciation expense in the income statement and the carrying amount in the statement of financial position.
**Answer**: Depreciation expense = $50,000 × 20% = $10,000 per annum. This is charged as an expense in the income statement (usually shown separately or included in operating expenses).
In the statement of financial position, the machinery is shown at carrying amount (net book value): Cost $50,000 - Accumulated depreciation $10,000 = Carrying amount $40,000. The accumulated depreciation of $10,000 is the total depreciation to date on this asset. If there was an opening accumulated depreciation balance, add the current year's depreciation to it.
Note: The cost of $50,000 does not appear in the income statement — only the depreciation charge ($10,000) appears as an expense. The purchase of the asset is a capital expenditure, not a revenue expenditure.
**Explanation**: A common error is including the full cost of the asset as an expense — only the depreciation charge (allocation of cost) appears in the income statement. The asset remains on the statement of financial position at its carrying amount.
**Tags**: depreciation, income-statement, carrying-amount

### Question Q-5.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business's trial balance shows receivables $25,000. At year end: (a) an irrecoverable debt of $2,000 is written off; (b) an allowance for receivables of 5% is to be made against the remaining receivables. Show the income statement charges and the statement of financial position presentation.
**Answer**: (a) Write off irrecoverable debt: Receivables = $25,000 - $2,000 = $23,000. Irrecoverable debts expense in income statement = $2,000.
(b) Allowance: 5% × $23,000 = $1,150. If this is the first year of allowance (no opening allowance in trial balance), the full $1,150 is charged as an increase in allowance in the income statement.
Income statement: Irrecoverable debts expense $2,000 + Increase in allowance $1,150 = $3,150 total charge.
Statement of financial position: Receivables $23,000 less allowance $1,150 = net receivables $21,850.
If the trial balance already showed an opening allowance of, say, $800, the income statement charge for the allowance would be $1,150 - $800 = $350 increase, and the total charge would be $2,000 + $350 = $2,350.
**Explanation**: A common error is calculating the allowance on the full $25,000 without first deducting the written-off debt. The irrecoverable debt must be written off FIRST, then the allowance is calculated on the REMAINING receivables. The order matters: write off, then allowance.
**Tags**: irrecoverable-debts, allowance-for-receivables, net-receivables

### Question Q-5.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business's trial balance (extract) at 31 December 20X4 shows: Sales $150,000; Purchases $75,000; Opening inventory $12,000; Rent $6,000; Wages $25,000; Allowance for receivables at 1 Jan 20X4 $1,000; Receivables $20,000. Adjustments: (a) Closing inventory valued at $14,000. (b) Rent accrued $800. (c) Wages prepaid $1,500. (d) Allowance for receivables to be increased to 6% of receivables. Prepare the income statement for the year.
**Answer**:
Income statement for the year ended 31 December 20X4:

Sales: $150,000
Less COGS: Opening inventory $12,000 + Purchases $75,000 - Closing inventory $14,000 = $73,000
Gross profit: $150,000 - $73,000 = $77,000

Operating expenses:
  Rent ($6,000 + $800 accrual): $6,800
  Wages ($25,000 - $1,500 prepayment): $23,500
  Increase in allowance (6% × $20,000 = $1,200; existing $1,000; increase $200): $200
  Total operating expenses: $30,500

Net profit: $77,000 - $30,500 = $46,500

**Explanation**: A common error is not making all adjustments before calculating expenses. Each adjustment must be applied: accruals add to the trial balance figure, prepayments subtract, and the change in allowance is the difference between new and old amounts. The allowance calculation is on receivables ($20,000) — the trial balance receivables figure is before any write-off (none given here).
**Tags**: income-statement, multiple-adjustments, net-profit

### Question Q-5.2-06
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business has the following trial balance figures at 31 December 20X4: Sales $280,000; Purchases $140,000; Opening inventory $18,000; Wages $42,000; Rent $16,000; Electricity $8,000; Motor vehicles at cost $60,000; Equipment at cost $20,000; Accumulated depreciation - vehicles $24,000; Receivables $30,000; Allowance for receivables $1,500. Adjustments: (a) Closing inventory $22,000. (b) Wages accrued $3,000. (c) Rent prepaid $2,000. (d) Vehicles depreciated 20% reducing balance. (e) Equipment depreciated 10% straight-line. (f) Irrecoverable debt of $1,500 to be written off. (g) Allowance for receivables to be adjusted to 5% of remaining receivables. Prepare the income statement.
**Answer**:
Income statement for the year ended 31 December 20X4:

Sales: $280,000
Less COGS: $18,000 + $140,000 - $22,000 = $136,000
Gross profit: $280,000 - $136,000 = $144,000

Operating expenses:
  Wages ($42,000 + $3,000): $45,000
  Rent ($16,000 - $2,000): $14,000
  Electricity: $8,000
  Vehicle depreciation ($60,000 - $24,000 = $36,000 × 20%): $7,200
  Equipment depreciation ($20,000 × 10%): $2,000
  Irrecoverable debts: $1,500
  Change in allowance: new = 5% × ($30,000 - $1,500) = $1,425; existing $1,500; decrease $75 (credit)
  Total: $45,000 + $14,000 + $8,000 + $7,200 + $2,000 + $1,500 - $75 = $77,625

Net profit: $144,000 - $77,625 = $66,375

**Explanation**: Key points: (1) Vehicles use reducing balance — depreciation is on carrying amount ($60,000 - $24,000 = $36,000), not cost. (2) Equipment uses straight-line — on cost ($20,000). (3) Irrecoverable debt written off FIRST, then allowance on remaining ($30,000 - $1,500 = $28,500 × 5% = $1,425). (4) Existing allowance $1,500 → new $1,425 → decrease of $75 (income). (5) Decrease in allowance REDUCES total expenses.
**Tags**: comprehensive, all-adjustments, multi-step

### Question Q-5.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: How do year-end adjustments affect both the income statement AND the statement of financial position? For each of the following adjustments, show the dual effect: (a) accrual of wages $2,000; (b) prepayment of insurance $1,500; (c) depreciation charge $5,000; (d) irrecoverable debt written off $800.
**Answer**: Every adjustment has a dual effect (double entry):
(a) Accrual of wages $2,000: Debit Wages expense (SPL, expense increases) $2,000; Credit Accruals (SOFP, current liability increases) $2,000. Net profit decreases by $2,000; liabilities increase by $2,000.
(b) Prepayment of insurance $1,500: Debit Prepayments (SOFP, current asset increases) $1,500; Credit Insurance expense (SPL, expense decreases) $1,500. Net profit increases by $1,500; assets increase by $1,500.
(c) Depreciation charge $5,000: Debit Depreciation expense (SPL, expense increases) $5,000; Credit Accumulated depreciation (SOFP, contra-asset increases, reducing carrying amount) $5,000. Net profit decreases by $5,000; net assets decrease by $5,000.
(d) Irrecoverable debt $800: Debit Irrecoverable debts expense (SPL, expense increases) $800; Credit Receivables (SOFP, current asset decreases) $800. Net profit decreases by $800; assets decrease by $800.
**Explanation**: A common error is only showing the income statement effect and forgetting the statement of financial position effect — every adjustment affects BOTH. The dual entry ensures the accounting equation remains balanced: Assets = Capital + Liabilities. Expenses reduce capital (through reduced profit), and the other entry adjusts assets or liabilities accordingly.
**Tags**: dual-effect, income-statement, statement-of-financial-position

### Question Q-5.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: From a trial balance, a business has: Sales $200,000; Purchases $150,000; Opening inventory $15,000; Wages $30,000; Rent $12,000. The business applies a markup of 25% on cost. Adjustments needed: (a) wages accrued $2,500; (b) rent prepaid $1,000. However, the closing inventory figure is missing. Calculate: (a) the COGS implied by the markup, (b) the missing closing inventory, (c) the net profit after adjustments.
**Answer**: (a) Markup 25% means Sales = 125% of COGS. COGS = $200,000 / 1.25 = $160,000. Gross profit = $200,000 - $160,000 = $40,000.
(b) COGS = Opening inventory + Purchases - Closing inventory. $160,000 = $15,000 + $150,000 - Closing inventory. Closing inventory = $165,000 - $160,000 = $5,000.
(c) Wages adjusted = $30,000 + $2,500 = $32,500. Rent adjusted = $12,000 - $1,000 = $11,000. Total operating expenses = $32,500 + $11,000 = $43,500. Net profit = $40,000 - $43,500 = -$3,500 (net loss of $3,500).
**Explanation**: This type of question requires working in order: use markup to find COGS, use COGS formula to find the missing closing inventory, then apply adjustments to expenses and calculate net profit. A common error is calculating wages or rent at their trial balance amounts without making the adjustments — wages must include the accrual ($32,500, not $30,000) and rent must exclude the prepayment ($11,000, not $12,000).
**Tags**: missing-figure, markup, comprehensive

### Question Q-5.2-09
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Prepare the income statement for the year ended 31 December 20X4 from the following trial balance extract and adjustments. Trial balance: Sales $350,000; Opening inventory $25,000; Purchases $190,000; Carriage inwards $7,000; Wages $55,000; Rent $20,000; Electricity $10,000; Motor vehicles at cost $80,000; Accumulated depreciation - vehicles $32,000; Receivables $40,000; Allowance for receivables $2,000. Adjustments: (i) Closing inventory $28,000. (ii) Wages accrued $4,000. (iii) Rent prepaid $3,000. (iv) Vehicles depreciated 25% reducing balance. (v) $2,000 irrecoverable debt written off. (vi) Allowance adjusted to 5% of remaining receivables.
**Answer**:
Income statement for the year ended 31 December 20X4:

Sales: $350,000
Less COGS: Opening $25,000 + Purchases $190,000 + Carriage inwards $7,000 - Closing $28,000 = $194,000
Gross profit: $350,000 - $194,000 = $156,000

Operating expenses:
  Wages ($55,000 + $4,000): $59,000
  Rent ($20,000 - $3,000): $17,000
  Electricity: $10,000
  Vehicle depreciation (($80,000 - $32,000) × 25%): $12,000
  Irrecoverable debts: $2,000
  Change in allowance: new = 5% × ($40,000 - $2,000) = $1,900; existing $2,000; decrease $100 (credit)
  Total expenses: $59,000 + $17,000 + $10,000 + $12,000 + $2,000 - $100 = $99,900

Net profit: $156,000 - $99,900 = $56,100

**Explanation**: Critical order of operations: (1) COGS includes carriage inwards in purchases. (2) Vehicle depreciation uses reducing balance on carrying amount ($80,000 - $32,000 = $48,000 × 25% = $12,000). (3) Irrecoverable debt written off FIRST ($40,000 - $2,000 = $38,000), then allowance on remaining (5% × $38,000 = $1,900). (4) Existing allowance $2,000 → new $1,900 → decrease of $100, which is a CREDIT to SPL (reduces expenses). (5) The decrease in allowance partially offsets the irrecoverable debts expense.
**Tags**: comprehensive, full-income-statement, all-adjustments
