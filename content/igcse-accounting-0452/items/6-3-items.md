# Items: 6.3 — Efficiency ratios

### Question Q-6.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the inventory turnover ratio? How is it calculated in both 'times' and 'days' formats?
**Answer**: The inventory turnover ratio measures how many times a business sells and replaces its inventory during a period. In 'times' format: Inventory turnover = Cost of sales / Average inventory (or closing inventory if average not available). This shows how many times per year the inventory is sold and replaced. In 'days' format: Inventory turnover days = (Inventory / Cost of sales) × 365. This shows the average number of days inventory is held before being sold. A higher turnover (more times) or lower turnover days indicates more efficient inventory management — the business is selling stock quickly and not tying up capital in unsold goods. A low turnover or high days figure may indicate obsolete stock, over-ordering, or declining demand.
**Explanation**: Both formats measure the same thing. A common error is using revenue instead of cost of sales in the denominator — inventory is valued at cost, so cost of sales is the matching figure. Using revenue gives an artificially high turnover.

### Question Q-6.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the trade receivables collection period? How is it calculated and what does it indicate?
**Answer**: The trade receivables collection period (also called receivables days or debtors days) measures the average number of days it takes for a business to collect payment from its credit customers. In 'times' format: Receivables turnover = Revenue / Trade receivables (how many times receivables are collected per year). In 'days' format: Receivables collection period = (Trade receivables / Revenue) × 365. A shorter period indicates efficient credit control — the business collects cash quickly. A period longer than the credit terms offered (e.g. 60 days when terms are 30 days) suggests poor credit control, customer payment difficulties, or that the business is effectively financing its customers. However, a very short period may indicate overly strict credit terms that could lose sales.
**Explanation**: Use credit sales (or revenue if credit/cash split is not given) in the denominator. A common error is using cost of sales — receivables relate to sales at selling price, not cost.
**Tags**: receivables-collection-period, efficiency, calculation, foundation

### Question Q-6.3-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the trade payables payment period? How is it calculated and what does it indicate about the business's relationship with suppliers?
**Answer**: The trade payables payment period (also called payables days or creditors days) measures the average number of days it takes for a business to pay its suppliers. In 'times' format: Payables turnover = Credit purchases / Trade payables (how many times payables are paid per year). In 'days' format: Payables payment period = (Trade payables / Credit purchases) × 365. When purchases are not given, cost of sales may be used as a proxy. A longer period may indicate the business is using suppliers as a source of free finance (delaying payments to conserve cash), which could damage supplier relationships and result in lost discounts or supply restrictions. A very short period may indicate the business is paying too quickly (not taking advantage of available credit terms) unless early payment discounts are available. The period should be compared with the credit terms agreed with suppliers.
**Explanation**: Use purchases (or cost of sales as proxy) in the denominator. A common error is using revenue — payables relate to purchases at cost, not sales. Always compare the calculated period with the agreed credit terms.
**Tags**: payables-payment-period, efficiency, calculation, standard

### Question Q-6.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is the asset turnover ratio? How is it calculated and what does it measure?
**Answer**: The asset turnover ratio measures how efficiently a business uses its assets to generate revenue. It is calculated as: Asset turnover = Revenue / Capital employed (or Revenue / Total assets). Where capital employed = total assets minus current liabilities. The ratio shows how many dollars of revenue are generated for each dollar of assets employed. A higher ratio indicates more efficient asset utilization — the business is generating more revenue from its asset base. A declining ratio may indicate that assets are becoming less productive, that the business has over-invested in assets, or that revenue is falling relative to the asset base. It is particularly useful when compared over time or against competitors in the same industry.
**Explanation**: Asset turnover is an efficiency ratio, not a profitability ratio. A common error is confusing it with ROCE — ROCE uses profit in the numerator, asset turnover uses revenue. They are related: ROCE = Net profit margin × Asset turnover (the DuPont formula).
**Tags**: asset-turnover, efficiency, calculation, standard

### Question Q-6.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business has: cost of sales $300,000, inventory $45,000, revenue $500,000, trade receivables $60,000, trade payables $50,000, purchases $320,000. Calculate all efficiency ratios. Comment on the working capital cycle.
**Answer**:
- Inventory turnover = $300,000 / $45,000 = 6.67 times
- Inventory turnover days = ($45,000 / $300,000) × 365 = 54.8 days
- Receivables collection period = ($60,000 / $500,000) × 365 = 43.8 days
- Receivables turnover = $500,000 / $60,000 = 8.33 times
- Payables payment period = ($50,000 / $320,000) × 365 = 57.0 days
- Payables turnover = $320,000 / $50,000 = 6.40 times
- Working capital cycle = 54.8 + 43.8 - 57.0 = 41.6 days

Comment: The business holds inventory for about 55 days, takes 44 days to collect from customers, and takes 57 days to pay suppliers. The working capital cycle of 41.6 days means the business must finance its operations for about 42 days between paying suppliers (indirectly, through delayed payments) and receiving cash from customers. The payables period (57 days) is longer than the receivables period (44 days), which is positive — the business is using supplier credit to partially fund its operations. However, the inventory holding period of 55 days may be high depending on the industry.
**Explanation**: Always use matching figures: cost of sales for inventory, revenue for receivables, purchases for payables. A common error is mixing revenue with cost-based denominators.
**Tags**: efficiency-ratios, working-capital-cycle, calculation, standard

### Question Q-6.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business offers 30-day credit terms but its receivables collection period is 52 days. What are the implications and what actions could the business take?
**Answer**: A receivables period of 52 days against 30-day terms means customers are taking an average of 22 extra days to pay. Implications: 1) The business is effectively providing free finance to customers for 22 additional days beyond agreed terms. 2) Cash flow is strained — the business must fund operations while waiting for payment. 3) There is an increased risk of bad debts — the longer debts remain unpaid, the higher the risk of non-payment. 4) The business may need to borrow more (and pay interest) to cover the cash shortfall. Actions: 1) Strengthen credit control procedures — send reminders, follow up promptly on overdue accounts. 2) Offer early payment discounts (e.g. 2% discount for payment within 10 days) to incentivize prompt payment. 3) Conduct credit checks on new customers before offering credit. 4) Set credit limits for individual customers. 5) Consider using receivables factoring — selling receivables to a factor for immediate cash. 6) Charge interest on overdue accounts (if legally permitted and contractually agreed).
**Explanation**: 22 days overdue is significant. A common error is suggesting stricter credit terms to all customers — this may lose good customers. The focus should be on improving collection efficiency, not reducing credit availability.
**Tags**: receivables-management, credit-control, evaluation, standard

### Question Q-6.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business's efficiency ratios over two years: Inventory turnover: Y1 8 times, Y2 5 times. Receivables days: Y1 35 days, Y2 50 days. Payables days: Y1 40 days, Y2 60 days. Asset turnover: Y1 2.5 times, Y2 1.8 times. Analyze the trend and identify the business's problems.
**Answer**: All efficiency ratios show significant deterioration: 1) **Inventory turnover** fell from 8 to 5 times (inventory days increased from 45.6 to 73 days) — the business is holding inventory much longer, suggesting over-ordering, declining demand, or obsolete stock. 2) **Receivables days** increased from 35 to 50 days — credit control has weakened substantially; customers are taking 15 days longer to pay, straining cash flow. 3) **Payables days** increased from 40 to 60 days — the business is taking 20 extra days to pay suppliers, suggesting cash flow problems and potentially damaging supplier relationships. 4) **Asset turnover** fell from 2.5 to 1.8 times — the business is generating less revenue per dollar of assets, indicating under-utilized assets or declining sales relative to asset investment. Overall picture: the business is becoming less efficient in all areas of working capital management. The longer payables period (60 days) vs receivables period (50 days) means suppliers are financing the business, but this is unsustainable. The combination of slow inventory turnover, slow collections, and delayed payments is a classic sign of cash flow distress and potential overtrading.
**Explanation**: Each ratio individually shows deterioration; together they paint a picture of systemic working capital management failure. A common error is analyzing ratios in isolation without linking them to the overall cash flow picture.
**Tags**: trend-analysis, efficiency-ratios, working-capital, challenge

### Question Q-6.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate whether a fresh produce wholesaler and a luxury car dealer would have different expected efficiency ratios. Explain why for each ratio.
**Answer**: Yes, expected ratios differ dramatically by industry. **Fresh produce wholesaler**: 1) Very high inventory turnover (15-20+ times, or 18-24 days) — produce is perishable and must be sold quickly. 2) Short receivables days (10-20 days) — many customers pay cash or on very short terms due to the fast-moving nature of the business. 3) Short payables days (15-30 days) — suppliers also expect quick payment for perishable goods. 4) High asset turnover (5-8 times) — relatively low capital investment in assets compared to revenue. 5) Very short working capital cycle (possibly negative). **Luxury car dealer**: 1) Low inventory turnover (3-5 times, or 73-122 days) — cars are high-value, low-volume items that take longer to sell. 2) Longer receivables days (30-60 days) — some sales may be on credit or involve financing arrangements. 3) Moderate payables days (30-45 days) — manufacturers may offer standard credit terms. 4) Lower asset turnover (1-2 times) — significant capital investment in showroom and inventory. 5) Long working capital cycle (60-120 days). The key principle is that efficiency ratio benchmarks are entirely industry-specific and reflect the nature of the business's operations, products, and supply chain.
**Explanation**: A common error is applying the same benchmark to both businesses. Always consider the product nature (perishable vs durable), sales volume, and supply chain structure.
**Tags**: industry-comparison, efficiency-ratios, evaluation, challenge

### Question Q-6.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business has: Revenue $800,000, cost of sales $480,000, inventory $72,000, trade receivables $90,000, trade payables $65,000, purchases $500,000, non-current assets $300,000, current assets (excluding inventory and receivables) $38,000, current liabilities (excluding payables) $25,000. Calculate all efficiency ratios and the working capital cycle. If the business could reduce inventory by 20% and improve receivables collection by 10 days, what would be the new working capital cycle?
**Answer**:

**Current position:**
- Inventory turnover = $480,000 / $72,000 = 6.67 times
- Inventory days = ($72,000 / $480,000) × 365 = 54.8 days
- Receivables days = ($90,000 / $800,000) × 365 = 41.1 days
- Payables days = ($65,000 / $500,000) × 365 = 47.5 days
- Asset turnover = $800,000 / ($300,000 + $72,000 + $90,000 + $38,000 - $65,000 - $25,000) = $800,000 / $410,000 = 1.95 times
- Working capital cycle = 54.8 + 41.1 - 47.5 = 48.4 days

**After improvements:**
- New inventory = $72,000 × 0.8 = $57,600
- New inventory days = ($57,600 / $480,000) × 365 = 43.8 days (reduction of 11.0 days)
- New receivables days = 41.1 - 10 = 31.1 days
- Payables days unchanged at 47.5 days
- New working capital cycle = 43.8 + 31.1 - 47.5 = 27.4 days

**Impact:** The working capital cycle improved from 48.4 to 27.4 days — a reduction of 21.0 days. This means the business needs 21 fewer days of financing to fund its operations. The inventory reduction contributed 11.0 days and the receivables improvement contributed 10.0 days. This would significantly improve cash flow and reduce the need for external borrowing.

**Explanation**: Always calculate all component ratios before the working capital cycle. A common error is not recalculating inventory days from the new inventory value (instead of simply reducing the days by a percentage).

**Tags**: comprehensive, efficiency-ratios, working-capital-cycle, improvement, calculation, challenge
