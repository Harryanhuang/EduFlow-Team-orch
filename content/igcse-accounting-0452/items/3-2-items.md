# Items: 3.2 — Types of errors

### Question Q-3.2-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is an error of omission? Give an example.
**Answer**: An error of omission occurs when a complete transaction is entirely omitted from the books — neither the debit nor the credit entry is recorded. For example, a purchase of goods for $500 on credit from supplier ABC is completely forgotten and no entry is made in either the Purchases account or the Trade payables account. The trial balance will still agree because neither side of the transaction was recorded.
**Explanation**: The key feature is that nothing appears in the books at all. A common error is thinking an error of omission means "forgot the credit entry only" — that would be a single entry error, which the trial balance CAN detect. Omission means the whole transaction is missing.
**Tags**: error-of-omission, definition, undetectable

### Question Q-3.2-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the difference between an error of commission and an error of principle?
**Answer**: An error of commission occurs when an amount is posted to the wrong account but within the same category — e.g., crediting supplier Brown instead of supplier Green (both are trade payables). An error of principle occurs when an amount is posted to the wrong type of account entirely — e.g., recording the purchase of a non-current asset (motor van) as an expense (motor expenses). Both maintain debit = credit equality, so neither is detected by a trial balance.
**Explanation**: Commission = wrong account, same type. Principle = wrong type of account. A common error is confusing the two — if the accounts are of the same nature (two payables, two receivables) it's commission; if one is an asset and the other an expense, it's principle.
**Tags**: error-of-commission, error-of-principle, distinction

### Question Q-3.2-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Match each error type to its description: (a) Error of original entry, (b) Error of reversal, (c) Compensating error. Descriptions: (1) Two or more errors cancel each other out so the trial balance still agrees. (2) The wrong amount is used in both the debit and credit entries. (3) The debit and credit entries are completely swapped.
**Answer**: (a) → (2): Error of original entry — the wrong amount is recorded on both sides, e.g., a $650 purchase is recorded as $560 in both debit and credit. (b) → (3): Error of reversal — debit is recorded as credit and credit as debit, e.g., a cash sale of $200 is recorded as Debit Sales $200, Credit Cash $200 instead of Debit Cash $200, Credit Sales $200. (c) → (1): Compensating error — e.g., Purchases overstated by $100 and Sales understated by $100, the errors happen to cancel.
**Explanation**: Each error type has a unique characteristic. A common error is mixing up original entry (wrong amount on both sides) with reversal (sides swapped). Original entry still has the right accounts; reversal has the right amounts but wrong sides.
**Tags**: error-of-original-entry, error-of-reversal, compensating-error, matching

### Question Q-3.2-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business pays insurance of $800 by cheque. The bookkeeper records: Debit Insurance $800, Debit Bank $800. The trial balance disagrees. What type of error is this, and will the trial balance detect it?
**Answer**: This is not one of the six "undetectable" errors — it is a single-sided error (both entries are debits, no credit). The trial balance WILL detect it because debits exceed credits by $1,600 (two debits of $800 instead of one debit and one credit). The correct entry should have been Debit Insurance $800, Credit Bank $800.
**Explanation**: Not every error is one of the six undetectable types. A common error is labelling this as an "error of reversal" — but reversal means debit and credit are swapped, not that both entries are on the same side. This is a posting error that the trial balance catches.
**Tags**: error-identification, trial-balance-detectable, analysis

### Question Q-3.2-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A purchase of machinery for $10,000 on credit is recorded as: Debit Machinery expenses $10,000, Credit Trade payables $10,000. Identify the error type and explain its effect on the financial statements.
**Answer**: This is an error of principle — machinery is a non-current asset (capital expenditure) but was recorded as an expense (revenue expenditure). The debit was made to "Machinery expenses" instead of "Machinery" (asset account). Effect: Expenses are overstated by $10,000, so profit is understated by $10,000. Non-current assets are understated by $10,000. The trial balance will still agree because debits ($10,000) equal credits ($10,000).
**Explanation**: Capital vs revenue classification is the key. A common error is calling this an error of commission — it is principle because an asset account should have been used, not an expense account. The types of accounts are fundamentally different.
**Tags**: error-of-principle, financial-statement-effect, capital-vs-revenue

### Question Q-3.2-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A credit sale of $450 to customer Ali was correctly recorded in the Sales account but was posted as $540 in Ali's receivables account. Is the trial balance affected? What type of error is this?
**Answer**: The trial balance will NOT agree. The credit to Sales is $450 but the debit to Ali's account is $540 — debits exceed credits by $90. This is NOT one of the six undetectable errors; it is a transposition error ($450 vs $540) that causes an imbalance. The correct entry should have been Debit Ali (receivables) $450, Credit Sales $450.
**Explanation**: The amounts differ between debit ($540) and credit ($450), so the trial balance will show a $90 difference. A common error is calling this an "error of original entry" — original entry means the same wrong amount is used on both sides; here the amounts differ, so the trial balance catches it.
**Tags**: transposition-error, trial-balance-detectable, calculation

### Question Q-3.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: The following errors are discovered after a trial balance has been prepared: (1) A cash purchase of goods $200 was not recorded at all. (2) A payment of $150 to creditor P was correctly recorded in the Cash account but posted to creditor Q's account. (3) Rent received $300 was recorded as $30 in both the Rent received account and the Cash account. For each error, identify the type and state whether the trial balance agreed before the errors were found.
**Answer**: (1) Error of omission — the entire transaction is missing. Trial balance agreed (neither debit nor credit was recorded, so no imbalance). (2) Error of commission — the amount is correct and the account type is correct (trade payables), but posted to the wrong individual creditor's account. Trial balance agreed (debit $150 = credit $150). (3) Error of original entry — the wrong amount ($30 instead of $300) was used on both sides. Trial balance agreed (debit $30 = credit $30, even though both should have been $300).
**Explanation**: All three are undetectable by trial balance. For (3), the key is that both sides used $30 — the error was in the original amount, not in posting. A common error is calling (3) a transposition error — transposition means different amounts on each side; here both sides are the same (wrong) amount.
**Tags**: error-identification, multi-scenario, analysis

### Question Q-3.2-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business's trial balance agrees. Later it is discovered that: (a) a credit sale of $600 to B was completely omitted; (b) repairs to a motor van $400 were debited to the Motor van (asset) account instead of Repairs expense; (c) two unrelated errors were made — a debit of $150 was recorded twice in the Sundry expenses account (a second $150 posting with no source document), and a credit of $150 was recorded twice in the Trade receivables account (an extra $150 reduction posted to customer D's account by mistake). Identify each error type and explain whether the trial balance agreement was coincidental or genuine.
**Answer**: (a) Error of omission — the sale was not recorded at all. Trial balance agreed because neither side was entered. (b) Error of principle — repairs (revenue expenditure, expense) were debited to the asset account (capital expenditure). Trial balance agreed because debit $400 = credit $400, just the wrong account type. (c) Compensating error — the double debit in Sundry expenses inflated the debit total by an extra $150, and the double credit in Trade receivables inflated the credit total by an extra $150. Both sides are overstated by the same amount, so the trial balance still agrees. The agreement was coincidental, not genuine — the totals are wrong even though they match.
**Explanation**: The compensating error works because each error independently adds an equal excess to opposite sides of the trial balance. One extra debit of $150 and one extra credit of $150 cancel out in the column totals. A common error is thinking that "Purchases understated + Sales overstated" is compensating — those errors both push the trial balance in the same direction (debit too low and credit too high both make credits appear larger), so they do NOT offset. True compensating errors require one excess on the debit side and an equal excess on the credit side.
**Tags**: compensating-error, error-of-principle, error-of-omission, evaluation

### Question Q-3.2-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: "All errors that are not detected by a trial balance will be corrected when the financial statements are prepared." Do you agree? Explain why or why not, giving examples.
**Answer**: Disagree. Preparing financial statements does not automatically correct errors — the financial statements are derived from the ledger balances, which still contain the errors. Examples: (1) An error of omission means a transaction is missing from the ledger; it will also be missing from the financial statements unless specifically identified and added. (2) An error of principle (capital expenditure recorded as expense) means the profit will be wrong and assets will be wrong in the financial statements — the error must be actively identified and corrected via journal entries. (3) An error of commission (wrong supplier) does not affect the financial statement totals but means individual account balances are wrong. Errors are only corrected by identifying them and making correcting journal entries, not simply by preparing statements.
**Explanation**: Financial statements reflect what's in the books; if the books have errors, the statements will too. A common error is thinking that preparing statements "fixes" errors — it only reveals some errors (e.g., an asset balance that looks wrong), but does not correct them automatically.
**Tags**: evaluation, error-correction, financial-statements
