# Items: 2.5 — The cash book

### Question Q-2.5-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is a cash book? Why does it serve as both a book of original entry and a ledger account?
**Answer**: The cash book records all cash and bank transactions (receipts and payments). It serves as a book of original entry because transactions are first recorded in it, and as a ledger account because the cash and bank columns themselves act as the Cash and Bank accounts in the ledger — there is no need to post to separate Cash or Bank accounts in the general ledger.
**Explanation**: The cash book's dual nature is unique among accounting books. A common error is thinking cash book entries still need to be posted to Cash and Bank ledger accounts — the cash book columns ARE those accounts.
**Tags**: cash-book, definition, dual-function

### Question Q-2.5-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Describe the structure of a three-column cash book.
**Answer**: A three-column cash book has three columns on each side (debit/receipts and credit/payments): a discount column, a cash column, and a bank column. The debit side records receipts (discount allowed, cash received, banked amounts) and the credit side records payments (discount received, cash paid, bank payments). The cash column records physical cash; the bank column records bank transactions.
**Explanation**: The three columns on each side allow separate tracking of discounts, cash-in-hand, and bank balances. A common error is putting discount allowed on the credit side — discount allowed is a cost to the business and appears on the debit (receipts) side alongside the related customer payment.
**Tags**: three-column-cash-book, structure

### Question Q-2.5-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is discount allowed? On which side of the cash book is it recorded?
**Answer**: Discount allowed is a reduction in the amount a customer pays, given by the business to encourage prompt payment (e.g., "2% discount if paid within 14 days"). It is recorded on the debit side of the cash book in the discount column because it represents a cost to the business — the business receives less cash than the full invoice amount.
**Explanation**: Discount allowed is an expense and appears on the debit side. A common error is recording it on the credit side because it is a "discount" — the key is that it costs the business money, so it sits on the same side as other debits.
**Tags**: discount-allowed, expense, cash-book

### Question Q-2.5-04
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is discount received? On which side of the cash book is it recorded?
**Answer**: Discount received is a reduction in the amount the business pays to a supplier, given by the supplier for prompt payment. It is recorded on the credit side of the cash book in the discount column because it represents a saving (income) to the business — the business pays less than the full amount owed.
**Explanation**: Discount received is a gain and appears on the credit side. A common error is confusing it with discount allowed — discount allowed (we give) is debit; discount received (we get) is credit.
**Tags**: discount-received, income, cash-book

### Question Q-2.5-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Ahmed owes $500. The business offers a 5% discount for payment within 14 days. Ahmed pays $475 within the period. Show how this is recorded in the three-column cash book.
**Answer**: Debit side: Cash column $475, Discount allowed column $25. The total of $500 ($475 + $25) settles Ahmed's account in full. The $475 is the actual cash received; the $25 is the discount allowed (5% of $500). The credit entry for the full $500 is posted to Ahmed's personal account in the receivables ledger.
**Explanation**: The discount column total is posted to the discount allowed account in the general ledger (debit). A common error is recording only $475 without the discount entry — the full $500 must be accounted for to clear the customer's account.
**Tags**: discount-allowed, application, cash-book

### Question Q-2.5-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What is a bank overdraft? How is it shown in the cash book?
**Answer**: A bank overdraft occurs when the business withdraws more money from its bank account than is available, creating a negative bank balance. It is a liability (the business owes the bank money). In the cash book, a bank overdraft appears as a credit balance in the bank column — the credit side total exceeds the debit side total. When balancing the bank column, the overdraft balance b/f is shown on the debit side (opposite to a normal positive balance).
**Explanation**: An overdraft is a liability, so its balance is credit. A common error is thinking a bank overdraft should show as a negative figure in the debit column — it is a positive credit balance in the bank column.
**Tags**: bank-overdraft, liability, cash-book

### Question Q-2.5-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: The cash book bank column shows: Debit side total $15,000, Credit side total $18,000. What does this mean and what is the balance?
**Answer**: The credit side exceeds the debit side by $3,000 ($18,000 - $15,000). This means the business has a bank overdraft of $3,000 — it has paid out more than it has received through the bank. The balance c/f of $3,000 is entered on the debit side to balance, and the balance b/f of $3,000 (credit) is carried forward as an overdraft liability.
**Explanation**: When the credit side of the bank column is larger, the business is overdrawn. A common error is calling this a "bank balance" (positive asset) — it is an overdraft (liability).
**Tags**: bank-overdraft, balance-calculation, application

### Question Q-2.5-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business receives $800 from a credit customer less 5% discount allowed, and pays a supplier $600 less 10% discount received. Both amounts go through the bank. Show the entries in the three-column cash book.
**Answer**: Debit side: Bank column $760 ($800 x 95%), Discount allowed column $40 ($800 x 5%). Credit side: Bank column $540 ($600 x 90%), Discount received column $60 ($600 x 10%). The net bank effect is $760 - $540 = $220 increase. The discount allowed of $40 is an expense (posted to debit of discount allowed account); the discount received of $60 is income (posted to credit of discount received account).
**Explanation**: Calculate the net amounts after discount. A common error is recording the gross amounts ($800 and $600) in the bank column — the bank column records actual money moved, not the original invoice amounts.
**Tags**: discount, application, three-column, calculation

### Question Q-2.5-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Why is a contra entry (cash deposited into bank or cash withdrawn from bank for business use) recorded on both sides of the cash book but not posted to the ledger?
**Answer**: A contra entry affects both the cash and bank columns of the cash book simultaneously. For example, depositing cash into the bank: credit the cash column (cash leaves the office) and debit the bank column (money enters the bank). It is recorded on both sides of the same book, so the double entry is complete within the cash book itself — no separate ledger posting is needed. Contra entries are marked with a "C" in the ledger reference column to show they are self-balancing.
**Explanation**: The cash book IS the ledger for cash and bank, so internal transfers within it are complete. A common error is posting contra entries to the ledger as if they were external transactions — this would double-count the entry.
**Tags**: contra-entry, cash-book, double-entry
