# Items: 4.1 — Depreciation straight line method

### Question Q-4.1-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is depreciation? Why do businesses charge depreciation on non-current assets?
**Answer**: Depreciation is the systematic allocation of the cost of a non-current asset over its useful life. It represents the consumption of the asset's economic benefits over time. Businesses charge depreciation for two main reasons: (1) Matching concept — to match the cost of the asset against the revenue it helps generate in each accounting period, giving a true and fair view of profit. (2) Prudence — to reflect that the asset's value is being used up, so the statement of financial position does not overstate the asset's worth. Depreciation is NOT a method of valuing the asset at its market price.
**Explanation**: A common error is thinking depreciation records a fall in market value — it does not. Depreciation is about cost allocation, not valuation. Even if the asset's market value rises, depreciation is still charged on the original cost.
**Tags**: depreciation, purpose, matching-concept

### Question Q-4.1-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the formula for straight-line depreciation? Calculate the annual depreciation charge for a machine costing $12,000 with a residual value of $2,000 and a useful life of 5 years.
**Answer**: Straight-line depreciation formula: Annual depreciation charge = (Cost - Residual value) / Useful life in years. For the machine: ($12,000 - $2,000) / 5 = $10,000 / 5 = $2,000 per year. The residual value (also called scrap value) is the estimated amount the business expects to receive when the asset is disposed of at the end of its useful life. The depreciable amount is the cost minus residual value — this is the total amount that will be charged as depreciation over the asset's life.
**Explanation**: A common error is dividing the full cost by useful life without deducting residual value first — this overstates the annual depreciation. The residual value is never depreciated because the business expects to recover it at disposal.
**Tags**: straight-line, formula, calculation

### Question Q-4.1-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: A delivery van costs $30,000. Estimated residual value $6,000. Useful life 4 years. Show the accumulated depreciation at the end of each year using the straight-line method.
**Answer**: Annual depreciation = ($30,000 - $6,000) / 4 = $6,000 per year.
End of year 1: Accumulated depreciation $6,000. Carrying amount (net book value) $30,000 - $6,000 = $24,000.
End of year 2: Accumulated depreciation $12,000. Carrying amount $18,000.
End of year 3: Accumulated depreciation $18,000. Carrying amount $12,000.
End of year 4: Accumulated depreciation $24,000. Carrying amount $6,000 (= residual value).
Accumulated depreciation is the total depreciation charged on an asset since it was acquired. Carrying amount (net book value) = Cost - Accumulated depreciation.
**Explanation**: A common error is confusing accumulated depreciation with the annual charge — accumulated is cumulative, the annual charge is constant under straight line. Also, the carrying amount at the end of the useful life should equal the residual value, not zero.
**Tags**: accumulated-depreciation, carrying-amount, calculation

### Question Q-4.1-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business buys equipment for $8,500 on 1 January 20X1. Residual value $500. Useful life 8 years. The business charges depreciation using the straight-line method. Show: (a) the depreciation expense in the statement of profit or loss for each of the first 3 years, (b) the carrying amount at the end of year 3.
**Answer**: Annual depreciation = ($8,500 - $500) / 8 = $8,000 / 8 = $1,000 per year.
(a) Depreciation expense in the statement of profit or loss: Year 1 = $1,000, Year 2 = $1,000, Year 3 = $1,000. Under straight-line, the expense is constant each year.
(b) Accumulated depreciation at end of year 3 = $1,000 × 3 = $3,000. Carrying amount = $8,500 - $3,000 = $5,500.
**Explanation**: A common error is thinking the depreciation expense changes each year under straight-line — it is constant. The carrying amount decreases each year because accumulated depreciation increases. The depreciation expense always appears in the statement of profit or loss; the accumulated depreciation appears on the statement of financial position as a deduction from cost.
**Tags**: profit-or-loss, carrying-amount, straight-line

### Question Q-4.1-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A machine costs $20,000. After 3 years of straight-line depreciation, the accumulated depreciation is $9,000. The residual value was $2,000. What is the useful life of the machine? What is the carrying amount at the end of year 5?
**Answer**: Annual depreciation charge = $9,000 / 3 years = $3,000 per year. Using the formula: Annual depreciation = (Cost - Residual value) / Useful life. So $3,000 = ($20,000 - $2,000) / Useful life = $18,000 / Useful life. Useful life = $18,000 / $3,000 = 6 years. Carrying amount at end of year 5: Accumulated depreciation = $3,000 × 5 = $15,000. Carrying amount = $20,000 - $15,000 = $5,000.
**Explanation**: A common error is trying to calculate useful life by dividing cost by annual depreciation without considering residual value. The formula uses the depreciable amount (cost - residual value), not the full cost. Always work backwards from the formula: annual charge = depreciable amount / life, so life = depreciable amount / annual charge.
**Tags**: reverse-calculation, useful-life, straight-line

### Question Q-4.1-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business buys a machine for $15,000 on 1 April 20X3. Residual value $3,000. Useful life 4 years. Depreciation is charged using the straight-line method. The financial year ends 31 December. Calculate the depreciation charge for the year ended 31 December 20X3 and the carrying amount at that date.
**Answer**: Annual depreciation = ($15,000 - $3,000) / 4 = $12,000 / 4 = $3,000 per year. The machine was owned for 9 months in the year ended 31 December 20X3 (April to December). Depreciation for 9 months = $3,000 × 9/12 = $2,250. Carrying amount at 31 December 20X3 = $15,000 - $2,250 = $12,750.
**Explanation**: A common error is charging a full year's depreciation when the asset was not owned for the full year. Depreciation must be time-apportioned for the period the asset was actually owned. Always check the purchase date against the financial year end to determine how many months to charge.
**Tags**: time-apportionment, partial-year, calculation

### Question Q-4.1-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business buys equipment for $25,000 on 1 January 20X1. Residual value $5,000. Useful life 5 years. Straight-line depreciation is used. On 30 June 20X3 the equipment is sold for $12,000. Calculate the profit or loss on disposal and show the journal entry.
**Answer**: Annual depreciation = ($25,000 - $5,000) / 5 = $4,000 per year. The asset was owned for 2.5 years (Jan 20X1 to Jun 20X3). Accumulated depreciation at disposal = $4,000 × 2.5 = $10,000. Carrying amount at disposal = $25,000 - $10,000 = $15,000. Sale proceeds $12,000. Loss on disposal = $15,000 - $12,000 = $3,000 loss (carrying amount exceeds proceeds).
Journal entry: Debit Cash $12,000, Debit Accumulated depreciation $10,000, Debit Loss on disposal $3,000, Credit Equipment (cost) $25,000. Narration: "Disposal of equipment sold for $12,000, loss on disposal $3,000."
**Explanation**: A common error is comparing the sale proceeds with the original cost instead of the carrying amount. The profit or loss on disposal is always: Sale proceeds minus carrying amount (cost minus accumulated depreciation to the date of disposal). The accumulated depreciation must be removed from the books at disposal, along with the original cost.
**Tags**: asset-disposal, profit-or-loss-on-disposal, journal-entry

### Question Q-4.1-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A company has a policy of charging depreciation on a straight-line basis. During the year ended 31 December 20X4, the following information relates to machinery: Cost at 1 January 20X4: $80,000. Accumulated depreciation at 1 January 20X4: $14,400. On 1 July 20X4, additional machinery was purchased for $20,000 (residual value $4,000, useful life 8 years). The original machinery (cost $80,000) was purchased on 1 January 20X2 with residual value $8,000 and useful life 10 years. Calculate the total depreciation charge for the year ended 31 December 20X4.
**Answer**: Two assets to consider: Original machinery: Annual depreciation = ($80,000 - $8,000) / 10 = $7,200 per year. This asset was owned for the full year 20X4, so charge = $7,200. Verification: accumulated depreciation at 1 Jan 20X4 = $7,200 × 2 years (20X2 and 20X3) = $14,400 — matches the given figure. New machinery: Annual depreciation = ($20,000 - $4,000) / 8 = $16,000 / 8 = $2,000 per year. Owned for 6 months (July to December), so charge = $2,000 × 6/12 = $1,000. Total depreciation charge for 20X4 = $7,200 + $1,000 = $8,200.
**Explanation**: A common error is treating all machinery as one pool and applying a single depreciation rate. Each asset (or batch of assets acquired at different times) must be depreciated separately based on its own cost, residual value, useful life, and ownership period. The accumulated depreciation at the start of the year ($14,400) is consistent with 2 years of $7,200 annual depreciation on the original machinery — this confirms the parameters are self-consistent.
**Tags**: multiple-assets, time-apportionment, multi-step

### Question Q-4.1-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: "Straight-line depreciation is the best method because it is simple and consistent." Discuss this statement. Compare straight-line with the reducing balance method and explain when each method is more appropriate.
**Answer**: Straight-line is simple and consistent, but whether it is "best" depends on how the asset's economic benefits are consumed. Straight-line charges equal amounts each year — this is appropriate when the asset provides equal benefits throughout its life (e.g., a building, where the benefit does not significantly decline each year). Reducing balance charges more depreciation in early years and less in later years — this is appropriate when the asset is more productive when new and loses value faster initially (e.g., vehicles, computers, mobile phones). A vehicle loses most of its value in the first few years; a computer becomes obsolete quickly. In these cases, reducing balance better matches the actual consumption pattern. The matching concept requires that depreciation reflects how economic benefits are consumed, so neither method is universally "best" — the choice depends on the nature of the asset.
**Explanation**: A common error is saying straight-line is always better because it is simpler. Simplicity is not the criterion — the matching concept is. Examiners expect candidates to evaluate based on whether the depreciation pattern reflects the asset's actual usage and benefit consumption.
**Tags**: evaluation, straight-line-vs-reducing-balance, matching-concept
