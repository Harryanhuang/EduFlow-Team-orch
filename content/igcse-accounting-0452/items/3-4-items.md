# Items: 3.4 — Bank reconciliation

### Question Q-3.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Why might the balance on the bank statement differ from the balance on the cash book (bank column)? List the three main categories of difference.
**Answer**: The bank statement balance and the cash book balance often differ at any given date. The three main categories of difference are: (1) Timing differences — transactions recorded in one book but not yet appearing on the other, such as unpresented cheques (issued by the business but not yet presented to the bank) and uncredited deposits (lodged by the business but not yet cleared by the bank). (2) Items on the bank statement not yet recorded in the cash book — standing orders, direct debits, bank charges, interest, and dishonoured cheques that the business only learns about when the statement arrives. (3) Errors — mistakes made either by the business (in the cash book) or by the bank (on the statement).
**Explanation**: A common error is thinking one balance must be wrong if they differ — they are simply records from two different points in time with different information. The bank reconciliation process identifies and explains every difference.
**Tags**: bank-reconciliation, differences, categories

### Question Q-3.4-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the purpose of a bank reconciliation statement? List two benefits it provides to a business.
**Answer**: A bank reconciliation statement explains the difference between the bank statement balance and the updated cash book balance at a specific date. Two key benefits: (1) It verifies the accuracy of both records — if every difference is identified and explained, it confirms neither record contains unexplained errors. (2) It updates the business for items on the bank statement not yet recorded in the cash book, such as bank charges, direct debits, standing orders, and dishonoured cheques, so the cash book can be brought up to date before final accounts are prepared.
**Explanation**: Bank reconciliation is a detective control, not a correction tool. A common error is thinking the reconciliation statement itself corrects the cash book — it does not; it identifies what needs correcting. The actual corrections are made by updating the cash book entries.
**Tags**: bank-reconciliation, purpose, benefits

### Question Q-3.4-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: The cash book shows a balance of $4,500 (debit). The bank statement shows $3,800 (credit). The difference is caused by: (a) unpresented cheques of $1,200, and (b) a deposit of $500 not yet credited by the bank. Show the bank reconciliation statement starting from the bank statement balance.
**Answer**: Bank reconciliation statement as at the date:
Bank statement balance (credit) $3,800
Add: Unpresented cheques $1,200
Less: Deposit not yet credited ($500)
Updated cash book balance: $4,500

Unpresented cheques are cheques the business has issued (credited in the cash book) but the bank has not yet paid — they reduce the bank's view of available funds, so we add them back to the bank statement balance. The deposit not yet credited is money the business has recorded as received (debited in the cash book) but the bank has not yet cleared — we subtract it from the bank statement balance because the bank does not yet reflect it. $3,800 + $1,200 - $500 = $4,500, matching the cash book.
**Explanation**: A common error is adding unpresented cheques to the cash book instead of adjusting the bank statement side. Unpresented cheques are already in the cash book — the bank statement is the one that needs adjusting. The reconciliation starts from one balance and works toward the other.
**Tags**: reconciliation-statement, timing-differences, practical

### Question Q-3.4-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: The bank statement shows: bank charges $45, interest received $30, standing order payment to the landlord $200, and a direct debit for insurance $80. None of these are in the cash book. The cash book shows a debit balance of $2,400 before adjustments. Update the cash book and calculate the adjusted balance.
**Answer**: The cash book must be updated for all items appearing on the bank statement but not yet recorded:
- Bank charges $45: Credit cash book $45 (expense reduces bank balance)
- Interest received $30: Debit cash book $30 (income increases bank balance)
- Standing order $200: Credit cash book $200 (payment reduces bank balance)
- Direct debit $80: Credit cash book $80 (payment reduces bank balance)
Adjusted cash book balance: $2,400 + $30 - $45 - $200 - $80 = $2,105 (debit).
**Explanation**: A common error is confusing which side of the cash book each entry goes on. Remember: bank charges, standing orders, and direct debits are payments (credit the cash book). Interest received is income (debit the cash book). The standing order and direct debit are both payments even though they are initiated differently — both reduce the bank balance.
**Tags**: cash-book-update, standing-order, direct-debit

### Question Q-3.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business receives its bank statement and notices a cheque from customer Ana for $350 was dishonoured (returned unpaid). The original receipt had been recorded in the cash book. Explain the effect on the cash book and write the correcting entry. Also explain why a standing order payment does NOT require this type of correction.
**Answer**: The dishonoured cheque means the $350 that was previously debited in the cash book (when the cheque was received) must be reversed. Correction: Credit cash book $350, Narration: "Correction — cheque from Ana dishonoured, payment not received." The business still has a receivable from Ana — she now owes the $350 again. A standing order payment does not require this type of reversal because a standing order is an actual payment made by the bank on the business's instructions — the money has genuinely left the account. The standing order simply needs to be recorded in the cash book (credit side) because the business did not record it earlier; it is not reversing a previous entry.
**Explanation**: A common error is treating dishonoured cheques the same as unrecorded payments — they are fundamentally different. A dishonoured cheque reverses a previously recorded receipt (the money never arrived); a standing order records a genuine payment that was missed.
**Tags**: dishonoured-cheque, cash-book, standing-order

### Question Q-3.4-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: On 31 December, the cash book (bank column) shows a debit balance of $6,800. The bank statement shows a credit balance of $5,950. The following differences are identified: (1) Cheques issued but not yet presented: $1,400. (2) A cheque deposited for $850 has not yet been cleared by the bank. (3) Bank charges of $60 appearing on the statement are not in the cash book. (4) A cheque from customer Ben for $320 was dishonoured but still recorded in the cash book. Update the cash book and prepare the bank reconciliation statement.
**Answer**: Step 1 — Update the cash book for items (3) and (4):
Opening balance: $6,800 debit
Less: Bank charges $60 (credit)
Less: Dishonoured cheque $320 (credit)
Adjusted cash book balance: $6,800 - $60 - $320 = $6,420 debit.

Step 2 — Bank reconciliation statement from bank statement balance:
Bank statement balance (credit) $5,950
Add: Unpresented cheques $1,400
Less: Uncredited deposit ($850)
Reconciled balance: $5,950 + $1,400 - $850 = $6,500.

Wait — this gives $6,500, not $6,420. The $80 difference means there is still an unexplained error. Rechecking: all identified items have been processed. The $80 discrepancy suggests an unidentified error. In an exam, you would state "reconciled balance does not match adjusted cash book — $80 unexplained difference remains."
**Explanation**: A common error is including bank charges and dishonoured cheques in the bank reconciliation statement — they should NOT appear there because they are already in the adjusted cash book. The reconciliation statement only deals with timing differences (unpresented cheques and uncredited deposits) after the cash book has been updated.
**Tags**: reconciliation-statement, cash-book-update, multi-step

### Question Q-3.4-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: During bank reconciliation, the accountant finds that the bank statement balance is $3,200 (credit) but the adjusted cash book balance is $3,650 (debit). The only timing differences are: unpresented cheques of $900 and a lodgement not yet credited of $450. Explain why these timing differences alone do not account for the full $450 difference between the two balances, and identify what additional error could explain it.
**Answer**: Starting from bank statement $3,200: add unpresented cheques $900 = $4,100; subtract uncredited lodgement $450 = $3,650. This matches the adjusted cash book exactly. So the timing differences DO fully explain the difference. There is no additional error. The $450 difference between bank statement ($3,200) and cash book ($3,650) is entirely accounted for by: unpresented cheques ($900 — cheques the business has paid out but the bank has not yet processed) and uncredited lodgement ($450 — money the business has deposited but the bank has not yet cleared). $3,200 + $900 - $450 = $3,650.
**Explanation**: A common error is overthinking and looking for hidden errors when the timing differences already reconcile perfectly. The key is to work through the reconciliation calculation: bank statement balance + unpresented cheques - uncredited deposits = adjusted cash book balance. If the numbers match, no further errors exist.
**Tags**: reconciliation, analysis, timing-differences

### Question Q-3.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business's bank statement shows a payment of $1,500 to supplier Zebra that the business does not recognize. Investigation reveals: (a) the bank made an error and credited the wrong account (another customer's cheque was wrongly debited to this business's account), and (b) separately, the cash book shows a cheque issued to supplier Tiger for $520 but the bank statement shows $250. Classify each as a bank error or company error, and explain how each should be treated in the bank reconciliation.
**Answer**: (a) The $1,500 payment the business does not recognize is a bank error — the bank debited the wrong account. Treatment: The business does NOT adjust its cash book for a bank error. Instead, the bank error is noted on the bank reconciliation statement as an adjustment to the bank statement balance (add $1,500 back to the bank statement balance, since the bank wrongly reduced the balance). The business should contact the bank to have the error corrected on their records. (b) The cheque for $520 recorded in the cash book but showing as $250 on the bank statement is also a bank error (assuming the business wrote the cheque for $520 and the bank processed it incorrectly). Treatment: Similarly, adjust the bank statement side of the reconciliation by the $270 difference. The cash book remains unchanged because the business recorded the correct amount. If the business made the error (wrote $250 in the cash book instead of $520), then the cash book would need correcting — but the question states the cash book shows $520, which is correct.
**Explanation**: A common error is adjusting the cash book for bank errors — the cash book should only be corrected for the business's own errors. Bank errors are corrected by the bank, not by the business. The bank reconciliation statement adjusts the bank statement balance for bank errors, and the adjusted cash book for the business's own errors.
**Tags**: bank-error, company-error, reconciliation

### Question Q-3.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: "A bank reconciliation statement is only needed if the business suspects fraud." Do you agree? Explain the relationship between regular bank reconciliation and internal controls, and describe what a business with a large number of bank transactions should do if the reconciliation does not balance after the first attempt.
**Answer**: Disagree. Bank reconciliation is a routine accounting procedure, not a fraud-detection tool alone. Every business with a bank account should prepare a bank reconciliation at regular intervals (typically monthly when the bank statement arrives), regardless of whether fraud is suspected. The relationship to internal controls: bank reconciliation is a key detective control that verifies the accuracy of cash records, identifies unrecorded transactions (bank charges, dishonoured cheques), and detects errors by either the business or the bank. If the reconciliation does not balance after the first attempt: (1) Recheck the arithmetic in both the cash book and the reconciliation statement. (2) Compare each item on the bank statement with the cash book to ensure all items are accounted for. (3) Check for transposition errors (e.g., $540 recorded as $450 — the difference will be divisible by 9). (4) Verify opening balances agree with the previous reconciliation. (5) If still unresolved, the difference may be placed in a suspense account temporarily while further investigation continues, but this should not be a permanent solution.
**Explanation**: A common error is thinking bank reconciliation is optional or only for large discrepancies — even small differences indicate an error that should be found. The transposition error check (difference divisible by 9) is a practical technique that examiners often expect candidates to mention.
**Tags**: bank-reconciliation, internal-control, evaluation
