# Items: 7.4 — Information quality characteristics

### Question Q-7.4-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What are the two fundamental qualitative characteristics of useful financial information?
**Answer**: The two fundamental qualitative characteristics are **relevance** and **faithful representation**. **Relevance** means the information is capable of making a difference in users' decisions — it has predictive value (helps forecast future outcomes), confirmatory value (confirms or corrects past assessments), or both. Information is relevant if its omission or misstatement could influence decisions. **Faithful representation** means the information accurately reflects the economic phenomena it purports to represent. It must be complete (all necessary information included), neutral (free from bias — not manipulated to achieve a particular result), and free from error (no errors in the process or description). Both characteristics must be present for information to be useful — relevant information that is not faithfully represented is misleading, and faithfully represented information that is not relevant is useless.
**Explanation**: These are the two fundamental characteristics. A common error is listing all characteristics equally — relevance and faithful representation are fundamental; the others (comparability, verifiability, timeliness, understandability) are enhancing characteristics that improve usefulness but cannot make irrelevant or misrepresenting information useful.
**Tags**: relevance, faithful-representation, quality-characteristics, foundation

### Question Q-7.4-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What are the four enhancing qualitative characteristics of financial information?
**Answer**: The four enhancing characteristics are: 1) **Comparability** — information can be compared across periods (trend analysis) and across entities (inter-firm comparison). Consistency in accounting policies supports comparability. 2) **Verifiability** — different knowledgeable and independent observers would reach consensus that the information is a faithful representation. Source documents, calculations, and clear methods support verifiability. 3) **Timeliness** — information is available to decision-makers in time to influence their decisions. Delayed information loses its relevance. 4) **Understandability** — information is presented clearly and concisely so that users with reasonable knowledge of business and accounting can comprehend it. Complex information should not be excluded simply because it is difficult — it should be presented as clearly as possible. These characteristics enhance the usefulness of information that is already relevant and faithfully represented.
**Explanation**: Enhancing characteristics improve usefulness but cannot make irrelevant or misrepresenting information useful. A common error is treating all characteristics as equally important — the fundamental ones come first.
**Tags**: enhancing-characteristics, comparability, verifiability, timeliness, understandability, foundation

### Question Q-7.4-03
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business prepares its financial statements but delays publication by 6 months. The information is accurate and complete. Which qualitative characteristics are affected and why?
**Answer**: **Timeliness** is the primary characteristic affected — information available 6 months late may no longer influence users' decisions. By the time the statements are published, economic conditions, market positions, and financial circumstances may have changed significantly. **Relevance** is also affected — information that was relevant at the year-end date may have become less relevant or irrelevant by the time it is published. For example, a receivables balance that was collectible at year-end may have become uncollectible by the time the statements are published. **Comparability** may also be reduced if the business's competitors publish their statements within 3 months — users cannot make timely comparisons. Faithful representation is NOT affected — the information is still accurate and complete. However, faithful representation alone cannot compensate for the loss of timeliness and relevance.
**Explanation**: Timeliness and relevance are linked — delayed information loses relevance. A common error is thinking accurate information is always useful regardless of timing. Accuracy without timeliness produces a reliable but obsolete picture.
**Tags**: timeliness, relevance, delayed-reporting, standard

### Question Q-7.4-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business uses highly complex financial instruments. Management argues that the information should be excluded from the financial statements because users would not understand it. Evaluate this argument with reference to qualitative characteristics.
**Answer**: This argument misapplies the understandability characteristic. Understandability requires that information be presented clearly and concisely for users with reasonable knowledge of business and accounting. It does NOT mean that complex information should be excluded. In fact: 1) **Relevance** — information about complex financial instruments is likely highly relevant because it affects the business's risk profile and financial position. 2) **Faithful representation** — excluding complex instruments would make the financial statements incomplete and therefore not a faithful representation. 3) **Understandability** — complex information should be presented as clearly as possible, with appropriate disclosures and explanations in the notes. If necessary, the business can provide additional narrative explanation. The solution is not exclusion but better communication. The qualitative characteristics hierarchy is clear: relevant and faithfully represented information must be included; understandability guides how it is presented, not whether it is included.
**Explanation**: Understandability guides presentation, not inclusion. A common error is excluding complex information in the name of understandability — this violates faithful representation (completeness) and relevance.
**Tags**: understandability, complex-instruments, faithful-representation, standard

### Question Q-7.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: How do the qualitative characteristics of relevance and faithful representation sometimes conflict? Give an example and explain how the conflict should be resolved.
**Answer**: Relevance and faithful representation can conflict when the most relevant information is difficult to measure reliably. Example: **Fair value measurement of property**. The market value of a property may be highly relevant (users want to know current value), but if there is no active market and the valuation requires significant judgment and assumptions, the faithful representation may be questionable (the value may not be verifiable or free from error). Another example: **Contingent assets**. A possible future gain from a lawsuit may be relevant information, but if the outcome is highly uncertain, recognizing it would not be a faithful representation. Resolution: When the two conflict, the information should be: 1) Included if it is relevant AND can be faithfully represented (even if measurement involves some estimation — estimates are acceptable if the method is clearly disclosed). 2) Disclosed in notes rather than recognized in the statements if it is relevant but cannot be reliably measured (e.g. contingent assets disclosed but not recognized). 3) The method and assumptions should be fully disclosed so users can assess the reliability.
**Explanation**: Conflict between relevance and faithful representation is common. A common error is thinking one always trumps the other — the correct approach is to include the information with appropriate disclosure of uncertainty.
**Tags**: relevance, faithful-representation, conflict, resolution, standard

### Question Q-7.4-06
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Evaluate how a change in accounting policy from historical cost to fair value for non-current assets affects each of the six qualitative characteristics.
**Answer**: **Relevance**: Increases — fair value provides current information about asset values, which is more useful for decision-making than historical cost. Users can assess the business's current resource base. **Faithful representation**: May decrease — fair value estimates often involve judgment, assumptions, and market models that may not be verifiable or free from error. If the market is inactive, fair value becomes subjective. **Comparability**: Mixed effect — improves comparability with other businesses using fair value, but reduces comparability with the business's own prior periods (unless restated). **Verifiability**: Decreases — historical cost is easily verified from purchase documents. Fair value requires valuation techniques and assumptions that different valuers may estimate differently. **Timeliness**: May decrease — fair value assessments take more time and may delay financial statement preparation. **Understandability**: May decrease — fair value measurements and the assumptions behind them are more complex than historical cost and harder for non-specialist users to understand. Overall: The change trades off reliability (faithful representation, verifiability) for relevance. Whether the trade-off is worthwhile depends on the nature of the assets and the availability of reliable market data.
**Explanation:** The six characteristics can point in different directions. A common error is evaluating only relevance and ignoring the impact on faithful representation and verifiability. The best assessment considers all six characteristics and weighs the trade-offs.
**Tags:** historical-cost, fair-value, qualitative-characteristics, evaluation, challenge

### Question Q-7.4-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A lender is assessing a loan application using financial statements prepared under different accounting policies than the lender's own business. The borrower uses FIFO for inventory, straight-line depreciation, and recognizes revenue on delivery. The lender uses AVCO, reducing balance, and recognizes revenue on cash receipt. Discuss how the qualitative characteristics of comparability and understandability are affected, and what the lender should do.
**Answer**: **Comparability** is affected because the different policies make direct ratio comparison misleading. For example: FIFO vs AVCO affects inventory values and cost of sales; straight-line vs reducing balance affects asset values and depreciation expense; revenue recognition timing affects reported revenue and profit in any given period. The lender cannot directly compare the borrower's ratios with its own or with industry benchmarks without adjusting for policy differences. **Understandability** is affected because the lender needs to understand the borrower's policies to interpret the figures correctly. If the policies are not clearly disclosed, the lender may misinterpret the financial position. **What the lender should do**: 1) Read the notes to the borrower's financial statements to understand the accounting policies used. 2) Adjust the borrower's figures to a common basis for comparison (e.g. recalculate cost of sales under AVCO, recalculate depreciation under reducing balance). 3) Focus on trends within the borrower's own financial statements (which are comparable if policies are consistent over time) rather than cross-entity comparison. 4) Consider whether the borrower's policies are appropriate for its industry. 5) Request additional information if policies are unclear or if the lender suspects manipulation.
**Explanation**: Comparability requires consistent policies. A common error is comparing ratios directly without adjusting for policy differences. The lender must normalize the data before making comparisons.
**Tags**: comparability, understandability, policy-differences, lending-assessment, challenge

### Question Q-7.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business's management wants to present the most favorable picture possible to attract investors. They consider: (a) delaying recognition of a probable lawsuit loss, (b) using an overly optimistic estimate for the useful life of non-current assets, (c) presenting revenue growth prominently while downplaying increasing debt levels. Evaluate each action against the qualitative characteristics and identify which are violated.
**Answer**: (a) **Delaying recognition of probable lawsuit loss**: Violates **faithful representation** — the financial statements would be incomplete and biased. Also violates **neutrality** (a component of faithful representation) because the omission is designed to achieve a favorable result. May also violate **relevance** if the lawsuit loss is material to users' decisions. (b) **Overly optimistic useful life estimate**: Violates **faithful representation** — the estimate is not neutral (biased towards lower depreciation and higher profit) and may not be free from error if it does not reflect the actual pattern of economic benefits. Also violates **verifiability** — independent observers would not agree with the overly optimistic estimate. (c) **Presenting revenue growth prominently while downplaying debt**: Violates **faithful representation** (neutrality) — the presentation is biased to achieve a particular impression. Also reduces **understandability** because users may not get a balanced view of the business's financial position. If debt information is buried in notes rather than clearly presented, it also reduces **completeness**. All three actions represent a fundamental failure of faithful representation and neutrality. The qualitative characteristics exist to protect users from exactly this type of manipulation. Investors who rely on biased information may make incorrect decisions.
**Explanation**: Neutrality is a key component of faithful representation. A common error is focusing on relevance while ignoring that biased presentation violates faithful representation regardless of whether the information is technically included somewhere.
**Tags**: faithful-representation, neutrality, bias, manipulation, comprehensive, challenge

### Question Q-7.4-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: Compare two approaches to preparing financial statements: Approach A emphasizes relevance — using fair values, forward-looking estimates, and management's best judgments. Approach B emphasizes faithful representation — using historical cost, verifiable transactions, and conservative estimates. Evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of each approach and explain which characteristics are best served by each.
**Answer**: **Approach A (relevance-focused)**: Strengths: Provides current, decision-useful information. Fair values reflect current market conditions. Forward-looking estimates help users assess future prospects. Particularly valuable for assets with active markets (investments, property). Weaknesses: Fair value estimates may not be verifiable — different valuers produce different results. Forward-looking estimates may be unreliable, especially for uncertain events. Management judgment introduces bias risk. During market volatility, fair values may fluctuate dramatically, creating volatility in reported figures that does not reflect underlying business performance. Best serves: relevance, timeliness. May compromise: faithful representation, verifiability. **Approach B (faithful representation-focused)**: Strengths: Historical cost is objectively verifiable from transaction documents. Conservative estimates reduce the risk of overstating assets and profits. Consistent and reliable over time. Less susceptible to manipulation. Weaknesses: Historical cost may become irrelevant over time — a building purchased 30 years ago at $100,000 may now be worth $2 million, but the balance sheet shows a heavily depreciated $100,000. Conservative estimates may understatement assets and profits, misleading users about the business's true position. May not reflect economic reality. Best serves: faithful representation, verifiability, comparability. May compromise: relevance. **Conclusion**: Neither approach is perfect. The ideal is a balanced approach that maximizes both relevance and faithful representation — using fair value where reliable market data exists, and historical cost where it does not. Accounting standards reflect this balance by requiring fair value for some items (financial instruments) and historical cost for others (property, plant and equipment).
**Explanation**: The relevance vs reliability trade-off is the central tension in accounting. A common error is declaring one approach "better" — the correct answer recognizes that both have value and the best approach depends on the nature of the item being measured.
**Tags**: relevance-vs-reliability, historical-cost, fair-value, comprehensive, evaluation, challenge
