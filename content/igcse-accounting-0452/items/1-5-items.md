# Items: 1.5 — Capital and revenue items

### Question Q-1.5-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is capital expenditure? Give two examples.
**Answer**: Capital expenditure is spending on the purchase of, or improvement to, non-current assets. It increases the value or useful life of the asset. Examples: purchasing a delivery van, building an extension to a factory, installing a new machine.
**Explanation**: The key test is whether the spending acquires or improves a non-current asset. A common error is thinking any large purchase is capital expenditure — the size of the amount is not the criterion; the nature of the purchase is.
**Tags**: capital-expenditure, definition, examples

### Question Q-1.5-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is revenue expenditure? Give two examples.
**Answer**: Revenue expenditure is spending on the day-to-day running of the business — it maintains existing assets and generates revenue in the current period. Examples: paying wages, buying inventory for resale, repair and maintenance costs, rent, electricity bills.
**Explanation**: Revenue expenditure keeps the business operating but does not acquire or improve non-current assets. A common error is classifying repair costs as capital expenditure — repairs maintain the existing state; they do not enhance value.
**Tags**: revenue-expenditure, definition, examples

### Question Q-1.5-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the difference between capital receipt and revenue receipt? Give one example of each.
**Answer**: Capital receipt is income from selling non-current assets or from issuing shares/capital introduction. Example: proceeds from selling an old machine. Revenue receipt is income earned from the normal trading activities of the business. Example: sales revenue, rent received, interest received on a bank deposit.
**Explanation**: Capital receipts come from disposing of long-term assets or raising capital; revenue receipts come from regular business operations. A common error is treating the sale of a non-current asset as sales revenue.
**Tags**: capital-receipt, revenue-receipt, distinction

### Question Q-1.5-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: Classify each of the following as capital or revenue expenditure: (a) $5,000 spent on repairing a delivery van, (b) $25,000 spent on a new delivery van, (c) $3,000 spent on installing a new engine that increases the van's capacity, (d) $200 spent on annual van insurance.
**Answer**: (a) Revenue expenditure — repair maintains the van in its current condition. (b) Capital expenditure — purchase of a non-current asset. (c) Capital expenditure — the new engine improves the asset's capacity, adding value beyond its original state. (d) Revenue expenditure — insurance is a recurring operating cost.
**Explanation**: The key distinction is whether spending adds value/improves the asset (capital) or simply maintains it (revenue). A common error is classifying the new engine as revenue expenditure because it is a "part" — but it enhances performance, which makes it capital.
**Tags**: classification, application, capital-expenditure, revenue-expenditure

### Question Q-1.5-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What happens to profit and asset values if capital expenditure is incorrectly classified as revenue expenditure?
**Answer**: Profit will be understated (too low) because the expenditure is charged as an expense in the income statement rather than recorded as an asset. Non-current assets will be understated in the statement of financial position because the asset acquisition is not recorded. In subsequent years, profit may be overstated because there will be no depreciation charge on the unrecorded asset.
**Explanation**: Misclassification distorts both the income statement and the statement of financial position. A common error is getting the direction wrong — recording a capital purchase as an expense increases total expenses, reducing profit.
**Tags**: misclassification, effect, profit, assets

### Question Q-1.5-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: What happens to profit and asset values if revenue expenditure is incorrectly classified as capital expenditure?
**Answer**: Profit will be overstated (too high) because the expenditure is recorded as an asset rather than an expense in the income statement. Non-current assets will be overstated in the statement of financial position because the asset figure includes spending that should have been expensed. Additionally, depreciation will be overstated in future years because depreciation is calculated on the inflated asset value.
**Explanation**: The opposite effect of Q-1.5-05. A common error is confusing the direction — recording an expense as an asset reduces expenses in the current year, inflating profit.
**Tags**: misclassification, effect, profit, assets

### Question Q-1.5-07
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business spends $8,000 on extending a building (capital expenditure) but records it as repairs (revenue expenditure). State the effect on: (a) net profit, (b) non-current assets, (c) capital.
**Answer**: (a) Net profit is understated by $8,000 because the $8,000 is treated as an expense instead of an asset. (b) Non-current assets are understated by $8,000 because the building extension is not recorded in the asset register. (c) Capital is understated because the lower profit means less profit is added to capital in the statement of financial position.
**Explanation**: All three figures are affected because the misclassification flows through the financial statements. A common error is only identifying one effect — the question asks for all three.
**Tags**: misclassification, effect, application

### Question Q-1.5-08
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Is the cost of buying inventory capital expenditure or revenue expenditure? Explain your answer.
**Answer**: Buying inventory is revenue expenditure. Inventory is a current asset held for resale in the ordinary course of business — it is not a non-current asset. The cost of inventory is treated as an expense (cost of sales) when the goods are sold, matched against the sales revenue in the income statement.
**Explanation**: A common error is classifying inventory purchases as capital expenditure because inventory is an "asset" — but inventory is a current asset, and the purchase of items for resale is a revenue (operating) activity, not a capital investment.
**Tags**: inventory, revenue-expenditure, explanation

### Question Q-1.5-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: "A business spends $500 on a new photocopier and $50 on repairing the old one. The owner argues both should be treated the same way because the amounts are small." Do you agree? Explain how each should be treated and why the amount spent is not the deciding factor.
**Answer**: Disagree. The $500 photocopier is capital expenditure because it acquires a new non-current asset that will be used for more than one year. The $50 repair is revenue expenditure because it maintains the existing asset in working order. The deciding factor is the nature of the spending (acquiring/improving vs. maintaining), not the amount. Even a small-cost item like a $5 calculator used for 5 years could technically be capital expenditure, though in practice a materiality threshold may be applied. The key principle: if the spending brings future economic benefit beyond the current period, it is capital; if it only maintains current capacity, it is revenue.
**Explanation**: The materiality concept allows small capital items to be expensed for practicality, but the underlying classification principle remains. A common error is agreeing that amount determines classification — the syllabus clearly states the distinction is based on purpose (acquisition/improvement vs. maintenance).
**Tags**: evaluation, materiality, capital-expenditure, revenue-expenditure
