# Items: 3.3 — Correction of errors

### Question Q-3.3-01
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the purpose of a suspense account? When is it opened?
**Answer**: A suspense account is a temporary account opened when the trial balance does not agree — that is, when total debits do not equal total credits. It holds the difference so that the trial balance can be balanced while the errors are investigated. Once the errors are found and corrected, the suspense account balance reduces to zero. If the suspense account still has a balance after all errors are corrected, it means there are still unidentified errors.
**Explanation**: The suspense account is not a permanent account — it is a placeholder. A common error is thinking a suspense account is used to hide errors. It does not hide them; it temporarily holds the imbalance so bookkeeping can continue while errors are traced. The balance in the suspense account equals the amount of the trial balance difference.
**Tags**: suspense-account, purpose, definition

### Question Q-3.3-02
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: What is the general format of a journal entry used to correct an error? What must every journal entry include?
**Answer**: A correcting journal entry has: the date, the two (or more) accounts to be debited and credited with amounts, and a narration (brief explanation). Every journal entry must have equal total debits and total credits, and the narration should describe what error is being corrected. Example: to correct a purchase of $500 wrongly debited to Repairs instead of Purchases — Debit Purchases $500, Credit Repairs $500, Narration: "Correction of error — purchase wrongly debited to Repairs account."
**Explanation**: Journal entries are the formal way to correct errors in the ledger. A common error is writing the journal without a narration or without ensuring debits = credits. The narration is essential so that anyone reading the ledger later understands why the entry was made.
**Tags**: journal-entry, format, correction

### Question Q-3.3-03
**Difficulty**: Foundation
**Question**: Write the correcting journal entries for each error: (a) A $300 purchase on credit was debited to Purchases $300 but credited to Trade receivables $300 instead of Trade payables. (b) A sale of $150 to Kavi was omitted entirely.
**Answer**: (a) Debit Trade receivables $300, Credit Trade payables $300, Narration: "Correction — credit on sale wrongly posted to Trade receivables instead of Trade payables." The Purchases debit was correct; only the credit side needs moving from the wrong account to the right one. (b) Debit Trade receivables (Kavi) $150, Credit Sales $150, Narration: "Correction — credit sale of $150 to Kavi omitted." The entire transaction was missing, so both sides must be entered.
**Explanation**: For (a), one side of the original entry was correct (Purchases debit $300) — only the credit needs correcting by removing it from Trade receivables and posting it to Trade payables. For (b), since nothing was recorded, both debit and credit must be added. A common error is also debiting Purchases again in (a) — that would double-count the purchase.
**Tags**: journal-entry, error-correction, practical

### Question Q-3.3-04
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: The trial balance shows debits exceed credits by $310. A suspense account is opened. Later these errors are found: (1) A sale of $200 was recorded as $290 in both the Sales account and the customer's account. (2) A purchase of $120 was posted to the Purchases account but not to the Trade payables account. Write the correcting journal entries and show how the suspense account is cleared.
**Answer**: Error (1): The wrong amount was used on both sides — both should be $200, not $290. Correction: Debit Sales $90, Credit Trade receivables $90, Narration: "Correction — sale recorded as $290 instead of $200." This reduces both the overstate. Error (2): Only one side was posted. Correction: Debit Purchases $120, Credit Suspense account $120, Narration: "Correction — credit entry for purchase of $120 omitted." Now for the suspense account: it was opened with a debit balance of $310 (to make the trial balance agree). After correction (2), the suspense account is credited $120. The remaining $190 debit balance in the suspense account means there are still unidentified errors of $190. Error (1) did not affect the suspense account because both sides used the same wrong amount.
**Explanation**: Only errors that cause a trial balance imbalance involve the suspense account. Error (1) had equal debits and credits ($290 each) so it never caused an imbalance — it is corrected directly between the two accounts. Error (2) had only a debit (Purchases $120) with no credit, so the missing credit goes to the suspense account. A common error is correcting error (1) through the suspense account — it should not be.
**Tags**: suspense-account, journal-entry, partial-clearance

### Question Q-3.3-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A suspense account has a credit balance of $420. The following errors are discovered: (1) A payment of $180 to a trade payable was correctly credited to Cash but debited to the correct Trade payables account — however, the amount was entered as $810 in the Trade payables account. (2) An irrecoverable debt of $240 was written off correctly in the Trade receivables account but not in the Irrecoverable debts expense account. Prepare correcting journal entries and show the suspense account after correction.
**Answer**: Error (1): The debit to Trade payables was $810 but should have been $180 — overstated by $630. Correction: Debit Suspense account $630, Credit Trade payables $630, Narration: "Correction — Trade payables debited $810 instead of $180, excess reversed." The suspense account was credited $420 (meaning credits exceeded debits by $420). The credit balance in suspense arose because somewhere a credit was recorded without a matching debit. Error (1) contributed $630 of excess debit, which means it does not explain the $420 credit difference — it actually made the debit side bigger. Error (2): The credit to Trade receivables ($240) was made but the debit to Irrecoverable debts was missing — credits exceeded debits by $240, contributing to the suspense account balance. Correction: Debit Irrecoverable debts expense $240, Credit Suspense account $240. After both corrections: Suspense account started with credit $420. Correction (1) debited suspense $630. Correction (2) credited suspense $240. Net: $420 credit - $630 debit + $240 credit = $30 credit remaining. The remaining $30 credit balance means there is still $30 of unidentified error (credits exceed debits by $30).
**Explanation**: The key is tracking how each error contributed to the original suspense balance. A common error is not considering whether an error increased or decreased the imbalance before posting to suspense. The suspense account is cleared by posting the correcting entry on the opposite side.
**Tags**: suspense-account, multi-error, calculation

### Question Q-3.3-06
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: After preparing a trial balance, a difference of $960 was found and placed in a suspense account. The following errors were later identified: (1) The purchases day book was undercast (added up short) by $400. (2) A discount allowed of $250 was correctly credited to Trade receivables but not debited to Discount allowed. (3) A cash sale of $710 was correctly recorded in the Cash account but was posted as $170 in the Sales account. Show the suspense account ledger and determine whether it has a zero balance after corrections.
**Answer**: First, determine the original suspense account position. Purchases day book undercast by $400 means Purchases (debit) is $400 too low. Discount allowed not debited means debit is $250 too low. Sales posted $170 instead of $710 means credit is $540 too low ($710 - $170). Total debit shortage: $400 + $250 = $650. Total credit shortage: $540. Debits fall short by $650, credits fall short by $540 — so credits exceed debits by $110. The suspense account must have a debit balance of $110 to make the trial balance agree. But the question states the difference was $960. This means there is an additional error of $850 ($960 - $110) that is not yet identified. Corrections: (1) Debit Suspense $400, Credit Purchases $400 (undercast corrected). (2) Debit Discount allowed $250, Credit Suspense $250 (missing debit supplied). (3) Debit Suspense $540, Credit Sales $540 (sales undercast corrected). Suspense account: Opening debit $960 + Correction (1) debit $400 + Correction (3) debit $540 - Correction (2) credit $250 = $1,650 debit balance remaining. The suspense account is NOT zero — there are additional errors not yet identified totaling $1,650.
**Explanation**: Day book undercasting is a common source of suspense. The purchases day book total is posted as a single debit to Purchases — if the total is wrong, only the Purchases account is wrong. A common error is thinking each individual purchase entry is wrong — only the summary posting is affected.
**Tags**: suspense-account, day-book, suspense-ledger

### Question Q-3.3-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business reported a net profit of $45,000. Later these errors were found: (1) A non-current asset purchase of $3,000 was debited to Purchases (expense). (2) A sale of $1,200 was completely omitted. (3) Rent expense of $800 was recorded as $80 on both sides. (4) Irrecoverable debts written off of $500 were debited to Trade payables instead of Irreconable debts expense. Calculate the revised net profit.
**Answer**: Start with reported profit $45,000. (1) Asset purchase $3,000 wrongly debited to Purchases — expenses are overstated by $3,000, so profit is understated by $3,000. Correction: add $3,000 to profit → $48,000. (2) Sale $1,200 omitted — revenue is understated by $1,200, profit is understated. Correction: add $1,200 → $49,200. (3) Rent recorded $80 instead of $800 — expenses understated by $720 ($800 - $80), profit overstated by $720. Correction: subtract $720 → $48,480. (4) Debts $500 debited to Trade payables instead of expense — expenses understated by $500 (the expense was never recorded), profit overstated by $500. Correction: subtract $500 → $47,980. Revised net profit: $47,980.
**Explanation**: Profit adjustment requires working through each error's effect on revenue or expenses. A common error is treating error (1) as having no effect because both sides are $3,000 — the trial balance agrees, but the profit is wrong because an asset purchase (capital expenditure) should not appear in the income statement as a purchase expense. Error (4) is also easy to miss: debiting Trade payables instead of the expense means the expense was never charged against profit.
**Tags**: revised-profit, profit-adjustment, evaluation

### Question Q-3.3-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: The trial balance did not agree and a suspense account was opened with a debit balance of $200. All errors have now been identified and corrected except one remaining error. After posting all known corrections, the suspense account still shows a debit balance of $75. What does this tell you? Write the journal entry that will clear the remaining suspense balance if the final error is identified as: a sales day book overcast (total overstated) by $75.
**Answer**: The remaining $75 debit balance in the suspense account after all known corrections means the unidentified error causes debits to exceed credits by $75. A sales day book overcast by $75 means the sales total was $75 too high — the Sales account (credit) was credited $75 too much. This means credits exceeded debits by $75, which would normally create a $75 debit balance in the suspense account — matching exactly what remains. Correction: Debit Sales $75, Credit Suspense account $75, Narration: "Correction — sales day book overcast by $75." After this entry, the suspense account balance becomes zero, confirming all errors have been identified and corrected.
**Explanation**: The suspense account balance after corrections equals the remaining unidentified error. A sales day book overcast means Sales (a credit account) is too high — to correct, we debit Sales to reduce it and credit Suspense to clear the balance. A common error is debiting Suspense instead of crediting it — the suspense account has a debit balance, so we need to credit it to reduce it to zero.
**Tags**: suspense-account, day-book, suspense-clearance

### Question Q-3.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: "A suspense account should always have a zero balance at the end of the financial year." Do you agree? Discuss what a non-zero suspense balance implies and whether financial statements should be prepared with a suspense account balance.
**Answer**: Agree — a suspense account should be cleared to zero before final financial statements are prepared. A non-zero suspense balance means the books contain unidentified errors: debits do not equal credits, and the exact cause is unknown. If financial statements are prepared with a suspense account balance, the statement of financial position will not balance (assets will not equal liabilities plus capital), because the suspense account balance is an unexplained plug figure. The suspense account is temporary by nature — it exists only while errors are investigated. Best practice: make every effort to identify and correct errors before the year end. If errors cannot be identified by year end, the suspense balance should be disclosed in the notes to the financial statements as an unresolved difference, but the accounts are still technically incomplete.
**Explanation**: The suspense account is a red flag, not a solution. A common error is thinking it is acceptable to leave a suspense account balance in the final accounts — it is not, because it represents a fundamental accounting inequality that has not been explained. Exam questions often ask candidates to evaluate whether financial statements are "complete" when a suspense account exists — the answer is always that they are not fully reliable.
**Tags**: suspense-account, evaluation, financial-statements
