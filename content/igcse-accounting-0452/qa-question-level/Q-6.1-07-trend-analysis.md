# Question Q-6.1-07

**Difficulty**: Challenge

**Question**: A business's liquidity ratios over three years: Year 1 — current ratio 1.8:1, acid test 1.1:1; Year 2 — current ratio 1.5:1, acid test 0.9:1; Year 3 — current ratio 1.3:1, acid test 0.7:1. Analyze the trend, identify possible causes and concerns.

**Answer**: Both ratios show a clear declining trend, indicating worsening liquidity. The current ratio fell from 1.8:1 (healthy) to 1.3:1 (below the 1.5:1 benchmark), and the acid test from 1.1:1 (acceptable) to 0.7:1 (concerning — the business cannot cover current liabilities without selling inventory). The gap between ratios has narrowed (0.7 in Year 1, 0.6 in Years 2-3), suggesting inventory is a growing proportion of remaining current assets. Possible causes: 1) Current liabilities growing faster than current assets (e.g. trade payables from delayed payments). 2) Cash balances declining (e.g. used to fund expansion). 3) Receivables growing more slowly than payables. Concerns: The business may struggle to pay suppliers on time, may need increased short-term borrowing, and could be approaching overtrading. Immediate action should focus on improving cash inflows (tighter credit control) and managing payables responsibly.

**Explanation**: Trend analysis is more valuable than a single year. A common error is stating ratios without interpreting the trend or linking causes to the decline pattern.
