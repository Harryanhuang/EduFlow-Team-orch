# Question Q-6.1-04

**Difficulty**: Standard

**Question**: What is the working capital cycle (cash conversion cycle)? How is it calculated and why does it matter?

**Answer**: The working capital cycle (also called the cash conversion cycle) measures the number of days it takes for a business to convert its investment in inventory and other current assets into cash from customers. It is calculated as: Working capital cycle = Inventory turnover days + Receivables collection days - Payables payment days. Where: Inventory turnover days = (Inventory / Cost of sales) × 365; Receivables collection days = (Receivables / Revenue) × 365; Payables payment days = (Payables / Cost of purchases) × 365. A shorter cycle is generally better — the business receives cash from customers faster than it pays suppliers. A long or negative cycle may indicate cash flow problems. It matters because it directly affects the business's cash flow and need for external financing.

**Explanation**: The key insight is that three separate ratios combine into one measure of cash flow efficiency. A common error is adding payables days instead of subtracting them — payables delay cash outflows, which shortens the cycle.
