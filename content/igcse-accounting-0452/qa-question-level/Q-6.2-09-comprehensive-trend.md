# Question Q-6.2-09

**Difficulty**: Challenge

**Question**: A business's profitability data: Revenue: Y1 $400,000, Y2 $480,000, Y3 $520,000. Cost of sales: Y1 $240,000, Y2 $312,000, Y3 $364,000. Operating expenses: Y1 $100,000, Y2 $115,000, Y3 $130,000. Capital employed: Y1 $300,000, Y2 $350,000, Y3 $400,000. Calculate all profitability ratios for each year and analyze the trend.

**Answer**:

**Year 1:**
- Gross profit = $160,000 | Gross margin = 40.0%
- Net profit = $60,000 | Net margin = 15.0%
- ROCE = ($60,000 / $300,000) × 100% = 20.0%

**Year 2:**
- Gross profit = $168,000 | Gross margin = ($168,000 / $480,000) × 100% = 35.0%
- Net profit = $53,000 | Net margin = ($53,000 / $480,000) × 100% = 11.0%
- ROCE = ($53,000 / $350,000) × 100% = 15.1%

**Year 3:**
- Gross profit = $156,000 | Gross margin = ($156,000 / $520,000) × 100% = 30.0%
- Net profit = $26,000 | Net margin = ($26,000 / $520,000) × 100% = 5.0%
- ROCE = ($26,000 / $400,000) × 100% = 6.5%

**Trend analysis:** All three ratios deteriorate significantly. Revenue grew 30% but profitability collapsed. Gross margin fell from 40% to 30% — cost of sales grew 52% while revenue grew only 30%, suggesting rising material costs or price pressure. Net margin fell from 15% to 5%. ROCE collapsed from 20% to 6.5% — profit fell while capital employed grew 33%. The business is expanding but becoming much less efficient. The ROCE of 6.5% may be below the cost of capital, meaning value destruction. Actions: investigate why cost of sales is growing faster than revenue, review pricing strategy, and consider whether capital expansion is justified by returns.

**Explanation**: Revenue growth with falling margins is a classic warning sign. A common error is concluding the business is "doing well" because revenue is growing — the ratios show the opposite.
