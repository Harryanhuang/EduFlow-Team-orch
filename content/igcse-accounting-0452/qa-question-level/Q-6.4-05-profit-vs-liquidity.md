# Question Q-6.4-05

**Difficulty**: Standard

**Question**: A business has improving profitability (gross margin 35% to 40%, net margin 12% to 18%) but deteriorating liquidity (current ratio 2.1:1 to 1.2:1, acid test 1.3:1 to 0.6:1). What could explain this? What are the risks?

**Answer**: The business is prioritizing profitability at the expense of liquidity. Possible explanations: 1) **Reducing inventory aggressively** — improves cost efficiency (better gross margin) but reduces current assets, worsening liquidity. 2) **Using cash to pay down debt** — improves profitability (less interest) but reduces cash balances. 3) **Paying suppliers more quickly** for discounts — improves gross margin but reduces cash. 4) **Investing heavily in non-current assets** — may improve future profitability but ties up cash now. Risks: 1) Cash flow difficulties despite appearing profitable — "profit rich, cash poor". 2) Struggling to pay suppliers if acid test drops below 0.6:1. 3) Missing opportunities due to lack of liquid funds. 4) Over-reliance on short-term borrowing increases financial risk.

**Explanation**: Profitability and liquidity can move in opposite directions. A common error is assuming improving profitability means the business is doing well overall. Always check both dimensions.
