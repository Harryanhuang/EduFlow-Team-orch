# Question Q-6.1-05

**Difficulty**: Standard

**Question**: What is overtrading? What liquidity symptoms indicate a business may be overtrading?

**Answer**: Overtrading occurs when a business expands too quickly without sufficient long-term finance to support the growth. Liquidity symptoms include: 1) **Deteriorating current ratio** — current liabilities grow faster than current assets. 2) **Low or declining acid test ratio** — the business struggles to maintain liquid assets. 3) **Lengthening receivables collection period** — extended credit offered to attract sales but slow collection. 4) **Increasing inventory levels** — more stock bought for growth but not selling fast enough. 5) **Extending payables payment period** — delaying supplier payments to conserve cash, damaging relationships. 6) **Frequent use of bank overdraft** — reliance on short-term borrowing for day-to-day operations. 7) **High revenue growth but low cash balances** — growing on paper but cash-poor.

**Explanation**: Overtrading is growth-without-finance. A common error is confusing it with general mismanagement — the key is that growth itself creates liquidity pressure because long-term finance has not increased to match.
