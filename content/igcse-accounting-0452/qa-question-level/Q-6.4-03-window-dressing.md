# Question Q-6.4-03

**Difficulty**: Foundation

**Question**: What is window dressing in financial statements? How can it be identified through ratio analysis?

**Answer**: Window dressing refers to actions taken to make financial statements look better than the underlying reality, typically at year end. Common techniques: 1) **Delaying inventory purchases** near year end — reduces payables and current liabilities, improving the current ratio. 2) **Aggressive receivables collection** before year end — reduces receivables days. 3) **Repaying short-term loans** just before year end — reduces gearing, then re-borrowing after. 4) **Selling assets and leasing back** — improves asset turnover and ROCE. 5) **Capitalizing expenses** that should be expensed — improves profit margins. Identification: compare year-end figures with monthly averages — a significant year-end improvement in a ratio is suspicious. Compare policies with prior years for changes. Look for one-off gains in the income statement. Window dressing is legal but misleading.

**Explanation**: A common error is confusing window dressing with fraud — window dressing uses legal accounting choices; fraud involves illegal misstatement.
