# Question Q-6.1-08

**Difficulty**: Challenge

**Question**: Evaluate whether a supermarket chain and a furniture retailer would have different 'acceptable' current and acid test ratios. Explain why.

**Answer**: Yes, acceptable ratios differ significantly by industry. A **supermarket chain** typically has: 1) Low current ratio (possibly below 1:1) — acceptable because inventory turns over very quickly, large daily cash generation, and strong bargaining power to delay supplier payments. Their working capital cycle may be negative (receive cash from customers before paying suppliers). 2) Low acid test ratio — supermarkets hold minimal receivables (mostly cash/card sales), so the acid test is naturally low but not concerning. A **furniture retailer** typically has: 1) Higher current ratio (1.5:1 to 2:1) — furniture has slower inventory turnover, meaning more capital tied up in stock. Receivables may be significant if credit terms are offered. 2) Higher acid test ratio needed — because inventory cannot be quickly converted to cash, more liquid assets are needed to cover short-term obligations. The key principle is that ratio benchmarks are industry-specific. Comparing ratios across industries without context leads to incorrect conclusions.

**Explanation**: Context matters. A common error is applying the same benchmark to all businesses. Always consider industry, business model, and working capital cycle when interpreting ratios.
