# Question Q-7.1-08

**Difficulty**: Challenge

**Question**: Evaluate how prudence, going concern, and accruals interact when a business has received a large order (revenue) but the customer's financial position is uncertain. Which concepts apply and how do they influence treatment?

**Answer**: Three concepts interact: **Prudence**: Revenue should not be recognized if there is significant uncertainty about collection. Even if goods are delivered, if the customer's position is doubtful, consider whether collection is probable. If doubtful, create an allowance for receivables or do not recognize revenue. **Going concern**: If the large order is a significant revenue portion and customer uncertainty threatens the business, the going concern assumption may be in doubt. Assess whether sufficient alternative revenue or funding exists for the next 12 months. **Accruals**: If revenue is recognized (goods delivered, performance obligation satisfied), record it in the delivery period regardless of cash timing. But prudence tempers this — if collection is not probable, do not recognize revenue even though delivery occurred. **Interaction**: Accruals says "record when earned"; prudence says "only if collection is probable"; going concern asks "can we survive without it?" Correct treatment: 1) If delivery occurred and collection is probable — recognize revenue and receivable. 2) If delivery occurred but collection doubtful — do not recognize revenue, or recognize with full allowance. 3) If uncertainty threatens survival — disclose going concern issues.

**Explanation**: Accounting concepts often interact and sometimes conflict. A common error is applying one concept in isolation — correct treatment requires balancing all relevant concepts.
