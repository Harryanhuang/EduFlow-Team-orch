### Question Q-3.4-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: During bank reconciliation, the accountant finds that the bank statement balance is $3,200 (credit) but the adjusted cash book balance is $3,650 (debit). The only timing differences are: unpresented cheques of $900 and a lodgement not yet credited of $450. Explain why these timing differences alone do not account for the full $450 difference between the two balances, and identify what additional error could explain it.
**Answer**: Starting from bank statement $3,200: add unpresented cheques $900 = $4,100; subtract uncredited lodgement $450 = $3,650. This matches the adjusted cash book exactly. So the timing differences DO fully explain the difference. There is no additional error. The $450 difference between bank statement ($3,200) and cash book ($3,650) is entirely accounted for by: unpresented cheques ($900 — cheques the business has paid out but the bank has not yet processed) and uncredited lodgement ($450 — money the business has deposited but the bank has not yet cleared). $3,200 + $900 - $450 = $3,650.
**Explanation**: A common error is overthinking and looking for hidden errors when the timing differences already reconcile perfectly. The key is to work through the reconciliation calculation: bank statement balance + unpresented cheques - uncredited deposits = adjusted cash book balance. If the numbers match, no further errors exist.
