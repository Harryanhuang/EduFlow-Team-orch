### Question Q-5.2-07
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: How do year-end adjustments affect both the income statement AND the statement of financial position? For each of the following adjustments, show the dual effect: (a) accrual of wages $2,000; (b) prepayment of insurance $1,500; (c) depreciation charge $5,000; (d) irrecoverable debt written off $800.
**Answer**: Every adjustment has a dual effect (double entry):
(a) Accrual of wages $2,000: Debit Wages expense (SPL, expense increases) $2,000; Credit Accruals (SOFP, current liability increases) $2,000. Net profit decreases by $2,000; liabilities increase by $2,000.
(b) Prepayment of insurance $1,500: Debit Prepayments (SOFP, current asset increases) $1,500; Credit Insurance expense (SPL, expense decreases) $1,500. Net profit increases by $1,500; assets increase by $1,500.
(c) Depreciation charge $5,000: Debit Depreciation expense (SPL, expense increases) $5,000; Credit Accumulated depreciation (SOFP, contra-asset increases, reducing carrying amount) $5,000. Net profit decreases by $5,000; net assets decrease by $5,000.
(d) Irrecoverable debt $800: Debit Irrecoverable debts expense (SPL, expense increases) $800; Credit Receivables (SOFP, current asset decreases) $800. Net profit decreases by $800; assets decrease by $800.
**Explanation**: A common error is only showing the income statement effect and forgetting the statement of financial position effect — every adjustment affects BOTH. The dual entry ensures the accounting equation remains balanced: Assets = Capital + Liabilities. Expenses reduce capital (through reduced profit), and the other entry adjusts assets or liabilities accordingly.
