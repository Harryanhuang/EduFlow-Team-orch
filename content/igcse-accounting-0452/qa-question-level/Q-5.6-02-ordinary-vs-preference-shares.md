# Question Q-5.6-02

**Difficulty**: Foundation

**Question**: What is share capital? Distinguish between ordinary shares and preference shares.

**Answer**: Share capital is money raised by a company through issuing shares to investors. **Ordinary shares**: 1) Carry voting rights at general meetings. 2) Dividends are variable — paid only if the company has sufficient profit and at the directors' discretion. 3) Last to receive payment in liquidation — after all debts, debentures, and preference shares are settled. 4) No fixed rate of return — returns depend on company performance. **Preference shares**: 1) Usually do not carry voting rights. 2) Receive a fixed dividend rate (e.g. 8% preference shares) before ordinary shareholders. 3) Have priority over ordinary shares in liquidation. 4) Dividends are often cumulative — if the company cannot pay in one year, unpaid dividends accumulate and must be paid before ordinary dividends resume.

**Explanation**: Ordinary shareholders are true owners with control but uncertain returns. Preference shareholders are closer to lenders — fixed income, priority, but no voting power. A common error is treating preference shares as debt — they are equity, and dividends are appropriations, not expenses.
