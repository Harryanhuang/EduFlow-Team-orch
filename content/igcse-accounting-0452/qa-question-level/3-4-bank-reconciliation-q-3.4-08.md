### Question Q-3.4-08
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: A business's bank statement shows a payment of $1,500 to supplier Zebra that the business does not recognize. Investigation reveals: (a) the bank made an error and credited the wrong account (another customer's cheque was wrongly debited to this business's account), and (b) separately, the cash book shows a cheque issued to supplier Tiger for $520 but the bank statement shows $250. Classify each as a bank error or company error, and explain how each should be treated in the bank reconciliation.
**Answer**: (a) The $1,500 payment the business does not recognize is a bank error — the bank debited the wrong account. Treatment: The business does NOT adjust its cash book for a bank error. Instead, the bank error is noted on the bank reconciliation statement as an adjustment to the bank statement balance (add $1,500 back to the bank statement balance, since the bank wrongly reduced the balance). The business should contact the bank to have the error corrected on their records. (b) The cheque for $520 recorded in the cash book but showing as $250 on the bank statement is also a bank error (assuming the business wrote the cheque for $520 and the bank processed it incorrectly). Treatment: Similarly, adjust the bank statement side of the reconciliation by the $270 difference. The cash book remains unchanged because the business recorded the correct amount. If the business made the error (wrote $250 in the cash book instead of $520), then the cash book would need correcting — but the question states the cash book shows $520, which is correct.
**Explanation**: A common error is adjusting the cash book for bank errors — the cash book should only be corrected for the business's own errors. Bank errors are corrected by the bank, not by the business. The bank reconciliation statement adjusts the bank statement balance for bank errors, and the adjusted cash book for the business's own errors.
