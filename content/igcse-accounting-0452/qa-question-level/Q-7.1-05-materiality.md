# Question Q-7.1-05

**Difficulty**: Standard

**Question**: What is the materiality concept? How does it affect decisions about what to include and how to present items?

**Answer**: The materiality concept states that financial statements should include all items significant enough to influence users' decisions. An item is material if its omission or misstatement could affect economic decisions. Applications: 1) **Small-value items** — a $10 calculator used for 5 years could be depreciated, but is immaterial — expensed immediately for practicality. 2) **Rounding** — figures rounded to nearest thousand or million because individual dollars are immaterial. 3) **Disclosure decisions** — a contingent liability's significance depends on the business's size. 4) **Aggregation** — small expense items grouped together. Materiality is relative — what is material for one business may not be for another. It depends on size, the item's proportion of total assets or profit, and the nature of the item.

**Explanation**: Materiality is about significance, not precision. A common error is thinking it has a fixed dollar threshold — it is relative to the business's size and users' needs. Consider both size and nature.
