# Question Q-6.1-09

**Difficulty**: Challenge

**Question**: A business has: Revenue: Year 1 $800,000, Year 2 $1,200,000. Cost of sales: Year 1 $480,000, Year 2 $780,000. Current assets: Year 1 $200,000 (inventory $80,000), Year 2 $280,000 (inventory $130,000). Current liabilities: Year 1 $120,000 (payables $60,000), Year 2 $200,000 (payables $110,000). Receivables: Year 1 $90,000, Year 2 $120,000. Calculate all liquidity ratios and the working capital cycle for each year. Comment on the liquidity position and whether there are signs of overtrading.

**Answer**:

**Year 1:**
- Current ratio = $200,000 / $120,000 = 1.67:1
- Acid test = ($200,000 - $80,000) / $120,000 = 1.0:1
- Inventory days = ($80,000 / $480,000) × 365 = 60.8 days
- Receivables days = ($90,000 / $800,000) × 365 = 41.1 days
- Payables days = ($60,000 / $480,000) × 365 = 45.6 days
- Working capital cycle = 60.8 + 41.1 - 45.6 = 56.3 days

**Year 2:**
- Current ratio = $280,000 / $200,000 = 1.4:1
- Acid test = ($280,000 - $130,000) / $200,000 = 0.75:1
- Inventory days = ($130,000 / $780,000) × 365 = 61.0 days
- Receivables days = ($120,000 / $1,200,000) × 365 = 36.5 days
- Payables days = ($110,000 / $780,000) × 365 = 51.5 days
- Working capital cycle = 61.0 + 36.5 - 51.5 = 46.0 days

**Comment:** Revenue grew 50% but liquidity deteriorated: current ratio fell from 1.67:1 to 1.4:1, acid test from 1.0:1 to 0.75:1. Current liabilities grew 67% vs current assets 40%. Inventory increased 63% and payables 83% — the business is stretching supplier payments to fund growth. Receivables days improved (41.1 to 36.5), but this is overshadowed by rapid payables growth. The working capital cycle improved from 56.3 to 46.0 days, but this is driven by extending payables, not improved efficiency. These are classic overtrading symptoms — revenue growth outpacing available long-term finance. The business should raise long-term capital and manage working capital more carefully.

**Explanation**: Always calculate all three component ratios before commenting. The working capital cycle improvement here is misleading — it comes from paying suppliers more slowly, not operational improvement. A common error is concluding improvement based solely on the shorter cycle.
