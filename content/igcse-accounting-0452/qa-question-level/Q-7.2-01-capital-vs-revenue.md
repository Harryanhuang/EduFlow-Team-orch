# Question Q-7.2-01

**Difficulty**: Foundation

**Question**: What is the distinction between capital expenditure and revenue expenditure? Why does it matter for financial statements?

**Answer**: Capital expenditure is spending on non-current assets that provides long-term benefits (more than one period) — e.g. buying property, machinery, vehicles. Revenue expenditure is spending on day-to-day running — benefits consumed within the period — e.g. wages, rent, utilities, repairs. It matters because: 1) **Income statement** — revenue expenditure is expensed in the period, reducing profit. Capital expenditure is recorded as an asset and depreciated over its useful life. 2) **Balance sheet** — capital expenditure increases non-current assets; revenue expenditure does not. 3) **Profit measurement** — misclassifying capital as revenue understates current profit; misclassifying revenue as capital overstates it.

**Explanation**: The key test is duration of benefit. A common error is thinking capital expenditure only means buildings — it includes any expenditure providing benefits beyond the current period.
