# Question Q-7.2-08

**Difficulty**: Challenge

**Question**: A property developer spends $200,000 purchasing an old building, $80,000 demolishing it, $500,000 constructing a new office block, and $30,000 legal fees. Classify each. What if instead $50,000 was spent renovating the existing building to create additional rental units?

**Answer**:

**Scenario 1 — Demolition and rebuild:**
- Purchase $200,000 — Capital (acquisition)
- Demolition $80,000 — Capital (necessary to prepare site for new building)
- Construction $500,000 — Capital (creation of new asset)
- Legal fees $30,000 — Capital (directly attributable to acquisition)
- Total capitalized = $810,000

All costs are capital — they relate to acquisition and creation of a new asset. Demolition is part of the new building's cost.

**Scenario 2 — Renovation:**
- Renovation $50,000 for additional rental units — Capital (enhancement/addition). Creates new income-generating capacity beyond the original state. Not maintenance — creates additional value.

If $50,000 had been spent on repainting and repairing without adding anything new, it would be revenue (maintenance). The key: does it create something new/additional (capital) or restore what exists (revenue)?

**Explanation**: Demolition costs are capital when part of a plan to create a new asset. A common error is treating demolition as an expense — it is a necessary cost of the new building.
