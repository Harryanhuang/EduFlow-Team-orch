### Question Q-3.3-09
**Difficulty**: Challenge
**Question**: "A suspense account should always have a zero balance at the end of the financial year." Do you agree? Discuss what a non-zero suspense balance implies and whether financial statements should be prepared with a suspense account balance.
**Answer**: Agree — a suspense account should be cleared to zero before final financial statements are prepared. A non-zero suspense balance means the books contain unidentified errors: debits do not equal credits, and the exact cause is unknown. If financial statements are prepared with a suspense account balance, the statement of financial position will not balance (assets will not equal liabilities plus capital), because the suspense account balance is an unexplained plug figure. The suspense account is temporary by nature — it exists only while errors are investigated. Best practice: make every effort to identify and correct errors before the year end. If errors cannot be identified by year end, the suspense balance should be disclosed in the notes to the financial statements as an unresolved difference, but the accounts are still technically incomplete.
**Explanation**: The suspense account is a red flag, not a solution. A common error is thinking it is acceptable to leave a suspense account balance in the final accounts — it is not, because it represents a fundamental accounting inequality that has not been explained. Exam questions often ask candidates to evaluate whether financial statements are "complete" when a suspense account exists — the answer is always that they are not fully reliable.
