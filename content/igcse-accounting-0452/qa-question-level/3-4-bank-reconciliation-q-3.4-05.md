### Question Q-3.4-05
**Difficulty**: Standard
**Question**: A business receives its bank statement and notices a cheque from customer Ana for $350 was dishonoured (returned unpaid). The original receipt had been recorded in the cash book. Explain the effect on the cash book and write the correcting entry. Also explain why a standing order payment does NOT require this type of correction.
**Answer**: The dishonoured cheque means the $350 that was previously debited in the cash book (when the cheque was received) must be reversed. Correction: Credit cash book $350, Narration: "Correction — cheque from Ana dishonoured, payment not received." The business still has a receivable from Ana — she now owes the $350 again. A standing order payment does not require this type of reversal because a standing order is an actual payment made by the bank on the business's instructions — the money has genuinely left the account. The standing order simply needs to be recorded in the cash book (credit side) because the business did not record it earlier; it is not reversing a previous entry.
**Explanation**: A common error is treating dishonoured cheques the same as unrecorded payments — they are fundamentally different. A dishonoured cheque reverses a previously recorded receipt (the money never arrived); a standing order records a genuine payment that was missed.
